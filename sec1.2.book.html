1.2 Basic Concepts
Basic Concepts
A differential equation is an equation that contains one or more derivatives of an unknown function. The order of a differential equation is the order of the highest derivative that it contains. A differential equation is an ordinary differential equation if it involves an unknown function of only one variable, or a partial differential equation if it involves partial derivatives of a function of more than one variable. For now we’ll consider only ordinary differential equations, and we’ll just call them differential equations.
The simplest differential equations are first order equations of the form \[ {dy\over dx}=f(x) \] where \(f=f(x)\) is a known function of only \(x\) and we can solve them using calculus
When a linear differential equation only has the derivative of the dependent variable present and not the dependent variable itself, we will call the equation integrable since we can immediately solve by either finding the antiderivative or by integrating. \begin {align*} \frac{dy}{dx} = & f(x) \Longrightarrow y = c + \int f(x) dx \end {align*}
We already know from calculus from the power rule how to find functions that satisfy this kind of equation by finding the antiderivative. For example, if \[ \frac{dy}{dx}=x^3\] then \[ y=\int x^3 dx=\frac{1}{4} x^4 + c \] where \(c\) is an arbitrary constant.
Solve the differential equation \( \displaystyle \frac{dy}{dx}=x^2 \)
We can even solve second order differential equations of this form \[ {d^2y\over dx^2}=x^3 \] The antiderivative of the second derivative is the first derivative \[ \frac{dy}{dx} = \int \Bigg({d^2y\over dx^2} \Bigg) dx = \int x^3 dx = \frac{1}{4} x^4 + c \] But to get the function we simply antidifferentiate again \[ y = \int \Bigg( \frac{dy}{dx} \Bigg) dx = \int \Big( \frac{1}{4} x^4 + c_1 \Big) dx = \frac{1}{20} x^5 + c_1x + c_2 \] Notice this time our solution has two arbitrary constants \(c_1\) and \(c_2\)
Use antiderivative properties to show \( \displaystyle \int \Big( \frac{1}{4} x^4 + c_1 \Big) dx = \frac{1}{20} x^5 + c_1x + c_2 \)
Again we can solve any other simple higher order differential equations by repeated antidifferentiation or repeated integration \begin {equation} \label {eq:1.2.1} \frac{d^ny}{dx^n}=f(x) \end {equation}
Solve the differential equation \( \displaystyle \frac{dy}{dx} = \sqrt[5]{x} \)
Solve the differential equation \( \displaystyle \frac{d^2y}{dx^2} = \frac{1}{x^8} \)
Solve the differential equation \( \displaystyle \frac{d^3y}{dx^3} = \cos x \)
Solve the differential equation \( \displaystyle \frac{d^4y}{dx^4} = e^x \)
Since we already sovled these in calculus, we ususally won't consider differential equations like (1.2.1) and we’ll usually consider differential equations that can be written as \begin {equation} \label {eq:1.2.2} \frac{d^ny}{dx^n}=f\left(x,y,\frac{dy}{dx}, \dots ,\frac{d^{n-1}y}{dx^{n-1}}\right) \end {equation} where either the function itself, \(y\), or one of its derivatives \(\displaystyle \frac{dy}{dx}\), …, \(\displaystyle \frac{d^{n-1}y}{dx^{n-1}}\) is also present in the equation. While we will be using antidifferentiation to solve these differential equations as well, it will not be as straightforward as a basic calculus problem.
Here are some examples of differential equations we will be solving \[ \begin {array}{rcll} \displaystyle {dy\over dx}-x^2&=&0&\mbox { (first order)}\\ \\ \displaystyle {dy\over dx}+2xy^2&=&-2&\mbox { (first order)}\\ \\ \displaystyle {d^2y\over dx^2}+2\displaystyle {dy\over dx}+y&=&2x&\mbox { (second order)}\\ \\ \displaystyle x\frac{d^3y}{dx^3}+y^2&=&\sin x &\mbox { (third order)}\\ \\ \displaystyle \frac{d^ny}{dx^n}+x\frac{dy}{dx}+3y&=&x&\mbox { ($n$-th order)} \end {array} \] Differential equations we encounter may be written in this form, but we can easily rearrange them to make them in the form of (1.2.2) so we can easily identify \( f\left(x,y,{dy\over dx},{d^2y\over dx^2} \dots,{d^ny\over dx^n}\right)\) \begin{align*} {dy\over dx} & = \overset{f(x)}{\overbrace{x^2}} \\ \\ {dy\over dx} & = \overset{f(x,y)}{\overbrace{-2 - 2xy^2}} \\ \\ {d^2y\over dx^2} & = \overset{f\left(x,y,{dy\over dx}\right)}{\overbrace{2x - y -2{dy\over dx}}} \\ \\ {d^3y\over dx^3} & = \overset{f(x,y)}{\overbrace{\frac{\sin{x}}{x} - \frac{y^2}{x}}} \\ \\ {d^ny\over dx^n} &= \overset{f\left(x,y,{dy\over dx}\right)}{\overbrace{x - 3y -x{dy\over dx}}} \end{align*}
Solutions of Differential Equations
A solution of a differential equation is a function that satisfies the differential equation on some open interval thus \(y(x)\) is a solution of (1.2.2) if \(y\) is \(n\) times differentiable and \[ \frac{d^ny}{dx^n}=f\left(x,y,\frac{dy}{dx}, \dots ,\frac{d^{n-1}y}{dx^{n-1}}\right) \] for all \(x\) in some open interval \((a,b).\) We also say that \(y(x)\) is a solution of (1.2.2) on \((a,b)\)
We only consider functions that satisfy a differential equation on an open interval to be solutions. For instance, consider the differential equation \[ x\frac{dy}{dx}+x^2=3x \] The function \(y(x) = x^2\) does satisfy the differential equation but only at two isolated points \(x=0\) and \(x=1\)
Show that \( y(x) = x^2 \) satisfies \( \displaystyle x\frac{dy}{dx}+x^2=3x \) but only at \(x=0\) and \(x=1\)
Although \( y(x)=x^2 \) does satisfy \( \displaystyle x{dy\over dx}+x^2=3x\) it is only true at two isolated points and since this is not an open interval, then we do not consider \( y(x)=x^2 \) to be a solution of the differential equation.
The graph of a solution of a differential equation is a solution curve. More generally, a curve \(C\) is said to be an integral curve of a differential equation if every function \(y=y(x)\) whose graph is a segment of \(C\) is a solution of the differential equation. Thus, any solution curve of a differential equation is an integral curve, but an integral curve need not be a solution curve
Show that all the segments of the circle \begin {equation} \label {eq:1.2.3} x^2+y^2=9 \end {equation} are solution curves and the circle itself is an integral curve of the differential equation \begin {equation} \label {eq:1.2.4} \frac{dy}{dx}=-{x\over y} \end {equation}
Solution
Although the circle (1.2.3) is not a function, we can solve for \( y \) to obtain two separate functions that are segments of the circle: \[ y_1(x)=\sqrt {9-x^2}\quad \mbox{ and } \quad y_2(x)=-\sqrt {9-x^2}\]
Solve \( x^2+y^2=9 \) explicitly for \( y \) and show how to get the equations of the semi-circle segments
By differentiating and substituting, it can be easily verified that both of the semi-circle segments, \( y_1 \) and \( y_2 \), are solutions to the differential equation.
Show that \( y_1 \) and \( y_2 \) satisfy \( \displaystyle \frac{dy}{dx}=-{x\over y}\)
Note that all the segments of the circle that are functions (i.e. the semi-circles \( y_1 \) and \( y_2 \)) are solutions to the differential equation (1.2.4) and thus called solution curves. Since all of the segments of the circle are solutions, the circle itself is an integral curve of the equation, but it is not a solution curve since the circle itself is not the graph of a function and is not itself a solution to the differential equation.

Substituting \( y \) (1.2.5) and \( \displaystyle \frac{dy}{dx}={2x\over 3} - {1\over x^2} \) into (1.2.6) yields \begin{align*} x \frac{dy}{dx} + y & = x^2 \\ x\left( \frac{2x}{3} - \frac{1}{x^2} \right) + \left({x^2 \over 3} + {1 \over x}\right) & \overset{?}{=} x^2 \\ \frac{2x^2}{3} - \frac{1}{x} + {x^2 \over 3} + {1 \over x} & \overset{?}{=} x^2 \\ \frac{3}{3} x^2 & \overset{\checkmark}{=} x^2 \\ \end{align*} Note that (1.2.5) satisfies the differential equation on all \( x \ne 0 \) since \( y \) is undefined at \( x = 0 \) therefore \( y \) is a solution of (1.2.6) on the open intervals \((-\infty ,0)\) and \((0,\infty )\) and \( y \) is not a solution on any open interval that contains \(x=0\) since \(y\) is not defined at \(x=0\)

Calculate \( \displaystyle \frac{dy}{dx} \) and \( \displaystyle \frac{d^2y}{dx^2} \) for \( \displaystyle y=(c_1+c_2x)e^{-x}+2x-4 \) using the product rule
Solution
First note that (1.2.7) is defined for all values of \(x\) on \( (-\infty, \infty) \)
Differentiating (1.2.7) twice yields \[ \frac{dy}{dx} = 2 + (-c_1 + c_2 -c_2x) e^{-x} \quad \text{and} \quad \frac{d^2y}{dx^2} = (c_1 - 2c_2 + c_2x) e^{-x} \] Substituting in to (1.2.8) \begin{align*} \begin{array}{rlllllrll} \displaystyle \frac{d^2y}{dx^2} = & & & & & ( & c_1 & - & 2c_2 & + & c_2 x & )e^{-x} \\ 2 \displaystyle \frac{dy}{dx} = & & & 4 & + & ( & -2c_1 & + & 2c_2 & - & 2c_2 x & )e^{-x} \\ + \quad \quad \quad \quad \quad y = & 2x & - & 4 & + & ( & c_1 & & & + & c_2 x & )e^{-x} \\ \hline \\ \displaystyle \frac{d^2y}{dx^2} + 2 \displaystyle\frac{dy}{dx} + y = & 2x & & & + & ( & \overset{1-2+1}{\overbrace{0}}c_1 & + & \overset{-2+2}{\overbrace{0}}c_2 & + & \overset{1-2+1}{\overbrace{0}} c_2x & )e^{-x} \\ \\ \displaystyle \frac{d^2y}{dx^2} + 2 \displaystyle\frac{dy}{dx} + y \overset{\checkmark}{=} & 2x & & & & & & & & & & \\ \end{array} \end{align*} Therefore (1.2.7) is a solution of (1.2.8) on \((-\infty ,\infty )\)
Find all solutions of \begin {equation} \label {eq:1.2.9} \frac{d^ny}{dx^n}=e^{2x}\end {equation}
Solution
Solution   Antidifferentiating repeatedly (1.2.9) yields \begin{align*} \frac{d^{n-1}y}{dx^{n-1}} = & \frac{1}{2} e^{2x} + k_1 \\ \\ \frac{d^{n-2}y}{dx^{n-2}} = & \frac{1}{4} e^{2x} + k_1x + k_2 \\ \\ \frac{d^{n-3}y}{dx^{n-3}} = & \frac{1}{8} e^{2x} + \frac{1}{2}k_1x^2 + k_2x + k_3 \\ \\ \frac{d^{n-4}y}{dx^{n-4}} = & \frac{1}{16} e^{2x} + \frac{1}{6}k_1x^3 + \frac{1}{2}k_2x^2 + k_3x + k_4 \\ \\ \frac{d^{n-5}y}{dx^{n-5}} = & \frac{1}{32} e^{2x} + \frac{1}{24}k_1x^4 + \frac{1}{6}k_2x^3 + \frac{1}{2}k_3x^2 + k_4x + k_5 \\ \\ \end{align*}
Show all the work for calculating the first five antiderivatives
Notice the pattern on the \( e^{2x} \) terms; the \( n^{th} \) derivative has a factor of \( \displaystyle \frac{1}{2^n} \): \[ \frac{1}{2}, \frac{1}{4}, \frac{1}{8}, \frac{1}{16}, \frac{1}{32}, \dots \]
Notice the pattern on the rest of the terms; the coefficients are factorials: \[ \underset{0!}{\underbrace{1}}, \underset{1!}{\underbrace{1}}, \underset{2!}{\underbrace{2}}, \underset{3!}{\underbrace{6}}, \underset{4!}{\underbrace{24}}, \dots \]
Incorporating these patterns, flipping the order of the polynomial terms, and renaming the arbitrary constants, we can rewrite the fifth antiderivative more simply: \begin{align*} \frac{d^{n-5}y}{dx^{n-5}} = & \frac{1}{32} e^{2x} + \frac{1}{24}k_1x^4 + \frac{1}{6}k_2x^3 + \frac{1}{2}k_3x^2 + k_4x + k_5 \\ = & \frac{1}{2^5} e^{2x} + \frac{1}{4!}k_1x^4 + \frac{1}{3!}k_2x^3 + \frac{1}{2!}k_3x^2 + \frac{1}{1!}k_4x + \frac{1}{0!}k_5 \\ = & \frac{1}{2^5} e^{2x} + \frac{1}{0!}K_0 + \frac{1}{1!} K_1 x + \frac{1}{2!} K_2 x^2 + \frac{1}{3!} K_3 x^3 + \frac{1}{4!} K_4 x^4 \\ = & \frac{1}{2^5} e^{2x} + \underbrace{ c_0 + c_1 x + c_2 x^2 + c_3 x^3 + c_4 x^4 } \\ = & \frac{1}{2^5} e^{2x} + \sum_{n=0}^{4} { c_n x^n } \end{align*}
Therefore all solutions of (1.2.9) can be written as \begin {equation} \label {eq:1.2.10} y(x) = \frac{1}{2^n} e^{2x} + c_0 + c_1 x + c_2 x^2 + \cdots +c_n x^n = \frac{1}{2^n} e^{2x} + \sum_{n=0}^{n} { c_n x^n } \end {equation} where we renamed the arbitrary constants to make the formula as simple as possible. As a general rule, arbitrary constants appearing in solutions of differential equations should be simplified whenever possible.

Initial Value Problems
In Example 1.2.4 we found that the differential equation \( \displaystyle \frac{d^ny}{dx^n}=e^{2x}\) has an infinite family of solutions (1.2.10) that depend upon the \(n\) arbitrary constants \(c_1\), \(c_2\), …, \(c_n\). Without additional conditions, there’s no reason to prefer one solution of a differential equation over another and we might opt for the general solution including the arbitrary constants \begin {equation*} y(x) = \frac{1}{2^n} e^{2x} + \sum_{n=0}^{n} { c_n x^n } \end {equation*} However, in real-life applications of differential equations, we often want to find a particular solution of a differential equation that satisfies one or more specific initial conditions which we call an initial value problem (IVP).
Find a solution of the differential equation such that \(y(1)=2\) \[ \frac{dy}{dx}=x^3 \]
Solution
At the beginning of this section we solved this differential equation \(\displaystyle \frac{dy}{dx}=x^3\) by finding the antiderivative \[ y=\int x^3 dx=\frac{1}{4} x^4 + c \] The antiderivative represents the general solution of the differential equation and includes the arbitrary constant of integration. We can determine a particular value of \(c\) that satisfies the given initial condition \(y(1)=2\), by simply evaluating the solution at \( x=2 \) \begin{align*} y(x) = & \frac{1}{4}x^{4} + c \\ \\ \underset{2}{\underbrace{y(1)}} = & \frac{1}{4}\left(1\right)^{4} + c \\ 2 = & \frac{1}{4}+ c \Longrightarrow c = \frac{7}{4} \\ \end{align*} Once we have a particular \( c = \frac{7}{4} \) simply substituting it back in to the general solution to obtain the particular solution: \[ y(x) = \frac{1}{4}x^{4} + \frac{7}{4} = \frac{1}{4}\left(x^2 + 7\right) \]
Figure 1.2.2 shows the graph of this solution. Note that imposing the condition \(y(1)=2\) is equivalent to requiring the graph of \(y\) to pass through the point \((1,2)\).
We can rewrite the problem considered in Example 1.2.5 more briefly as an initial value problem (IVP): \[ \frac{dy}{dx}=x^3 \quad y(1)=2\] First we write the differential equation followed by the requirement that \(y(1)=2\) which we call initial condition.
IVPs can also be written for higher order differential equations with additional initial conditions. For instance, consider the second order differential equation: \begin {equation} \label {eq:1.2.11} \frac{d^2y}{dx^2} - 2\frac{dy}{dx}+3y=e^x \quad y(0)=1 \quad \left.\frac{dy}{dx}\right|_0=2 \end {equation} In this case there are two initial conditions, one for \(y\) and one for \(\displaystyle \frac{dy}{dx}\) and both are required to have specified values at \(x=0\). In general, an initial value problem for an \(n^{th}\) order differential equation requires \(n \) initial conditions specified on \(y\) and its first \(n-1\) derivatives at some initial point \(x_0\).
We’ll denote an initial value problem for a differential equation by writing the initial conditions after the differential equation, as in (1.2.11). For example, we would write an IVP for (1.2.2) as \begin {align}\label {eq:1.2.12} \frac{d^ny}{dx^n}=f\left(x,y,\frac{dy}{dx},\frac{d^2y}{dx^2}, \dots ,\frac{d^{n-1}y}{dx^{n-1}}\right) \quad y(x_0) = & k_0 \nonumber \\ \quad \left.\frac{dy}{dx}\right|_{x_0} = & k_1 \nonumber \\ \quad \left.\frac{d^2y}{dx^2}\right|_{x_0} = & k_2 \nonumber \\ & \vdots \nonumber \\ \quad \left.\frac{d^{n-1}y}{dx^{n-1}}\right|_{x_0} = & k_{n-1} \end {align} Consistent with our earlier definition of a solution of the differential equation in (1.2.12), we say that \(y\) is a solution of the initial value problem (1.2.12) if \(y\) is \(n\) times differentiable and \[ \frac{d^ny}{dx^n}=f\left(x,y,\frac{dy}{dx},\frac{d^2y}{dx^2}, \dots ,\frac{d^{n-1}y}{dx^{n-1}}\right) \] for all \(x\) in some open interval \((a,b)\) that contains \(x_0\), and \(y\) satisfies the initial conditions in (1.2.12). We also say that \( y(x) \) is a solution of (1.2.12) on the open interval \( (a,b) \) and the largest open interval that contains \( x_0 \) on which \( y(x) \) is defined and satisfies the differential equation is called the interval of validity (IOV) of \(y(x)\)
In Example 1.2.5 we found that (1.2.13) is a solution of the IVP \[ \frac{dy}{dx} = x^3 \quad y(1) = 2 \] Determine the interval of validity (IOV) of this solution. \begin {equation} \label {eq:1.2.13} y = \frac{1}{4} \left( x^4 + 7 \right) \end {equation}
Solution
Since the function in (1.2.13) is defined for all \(x\) values, the interval of validity is \((-\infty ,\infty ) \)

Free Fall Under Constant Gravity
The term initial value problem originated in problems of motion where the independent variable is \(t\) (representing elapsed time), and the initial conditions are the position and velocity of an object at the initial time of an experiment.
An object falls under the influence of gravity near Earth’s surface, where it can be assumed that the magnitude of the acceleration due to gravity is a constant \(g\). Assume that gravity is the only force acting on the object.
  1. Using Newton’s 2nd law of motion, construct a mathematical model for the motion of the object in the form of an IVP for a second order differential equation, assuming that the height and velocity of the object at time \(t=0\) are known.
  2. Solve the IVP to obtain the height as a function of time
Solution
  1. Recall Newton’s 2nd law of motion, \( F = ma \), the force \( F \) acting on the object is equal to the mass \( m \) of the object times the acceleration \( a \) of the object. Acceleration is the derivative of velocity and velocity is the derivative of position so position is the second derivative of acceleration and can be written as \( a = \ddot{y} \) where \(y(t)\) represent the position (height) of the object at time \(t\). The only force acting on the object is gravity and the acceleration due to gravity is in the downward (negative) direction, so the total force \( F \) is given by \( -mg \). Thus Newton’s 2nd law of motion can be rewritten to represent a second order differential equation modeling this situation: \begin{equation*} F = ma \Longrightarrow -mg = m \ddot{y} \Longrightarrow -g = \ddot{y} \end{equation*} where the dot notation indicates differentiation with respect to \(t\). If \(y_0 \) denotes the initial height and \(v_0\) denotes the initial velocity when \(t=0\), then \(y\) is a solution of the initial value problem \begin {equation} \label {eq:1.2.17} \ddot{y}=-g \quad y(0)=y_0 \quad \dot{y}(0)=v_0 \end {equation}
  2. The IVP can be solved by antidifferentiating \begin {align*} \ddot{y} = -g \Longrightarrow \int \ddot{y} \, dt = & -g \int dt \\ \Longrightarrow \dot{y} = & -gt + c \end {align*} Applying the initial condition \( \dot{y}(0)=v_0 \) we can show \(c=v_0\) so our equation becomes \( \dot{y} = -gt + v_0 \) and not we just antidifferentiate again: \begin {align*} \dot{y} = -gt + v_0 \Longrightarrow \int \dot{y} dt = & -g \int t dt + v_0 \int dy \\ \Longrightarrow y = & -\frac{1}{2}gt^2 + v_0 t + c \\ \end {align*} Applying the initial condition \(y(0)=y_0\) we can show \(c=y_0\) so the solution of (1.2.17) is \[ y = -\frac{1}{2}gt^2 + v_0 t + y_0 \]
