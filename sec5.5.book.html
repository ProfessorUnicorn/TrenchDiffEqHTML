5.5 The Method of Undetermined Coefficients II
Undetermined Coefficients
In this section we consider the constant coefficient equation \begin {equation} \label {eq:5.5.1} a\frac{d^2y}{dx^2}+b\frac{dy}{dx}+cy =\Big( P(x)\cos \omega x+Q(x)\sin \omega x \Big) \, e^{\lambda x} \end {equation} Where \(\lambda \) and \(\omega \) are real numbers, \(\omega \ne 0\), and \(P(x) \) and \(Q(x) \) are polynomials. We want to find a particular solution of (5.5.1). As in Section 5.4, the procedure that we will use is called the method of undetermined coefficients.
We begin with the case where \(\lambda =0\) in (5.5.1); thus, we we want to find a particular solution of \begin {equation} \label {eq:5.5.2} a\frac{d^2y}{dx^2}+b\frac{dy}{dx}+cy=P(x)\cos \omega x+Q(x)\sin \omega x \end {equation} where \(P(x)\) and \(Q(x)\) are polynomials.
Differentiating \(x^r\cos \omega x\) and \(x^r\sin \omega x\) yields
\begin {eqnarray*} \displaystyle {d\over dx}x^r\cos \omega x&=&-\omega x^r\sin \omega x+ rx^{r-1}\cos \omega x \\ \text {and} \displaystyle {d\over dx}x^r\sin \omega x&=&\phantom {-}\omega x^r\cos \omega x+ rx^{r-1}\sin \omega x \end {eqnarray*}
This implies that if \[ y_p=A(x)\cos \omega x+B(x)\sin \omega x \] where \(A\) and \(B\) are polynomials, then \[ a\frac{d^2y_p}{dx^2}+b\frac{dy_p}{dx}+cy_p=F(x)\cos \omega x+G(x)\sin \omega x\] where \(F\) and \(G\) are polynomials with coefficients that can be expressed in terms of the coefficients of \(A\) and \(B\). This suggests that we try to choose \(A\) and \(B\) so that \(F=P\) and \(G=Q\), respectively. Then \(y_p\) will be a particular solution of (5.5.2). The next theorem tells us how to choose the proper form for \(y_p\). For the proof see Exercise 37.
Theorem 5.5.1 Suppose \(\omega \) is a positive number and \(P\) and \(Q\) are polynomials. Let \(k\) be the larger of the degrees of \(P\) and \(Q\). Then the equation \[ a\frac{d^2y}{dx^2}+b\frac{dy}{dx}+cy=P(x)\cos \omega x+Q(x)\sin \omega x \] has a particular solution \begin {equation} \label {eq:5.5.3} y_p=A(x)\cos \omega x+B(x)\sin \omega x \end {equation} where \[ A(x)=A_0+A_1x+\cdots +A_kx^k \quad \mbox{ and } \quad B(x)=B_0+B_1x+\cdots +B_kx^k\] provided that \(\cos \omega x\) and \(\sin \omega x\) are not solutions of the complementary equation. The solutions of \[ a(\frac{d^2y}{dx^2}+\omega ^2y)=P(x)\cos \omega x+Q(x)\sin \omega x \] \((\)for which \(\cos \omega x\) and \(\sin \omega x\) are solutions of the complementary equation\()\) are of the form (5.5.3), where \[ A(x)=A_0x+A_1x^2+\cdots +A_kx^{k+1} \quad \mbox{ and } \quad B(x)=B_0x+B_1x^2+\cdots +B_kx^{k+1}\]
For an analog of this theorem thatâ€™s applicable to (5.5.1), see Exercise 38.
Sines and Cosines \( \cos \omega x + \sin \omega x \)
Given the differential equation \begin {equation} \label {eq:5.5.4} \frac{d^2y}{dx^2}-2\frac{dy}{dx}+y = 5\cos 2x+10\sin 2x \end {equation}
  1. Solve the complementary equation
  2. Find a particular solution
  3. Find the general solution
Solution
  1. Solve the complementary equation \begin {equation*} \frac{d^2y}{dx^2}-2\frac{dy}{dx}+y = 0 \end {equation*}
    Find the characteristic equation
    Use the method in Example 5.2.1 to find a fundamental set of solutions
    Solving the characteristic equation we obtain a fundamental set of solutions \[ y_1 = e^{x} \quad y_2 = xe^{x} \]
  2. Now we will try to find a particular solution for the nonhomogeneous equation by guessing a solution based on the forcing function \[ \frac{d^2y}{dx^2}-2\frac{dy}{dx}+y = \underset{f(x)}{\underbrace{5\cos 2x+10\sin 2x}} \]
    Since the forcing function \( f(x) = 5\cos 2x+10\sin 2x \) is a linear combination of sines and cosines, we can guess a linear combination of sines and cosines with the same argument \(2x\) \[ y = A\cos 2x + B\sin 2x \]
    Differentiate \(y = A\cos 2x + B\sin 2x \) twice
    After differentiating we obtain \( dy/dx = 2B\cos 2x - 2A\sin 2x \) and \(d^2y/dx^2 = -4A\cos 2x + 4B\sin 2x \) which we substitute into the nonhomogeneous equation (5.4.4) \begin{array}{rrrrr} \displaystyle \frac{d^2y}{dx^2} &=& -4A\cos 2x& - 4B\sin 2x& \\ \displaystyle -2\frac{dy}{dx} &=& -4B\cos 2x& + 4A\sin 2x& \\ + \quad\quad\quad\quad\quad y &=& A\cos 2x& + B\sin 2x& \\ \hline \underbrace{ \displaystyle \frac{d^2y}{dx^2} -2\frac{dy}{dx} + y} &=& & \\ 5\cos 2x+10\sin 2x &=& {\small (-3A - 4B)}\cos 2x& + {\small (4A-3B)}\sin 2x& \\ \end{array}
    Equating the coefficients on both sides of the equation to obtain a system of equations: \begin{align*} \left\{ \begin{array}{r r r} -3A - 4B &=& 5 \\ 4A - 3B &=& 10 \\ \end{array} \right. \end{align*}
    Solve the system for \(A\) and \(B\)
    Solving the system we find \(A = 1\) and \(B = -2\) and substituting into \(y = A\cos 2x + B\sin 2x \) we obtain \[ y = \cos 2x - 2 \sin 2x \]
    Verify \( y = \cos 2x - 2 \sin 2x \) is a solution of (5.5.4)
  3. Now that we have a fundamental set of solutions to the complementary equation \( \{ y_1, y_2\} = \{ e^{x}, xe^{x} \}\) and a particular solution to the nonhomogeneous equation \( y_p = \cos 2x - 2 \sin 2x \), then we can use Theorem 5.3.2 to find the general solution \( y = y_p + c_1 y_1 + c_2 y_2 \) \[ y = \cos 2x - 2 \sin 2x + ( c_1 + c_2 x ) e^{x} \]
    Verify \( y = \cos 2x - 2 \sin 2x + ( c_1 + c_2 x ) e^{x} \) is a solution of (5.5.4)
Given the differential equation \begin {equation} \label {eq:5.5.5} \frac{d^2y}{dx^2}+4y = 8\cos 2x+12\sin 2x \end {equation}
  1. Solve the complementary equation
  2. Find a particular solution
  3. Find the general solution
Solution
  1. Solve the complementary equation \begin {equation*} \frac{d^2y}{dx^2}+4y = 0 \end {equation*}
    Find the characteristic equation
    Use the method in Example 5.2.3 to find a fundamental set of solutions
    Solving the characteristic equation we obtain a fundamental set of solutions \[ y_1 = \cos 2x \quad y_2 = \sin 2x \]
  2. Now we will try to find a particular solution for the nonhomogeneous equation by guessing a solution based on the forcing function \[ \frac{d^2y}{dx^2}+4y = \underset{f(x)}{\underbrace{8\cos 2x+12\sin 2x}} \]
    Now if we approach this problem the same way we did in Example 5.5.1, we might just notice that since the forcing function is \( f(x) = 8\cos 2x+12\sin 2x \) then a reasonable guess for a solution might be \( y = A\cos 2x+B\sin 2x \), however, this is where we run into an issue. While guessing like this worked in the past, it won't work here, since \(\cos 2x \) and \(\sin 2x \) are actually both solutions of the complementary equation. Let's try it to find out what happens if we were to use \[ y = A\cos 2x + B\sin 2x \]
    Differentiate \(y = A\cos 2x + B\sin 2x \) twice
    After differentiating twice we find \(d^2y/dx^2 = -4A\cos 2x + 4B\sin 2x \) which we substitute into the nonhomogeneous equation (5.5.5) \begin{array}{rrrrr} \displaystyle \frac{d^2y}{dx^2} &=& -4A\cos 2x& - 4B\sin 2x& \\ + \quad\quad\quad\quad\quad 4y &=& 4A\cos 2x& + 4B\sin 2x& \\ \hline \underbrace{ \displaystyle \frac{d^2y}{dx^2} + 4y} &=& {\small (4A-4A)}\cos 2x& + {\small (4B-4B)}\sin 2x& \\ 8\cos 2x+12\sin 2x &=& {0}\, \cos 2x& + \, {0}\, \sin 2x& \\ \end{array} Since \(\cos 2x \) and \(\sin 2x \) are solutions of the complementary equation, then the entire right side of the equation simplified to zero. Therefore this guess will not help us find a particular solution. No problem, we will just update our guess to \begin{align} y = x \big(A\cos 2x + B\sin 2x \big) =Ax \cos 2x + Bx \sin 2x \end{align}
    Differentiate \(y = x \big(A\cos 2x + B\sin 2x \big) \) twice
    After differentiating twice we find \begin{align*} \frac{dy}{dx} = & \big( A + 2Bx \big) \cos 2x + \big( B - 2Ax \big) \sin 2x \\ \frac{d^2y}{dx^2} = & \big( 4B - 4Ax \big) \cos 2x + \big( -4A - 4Bx \big) \sin 2x \\ \end{align*} Then substituting into the nonhomogeneous equation (5.5.5) \begin{array}{rrrrr} \displaystyle \frac{d^2y}{dx^2} &=& {\small \big( 4B - 4Ax \big)}\cos 2x& + {\small \big( -4A - 4Bx \big)}\sin 2x& \\ + \quad\quad\quad\quad\quad 4y &=& {\small 4Ax} \, \cos 2x& + \, {\small 4Bx} \, \sin 2x& \\ \hline \underbrace{ \displaystyle \frac{d^2y}{dx^2} + 4y} &=& & \\ 8\cos 2x+12\sin 2x &=& {4B}\, \cos 2x& - \, {4A}\, \sin 2x& \\ \end{array}

    Our equation simplifies to \begin{align*} \underbrace{8}\cos 2x+\overbrace{12}\sin 2x = \underbrace{{4B}}\, \cos 2x - \, \overbrace{{4A}}\, \sin 2x \end{align*}

    Equating the coefficients, we find \(A\) and \(B\) \begin{align*} \left\{ \begin{array}{r r r} 4B &=& 8 \\ -4A &=& 12 \\ \end{array} \right. \longrightarrow \left\{ \begin{array}{r r r} B &=& 2 \\ A &=& -3 \\ \end{array} \right. \end{align*} Therefore we have our particular solution \( y = Ax \cos 2x + B x\sin 2x \) \[y = 2x \sin 2x -3x \cos 2x \]
    Verify \( y = 2x \sin 2x -3 \cos 2x \) is a solution of (5.5.5)
  3. Now that we have a fundamental set of solutions to the complementary equation \( \{ y_1, y_2\} = \{ \cos 2x, \sin 2x \}\) and a particular solution to the nonhomogeneous equation \( y_p = 2x \sin 2x -3x \cos 2x \), then we can use Theorem 5.3.2 to find the general solution \( y = y_p + c_1 y_1 + c_2 y_2 \) \[ y = (c_1 -3x) \cos 2x + (c_2 + 2x)\sin 2x \]
    Verify \( y = (c_1 -3x) \cos 2x + (c_2 + 2x)\sin 2x \) is a solution of (5.5.5)

Given the differential equation \begin {equation} \label {eq:5.5.7} \frac{d^2y}{dx^2}+3\frac{dy}{dx}+2y = (20x + 16)\cos x+10\sin x \end {equation}
  1. Solve the complementary equation
  2. Find a particular solution
  3. Find the general solution
Solution
  1. Solve the complementary equation \begin {equation*} \frac{d^2y}{dx^2}+3\frac{dy}{dx}+2y = 0 \end {equation*}
    Find the characteristic equation
    Use the method in Example 5.2.1 to find a fundamental set of solutions
    Solving the characteristic equation we obtain a fundamental set of solutions \[ y_1 = e^{-x} \quad y_2 = e^{-2x} \]
  2. Now we will try to find a particular solution for the nonhomogeneous equation by guessing a solution based on the forcing function \[ \frac{d^2y}{dx^2}+3\frac{dy}{dx}+2y = \underset{f(x)}{\underbrace{(20x + 16)\cos x+10\sin x}} \]
    We start by identifying the forcing function \( f(x) = (20x + 16)\cos x+10\sin x \) to help us formulate a reasonable guess for a solution. We won't be able to guess a simple linear combination of cosine and sine \[ y = A\cos x + B\sin x \] This won't work since there is not way to obtain the \(20x\) term from that guess, so we may be tempted to consider just tacking on an extra factor of \(x\) like we did in Example 5.5.2 \[ y = Ax\cos x + Bx\sin x \] These might seem simple enough but it won't work because we do not have enough variables to solve for the coefficients. Notice there are 3 different coefficients we want to find for our forcing function \[ f(x) = 20x \cos x + 16\cos x +10\sin x \] So we will update our guess to include both a linear coefficient and a constant coefficient for each function: \begin{align} y = \big( Ax + B \big) \cos x + \big( Cx + D \big)\sin x \end{align}
    Differentiate \( y = \big(Ax + B \big) \cos x + \big( Cx + D \big)\sin x \) twice
    Differentiating we obtain: \begin{align} \frac{dy}{dx} = & \big( Cx + A + D \big) \cos x + \big( -Ax - B + C \big)\sin x \\ \nonumber\\ \frac{d^2y}{dx^2} = & \big( -Ax + 2C - B \big) \cos x + \big( -Cx -2A - D \big)\sin x \\ \end{align}
    Substituting into the nonhomogeneous equation (5.5.7) \begin{array}{rrrrr} \displaystyle \frac{d^2y}{dx^2} &=& {\small \big( -Ax + 2C - B \big)} \cos x & + {\small \big( -Cx -2A - D \big)}\sin x & \\ 3 \displaystyle \frac{dy}{dx} &=& {\small \big( 3Cx + 3A + 3D \big)} \cos x & + {\small \big( -3Ax - 3B + 3C \big)}\sin x & \\ + \quad\quad\quad\quad\quad 2y &=& {\small \big( 2Ax + 2B \big)} \cos x & + {\small \big( 2Cx + 2D \big)}\sin x & \\ \hline \underbrace{ \displaystyle \frac{d^2y}{dx^2} + 3 \frac{dy}{dx} + 2y} && & \\ (20x + 16)\cos x+10\sin x & \\ \end{array}

    Now adding up the cosines on the right we get \[ {\small \Big[\big( A + 3C \big)x+ \big( 3A + B + 2C + 3D \big) \Big]} \cos x \] Now adding up the sines on the right we get \[ {\small \Big[\big( -3A + C \big)x+ \big( -2A - 3B + 3C + D \big) \Big]} \sin x \] And equating with the terms on the left we have \[ (\overbrace{20x} + \overbrace{16})\cos x={\small \Big[\overset{20}{\overbrace{\big( A + 3C \big)}}x+ \overset{16}{\overbrace{\big( 3A + B + 2C + 3D \big)} \Big]}} \cos x \] \[ \underbrace{10}\sin x = {\small \Big[\underset{0}{\underbrace{\big( -3A + C \big)}}x+ \underset{10}{\underbrace{\big( -2A - 3B + 3C + D \big)}} \Big]} \sin x \] Thus we obtain the system of equations \begin{align*} \left\{ \begin{array}{r r r} 3A + B + 2C + 3D &=& 16 \\ -2A - 3B + 3C + D&=& 10 \\ A + 3C &=& 20 \\ -3A + C &=& 0 \\ \end{array} \right. \end{align*}
    Solve \( \left\{ \begin{array}{r r r} A + 3C &=& 20 \\ -3A + C &=& 0 \\ \end{array} \right. \quad \) for \(A\) and \(C\)
    Which we can solve the last two: \begin{align*} \left\{ \begin{array}{r r r} A + 3C &=& 20 \\ -3A + C &=& 0 \\ \end{array} \right. \longrightarrow \left\{ \begin{array}{r r r} A &=& 2 \\ C &=& 6 \\ \end{array} \right. \end{align*}
    Substitute the values for \(A\) and \(C\) in to \( \left\{ \begin{array}{r r r} 3A + B + 2C + 3D &=& 16 \\ -2A - 3B + 3C + D&=& 10 \\ \end{array} \right. \)
    Substituting in \(A=2\) and \(C=6\) we obtain: \begin{align*} \left\{ \begin{array}{r r r} 3\cdot 2 + B + 2\cdot 6 + 3D &=& 16 \\ -2\cdot 2 - 3B + 3\cdot 6 + D&=& 10 \\ \end{array} \right. \longrightarrow \left\{ \begin{array}{r r r} B + 3D &=& -2 \\ - 3B + D&=& -4 \\ \end{array} \right. \end{align*}
    Solve \( \left\{ \begin{array}{r r r} B + 3D &=& -2 \\ - 3B + D&=& -4 \\ \end{array} \right. \quad \) for \(B\) and \(D\)
    Solving for \(B\) and \(D\) \begin{align*} \left\{ \begin{array}{r r r} B + 3D &=& -2 \\ - 3B + D&=& -4 \\ \end{array} \right. \longrightarrow \left\{ \begin{array}{r r r} B &=& 1 \\ D &=& -1 \\ \end{array} \right. \end{align*}
    So we can finally substitute \(A,\)\(B,\) \(C\) and \(D\) back in \( y = \big( Ax + B \big) \cos x + \big( Cx + D \big)\sin x\) to get our particular solution \begin{align*} y = \big( 2x + 1 \big) \cos x + \big( 6x -1 \big)\sin x \end{align*}
    Verify \( y = \big( 2x + 1 \big) \cos x + \big( 6x -1 \big)\sin x \) is a solution of (5.5.5)
  3. Now that we have a fundamental set of solutions to the complementary equation \( \{ y_1, y_2\} = \{ e^{-x}, e^{-2x} \}\) and a particular solution to the nonhomogeneous equation \( y_p = \big( 2x + 1 \big) \cos x + \big( 6x -1 \big)\sin x \), then we can use Theorem 5.3.2 to find the general solution \( y = y_p + c_1 y_1 + c_2 y_2 \) \begin{align} y = \big( 2x + 1 \big) \cos x + \big( 6x -1 \big)\sin x + c_1 e^{-x} + c_2 e^{-2x} \end{align}
    Verify (5.5.11) is a solution of (5.5.7)

Given the differential equation \begin {equation} \label {eq:5.5.12} \frac{d^2y}{dx^2}+y=(8-4x)\cos x-(8x+8)\sin x \end {equation}

  2. Find a particular solution
  3. Find the general solution
Solution
  1. Solve the complementary equation \begin {equation*} \frac{d^2y}{dx^2}+y = 0 \end {equation*}
    Find the characteristic equation
    Use the method in Example 5.2.3 to find a fundamental set of solutions
    Solving the characteristic equation we obtain a fundamental set of solutions \[ y_1 = \cos x \quad y_2 = \sin x \]
  2. Now we will try to find a particular solution for the nonhomogeneous equation by guessing a solution based on the forcing function \[ \frac{d^2y}{dx^2}+y = \underset{f(x)}{\underbrace{(8-4x)\cos x-(8x+8)\sin x}} \]
    We start by identifying the forcing function \( f(x) = (8-4x)\cos x-(8x+8)\sin x \) to help us formulate a reasonable guess for a solution. It appears to be similar to Example 5.4.3 so perhaps we can guess to both a linear coefficient and a constant coefficient for each function: \begin{align} y = \big( Ax + B \big) \cos x + \big( Cx + D \big)\sin x \end{align}
    Differentiate \( y = \big(Ax + B \big) \cos x + \big( Cx + D \big)\sin x \) twice
    Differentiating we obtain: \begin{align*} \frac{dy}{dx} = & \big( Cx + A + D \big) \cos x + \big( -Ax - B + C \big)\sin x \\ \nonumber\\ \frac{d^2y}{dx^2} = & \big( -Ax + 2C - B \big) \cos x + \big( -Cx -2A - D \big)\sin x \\ \end{align*}
    Substituting into the nonhomogeneous equation (5.5.7) \begin{array}{rrrrr} \displaystyle \frac{d^2y}{dx^2} &=& {\small \big( -Ax + 2C - B \big)} \cos x & + {\small \big( -Cx -2A - D \big)}\sin x & \\ + \quad\quad\quad\quad\quad y &=& {\small \big( Ax + B \big)} \cos x & + {\small \big( Cx + D \big)}\sin x & \\ \hline \underbrace{ \displaystyle \frac{d^2y}{dx^2} + y} && & \\ (8-4x)\cos x-(8x+8)\sin x &=& {\big( 2c \big)} \cos x & + {\big( -2A \big)}\sin x & \\ \end{array} But all the \(x\) terms ended up cancelling out, so this will not produce a solution for us. This makes sense since \( \cos x \) and \( \sin x \) are both solutions to the complementary equation, thus causing terms to zero out in the calculations. So just like when this happened before in Example 5.5.2 we will simplify tack on an extra \(x\) factor to our guess: \begin{align} y = \big( Ax^2 + Bx \big) \cos x + \big( Cx^2 + Dx \big)\sin x \end{align}
    Differentiate \( y = \big( Ax^2 + Bx \big) \cos x + \big( Cx^2 + Dx \big)\sin x \) twice
    Differentiating we obtain: \begin{align*} \frac{dy}{dx} = & {\small \Big( Cx^2 + (2A+D)x + B \Big)} \cos x + {\small \Big( -Ax^2 + (2C-B)x + D \Big)}\sin x \\ \nonumber\\ \frac{d^2y}{dx^2} = & {\small \Big( -Ax^2 + (4C-B)x + 2(A+D) \Big)} \cos x + {\small \Big( -Cx^2 - (4A+D)x + 2(C-B) \Big)}\sin x \\ \end{align*}
    Substituting into the nonhomogeneous equation (5.5.12) \begin{array}{rrrrr} \displaystyle \frac{d^2y}{dx^2} &=& {\small \Big( -Ax^2 + (4C-B)x + 2(A+D) \Big)} \cos x & +{\small \Big( -Cx^2 - (4A+D)x + 2(C-B) \Big)}\sin x & \\ + \quad\quad\quad\quad\quad y &=& {\small \big( Ax^2 + Bx \big)} \cos x & + {\small \big( Cx^2 + Dx \big)}\sin x & \\ \hline \underbrace{ \displaystyle \frac{d^2y}{dx^2} + y} && & \\ \end{array}

    Now adding up the cosines and sines on the right and equating with the cosines and sines in (5.5.12) \[ (-4x + 8)\cos x = {\small \Big[\big( 0\big)x^2 + \overset{-4}{\overbrace{\big( 4c\big)}}x+ \overset{8}{\overbrace{\big( 2A + 2D \big)}} \Big]} \cos x \] \[ (-8x-8)\sin x = {\small \Big[\big( 0\big)x^2 + \underset{-8}{\underbrace{\big( -4 A \big)}}x+ \underset{-8}{\underbrace{\big( 2C-2B \big)}} \Big]} \sin x \] Thus we obtain the system of equations \begin{align*} \left\{ \begin{array}{r r r} 2C - 2B &=& -8 \\ 2A + 2D &=& 8 \\ -4A &=& -8 \\ 4C &=& -4 \\ \end{array} \right. \end{align*}
    Solve \( \left\{ \begin{array}{r r r} 2C - 2B &=& -8 \\ 2A + 2D &=& 8 \\ -4A &=& -8 \\ 4C &=& -4 \\ \end{array} \right. \quad \) for \(A,\) \(B,\) \(C,\) and \(D\)
    Solving for \(A,\) \(B,\) \(C,\) and \(D\) \begin{align*} \left\{ \begin{array}{r r r} 2C - 2B &=& -8 \\ 2A + 2D &=& 8 \\ -4A &=& -8 \\ 4C &=& -4 \\ \end{array} \right. \longrightarrow \left\{ \begin{array}{r r r} A &=& 2 \\ B &=& 3 \\ C &=& -1 \\ D &=& 2 \\ \end{array} \right. \end{align*}
    So we can finally substitute \(A,\)\(B,\) \(C\) and \(D\) back in \( y = \big( Ax^2 + Bx \big) \cos x + \big( Cx^2 + Dx \big)\sin x\) to get our particular solution \begin{align*} y = \big( 2x^2 + 3x \big) \cos x + \big( -x^2 +2x \big)\sin x \end{align*}
    Verify \( y = \big( 2x^2 + 3x \big) \cos x + \big( -x^2 +2x \big)\sin x \) is a solution of (5.5.12)
  3. Now that we have a fundamental set of solutions to the complementary equation \( \{ y_1, y_2\} = \{ \cos x, \sin x \}\) and a particular solution to the nonhomogeneous equation \( y_p = \big( 2x^2 + 3x \big) \cos x + \big( -x^2 +2x \big)\sin x \), then we can use Theorem 5.3.2 to find the general solution \( y = y_p + c_1 y_1 + c_2 y_2 \) \begin{align*} y = \big( 2x^2 + 3x + c_1 \big) \cos x + \big( -x^2 +2x + c_2 \big)\sin x \end{align*}
    Verify \(y = \big( 2x^2 + 3x + c_1 \big) \cos x + \big( -x^2 +2x + c_2 \big)\sin x\) is a solution of (5.5.12)

To find a particular solution of \begin {equation} \label {eq:5.5.15} a\frac{d^2y}{dx^2}+b\frac{dy}{dx}+cy=e^{\lambda x}\left (P(x)\cos \omega x+Q(x)\sin \omega x\right ) \end {equation} when \(\lambda \ne 0\), we recall from Section 5.4 that substituting \(y=ue^{\lambda x}\) into (5.5.15) will produce a constant coefficient equation for \(u\) with the forcing function \(P(x)\cos \omega x+Q(x)\sin \omega x\). We can find a particular solution \(u_p\) of this equation by the procedure that we used in Examples 5.5.1 â€“ 5.5.4. Then \(y_p=u_pe^{\lambda x}\) is a particular solution of (5.5.15).
Find a particular solution of \begin {equation} \label {eq:5.5.16} \frac{d^2y}{dx^2}-3\frac{dy}{dx}+2y=e^{-2x}\left [2\cos 3x-(34-150x)\sin 3x\right ] \end {equation}
Solution Let \(y=ue^{-2x}\). Then
\begin {eqnarray*} \frac{d^2y}{dx^2}-3\frac{dy}{dx}+2y&=&e^{-2x}\left [(\frac{d^2u}{dx^2}-4\frac{du}{dx}+4u)-3(\frac{du}{dx}-2u)+2u\right ]\\ &=&e^{-2x}(\frac{d^2u}{dx^2}-7\frac{du}{dx}+12u)\\ &=& e^{-2x}\left [2\cos 3x-(34-150x)\sin 3x\right ] \end {eqnarray*}
if \begin {equation} \label {eq:5.5.17} \frac{d^2u}{dx^2}-7\frac{du}{dx}+12u=2\cos 3x-(34-150x)\sin 3x \end {equation} Since \(\cos 3x\) and \(\sin 3x\) arenâ€™t solutions of the complementary equation \[ \frac{d^2u}{dx^2}-7\frac{du}{dx}+12u=0\] Theorem 5.5.1 tells us to look for a particular solution of (5.5.17) of the form \begin {equation} \label {eq:5.5.18} u_p=(A_0+A_1x)\cos 3x +(B_0+B_1x)\sin 3x \end {equation} Then
\begin {eqnarray*} \frac{du_p}{dx}&=&(A_1+3B_0+3B_1x)\cos 3x+(B_1-3A_0-3A_1x)\sin 3x\\ \text {and} \frac{d^2u_p}{dx^2}&=&(-9A_0+6B_1-9A_1x)\cos 3x-(9B_0+6A_1+9B_1x)\sin 3x \end {eqnarray*}
\begin {eqnarray*} \frac{d^2u_p}{dx^2}-7\frac{du_p}{dx}+12u_p&=&\left [3A_0-21B_0-7A_1+6B_1+(3A_1-21B_1)x\right ]\cos 3x\\ &&+\left [21A_0+3B_0-6A_1-7B_1+(21A_1+3B_1)x\right ]\sin 3x \end {eqnarray*}
Comparing the coefficients of \(x\cos 3x\), \(x\sin 3x\), \(\cos 3x\), and \(\sin 3x\) here with the corresponding coefficients on the right side of (5.5.17) shows that \(u_p\) is a solution of (5.5.17) if \begin {equation} \label {eq:5.5.19} \begin {array}{rcr} 3A_1-21B_1&=&0\phantom {.}\\ 21A_1+\phantom {2}3B_1&=&150\phantom {.}\\ 3A_0-21B_0-7A_1+\phantom {2}6B_1&=&\phantom {-3}2\phantom {.}\\ 21A_0+\phantom {2}3B_0-6A_1-\phantom {5}7B_1&=&-34 \end {array} \end {equation} Solving the first two equations yields \(A_1=7\), \(B_1=1\). Substituting these values into the last two equations of (5.5.19) yields
\begin {eqnarray*} \phantom {2}3A_0-21B_0&=&\phantom {-3}2+7A_1-6B_1=45\phantom {.}\\ 21A_0+\phantom {2}3B_0&=&-34+6A_1+7B_1=15 \end {eqnarray*}
Solving this system yields \(A_0=1\), \(B_0=-2\). Substituting \(A_0=1\), \(A_1=7\), \(B_0=-2\), and \(B_1=1\) into (5.5.18) shows that \[ u_p=(1+7x)\cos 3x-(2-x)\sin 3x \] is a particular solution of (5.5.17). Therefore \[ y_p=e^{-2x}\left [(1+7x)\cos 3x-(2-x)\sin 3x\right ] \] is a particular solution of (5.5.16).
Find a particular solution of \begin {equation} \label {eq:5.5.20} \frac{d^2y}{dx^2}+2\frac{dy}{dx}+5y=e^{-x}\left [(6-16x)\cos 2x-(8+8x)\sin 2x\right ] \end {equation}
Solution Let \(y=ue^{-x}\). Then
\begin {eqnarray*} \frac{d^2y}{dx^2}+2\frac{dy}{dx}+5y&=&e^{-x}\left [(\frac{d^2u}{dx^2}-2\frac{du}{dx}+u)+2(\frac{du}{dx}-u)+5u\right ]\\ &=&e^{-x}(\frac{d^2u}{dx^2}+4u)\\ &=& e^{-x}\left [(6-16x)\cos 2x-(8+8x)\sin 2x\right ] \end {eqnarray*}
if \begin {equation} \label {eq:5.5.21} \frac{d^2u}{dx^2}+4u=(6-16x)\cos 2x-(8+8x)\sin 2x \end {equation} Since \(\cos 2x\) and \(\sin 2x\) are solutions of the complementary equation \[ \frac{d^2u}{dx^2}+4u=0\] Theorem 5.5.1 tells us to look for a particular solution of (5.5.21) of the form \[ u_p=(A_0x+A_1x^2)\cos 2x+(B_0x+B_1x^2)\sin 2x\] Then
\begin {eqnarray*} \frac{du_p}{dx}&=&\left [A_0+(2A_1+2B_0)x+2B_1x^2\right ]\cos 2x \\ && +\left [B_0+(2B_1-2A_0)x-2A_1x^2\right ]\sin 2x\\ \text {and} \frac{d^2u_p}{dx^2}&=&\left [2A_1+4B_0-(4A_0-8B_1)x-4A_1x^2\right ]\cos 2x\\ && +\left [2B_1-4A_0-(4B_0+8A_1)x-4B_1x^2\right ]\sin 2x \end {eqnarray*}
so \[ \frac{d^2u_p}{dx^2}+4u_p=(2A_1+4B_0+8B_1x)\cos 2x+(2B_1-4A_0-8A_1x)\sin 2x\] Equating the coefficients of \(x\cos 2x\), \(x\sin 2x\), \(\cos 2x\), and \(\sin 2x\) here with the corresponding coefficients on the right side of (5.5.21) shows that \(u_p\) is a solution of (5.5.21) if \begin {equation} \label {eq:5.5.22} \begin {array}{rcr} 8B_1&=&-16\phantom {.}\\ -8A_1&=&-\phantom {1}8\phantom {.}\\ \phantom {-}4B_0+2A_1&=&6\phantom {.}\\ -4A_0+2B_1&=&-8 \end {array} \end {equation} The solution of this system is \(A_1=1\), \(B_1=-2\), \(B_0=1\), \(A_0=1\). Therefore \[ u_p=x[(1+x)\cos 2x+(1-2x)\sin 2x] \] is a particular solution of (5.5.21), and \[ y_p=xe^{-x}\left [(1+x)\cos 2x+(1-2x)\sin 2x\right ] \] is a particular solution of (5.5.20).
You can also find a particular solution of (5.5.20) by substituting \[ y_p=xe^{-x}\left [(A_0+A_1x)\cos 2x +(B_0+B_1x)\sin 2x\right ] \] for \(y\) in (5.5.20) and equating the coefficients of \(xe^{-x}\cos 2x\), \(xe^{-x}\sin 2x\), \(e^{-x}\cos 2x\), and \(e^{-x}\sin 2x\) in the resulting expression for \[ \frac{d^2y_p}{dx^2}+2\frac{dy_p}{dx}+5y_p \] with the corresponding coefficients on the right side of (5.5.20). (See Exercise 38). This leads to the same system (5.5.22) of equations for \(A_0\), \(A_1\), \(B_0\), and \(B_1\) that we obtained in Example 5.5.6. However, if you try this approach youâ€™ll see that deriving (5.5.22) this way is much more tedious than the way we did it in Example 5.5.6.
Principle of Superposition
The next example shows how to combine the method of undetermined coefficients and Theorem 5.3.3, the principle of superposition.
Find a particular solution of \begin {equation} \label {eq:5.5.23} \frac{d^2y}{dx^2}-7\frac{dy}{dx}+12y=4e^{2x}+5e^{4x} \end {equation}
Solution In Example 5.4.1we found that \(y_{p_1}=2e^{2x}\) is a particular solution of \[ \frac{d^2y}{dx^2}-7\frac{dy}{dx}+12y=4e^{2x}\] and in Example 5.4.2we found that \(y_{p_2}=5xe^{4x}\) is a particular solution of \[ \frac{d^2y}{dx^2}-7\frac{dy}{dx}+12y=5e^{4x}\] Therefore the principle of superposition implies that \(y_p=2e^{2x}+5xe^{4x}\) is a particular solution of (5.5.23).
