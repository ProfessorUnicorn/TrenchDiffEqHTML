2.6 Integrating Factors
Integrating Factors
In Section 2.5 we learned how differential equations that can be written in terms of an exact differential are called exact differential equations, whenever \( M_y(x,y)=N_x(x,y) \) for \begin{equation} \label {eq:2.6.1} M(x,y)\,dx+N(x,y)\,dy=0 \end {equation} However, it is not often the case that an equation is exact. There is a method to modify an existing equation into an exact equation, called multiplying by an integrating factor. For instance, let's show that (2.6.2) is not exact. \begin{equation} \label {eq:2.6.2} (\underset{M(x,y)}{\underbrace{3x+2y^2}}) dx + \underset{N(x,y)}{\underbrace{2xy}} dy = 0 \end {equation}
Calculate \( M_y(x,y) \) and \( N_x(x,y) \) using the power rule
\begin{equation*} \begin{array}{rllrrl} M(x,y) & = 3x+2y^2 &\quad & N(x,y) & = & 2xy \\ \Longrightarrow M_y(x,y) & = 4y &\quad & \Longrightarrow N_x(x,y) & = & 2y \\ \end{array} \end{equation*} \begin{equation*} \boxed{ M_y(x,y) = 4y \ne 2y = N_x(x,y) \quad \quad \text{Not Exact} } \end{equation*} Although the equation is currently not exact, if we multiply by the correct integrating factor, then the new equation will be exact. We'll use \( x \) for the integrating factor so we multiply both sides of (2.6.2) by \( x \) \begin{align}\label{eq:2.6.3} \Big( \left( 3x+2y^2 \right) dx + 2xy dy \Big) \cdot x = & \Big( 0 \Big) \cdot x \nonumber \\ ( \underset{x \cdot M(x,y)}{\underbrace{3x^2+2xy^2} }) dx + \underset{x \cdot N(x,y)}{\underbrace{2x^2y}} dy = & 0 \end{align} Now the new equation (2.6.3) is exact which we can verify by checking the partial derivatives
Calculate \( \frac{\partial}{\partial y} \left( 3x^2+2xy^2 \right) \) using the power rule
Calculate \( \frac{\partial}{\partial x} \left( 2x^2y \right) \) using the power rule
\begin{equation*} \boxed{ \frac{\partial}{\partial y} \left( 3x^2+2xy^2 \right) = 4xy = \frac{\partial}{\partial x} \left( 2x^2y \right) \quad \quad \checkmark \text{Exact} } \end{equation*} Now that we multiplied (2.6.2) by the integrating factor \( x \), the new equation (2.6.3) is exact, meaning we can solve with the same method we used last section, plus we already completed the first step! One small detail to keep track of is that the procedure refers to \( M(x,y) \) and \( N(x,y) \) but in our case, we multiplied both by \( x \) so when the exact procedure refers to \( M(x,y) \) we will use \( x \cdot M(x,y) \) and when the exact procedure refers to \( N(x,y) \) we will use \( x \cdot N(x,y) \) what we previously referred to as
\( \checkmark \) 1. Identify \( M(x,y) \) and \( N(x,y) \) and compute \( M_y(x,y) \) and \( N_x(x,y) \) and verify \( M_y(x,y)=N_x(x,y) \) to ensure that the equation is exact
2. Antidifferentiate \( M(x,y) = F_x(x,y) \) with respect to \( x \) and/or \( N(x,y) = F_y(x,y) \) with respect to \( y \) to obtain \( F(x,y) \)
Antidifferentiate \( \int \Big( 3x^2+2xy^2 \Big) dx \) using power rule
\begin{align*} F(x,y) = & \int F_x(x,y) dx = \int M(x,y) dx \\ = & \int \Big( 3x^2+2xy^2 \Big) dx = x^3 + y^2 x^2 + \phi(y) \\ \end{align*}
Antidifferentiate \( \int \Big( 2x^2y \Big) dy \) using power rule
\begin{align*} F(x,y) = & \int F_y(x,y) dy = \int N(x,y) dy \\ = & \int \Big( 2x^2y \Big) dy = x^2 y^2 + \psi(x) \\ \end{align*}
3. Differentiating with respect to the same variable to check that the antidifferentiation is correct
Find \( F_x(x,y) \) where \( F(x,y) = x^3 + y^2 x^2 + \phi(y) \)
Find \( F_y(x,y) \) where \( F(x,y) = x^2 y^2 + \psi(x) \)
\begin{equation*} \begin{array}{rllrrl} F(x,y) & = x^3 + y^2 x^2 + \phi(y) &\quad & F(x,y) & = & x^2 y^2 + \psi(x) \\ \Longrightarrow F_x(x,y) & = 3x^2 + 2xy^2 = M(x,y) \checkmark &\quad & \Longrightarrow F_y(x,y) & = & 2x^2 y = N(x,y) \checkmark \\ \end{array} \end{equation*}
4. Differentiate with respect to the other variable to obtain expressions for \( F_x(x,y) \) and \( F_y(x,y) \) in terms of \( \psi'(x) \) and \( \phi'(y) \)
Find \( F_x(x,y) \) by differentiating \( F(x,y) = x^2 y^2 + \psi(x) \)
Find \( F_y(x,y) \) by differentiating \( F(x,y) = x^3 + y^2 x^2 + \phi(y) \)
\begin{equation*} \begin{array}{rllrrl} F(x,y) & = x^3 + y^2 x^2 + \phi(y) &\quad & F(x,y) & = & x^2 y^2 + \psi(x) \\ \Longrightarrow F_y(x,y) & = 2 x^2 y + \phi'(y) &\quad & \Longrightarrow F_x(x,y) & = & 2x y^2 + \psi'(x) \\ \end{array} \end{equation*}
5. Set the new expressions equal to known expressions \( F_x(x,y) = M(x,y) \) and/or \( F_y(x,y) = N(x,y) \) and solve for \( \psi(x) \) and \( \phi(y) \)
\begin{equation*} \begin{array}{rllrrl} F_x(x,y) & = & M(x,y) &\quad & F_y(x,y) & = & N(x,y) \\ 2x y^2 + \psi'(x) & = & 3x^2 + 2xy^2 &\quad & 2 x^2 y + \phi'(y) & = & 2x^2y \\ \psi'(x) & = & 3x^2 &\quad & \phi'(y) & = & 0 \\ \psi(x) & = & 3 \int x^2 dx &\quad & \phi(y) & = & \int 0 dy \\ \psi(x) & = & x^3 &\quad & \phi(y) & = & k \\ \end{array} \end{equation*} We have obtained expressions for \( \psi(x) \) and \( \phi(y) \) and in both cases, again, we can take the constants of integration to zero.
6. Substitute \( \psi(x) \) and/or \( \phi(y) \) in to obtain \( F(x,y) \) and if possible verify they match. Set \( F(x,y)=c \) arbitrary constant which is an implicit solution. Solve explicitly for \( y = y(x) \) whenever possible
Substituting in \( \psi(x) = x^3 \) and \( \phi(y) = 0 \) we obtain \( F(x,y) \) \begin{equation*} \begin{array}{rllrrl} F(x,y) & = x^2 y^2 + \psi(x) &\quad & F(x,y) & = & x^3 + y^2 x^2 + \phi(y) \\ F(x,y) & = x^2 y^2 + x^3 &\quad & F(x,y) & = & x^3 + y^2 x^2 + 0 \\ \end{array} \end{equation*} Both expressions for \( F(x,y) = x^2 y^2 + x^3 \) agree and this is a great way to check your work so either way you opt to solve, you should get the same answer. Our final implicit solution is written in the form of (2.5.6) as \( F(x,y) = c \) \begin{equation*} x^4 y^3 + 2y^3 + x^3 = c \end{equation*} So we get the implicit solution: \begin{align*} x^2 y^2 + x^3 = & c \\ \end{align*}

Which can be solved explicitly for \( y\) \begin{align*} y(x) = & \sqrt{ \frac{c - x^3}{x^2} } \\ \end{align*}
Finding Integrating Factors
Consider the differential equation in (2.6.1) \begin{equation*} M(x,y) dx + N(x,y) dy = 0 \end{equation*} A function \( \mu=\mu(x,y) \) is an integrating factor for (2.6.1) if multiplying both sides by it creates an exact differential equation \begin{equation} \label{eq:2.6.4} \mu(x,y) M(x,y) dx + \mu(x,y) N(x,y) dy = 0 \end{equation} Where (2.6.4) is exact whenever \[ \frac{\partial}{\partial y} \Big( \mu(x,y) M(x,y) \Big) = \frac{\partial}{\partial x} \Big( \mu(x,y) N(x,y) \Big) \] When (2.6.4) is an exact then we call \( \mu(x,y) \) an integrating factor. We can use integrating factors \( \mu(x,y) \) to convert non-exact differential equations into exact differential equations.
It would be nice if we could say that (2.6.1) and (2.6.4) always have the same solutions, but this isn't so. For example, a solution \( y=y(x) \) of (2.6.4) such that \( \mu\big(x,y(x)\big)=0 \) on some interval could fail to be a solution of (2.6.1) (Exercise 1), while (2.6.1) may have a solution \( y=y(x) \) such that \( \mu \big(x,y(x) \big) \) isn't even defined (Exercise 2). However, if \( \mu(x,y) \) is defined and nonzero for all \( (x,y) ,\) then (2.6.1) and (2.6.4) are equivalent meaning they have the same solutions.
By applying the Exactness Condition (Theorem 2.5.2) to (2.6.4), we replace \( M(x,y) \) with \( \mu(x,y) M(x,y) \) written more simply as \( \mu M\) and \( N(x,y) \) with \( \mu(x,y) N(x,y) \) written more simply as \( \mu N\) and use the product rule to determine what the exactness condition requires. \begin{align} \label{eq:2.6.5} \frac{\partial}{\partial y} \Big( \mu M \Big) = & \frac{\partial}{\partial x} \Big( \mu N \Big) \nonumber \\ \mu \frac{\partial}{\partial y} \Big( M \Big) + M \frac{\partial}{\partial y} \Big( \mu \Big) = & \mu \frac{\partial}{\partial x} \Big( N \Big) + N \frac{\partial}{\partial x} \Big( \mu \Big) \nonumber \\ \mu M_y + \mu_y M = & \mu N_x + \mu_x N \nonumber \\ \mu M_y - \mu N_x = & \mu_x N - \mu_y M \nonumber \\ \mu \Big( M_y - N_x \Big) = & \mu_x N - \mu_y M \end{align} This reduces to the known result for exact equations that if \(M_y=N_x\) then (2.6.5) holds with \(\mu =1\), so (2.6.1) is exact.
At first glance (2.6.5) may look quite complicated since it involves partial derivatives of an unknown integrating factor \(\mu \) and we haven’t even studied methods for solving partial differential equations. However (2.6.5) is useful if we restrict our search to integrating factors that are products of a function of \( x \) and a function of \( y \) \begin{equation*} \mu(x,y) = P(x) Q(y) \end{equation*} We'll now develop a way to determine whether a given equation has such an integrating factor, and a method for finding the integrating factor in this case. Assuming \( \mu(x,y)=P(x)Q(y) \), we can get expressions for \( \mu_x \) and \( \mu_y \) \begin{equation*} \begin{array}{rllrrl} \frac{\partial}{\partial x} \Big( \mu (x,y) \Big) & = & \frac{\partial}{\partial x} \Big( P(x) Q(y) \Big) &\quad & \frac{\partial}{\partial y} \Big( \mu (x,y) \Big) & = & \frac{\partial}{\partial y} \Big( P(x) Q(y) \Big) \\ \mu_x (x,y) & = & Q(y) \frac{\partial}{\partial x} \Big( P(x) \Big) &\quad & \mu_y (x,y) & = & P(x) \frac{\partial}{\partial y} \Big( Q(y) \Big) \\ \mu_x & = & P'(x) Q(y) &\quad & \mu_y & = & P(x) Q'(y) \\ \end{array} \end{equation*} Substituting \( \mu = P(x) Q(y) \) and the expressions for \( \mu_x=P'(x) Q(y) \) and \( \mu_y=P(x) Q'(y) \) into (2.6.5), we obtain \begin{align} \label{eq:2.6.6} \mu \Big( M_y - N_x \Big) = & \mu_x N - \mu_y M \nonumber \\ P(x) Q(y) \Big( M_y - N_x \Big) = & P'(x) Q(y) N - P(x) Q'(y) M \\ M_y - N_x = & \frac{ P'(x) Q(y) N - P(x) Q'(y) M } {P(x) Q(y)} \nonumber \\ M_y - N_x = & \frac{ P'(x) Q(y) N } {P(x) Q(y)} - \frac{ P(x) Q'(y) M } {P(x) Q(y)} \nonumber \\ M_y - N_x = & \underset{}{\underbrace{\frac{ P'(x)} {P(x)}}} N - \underset{}{\underbrace{\frac{ Q'(y)} {Q(y)}}}M \label{eq:2.6.7} \end{align} We notice the factors \( \frac{ P'(x)} {P(x)} \) and \( \frac{ Q'(y)} {Q(y)} \) in (2.6.7) and recall that if we let \( p(x) = \frac{ P'(x)} {P(x)} \) and \( q(y) = \frac{ Q'(y)} {Q(y)} \), then those differential equations are separable and easily solvable. We'll solve them in a moment, but for now we will go ahead and make the replacement to obtain (2.6.8) \begin{align} M_y - N_x = & \underset{p(x)}{\underbrace{\frac{ P'(x)} {P(x)}}} N - \underset{q(y)}{\underbrace{\frac{ Q'(y)} {Q(y)}}}M \nonumber \\ M_y - N_x = & p(x)N - q(y)M \label{eq:2.6.8} \end{align} So, if we can find functions \( p(x) \) and \( q(y) \) such that \( p(x) = \frac{ P'} {P} \) and \( q(y) = \frac{ Q'} {Q} \) that also satisfy (2.6.8), then we can obtain an integrating factor \( \mu = P(x) Q(y) \) for (2.6.1). As we mentioned, we can easily solve for $P(x)$ and $Q(y)$ since those equations are \boldindex{separable}. To keep the solutions as simple as possible we will take all the constants of integration to be zero and choosing positive sign whenever possible \begin{align*} \frac{ P'} {P} = p(x) \Longrightarrow \frac{1} {P}\frac{dP} {dx} = & p(x) \\ \int \frac{1} {P}\frac{dP} {dx} dx = & \int p(x) dx \\ \int \frac{1} {P} dP = & \int p(x) dx \\ \ln \left| P \right| = & \int p(x) dx \\ e^{\ln \left| P \right|} = & e^{\int p(x) dx} \\ \left| P \right| = & e^{\int p(x) dx} \Longrightarrow P(x) = e^{\int p(x) dx} \\ \end{align*} \begin{align*} \frac{ Q'} {Q} = q(y) \Longrightarrow \frac{1} {Q}\frac{dQ} {dy} = & q(y) \\ \int \frac{1} {Q}\frac{dQ} {dy} dy = & \int q(y) dy \\ \int \frac{1} {Q} dQ = & \int q(y) dy \\ \ln \left| Q \right| = & \int q(y) dy \\ e^{\ln \left| Q \right|} = & e^{\int q(y) dy} \\ \left| Q \right| = & e^{\int q(y) dy} \Longrightarrow Q(y) = e^{\int q(y) dy} \\ \end{align*} Using separation of variables for both \( \frac{ P'} {P} = p(x) \) and \( \frac{ Q'} {Q} = q(y) \), we can easily obtain expressions for \( P(x) \) and \( Q(y) \) \begin{equation} \label{eq:2.6.9} P(x) = e^{\int p(x) dx} \quad \text{and} \quad Q(y) = e^{\int q(y) dy} \end{equation} Thus we have our integrating factor \( \mu(x,y) = P(x) \, Q(y) \) \begin{equation*} \mu(x,y) = P(x) Q(y) = \left( e^{\int p(x) dx} \right) \left( e^{\int q(y) dy} \right) \end{equation*}
There is no simple general method for ascertaining whether functions \( p=p(x) \) and \( q=q(y) \) exist. For now we will make extensive usage of (2.6.8) as a general condition to check for integrating factors of the form \( \mu(x,y) = P(x) Q(y) \)
This was an exploration of discovering integrating factor that are products of a function of \( x \) and a function of \( y ,\) i.e. |( \mu(x,y) = P(x) Q(y) .\) As mentioned, there is no general method for ascertaining whether these functions \( p=p(x) \) and \( q=q(y) \) exist. However, the next theorem gives simple sufficient conditions for the given equation to have an integrating factor that depends on only one of the independent variables \( x \) and \( y \), and for finding an integrating factor in this form, \( \mu(x,y) = \mu(x) = P(x) \) or \( \mu(x,y) = \mu(y) = Q(y) \). So now we'll go back and develop this case by the same logic, starting with integrating factors that are a function of \( x \) only: \begin{equation*} \mu(x,y) = \mu(x) = P(x) \end{equation*}
Assuming \( \mu(x,y)=\mu(x) = P(x) \) then \( \mu_x = P'(x) \) and \( \mu_y = 0 .\) Substituting \( \mu = P(x) \) and the expressions for \( \mu_x=P'(x) \) and \( \mu_y=0 \) into (2.6.5), we obtain: \begin{align*} \mu \Big( M_y - N_x \Big) = & \mu_x N - \mu_y M \nonumber \\ P(x) \Big( M_y - N_x \Big) = & P'(x) N \\ M_y - N_x = & \frac{P'(x)}{P(x)} N \\ \frac{M_y - N_x}{N} = & \underset{p(x)}{\underbrace{\frac{ P'(x)} {P(x)}}} \\ \frac{M_y - N_x}{N} = & p(x) \end{align*} This means that if \( \frac{M_y - N_x}{N} \) yields a function that is only dependent on \( x ,\) then an integrating factor \( P(x) \) exists that only depends on \( x ,\) and in fact is the solution to the separable differential equation \( p(x) = \frac{ P'(x)} {P(x)} ,\) which we already solved above: \begin{equation*} \mu(x) = P(x) = e^{\int p(x) dx} \end{equation*}
Similar logic applies for finding integrating factors that are a function of \( y \) only: \begin{equation*} \mu(x,y) = \mu(y) = Q(y) \end{equation*} Assuming \( \mu(x,y)=\mu(y) = Q(y) \) then \( \mu_x = 0 \) and \( \mu_y = Q'(y) \). Substituting \( \mu = Q(y) \) and the expressions for \( \mu_x=0 \) and \( \mu_y=Q'(y) \) into (2.6.5), we obtain: \begin{align*} \mu \Big( M_y - N_x \Big) = & \mu_x N - \mu_y M \nonumber \\ Q(y) \Big( M_y - N_x \Big) = & -Q'(y) M \\ M_y - N_x = & - \frac{Q'(y)}{Q(y)} M \\ \frac{M_y - N_x}{-M} = & \underset{q(y)}{\underbrace{\frac{ Q'(y)} {Q(y)}}} \\ \frac{N_x - M_y}{M} = & q(y) \end{align*} Again, this means that if \( \frac{N_x-M_y}{M} \) yields a function that is only dependent on \( y ,\) then an integrating factor \( Q(y) \) exists that only depends on \( y ,\) and in fact is the solution to the separable differential equation \( q(y) = \frac{ Q'(y)} {Q(y)} ,\) which we already solved above: \begin{equation*} \mu(y) = Q(y) = e^{\int q(y) dy} \end{equation*}
And this is exactly stated in the following theorem:
Theorem 2.6.1 \begin{equation}\label{eq:2.6.10} M(x,y) dx + N(x,y) dy = 0 \end{equation}
  1. Calculate \( \displaystyle \frac{M_y-N_x}{N} \) from (2.6.8), and if it is independent of \( y ,\) then an integrating factor for (2.6.10) is given by: \begin{equation}\label{eq:2.6.11} \mu(x) = \pm e^{\int p(x) dx} \end{equation} where \begin{equation*} p(x) = \frac{M_y-N_x}{N} \end{equation*}
  2. Calculate \( \displaystyle \frac{N_x-M_y}{M} \) from (2.6.8), and if it is independent of \( x ,\) then an integrating factor for (2.6.10) is given by: \begin{equation}\label{eq:2.6.12} \mu(x) = \pm e^{\int q(y) dy} \end{equation} where \begin{equation*} q(y) = \frac{N_x-M_y}{M} \end{equation*}
Proof (a) If \((M_y-N_x)/N\) is independent of \(y\), then (2.6.8) holds with \(p=(M_y-N_x)/N\) and \(q\equiv 0\). Therefore \[ P(x)=\pm e^{\int p(x)\,dx}\quad \mbox{ and} \quad Q(y)=\pm e^{\int q(y)\,dy}=\pm e^0=\pm 1\] so is an integrating factor for (2.6.11) on \(R\).
(b) If \((N_x-M_y)/M\) is independent of \(x\) then eqrefeq:2.6.8 holds with \(p\equiv 0\) and \(q=(N_x-M_y)/M\), and a similar argument shows that (2.6.12) is an integrating factor for (2.6.11) on \(R\).
Integrating Factor Examples
The next two examples show how to apply Theorem 2.6.1.
Solve the equation using an integrating factor \begin {equation} \label{eq:2.6.13} (2xy^3-2x^3y^3-4xy^2+2x)\,dx+(3x^2y^2+4y)\,dy=0 \end{equation}
Solution
We'll start by identifying \( M(x,y) \) and \( N(x,y) \) and computing their partial derivatives to verify that the equation is not exact. \begin{equation*} (\underset{M(x,y)}{\underbrace{2xy^3-2x^3y^3-4xy^2+2x}}) dx + (\underset{N(x,y)}{\underbrace{3x^2y^2+4y}}) dy = 0 \end{equation*}
Identify \( M(x,y) \) and calculate \( M_y(x,y) \)
Identify \( N(x,y) \) and calculate \( N_x(x,y) \)
We have verified that the equation is not exact since: \begin{equation*} \boxed{ M_y(x,y) = 6xy^2 - 6x^3y^2 - 8xy \ne 6xy^2 = N_x(x,y) \quad \quad \checkmark \text{Not Exact} } \end{equation*}
Now we'll look for an integrating factor \( \mu(x)=P(x) \) in the form of a function of \( x \) only by checking whether \( \frac{M_y-N_x}{N} \) is a function of \( x \) only (i.e. independent of \( y \)). \begin{align*} \frac{M_y-N_x}{N} = & \frac{(\overset{M_y}{\overbrace{6xy^2 - 6x^3y^2 - 8xy}}) - (\overset{N_x}{\overbrace{6xy^2}})}{\underset{N}{\underbrace{3x^2y^2+4y}}} \\ = & \frac{(6xy^2 - 6x^3y^2 - 8xy) - (6xy^2)}{3x^2y^2+4y} \\ = & \frac{- 6x^3y^2 - 8xy}{3x^2y^2+4y} \\ = & -\frac{2xy(3x^2y + 4)}{y(3x^2y+4)} \\ = & -2x \end{align*} Since \( \frac{M_y-N_x}{N} = -2x \) is a function of \( x \) only, then there exists an integrating factor \( \mu(x)=P(x) \) which is the solution to the differential equation \( p(x) = \frac{ P'(x)} {P(x)} \) where \( p(x) = \frac{M_y-N_x}{N} = -2x .\)
\begin{align*} \frac{ P'(x)} {P(x)} = & \frac{M_y-N_x}{N} \\ \frac{1} {P} \frac{dP} {dx} = & -2x \\ \int \Big( \frac{1} {P} \frac{dP} {dx} \Big) dx= & \int \Big( -2x \Big) dx \\ \int \frac{1} {P} dP= & -2 \int x \, dx \\ \ln \left| P \right| = & -2 \left( \frac{1}{2}x^2\right) + c \\ \ln \left| P \right| = & -x^2 + c \\ e^{\ln \left| P \right|} = & e^{-x^2 + c} \\ \left| P \right| = & ce^{-x^2} \\ P = & ce^{-x^2} \\ \mu(x) = P(x) = & e^{-x^2} \\ \end{align*} Now we multiply our integrating factor \( \mu(x)=P(x) = e^{-x^2} \) on both sides of (2.6.13) to obtain an exact equation (2.6.14) \begin{align} (2xy^3-2x^3y^3-4xy^2+2x) dx + (3x^2y^2+4y) dy = & 0 \nonumber \\ \underset{e^{-x^2} \cdot M(x,y)}{\underbrace{(2xy^3-2x^3y^3-4xy^2+2x)e^{-x^2}}} dx + \underset{e^{-x^2} \cdot N(x,y)}{\underbrace{(3x^2y^2+4y)e^{-x^2}}} dy = & 0 \label{eq:2.6.14} \end{align} Now we can use the exact method for solving.

Solve the equation using an integrating factor \begin {equation} \label {eq:2.6.18} 2xy^3\,dx+(3x^2y^2+x^2y^3+1)\,dy=0 \end {equation}
Solution
In (2.6.18), \[ M=2xy^3,\quad N=3x^2y^2+x^2y^3+1\] and \[ M_y-N_x=6xy^2-(6xy^2+2xy^3)=-2xy^3\] so (2.6.18) isn’t exact. Moreover, \[ {M_y-N_x\over N}=-{2xy^3\over 3x^2y^2+x^2y^2+1} \] is not independent of \(y\), so Theorem 2.6.1(a) does not apply. However, Theorem 2.6.1(b) does apply, since \[ {N_x-M_y\over M}={2xy^3\over 2xy^3}=1 \] is independent of \(x\), so we can take \(q(y)=1\). Since \[ \int q(y)\,dy=\int \,dy=y\] \(\mu (y)=e^y\) is an integrating factor. Multiplying (2.6.18) by \(\mu \) yields the exact equation \begin {equation} \label {eq:2.6.19} 2xy^3e^y\,dx+(3x^2y^2+x^2y^3+1)e^y\,dy=0\end {equation} To solve this equation, we must find a function \(F\) such that \begin {equation} \label {eq:2.6.20} F_x(x,y)=2xy^3e^y \end {equation} and \begin {equation} \label {eq:2.6.21} F_y(x,y)=(3x^2y^2+x^2y^3+1)e^y\end {equation} Integrating (2.6.20) with respect to \(x\) yields \begin {equation} \label {eq:2.6.22} F(x,y)=x^2y^3e^y+\phi (y)\end {equation} Differentiating this with respect to \(y\) yields \[ F_y=(3x^2y^2+x^2y^3)e^y+\phi '(y)\] and comparing this with (2.6.21) shows that \(\phi '(y)=e^y\). Therefore we set \(\phi (y)=e^y\) in (2.6.22) and conclude that \[ (x^2y^3+1)e^y=c \] is an implicit solution of (2.6.19). It is also an implicit solution of (2.6.18). Solve the equation using an integrating factor \begin {equation} \label {eq:2.6.23} (3xy+6y^2)\,dx+(2x^2+9xy)\,dy=0 \end {equation}
Solution
In (2.6.23) \[ M=3xy+6y^2,\ N=2x^2+9xy\] and \[ M_y-N_x=(3x+12y)-(4x+9y)=-x+3y\] Therefore \[ {M_y-N_x\over M}={-x+3y\over 3xy+6y^2} \quad \mbox{ and } \quad {N_x-M_y\over N}={x-3y\over 2x^2+9xy}\] so Theorem  2.6.1 does not apply. Following the more general argument that led to Theorem  2.6.1 , we look for functions \(p=p(x)\) and \(q=q(y)\) such that \[ M_y-N_x=p(x)N-q(y)M\] that is, \[ -x+3y=p(x)(2x^2+9xy)-q(y)(3xy+6y^2)\] Since the left side contains only first degree terms in \(x\) and \(y\), we rewrite this equation as \[ xp(x)(2x+9y)-yq(y)(3x+6y)=-x+3y\] This will be an identity if \begin {equation} \label {eq:2.6.24} xp(x)=A\mbox {\quad and\quad } yq(y)=B\end {equation} where \(A\) and \(B\) are constants such that \[ -x+3y=A(2x+9y)-B(3x+6y)\] or, equivalently, \[ -x+3y=(2A-3B)x+(9A-6B)y\] Equating the coefficients of \(x\) and \(y\) on both sides shows that the last equation holds for all \((x,y)\) if
\begin {eqnarray*} 2A-3B &=&-1 \\ 9A-6B &=&\phantom {-}3\end {eqnarray*}
which has the solution \(A=1\), \(B=1\). Therefore (2.6.24) implies that \[ p(x)={1\over x}\quad \mbox{ and } \quad q(y)={1\over y}\] Since \[ \int p(x)\,dx=\ln |x|\quad \mbox{ and} \quad \int q(y)\,dy=\ln |y|\] we can let \(P(x)=x\) and \(Q(y)=y\); hence, \(\mu (x,y)=xy\) is an integrating factor. Multiplying (2.6.23) by \(\mu \) yields the exact equation \[ (3x^2y^2+6xy^3)\,dx+(2x^3y+9x^2y^2)\,dy=0\] We leave it to you to use the method of Section 2.5 to show that this equation has the implicit solution \begin {equation} \label {eq:2.6.25} x^3y^2+3x^2y^3=c\end {equation} This is also an implicit solution of (2.6.23). Since \(x\equiv 0\) and \(y\equiv 0\) satisfy (2.6.25), you should check to see that \(x\equiv 0\) and \(y\equiv 0\) are also solutions of (2.6.23). (Why is it necesary to check this?)

  1. Use separation of variables to set up two integrals that must be solved in order to solve (2.6.26) \begin {equation} \label {eq:2.6.26} -y\,dx+(x+x^6)\,dy=0 \end {equation}
  2. Show that (2.6.26) is not exact and then show that (2.6.27) is an integrating factor for (2.6.26) by multiplying it on both sides and then showing the new equation is exact. \begin{equation} \label{eq:2.6.27} \mu (x,y)={1\over y(x+x^6)} \end{equation}
  3. Now that we converted (2.6.26) to an exact equation, use the exact method to try to solve and show that it fails and why
  4. Instead of the exact method, use a more general method and find an integrating factor of the form \( \mu(x,y)=x^ay^b \)
Solution
In (2.6.26) \[ M=-y,\ N=x+x^6\] and \[ M_y-N_x=-1-(1+6x^5)=-2-6x^5\] We look for functions \(p=p(x)\) and \(q=q(y)\) such that \[ M_y-N_x=p(x)N-q(y)M\] that is, \begin {equation} \label {eq:2.6.28} -2-6x^5=p(x)(x+x^6)+q(y)y\end {equation} The right side will contain the term \(-6x^5\) if \(p(x)=-6/x\). Then (2.6.28) becomes \[ -2-6x^5=-6-6x^5+q(y)y\] so \(q(y)=4/y\). Since \[ \int p(x)\,dx=-\int {6\over x}\,dx=-6\ln |x|=\ln {1\over x^6}\] and \[ \int q(y)\,dy=\int {4\over y}\,dy=4\ln |y|=\ln {y^4}\] we can take \(P(x)=x^{-6}\) and \(Q(y)=y^4\), which yields the integrating factor \(\mu (x,y)=x^{-6}y^4\). Multiplying (2.6.26) by \(\mu \) yields the exact equation \[ -{y^5\over x^6}\,dx+\left ({y^4\over x^5}+y^4\right ) \,dy=0\] We leave it to you to use the method of the Section 2.5 to show that this equation has the implicit solution \[ \left ({y\over x}\right )^5+y^5=k\] Solving for \(y\) yields \[ y=k^{1/5}x(1+x^5)^{-1/5}\] which we rewrite as \[ y=cx(1+x^5)^{-1/5} \] by renaming the arbitrary constant. This is also a solution of (2.6.26).
