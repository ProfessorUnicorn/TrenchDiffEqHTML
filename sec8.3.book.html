8.3 Solution of IVPs
Laplace Derivatives
In the rest of this chapter we’ll use the Laplace transform to solve IVPs for constant coefficient second order equations. To do this, we must know how the Laplace transform of a functions derivative \(\dot{f}(t)\) is related to the Laplace transform of \(f(t)\). The next theorem answers this question.
Theorem 8.3.1 Suppose \(f(t) \) is continuous on \([0,\infty )\) and of exponential order \(s_0\), and \(\dot{f}(t)\) is piecewise continuous on \([0,\infty ).\) Then \(f(t)\) and \(\dot{f}(t)\) have Laplace transforms for \(s > s_0,\) and \begin {equation} \label {eq:8.3.1} {\cal L} \big\{ \dot{f}(t) \big\}=s \, {\cal L} \big\{ f(t) \big\}-f(0) \end {equation}
We know from Theorem 8.1.6 that \({\cal L} \big\{ f(t) \big\}\) is defined for \(s>s_0\)
We first consider the case where \(\dot{f}(t)\) is continuous on \([0,\infty )\) and by definition (8.1.2) \begin {equation} \label {eq:8.3.2} {\cal L} \big\{ \dot{f}(t) \big\}=\int _0^\infty e^{-st} \, \dot{f}(t)\,dt \end {equation}
\begin {array}{rrl} U &=& e^{-st} &\quad\quad& dV &=& \dot{f}(t) \, dt \\ dU &=& -s \, e^{-st} dt &\quad\quad& V &=& f(t) \\ \end {array}
Now substituting into the IBP formula proves (8.3.1) \begin {align*} {\cal L} \big\{ \dot{f}(t) \big\} = & \lim_{x \to \infty} \Bigg.\Big[e^{-st}f(t)\Big]\Bigg|_{0}^{x} +s \int _0^\infty e^{-st} \, f(t)\,dt \\ = & \lim_{x \to \infty} \Big( e^{-sx}f(x) \Big) - f(0) +s \, {\cal L} \big\{ f(t) \big\}\\ = & - f(0) +s \, {\cal L} \big\{ f(t) \big\}\\ \end {align*}
  1.   Use (8.3.1) to calculate the Laplace transform of the derivative of \( f(t) = \cos \omega t \) \[ {\cal L} \big\{ \dot{f}(t) \big\} \]
  2.   Use (8.3.1) to solve the IVP \begin {equation} \label {eq:8.3.3} \dot{y}=ay \quad y(0)=y_0 \end {equation}
Solution
  1. First note that we already calculated the Laplace transform of \(f(t) = \cos \omega t\) in Example 8.1.4 \[ {\cal L} \big\{ \cos \omega t \big\} ={s\over s^2+\omega ^2}\] Now we'd like to find the Laplace transform of \[ \dot{f}(t) = \frac{d}{dt}\big( \cos \omega t \big) = - \omega \sin \omega t \] To calculate this using the definition (8.1.2) we would have to calculate the integral \begin {align*} {\cal L} \big\{ - \omega \sin \omega t \big\}= - \omega \int _0^\infty e^{-st} \, \sin \omega t \,dt \end {align*} But instead we will use Equation (8.3.1) \begin {align*} {\cal L} \big\{ \dot{f}(t) \big\} = & \, s \, {\cal L} \big\{ f(t) \big\}-f(0) \\ {\cal L} \left\{ - \omega \sin \omega t \right\} = & \, s \, {\cal L} \big\{ \cos \omega t \big\}-\cos 0 \\ = & \, s \, \left( {s\over s^2+\omega ^2} \right)-1 \\ = & \, {s^2\over s^2+\omega ^2} - {s^2+\omega ^2\over s^2+\omega ^2} \\ = & \, -{\omega ^2\over s^2+\omega ^2} \\ \end {align*} Now applying linearity we have \begin {align*} - \omega \, {\cal L} \left\{ \sin \omega t \right\} = & \, -{\omega ^2\over s^2+\omega ^2} \Longrightarrow {\cal L} \left\{ \sin \omega t \right\} = \, {\omega \over s^2+\omega ^2} \\ \end {align*} Which agrees with the result we obtained in Example 8.1.4 for the Laplace transform of \( \sin \omega t\)
  2. Solve \(\dot{y}=ay \quad y(0)=y_0\) using separation of variables (Section 2.1)
    Solving \(\dot{y}=ay \quad y(0)=y_0\) using using separation of variables and applying the initial condition \(y(0)=y_0 \) we obtain the solution \[ y = y_0 e^{at} \]
    We’ll now obtain this result by using the Laplace transform and applying (8.3.1) but to make the notation easier we'll start by letting capital \(Y(s)\) represent the Laplace transform of \(y\) \[ {\cal L} \left\{ y \right\} = Y(s) \] Now taking the Laplace transform of both sides of (8.3.3) and applying linearity and (8.3.1) we can solve for \(Y(s)\) to obtain \begin {align*} \dot{y} = & ay \tag{3} \\ {\cal L} \big\{ \dot{y} \big\} = & \, {\cal L} \big\{ ay \big\} \\ s \, {\cal L} \big\{ {y} \big\} - y(0) = & \, a \, {\cal L} \big\{ y \big\} \\ s \, Y(s) - y_0 = & \, a \, Y(s) \\ s \, Y(s) - a \, Y(s) = & \, y_0 \\ \big( s - a \big) Y(s) = & \, y_0 \\ Y(s) = & \, \frac{y_0}{s - a} \\ \end {align*}
  3. Finally we take the inverse Laplace transform of both sides to find \(y\) \begin {align*} {\cal L}^{-1} \Big[ Y(s) \Big] = & \, {\cal L}^{-1} \left[ \frac{y_0}{s - a} \right] \\ y = & \, y_0 \, \underset{e^{at}}{\underbrace{ {\cal L}^{-1} \left[ \frac{1}{s - a} \right]}} = \, y_0 \, e^{at} \\ \end {align*} Which agrees with the previous result!
    Verify \(y=y_0 \, e^{at}\) is a solution of the IVP (8.3.3)

While (8.3.1) helps us solve first order differential equations using the Laplace transform, in order to solve second order differential equations using the Laplace transform, we will need to know how to find the Laplace transform of the second derivative.
Theorem 8.3.2 Suppose \(f(t)\) and \(\dot{f}(t)\) are continuous on \([0,\infty )\) and of exponential order \(s_0,\) and that \(\ddot{f}(t)\) is piecewise continuous on \([0,\infty ).\) Then \(f(t)\), \(\dot{f}(t)\), and \(\ddot{f}(t)\) have Laplace transforms for \(s > s_0\) \begin {equation} \label {eq:8.3.4} {\cal L} \big\{ \dot{f}(t) \big\}=s \, {\cal L} \big\{ f(t) \big\}-f(0) \end {equation} And applying this same formula to the second derivative we obtain \begin {align} \label {eq:8.3.5} {\cal L} \big\{ \ddot{f}(t) \big\} = & \, s \, {\cal L} \big\{ \dot{f}(t) \big\}-\dot{f}(0) \nonumber \\ = & \, s \, \Big[ s \, {\cal L} \big\{ f(t) \big\}-f(0) \Big] -\dot{f}(0) \nonumber \\ = & \, s^2 \, {\cal L} \big\{ f(t) \big\}-s \, f(0) -\dot{f}(0) \end {align}
Laplace IVPs
We’ll now use the Laplace transform to solve IVPs for second order equations.
Use the Laplace transform to solve the IVP \begin {equation} \label {eq:8.3.6} \ddot{y}-6\dot{y}+5y=3e^{2t} \quad y(0)=2 \quad \dot{y}(0)=3 \end {equation}
Solution
  1. Taking Laplace transforms of both sides of the differential equation in (8.3.6) yields \begin {align} \label {eq:8.3.7} {\cal L} \big\{ \ddot{y}-6\dot{y}+5y \big\} \, = & \, {\cal L} \big\{ 3e^{2t}\big\} \nonumber\\ {\cal L} \big\{ \ddot{y} \big\} \quad - \quad 6 \, {\cal L} \big\{ \dot{y} \big\} \quad+\quad 5 \, {\cal L} \big\{ {y} \big\} \, = & \, 3 \, {\cal L} \big\{ e^{2t}\big\} \nonumber\\ \Big[ s^2 \, {\cal L} \big\{ y \big\}-s \, y(0) -\dot{y}(0) \Big] - 6 \, \Big[ s \, {\cal L} \big\{ y \big\}-y(0) \Big] + 5 \, {\cal L} \big\{ {y} \big\} = & \, 3 \left(\frac{1}{s-2} \right) \end {align} Now simplifying and collecting like terms and factoring out \(Y(s)\) we obtain \begin {align} \label {eq:8.3.8} \Big[ s^2 \, Y(s) -2s - 3 \Big] - 6 \, \Big[ s \, Y(s) - 2 \Big] + 5 \, Y(s) = & \, \frac{3}{s-2} \nonumber\\ s^2 \, Y(s) -2s - 3 -6sY(s) +12 + 5 \, Y(s) = & \, \frac{3}{s-2} \nonumber\\ s^2 \, Y(s) -6sY(s) + 5 \, Y(s) -2s +9 = & \, \frac{3}{s-2} \nonumber\\ \underbrace{\big( s^2 -6s + 5 \big)} Y(s) -(2s -9) = & \, \frac{3}{s-2} \end {align} And a good check on the calculations at this point is to note that the polynomial multiplied by \(Y(s)\) should be the characteristic polynomial of the complementary equation. Now solving for \(Y(s)\) and factoring the characteristic polynomial we obtain \begin {align*} \big( s^2 -6s + 5 \big) Y(s) = & \, \frac{3}{s-2} + \frac{(2s-9)(s-2)}{s-2} \\ Y(s) = & \, \frac{3+ (2s-9)(s-2)}{\big( s^2 -6s + 5 \big)\big(s-5 \big)} \\ Y(s) = & \, \frac{3+ (2s-9)(s-2)}{\big(s-2\big)\big(s-1 \big)\big(s-5 \big)} \\ \end {align*}
  2. At this point we have solved for \(Y(s)\) which is the Laplace transform of \(y(t)\) so all we have to do is take the inverse Laplace transform of this expression to find our solution. And hopefully the type of problem is familiar from Section 8.2 so we'll start by splitting up the expression using PFD \begin {align*} \frac{3+ (2s-9)(s-2)}{\big(s-2\big)\big(s-1 \big)\big(s-5 \big)} = & \, \frac{A}{\big(s-2\big)} + \frac{B}{\big(s-1 \big)} + \frac{C}{\big(s-5 \big)} \end {align*}
    Use Heaviside's method to calculate \(A,\) \(B,\) and \(C\)
    \begin {align*} A = & \Bigg. \frac{3+ (2s-9)(s-2)}{\big(s-1 \big)\big(s-5 \big)} \Bigg|_{s=2} = \frac{3+ (-5)(0)}{\big(1 \big)\big(-3 \big)} = -1\\ \\ B = & \Bigg. \frac{3+ (2s-9)(s-2)}{\big(s-2\big)\big(s-5 \big)} \Bigg|_{s=1} = \frac{3+ (-7)(-1)}{\big(-1\big)\big(-4 \big)} = \frac{5}{2} \\ \\ C = & \Bigg. \frac{3+ (2s-9)(s-2)}{\big(s-2\big)\big(s-1 \big)} \Bigg|_{s=5} = \frac{3+ (1)(3)}{\big(3\big)\big(4 \big)} =\frac{1}{2} \\ \end {align*} Substituting back in our coefficients we obtain \[ Y(s)=-\left({1\over s-2}\right) + {5\over 2}\left({1\over s-1}\right) +{1\over 2}\left({1\over s-5}\right) \] Finally to obtain \(y(t)\) we just take the inverse Laplace transform to obtain the solution of (8.3.6). \begin {align*} {\cal L}^{-1} \Big[ Y(s) \Big] = & \, - \underset{e^{2t}}{\underbrace{{\cal L}^{-1} \left[ {1\over s-2} \right]}} + {5\over 2} \underset{e^{t}}{\underbrace{ {\cal L}^{-1} \left[ {1\over s-1} \right]}} +{1\over 2} \underset{e^{5t}}{\underbrace{{\cal L}^{-1} \left[ {1\over s-5} \right]}} \\ y(t) = & \, \quad - e^{2t} \quad+\quad {5\over 2} e^{t} \quad+\quad {1\over 2} e^{5t} \\ \end {align*}
    Verify \(y=e^{2t} + {5\over 2} e^{t} + {1\over 2} e^{5t}\) is a solution of the IVP (8.3.6)




To obtain a generic form of (8.3.8), let’s apply the method of Example 8.3.2 to the general IVP \begin {equation} \label {eq:8.3.9} a\ddot{y}+b\dot{y}+cy=f(t) \quad y(0)=k_0 \quad \dot{y}(0)=k_1 \end {equation} Taking Laplace transforms of both sides of the differential equation in (8.3.9) and applying Theorem 8.3.2 yields \begin {align} \label {eq:8.3.10} {\cal L} \big\{ a \ddot{y}+ b \dot{y}+ c y \big\} \, = & \, {\cal L} \big\{ f(t) \big\} \nonumber\\ a \, {\cal L} \big\{ \ddot{y} \big\} \quad + \quad b \, {\cal L} \big\{ \dot{y} \big\} \quad+\quad c \, {\cal L} \big\{ {y} \big\} \, = & \, {\cal L} \big\{ f(t) \big\} \\ a \, \Big[ s^2 \, {\cal L} \big\{ y \big\}-s \, y(0) - \dot{y}(0) \Big] + b \, \Big[ s \, {\cal L} \big\{ y \big\}-y(0) \Big] + c \, {\cal L} \big\{ {y} \big\} = & \, {\cal L} \big\{ f(t) \big\} \nonumber \end {align} Now Substituting \({\cal L} \big\{ f(t) \big\} = F(s)\) and \({\cal L} \big\{ y(t) \big\} = Y(s)\) as well as our initial conditions \(y(0)=k_0\) and \(\dot{y}(0)=k_1 \) we obtain \begin {align} \label {eq:8.3.11} a \, \Big[ s^2 \, Y(s) - k_0 s - k_1 \Big] + b \, \Big[ s \, Y(s) - k_0 \Big] + c \, Y(s) = & \, F(s) \\ a s^2 \, Y(s) - a k_0 s - a k_1 \, + \, bs \, Y(s) - bk_0 \, + c \, Y(s) = & \, F(s) \nonumber \\ a s^2 \, Y(s) + bs \, Y(s) + c \, Y(s) \, - a k_0 s - a k_1 - bk_0 = & \, F(s) \nonumber \\ \underset{p(s)}{\underbrace{\Big( a s^2 + bs + c \Big)}} Y(s) \, - a \big( k_0 s + k_1 \big) - bk_0 = & \, F(s) \nonumber \end {align} The coefficient of \(Y(s)\) on the left is the characteristic polynomial of the complementary equation for (8.3.9) \[ p(s)=as^2+bs+c \] Substituting this and moving the terms involving \(k_0\) and \(k_1\) to the right side of (8.3.11) yields \begin {align} \label {eq:8.3.12} p(s) \, Y(s) \, = & \, F(s) + a \big( k_0 s + k_1 \big) + bk_0 \end {align} This equation corresponds to (8.3.8) of Example 8.3.2 but is a general representation. Although we will not apply this directly as a formula as we will opt to show our work (as in Example 8.3.2) we can use this as a check point.It may be easier to think of (8.3.12) rewritten as \begin {equation} \label {eq:8.3.13} p(s) \, Y(s)= \, F(s)+a \Big( \dot{y}(0) + s \, y(0) \Big) + b y(0) \end {equation}




Use the Laplace transform to solve the IVP \begin {equation} \label {eq:8.3.14} 2\ddot{y}+3\dot{y}+y=8e^{-2t} \quad y(0)=-4 \quad \dot{y}(0)=2 \end {equation}
Solution
  1. Taking Laplace transforms of both sides of the differential equation in (8.3.14) yields \begin {align*} {\cal L} \big\{ 2\ddot{y}+3\dot{y}+y \big\} \, = & \, {\cal L} \big\{ 8e^{-2t}\big\} \nonumber\\ 2 \, {\cal L} \big\{ \ddot{y} \big\} \quad + \quad 3 \, {\cal L} \big\{ \dot{y} \big\} \quad+\quad \, {\cal L} \big\{ {y} \big\} \, = & \, 8 \, {\cal L} \big\{ e^{-2t}\big\} \nonumber\\ 2 \, \Big[ s^2 \, {\cal L} \big\{ y \big\}-s \, y(0) -\dot{y}(0) \Big] + 3 \, \Big[ s \, {\cal L} \big\{ y \big\}-y(0) \Big] + \, {\cal L} \big\{ {y} \big\} = & \, 8 \left(\frac{1}{s+2} \right) \end {align*} Now simplifying and collecting like terms and factoring out \(Y(s)\) we obtain \begin {align*} 2 \, \Big[ s^2 \, Y(s) +4 s - 2 \Big] + 3 \, \Big[ s \, Y(s) +4 \Big] + \, Y(s) = & \, \frac{8}{s+2} \nonumber\\ 2s^2 \, Y(s) +8s - 4 +3sY(s) +12 + Y(s) = & \, \frac{8}{s+2} \nonumber\\ 2 s^2 \, Y(s) +3sY(s) + Y(s) +8s +8 = & \, \frac{8}{s+2} \nonumber\\ \big( 2s^2 +3s + 1 \big) Y(s) +8(s +1) = & \, \frac{8}{s+2} \end {align*} We'll pause to check our calculations at this point to note that the polynomial multiplied by \(Y(s)\) is the characteristic polynomial of the complementary equation. Solving for \(Y(s)\) and factoring the characteristic polynomial we obtain \begin {align*} \big( 2s^2 +3s + 1 \big) Y(s) +8(s +1) = & \, \frac{8}{s+2} \\ \big( 2s^2 +3s + 1 \big) Y(s) = & \, \frac{8}{s+2} - \frac{8(s +1)(s+2)}{s+2} \\ Y(s) = & \, \frac{8 - 8(s +1)(s+2)}{(s+2) \big( 2s^2 +3s + 1 \big) } \\ Y(s) = & \, \frac{8 - 8(s +1)(s+2)}{(s+2) \big( s + 1 \big) \big( 2s + 1 \big) } \\ \end {align*}
  2. At this point we have solved for \(Y(s)\) which is the Laplace transform of \(y(t)\) so all we have to do is take the inverse Laplace transform of this expression to find our solution. And hopefully the type of problem is familiar from Section 8.2 so we'll start by splitting up the expression using PFD \begin {align*} \frac{8 - 8(s +1)(s+2)}{(s+2) \big( 2s + 1 \big) \big( s + 1 \big) } = & \, \frac{A}{\big(s+2\big)} + \frac{B}{\big(s+1 \big)} + \frac{C}{\big(2s+1 \big)} \end {align*}
    Use Heaviside's method to calculate \(A,\) \(B,\) and \(C\)
    \begin {align*} A = & \Bigg. \frac{8 - 8(s +1)(s+2)}{\big( 2s + 1 \big) \big( s + 1 \big) } \Bigg|_{s=-2} = \frac{8 - 8(-1)(0)}{\big( -3 \big) \big( -1 \big) } = \frac{8}{3} \\ \\ B = & \Bigg.\frac{8 - 8(s +1)(s+2)}{(s+2) \big( 2s + 1 \big) } \Bigg|_{s=-1} = \frac{8 - 8(0)(1)}{(1) \big(-1 \big) } = - 8\\ C = & \Bigg. \frac{8 - 8(s +1)(s+2)}{(s+2) \big( s + 1 \big) } \Bigg|_{s=-\frac{1}{2}} = \frac{8 - 8 \left(\frac{1}{2}\right)\left(\frac{3}{2}\right)}{ \left(\frac{3}{2}\right) \left( \frac{1}{2} \right) } = \frac{2}{ \left(\frac{3}{4}\right) } = \frac{8}{3} \\ \\ \end {align*} Substituting back in our coefficients we obtain \[ Y(s)= \frac{8}{3} \left({1\over s+2}\right) -8\left({1\over s+1}\right) + \frac{8}{3} \left({1\over 2s+1}\right) \]
  3. And to take the Laplace transform we want the denominators in the form \(s-a\) so we factor out the \(2\) in the bottom of the last term to obtain \[ \frac{8}{3} \left({1\over 2s+1} \right) \cdot \frac{\left( \frac{1}{2} \right)}{\left(\frac{1}{2}\right)} =\frac{8}{3} \cdot \left( \frac{1}{2} \right) \left({1\over s+\frac{1}{2}}\right) =\frac{4}{3} \left({1\over s+\frac{1}{2} } \right) \] Finally to obtain \(y(t)\) we just take the inverse Laplace transform to obtain the solution of (8.3.14). \begin {align*} {\cal L}^{-1} \Big[ Y(s) \Big] = & \, \frac{8}{3} \, \underset{e^{-2t}}{\underbrace{{\cal L}^{-1} \left[ {1\over s+2} \right]}} -8 \, \underset{e^{-t}}{\underbrace{{\cal L}^{-1} \left[ {1\over s+1} \right]}} + \frac{4}{3} \, \underset{e^{-t/2}}{\underbrace{ {\cal L}^{-1} \left[ {1\over s+\frac{1}{2}} \right]}}\\ y(t) = & \, \quad \frac{8}{3} e^{-2t} \quad-\quad 8 e^{-t}\quad +\quad \frac{4}{3} e^{-t/2} \\ \end {align*}
    Verify \(y=\frac{8}{3} e^{-2t} - 8 e^{-t} + \frac{4}{3} e^{-t/2}\) is a solution of the IVP (8.3.14)



Use the Laplace transform to solve the IVP \begin {equation} \label {eq:8.3.15} \ddot{y}+2\dot{y}+2y=1 \quad y(0)=-3 \quad \dot{y}(0)=1 \end {equation}
Solution
  1. Taking Laplace transforms of both sides of the differential equation in (8.3.15) yields \begin {align*} {\cal L} \big\{ \ddot{y}+2\dot{y}+2y \big\} \, = & \, {\cal L} \big\{ 1 \big\} \nonumber\\ {\cal L} \big\{ \ddot{y} \big\} \quad + \quad 2 \, {\cal L} \big\{ \dot{y} \big\} \quad+\quad 2 \, {\cal L} \big\{ {y} \big\} \, = & \, {\cal L} \big\{ 1 \big\} \nonumber\\ \Big[ s^2 \, {\cal L} \big\{ y \big\}-s \, y(0) -\dot{y}(0) \Big] + 2 \, \Big[ s \, {\cal L} \big\{ y \big\}-y(0) \Big] + 2 \, {\cal L} \big\{ {y} \big\} = & \, \frac{1}{s} \end {align*} Substituting \( {\cal L} \big\{ y \big\} = Y(s)\) and the initial values we can simplify and collecting like terms and factor out the \(Y(s)\) to obtain \begin {align*} \Big[ s^2 \, Y(s) +3 s - 1 \Big] + 2\, \Big[ s \, Y(s) +3 \Big] + 2 \, Y(s) = & \, \frac{1}{s} \nonumber\\ s^2 \, Y(s) +3s - 1 +2sY(s) +6 + 2Y(s) = & \, \frac{1}{s} \nonumber\\ s^2 \, Y(s) +2sY(s) + 2Y(s) +3s +5 = & \, \frac{1}{s} \nonumber\\ \big( s^2 +2s + 2 \big) Y(s) +(3s +5) = & \, \frac{1}{s} \end {align*} We'll pause to check our calculations at this point to note that the polynomial multiplied by \(Y(s)\) is the characteristic polynomial of the complementary equation. Solving for \(Y(s)\) we obtain \begin {align*} \big( s^2 +2s + 2 \big) Y(s) +(3s +5) = & \, \frac{1}{s} \\ \big( 2s^2 +2s + 2 \big) Y(s) = & \, \frac{1}{s} - \frac{s(3s +5)}{s} \\ Y(s) = & \, \frac{1 - s(3s +5)}{s \big( 2s^2 +2s + 2 \big) } \\ \end {align*}
  2. And we find out that we cannot factor the characteristic polynomial since it is an irreducible quadratic. So we split it up using PFD \begin {equation*} {1-s(3s+5)\over s\left(s^2+2s+2\right)} = {A\over s} + {Bs+C\over s^2+2s+2} \end {equation*}
    Solve for \(A,\) \(B,\) and \(C\)
    Solving for \(A,\) \(B,\) and \(C\) (Example 8.2.8) and substituting back we obtain \begin {equation*} Y(s) = \frac{1}{2} \left( {1\over s} \right) - \frac{7}{2} \left( {s\over s^2+2s+2} \right) - {6\over s^2+2s+2} \end {equation*} Since \(Y(s)\) is the Laplace transform of \(y(t)\) all we have to do is take the inverse Laplace transform of this expression to find our solution. \begin {equation*} {\cal L}^{-1} \Big[ Y(s) \Big] = \frac{1}{2} \, {\cal L}^{-1} \left[ {1\over s} \right] - \frac{7}{2} \, {\cal L}^{-1} \left[ {s\over s^2+2s+2} \right] - 6 \, {\cal L}^{-1} \left[ {1\over s^2+2s+2} \right] \end {equation*} But we'll have to first write the denominator in the correct form so completing the square on the bottom we obtain (8.2.16) \begin {equation*} y(t) = \frac{1}{2} \, {\cal L}^{-1} \left[ {1\over s} \right] - \frac{7}{2} \, {\cal L}^{-1} \left[ {s\over (s+1)^2+1} \right] - 6 \, {\cal L}^{-1} \left[ {1\over (s+1)^2+1} \right] \end {equation*}
  3. Adding and subtracting \(1\) from the middle term we obtain \begin {equation*} - \frac{7}{2} \, {\cal L}^{-1} \left[ {(s + 1) - 1\over (s+1)^2+1} \right] = - \frac{7}{2} \, {\cal L}^{-1} \left[ {s + 1\over (s+1)^2+1} \right] + \frac{7}{2} \, {\cal L}^{-1} \left[ {1\over (s+1)^2+1} \right] \end {equation*} Substituting it back in and combining the like terms \begin {align*} y(t) = & \frac{1}{2} \, {\cal L}^{-1} \left[ {1\over s} \right] - \frac{7}{2} \, {\cal L}^{-1} \left[ {s + 1\over (s+1)^2+1} \right] + \underbrace{ \left(\frac{7}{2}-6\right) \, {\cal L}^{-1} \left[ {1\over (s+1)^2+1} \right]} \\ = & \frac{1}{2} \, \underset{1}{ \underbrace{ {\cal L}^{-1} \left[ {1\over s} \right]}} - \frac{7}{2} \, \underset{e^{-t}\cos t}{ \underbrace{ {\cal L}^{-1} \left[ {s + 1\over (s+1)^2+1} \right]}} - \frac{5}{2} \, \underset{e^{-t}\sin t}{ \underbrace{ {\cal L}^{-1} \left[ {1\over (s+1)^2+1} \right]}}\\ = & \, \quad \frac{1}{2} \quad - \quad \frac{7}{2} e^{-t}\cos t \quad - \frac{5}{2} e^{-t}\sin t \\ = & \, \quad \frac{1}{2} - \left( \frac{7}{2} \cos t + \frac{5}{2} \sin t \right) e^{-t}\\ \end {align*}
    Verify \(y=\frac{1}{2} - \left( \frac{7}{2} \cos t + \frac{5}{2} \sin t \right) e^{-t}\) is a solution of the IVP (8.3.15)




You may have noticed we applied Theorems 8.3.1 and 8.3.2 without verifying that the unknown function \(y(t)\) satisfies their hypotheses. This is characteristic of the formal manipulative way in which the Laplace transform is used to solve differential equations. Any doubts about the validity of the method for solving a given equation can be resolved by verifying that the resulting function \(y(t)\) is the solution of the given problem by differentiating and substituting.
