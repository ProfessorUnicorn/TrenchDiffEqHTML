8.4 The Unit Step Function
Unit Step Function
In the next section we’ll consider IVPs where \(a\), \(b\), and \(c\) are constants and \(f(t) \) is piecewise continuous. \[ a\ddot{y} + b\dot{y} + cy = f(t) \quad y(0)=k_0 \quad \dot{y}(0)=k_1 \] In this section we’ll develop procedures for using the table of Laplace transforms to find Laplace transforms of piecewise continuous functions, and to find the piecewise continuous inverses of Laplace transforms.

We’ll now develop the method of Example 8.4.1 into a systematic way to find the Laplace transform of a piecewise continuous function. It is convenient to introduce the unit step function, defined as \begin {equation} \label {eq:8.4.4} u(t)= \left\{ \begin {array}{rl} 0 & t<0 \\ 1 & t\ge 0 \end {array} \right. \end {equation} Thus, \(u(t)\) “steps” from the constant value \(0\) to the constant value \(1\) at \(t=0\). If we replace \(t\) by \(t-a \) in (8.4.4), then the step now occurs at \(t=a \) \[ u(t-a )= \left \{ \begin {array}{rl} 0 & t < a \\ 1 & t\ge a \end {array} \right . \]

The unit step function enables us to represent piecewise continuous functions conveniently. For example, consider the function \begin {equation} \label {eq:8.4.5} f(t)=\left\{ \begin {array}{rl} f_0(t) &0\le t<a_1\\ f_1(t) &t\ge a_1 \end {array}\right. \end {equation} where we assume that \(f_0(t)\) and \(f_1(t)\) are defined on \([0,\infty )\), even though they equal \(f(t)\) only on the indicated intervals. This assumption enables us to rewrite (8.4.5) as \begin {equation} \label {eq:8.4.6} f(t)=f_0(t)+ \Big (f_1(t)-f_0(t)\Big ) \, u(t-a_1) \end {equation} To verify this, note that if \(t<a_1\) then \(u(t-a_1)=0\) and (8.4.6) becomes \[ f(t)=f_0(t)+\Big (f_1(t)-f_0(t)\Big ) \cdot (0)=f_0(t)\] If \(t\ge a_1\) then \(u(t-a_1)=1\) and (8.4.6) becomes \[ f(t)=f_0(t)+ \Big (f_1(t)-f_0(t)\Big ) \cdot (1)=f_1(t)\]

Now we'll rewrite the piecewise function (8.4.1) from Example 8.4.1 in terms of the unit step function \begin {equation*} f(t)=\left \{ \begin {array}{cl} 2t+1 & 0\le t<2 \\ 3t & t\ge 2 \end {array} \right. \tag{1} \end {equation*} From the definition (8.4.5) we have \(f_0(t)=2t+1\) and \(f_1(t)=3t\) with the "step" occurring at \(a_1=2.\) Substituting these values into (8.4.6) we can rewrite \(f(t)\) as \begin {align*} f(t) = & (2t+1)+ \underset{(t-1)}{\underbrace{\Big ( 3t-(2t+1) \Big ) }} u(t-2) \\ = & (2t+1)+ \big(t-1\big) \, u(t-2) \\ \end {align*} Now using the definition (8.1.2) the Laplace transform of \(f(t)\) can be represented as \begin{align*} {\cal L} \Big\{ f(t) \Big\} = & \int _0^\infty e^{-st} f(t) \, dt \\ = & \int _0^\infty \Big( (2t+1)+ \big(t-1\big) \, u(t-2) \Big) \, e^{-st} \, dt \\ = & \underset{{\cal L} \Big\{ 2t+1 \Big\}}{\underbrace{\int _0^\infty (2t+1)\, e^{-st} \, dt}} + \int _0^\infty \big(t-1\big) \, u(t-2) \, e^{-st} \, dt \\ \end{align*} Now we can recognize the first integral as the Laplace transform of \((2t+1)\) and for the second integral, since \(u(t-a)\) is piecewise we split up the integral at the point of discontinuity to obtain \begin{align*} \int _0^\infty \big(t-1\big) \, u(t-2) \, e^{-st} \, dt = & \int _0^2 \underset{0}{\underbrace{\big(t-1\big) \cdot 0 \cdot e^{-st}}} \, dt + \int _2^\infty \big(t-1\big) \cdot 1 \cdot e^{-st} \, dt \\ = & \quad \int _0^2 0 \, dt \quad+\quad \int _2^\infty \big(t-1\big) e^{-st} \, dt \\ \end{align*} The first integral is now just the definite integral of zero, which is just zero, so we have \begin{align*} {\cal L} \Big\{ f(t) \Big\} = & \, {\cal L} \Big\{ 2t+1 \Big\} + \int _2^\infty \big(t-1\big) e^{-st} \, dt \\ \end{align*} Now just as we did in Example 8.4.1 we make a change of variables using substitution to rewrite the integral in terms of a Laplace transform. \begin{align*} {\cal L} \Big\{ f(t) \Big\} = & \, {\cal L} \Big\{ 2t+1 \Big\} + e^{-2s} \, {\cal L} \Big\{ t+1 \Big\} \end{align*}



Now we will write this property generically so we can apply it to future calculations. By the definition (8.1.2) the Laplace transform \[ {\cal L} \big\{ g(t) \, u(t-a) \big\} =\int _0^\infty e^{-st} \, u(t-{a}) \, g(t)\, dt \] Since \(u(t-a)\) is piecewise we split up the integral at the point of discontinuity to obtain \begin{align*} {\cal L} \big\{ g(t) \, u(t-a) \big\} = & \int _0^{a} e^{-st} \, \cdot 0 \cdot \, g(t)\, dt + \int _{a}^\infty e^{-st} \, \cdot 1 \cdot \, g(t)\, dt \\ = & \int _0^{a} 0 \, dt + \int _{a}^\infty e^{-st} \, g(t)\, dt \end{align*} The first integral is now just the definite integral of zero, which is just zero, so we have \begin{align*} {\cal L} \big\{ g(t) \, u(t-a) \big\} = & \int _{a}^\infty e^{-st} \, g(t)\, dt \end{align*} Now just as we did in Example 8.4.1 we will make a change of variables using substitution to rewrite the integral in terms of a Laplace transform. Notice that the limits of integration are from \({a}\) to infinity, but to write it in terms of a Laplace transform we need the limits of integration to be from \(0\) to infinity, so we make a substitution to shift the limits of integration \begin{array}{rrrrr} u &=& t-{a} &\quad& \dfrac{du}{dt}&=&1 \\ \Longrightarrow t &=& u+{a} &\quad& \Longrightarrow du &=& dt \end{array} Substituting we obtain \begin{align*} {\cal L} \big\{ g(t) \, u(t-{a}) \big\} = & \int _{t={a}}^\infty e^{-st} \, g(t)\, dt \\ = & \int _{u=0}^\infty e^{-s(u+{a})} \, g(u+{a})\, du \\ = & \int _{u=0}^\infty e^{-su} \, e^{-s{a}} \, g(u+{a})\, du \\ \end{align*} And since \(e^{-2s}\) is constant we can factor it out of the integral \begin{align*} {\cal L} \big\{ g(t) \, u(t-{a}) \big\} = & e^{-s{a}} \int _{u=0}^\infty e^{-su} \, g(u+{a})\, du \\ \end{align*} And finally since \(u\) is an arbitrary variable we can replace it with \(t\) so we can more readily recognize it as the Laplace transform of \(g(t+{a})\) \begin{align*} {\cal L} \big\{ g(t) \, u(t-{a}) \big\} = & e^{-s{a}} \underset{ {\cal L} \big\{ g(t+{a}) \big\}}{\underbrace{\int _{t=0}^\infty e^{-st} \, g(t+{a})\, dt}} \\ = & e^{-s{a}} {\cal L} \big\{ g(t+{a}) \big\} \\ \end{align*}

Replacing \(g(t)\) by \(g(t-{a} )\) in Theorem 8.4.1 yields the next theorem.
Theorem 8.4.2 Second Shifting Theorem
If \({a} \ge 0\) and \( {\cal L} \big\{ g(t) \big\} =G(s)\) exists for \(s>s_0\) then \({\cal L} \Big\{ g(t-{a}) \, u(t-{a} ) \Big\} \) exists for \(s>s_0\) and \[ {\cal L} \Big\{ g(t-{a}) \, u(t-{a} ) \Big\} = e^{-s{a} } \, {\cal L} \big\{ g(t) \big\} \] or equivalently \begin {equation} \label {eq:8.4.12} {\cal L}^{-1} \Big[ e^{-s{a} } \, G(s) \Big] = g(t-{a}) \, u(t-{a}) \end {equation}
Recall that the First Shifting Theorem (Theorem 8.1.3) states that multiplying a function by \(e^{at}\) corresponds to shifting the argument of its transform by \(a\) units. Theorem 8.4.2 states that multiplying a Laplace transform by the exponential \(e^{-{a} s}\) corresponds to shifting the argument of the inverse transform by \({a} \) units.
