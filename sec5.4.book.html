5.4 The Method of Undetermined Coefficients I
Undetermined Coefficients
In this section we consider the constant coefficient equation where \(r \) is a constant and \(G(x)\) is a polynomial. \begin {equation} \label {eq:5.4.1} a\frac{d^2y}{dx^2}+b\frac{dy}{dx}+cy=e^{r x}G(x) \end {equation}
From Theorem 5.3.2, the general solution of (5.4.1) is \(y=y_p+c_1y_1+c_2y_2\), where \(y_p\) is a particular solution of (5.4.1) and \(\{y_1,y_2\}\) is a fundamental set of solutions of the complementary equation \[ a\frac{d^2y}{dx^2}+b\frac{dy}{dx}+cy=0\] In Section 5.2 we showed how to find \(\{y_1,y_2\}\). In this section we’ll show how to find \(y_p\). The procedure that we’ll use is called the method of undetermined coefficients.
Our first example is similar to Exercises 16–21


Exponential Examples \( e^{r x}\)
Given the differential equation \begin {equation} \label {eq:5.4.2} \frac{d^2y}{dx^2}-7\frac{dy}{dx}+12y=4e^{2x} \end {equation}
  1. Solve the complementary equation
  2. Find a particular solution
  3. Find the general solution
Solution
  1. Solve the complementary equation \begin {equation} \label {eq:5.4.3} \frac{d^2y}{dx^2}-7\frac{dy}{dx}+12y=0 \end {equation}
    Differentiate and substitute \( y = e^{rx} \) to find and solve the characteristic equation
    We obtain the characteristic equation \[ r^2 -7r +12 = 0 \]
    Use the method in Example 5.2.1 to find a fundamental set of solutions
    Solving the characteristic equation we obtain a fundamental set of solutions \[ y_1 = e^{3x} \quad y_2 = e^{4x} \]
    Verify \( y_1 = e^{3x} \) and \( y_2 = e^{4x} \) are solutions of (5.4.3)
  2. Now we will try to find a particular solution for the nonhomogeneous equation by guessing a solution based on the forcing function \[ \frac{d^2y}{dx^2}-7\frac{dy}{dx}+12y = \underset{f(x)}{\underbrace{4e^{2x}}} \]
    Since the forcing function \( f(x) = 4e^{2x} \) is an exponential function linearly independent from the complementary solutions, we can guess an exponential function with the same argument \(2x\) \[ y = Ae^{2x} \]
    Differentiate \( Ae^{2x} \) twice
    After differentiating we obtain \( dy/dx = 2A e^{2x} \) and \(d^2y/dx^2 = 4A e^{2x} \) which we substitute into the nonhomogeneous equation (5.4.2) \begin{align*} \frac{d^2y}{dx^2}-7\frac{dy}{dx}+12y = & 4e^{2x} \\ \overset{\frac{d^2y}{dx^2} }{\overbrace{\Big( 4 A e^{2x} \Big)}} - 7 \overset{\frac{dy}{dx} }{\overbrace{\Big( 2 A e^{2x} \Big)}} + 12 \overset{y }{\overbrace{\Big( Ae^{2x} \Big)}} = & 4e^{2x} \\ \big( 4 A \big) e^{2x} - \big( 14 A \big) e^{2x} + \big( 12 A \big) e^{2x} = & 4e^{2x} \\ \big( 2 A \big) e^{2x} = & 4e^{2x} \\ A \, e^{2x} = & \frac{1}{2}e^{2x} \\ \end{align*} Again equating the coefficients we obtain \( A = \frac{1}{2} \) and substituting the value in the arbitrary exponential function \( y = Ae^{2x} \) to obtain the particular solution \[ y = \frac{1}{2}e^{2x} \]
    Verify \( y = \frac{1}{2}e^{2x} \) is a particular solution of (5.4.2)
  3. Now that we have a fundamental set of solutions to the complementary equation \( \{ y_1, y_2\} = \{ e^{3x}, e^{4x} \}\) and a particular solution to the nonhomogeneous equation \( y_p = \frac{1}{2}e^{2x} \), then we can use Theorem 5.3.2 to find the general solution \( y = y_p + c_1 y_1 + c_2 y_2 \) \[ y = \frac{1}{2}e^{2x} + c_1 e^{3x} + c_2 e^{4x} \]
    Verify \( y = \frac{1}{2}e^{2x} + c_1 e^{3x} + c_2 e^{4x} \) is a solution of (5.4.2)
Given the differential equation \begin {equation} \label {eq:5.4.4} \frac{d^2y}{dx^2}-7\frac{dy}{dx}+12y=5e^{4x} \end {equation}
  1. Solve the complementary equation
  2. Find a particular solution
  3. Find the general solution
Solution
  1. The complementary equation for (5.4.4) is \begin {equation*} \frac{d^2y}{dx^2}-7\frac{dy}{dx}+12y=0 \end {equation*} We notice right away that this is the same as (5.4.3) from Example 5.4.1 which we already solved and found a fundamental set of solutions \[ \{ y_1, y_2 \} = \{ e^{3x}, e^{4x} \} \]
  2. Now we will try to find a particular solution for the nonhomogeneous equation by guessing a solution based on the forcing function \[ \frac{d^2y}{dx^2}-7\frac{dy}{dx}+12y = \underset{f(x)}{\underbrace{5e^{4x}}} \]
    Now if we approach this problem the same way we did in Example 5.4.1, we might just notice that since the forcing function is \( f(x) = 5e^{4x} \) then a reasonable guess for a solution might be \( y = Ae^{4x}\), however, this is where we run into an issue. While guessing like this worked in the past, it won't work here, since \(e^{4x}\) is actually a solution of the complementary equation. Let's try it to find out what happens if we were to use \( Ae^{4x}\) \begin{align*} \frac{d^2y}{dx^2}-7\frac{dy}{dx}+12y = & 5e^{4x} \\ \overset{\frac{d^2y}{dx^2} }{\overbrace{\Big( 16 A e^{4x} \Big)}} - 7 \overset{\frac{dy}{dx} }{\overbrace{\Big( 4 A e^{4x} \Big)}} + 12 \overset{y }{\overbrace{\Big( Ae^{4x} \Big)}} = & 5e^{4x} \\ \underset{0}{\underbrace{\big( 16 A \big) e^{2x} - \big( 28 A \big) e^{2x} + \big( 12 A \big) e^{2x}}} = & 5e^{4x} \\ \end{align*} Since \(e^{4x}\) is a solution of the complementary equation, then the entire left side of the equation simplified to zero. Therefore this guess will not help us find a particular solution No problem, just as we have done many times in the past, we will just update our guess to \( y = ue^{4x}\) where \(u\) is some function of \(x\) to be determined.
    Differentiate \( y= ue^{4x} \) twice
    Differentiating we obtain: \begin{align*} \frac{dy}{dx} =& \frac{du}{dx}e^{4x} + 4ue^{4x} \\ \\ \frac{d^2y}{dx^2} =& \frac{d^2u}{dx^2}e^{4x} + 8\frac{du}{dx}e^{4x} + 16ue^{4x} \\ \end{align*}
    Substituting into the nonhomogeneous equation (5.4.4) \begin{array}{rrrrr} \displaystyle \frac{d^2y}{dx^2} &=& \displaystyle\frac{d^2u}{dx^2}e^{4x} + 8\displaystyle\frac{du}{dx}e^{4x}& + 16\,ue^{4x}& \\ \displaystyle -7\frac{dy}{dx} &=& -7\displaystyle\frac{du}{dx}e^{4x}& - 28\,ue^{4x}& \\ + \quad\quad\quad\quad\quad 12y &=& & +12\,ue^{4x}& \\ \hline \underbrace{ \displaystyle \frac{d^2y}{dx^2} -7\frac{dy}{dx} + 12y} &=& \displaystyle\frac{d^2u}{dx^2}e^{4x} + \displaystyle\frac{du}{dx}e^{4x}& + \,0\, ue^{4x}& \\ 5e^{4x} &=& \displaystyle\frac{d^2u}{dx^2}e^{4x} + \displaystyle\frac{du}{dx}e^{4x}& \\ \end{array} Cancelling out the \( e^{4x}\) we obtain a new equation \begin{align} \label {eq:5.4.5} \frac{d^2u}{dx^2} + \frac{du}{dx} = & \underset{f(x)}{\underbrace{5} } \end{align} Now to solve this equation we will again guess! Since the forcing function \(f(x)=5\) is just a constant we might think to guess a constant like we did in Example 5.3.1 but that equation had the function \(y\) in it whereas this one only has derivatives, so we need to guess the antiderivative of a constant \(\int c dx = cx \) which is just a linear function so a simple linear function is the best guess, so we will try \(u=Ax\)
    Differentiating \( u=Ax\) twice
    Differentiating we obtain \begin{align*} \frac{du}{dx} = A \quad\text{and}\quad y = 0 \end{align*} Substituting in to (5.4.5) \begin{array}{clclc} \underbrace{\displaystyle\frac{d^2u}{dx^2}} &+& \underbrace{\displaystyle\frac{du}{dx}} &=& 5 \\ 0 &+& A &=& 5 \end{array}
    Therefore \( A = 5 \) and we have our function \(u=5x\) which yields our particular solution \( y = ue^{4x} \) \[ y = 5x e^{4x} \]
    Verify \( y = 5x e^{4x} \) is a particular solution of (5.4.4)
  3. Now that we have a fundamental set of solutions to the complementary equation \( \{ y_1, y_2\} = \{ e^{3x}, e^{4x} \}\) and a particular solution to the nonhomogeneous equation \( y_p = 5x e^{4x} \), then we can use Theorem 5.3.2 to find the general solution \( y = y_p + c_1 y_1 + c_2 y_2 \) \[ y = (5x + c_1) e^{4x} + c_2 e^{3x} \]
    Verify \( y = (5x + c_1) e^{4x} + c_2 e^{3x} \) is a solution of (5.4.4)
Given the differential equation \begin {equation} \label {eq:5.4.6} \frac{d^2y}{dx^2}-8\frac{dy}{dx}+16y=2e^{4x} \end {equation}
  1. Solve the complementary equation
  2. Find a particular solution
  3. Find the general solution
Solution
  1. The complementary equation for (5.4.6) is \begin {equation*} \frac{d^2y}{dx^2}-8\frac{dy}{dx}+16y=0 \end {equation*}
    Differentiate and substitute \( y = e^{rx} \) to find and solve the characteristic equation
    We obtain the characteristic equation \[ r^2 -8r + 16 = 0 \]
    Use the method in Example 5.2.2 to find a fundamental set of solutions
    Solving the characteristic equation and using variation of parameters we have a fundamental set of solutions \[ y_1 = e^{4x} \quad y_2 = x e^{4x} \]
  2. Now we will try to find a particular solution for the nonhomogeneous equation by guessing a solution based on the forcing function \[ \frac{d^2y}{dx^2}-8\frac{dy}{dx}+16y = \underset{f(x)}{\underbrace{2e^{4x}}} \]
    While the forcing function \( f(x) = 2e^{4x} \) is we learned in Example 5.4.2 that we can't guess a solution of the form \(y=A e^{4x} \) since \(e^{4x} \) is a solution to the complementary equation. As it turns out, even guessing a solution of the form \(y=A x e^{4x} \) also won't work since \(xe^{4x} \) is also a solution to the complementary equation. No problem, we can use the same strategy again and guess \( y = ue^{4x}\) where \(u\) is some function of \(x\) to be determined.
    Differentiate \( y= ue^{4x} \) twice
    Differentiating we obtain: \begin{align*} \frac{dy}{dx} =& \frac{du}{dx}e^{4x} + 4ue^{4x} \\ \\ \frac{d^2y}{dx^2} =& \frac{d^2u}{dx^2}e^{4x} + 8\frac{du}{dx}e^{4x} + 16ue^{4x} \\ \end{align*}
    Substituting into the nonhomogeneous equation (5.4.6) \begin{array}{rrllr} \displaystyle \frac{d^2y}{dx^2} &=& \displaystyle\frac{d^2u}{dx^2}e^{4x} + 8\displaystyle\frac{du}{dx}e^{4x}& + 16\,ue^{4x}& \\ \displaystyle -8\frac{dy}{dx} &=& -8\displaystyle\frac{du}{dx}e^{4x}& - 32\,ue^{4x}& \\ + \quad\quad\quad\quad\quad 16y &=& & +16\,ue^{4x}& \\ \hline \underbrace{ \displaystyle \frac{d^2y}{dx^2} -8\frac{dy}{dx} + 16y} &=& \displaystyle\frac{d^2u}{dx^2}e^{4x} + \,0\,\displaystyle\frac{du}{dx}e^{4x}& + \,0\, ue^{4x}& \\ 2e^{4x} &=& \displaystyle\frac{d^2u}{dx^2}e^{4x} & & \\ \end{array} Cancelling out the \( e^{4x}\) we obtain a new equation \begin{align*} \frac{d^2u}{dx^2} = & 2 \end{align*} Now this equation is integrable! So we can just find the antiderivative twice.
    Antidifferentiate \( \frac{d^2u}{dx^2} = 2 \) twice
    So we obtain \(u = x^2 + c_1x + c_2\) and select a simple \(u\) with \(c_1=0\) and \(c_2=0\) and use \(u = x^2\) therefore we have our particular solution \(y=ue^{4x}\) \begin{align*} y=x^2e^{4x} \end{align*}
    Verify \( y=x^2e^{4x} \) is a particular solution of (5.4.6)
  3. Now that we have a fundamental set of solutions to the complementary equation \( \{ y_1, y_2\} = \{ e^{4x}, xe^{4x} \}\) and a particular solution to the nonhomogeneous equation \( y_p = x^2e^{4x} \), then we can use Theorem 5.3.2 to find the general solution \( y = y_p + c_1 y_1 + c_2 y_2 \) \[ y = ( x^2 + c_1 + c_2 x ) e^{4x} \]
    Verify \( y = ( x^2 + c_1 + c_2 x ) e^{4x} \) is a solution of (5.4.4)



Summary
Examples 5.4.1, 5.4.2, and 5.4.3 illustrate the form of a particular solution \(y_p\) of a constant coefficient equation \begin {equation} \label {eq:5.4.8} a\frac{d^2y}{dx^2}+b\frac{dy}{dx}+cy=ke^{r x} \end {equation} With complementary equation \begin {equation} a\frac{d^2y}{dx^2}+b\frac{dy}{dx}+cy=0 \end {equation}
  1. If \(e^{r x}\) isn’t a solution of the complementary equation (5.4.8) then \(y_p=Ae^{r x}\)
  2. If \(e^{r x}\) is a solution of (5.4.7) but \(xe^{r x}\) is not, then \(y_p=Axe^{r x}\)
  3. If both \(e^{r x}\) and \(xe^{r x}\) are solutions of (5.4.7), then \(y_p=Ax^2e^{r x}\)
We won't apply these as formulas but instead always show the work as we did in the examples
Exponential Polynomials \( e^{r x} \, P(x) \)
Examples 5.4.1, 5.4.2, and 5.4.3 covered the basic cases of solving equations with an exponential forcing function: \[ a\frac{d^2y}{dx^2}+b\frac{dy}{dx}+cy=ke^{r x}\]
However, we still have to determine what to do if the forcing function is the product of an exponential and a polynomial \( P(x) \) \[ a\frac{d^2y}{dx^2}+b\frac{dy}{dx}+cy= e^{r x}\,P(x)\]
We will stick with the same strategy we used in Examples 5.4.2, and 5.4.3 where we seek solutions of the form \(y=ue^{r x}\)
Differentiate \(y=ue^{r x}\) twice
Differentiating we obtain: \begin{align*} \frac{dy}{dx} =& \frac{du}{dx}e^{r x} + r ue^{4x} \\ \\ \frac{d^2y}{dx^2} =& \frac{d^2u}{dx^2}e^{r x} + 2r \frac{du}{dx}e^{r x} + r ^2 ue^{r x} \\ \end{align*}
Substituting into the nonhomogeneous equation \begin{array}{rrrrr} \displaystyle a\frac{d^2y}{dx^2} &=& a\displaystyle\frac{d^2u}{dx^2}e^{r x} + 2ar \displaystyle\frac{du}{dx}e^{r x}& + ar ^2 \,ue^{r x}& \\ \displaystyle +b\frac{dy}{dx} &=& +b \displaystyle\frac{du}{dx}e^{r x}& +b r \,ue^{r x}& \\ + \quad\quad\quad\quad\quad cy &=& & +c\,ue^{4x}& \\ \hline \underbrace{ \displaystyle a\frac{d^2y}{dx^2} +b\frac{dy}{dx} + cy} & \\ e^{r x} \, P(x) &=& {\small a}\displaystyle\frac{d^2u}{dx^2}e^{r x} + {\small (2a r + b)}\displaystyle\frac{du}{dx}e^{r x}& + {\small (a r ^2 + b r + c)} ue^{r x}& \\ \end{array} Cancelling out the \( e^{r x}\) we obtain a new equation \begin{align*} \, P(x) = & a\frac{d^2u}{dx^2} + \underset{p'(r)}{\underbrace{(2a r + b)}} \frac{du}{dx} + \underset{p(r)}{\underbrace{(a r ^2 + b r + c)}} u \end{align*} And we recognize \(p(r) = a r ^2 + b r + c\) as the characteristic polynomial which is the coefficient of \(u\) and its derivative \(p'(r) = 2 a r + b \) as the coefficient of \(du/dx\) so the equation for \(u\) will take on the form
\begin {equation} \label {eq:5.4.9} a\frac{d^2u}{dx^2}+ p'(r) \frac{du}{dx} + p(r) u = P(x) \end {equation} However, we won't memorize this as a formula but instead apply it as a process to help us come up with the equation for \(u\) in any particular case.


Note, however, that if \(e^{r x}\) is a solution of the complementary equation then \(p(r )=0\) so (5.4.9) reduces to \[ a\frac{d^2u}{dx^2}+ p'(r) \frac{du}{dx}=P(x)\] Whereas if both \(e^{r x}\) and \(xe^{r x}\) are solutions of the complementary equation then \(p(r)=a(r-r )^2 = 0\) and \(p'(r)=2a(r-r )= 0\) so (5.4.9) reduces to \[ a\frac{d^2u}{dx^2}=P(x)\]
Exponential Polynomial Examples \( e^{r x} \, P(x) \)
Given the differential equation \begin {equation} \label {eq:5.4.10} \frac{d^2y}{dx^2}-3\frac{dy}{dx}+2y=(x^2+2x-1) e^{3x} \end {equation}
  1. Solve the complementary equation
  2. Find a particular solution
  3. Find the general solution
Solution
  1. The complementary equation for (5.4.10) is \begin {equation*} \frac{d^2y}{dx^2}-3\frac{dy}{dx}+2y=0 \end {equation*}
    Differentiate and substitute \( y = e^{rx} \) to find and solve the characteristic equation
    We obtain the characteristic equation \[ r^2 -3r + 2 = 0 \]
    Use the method in Example 5.2.2 to find a fundamental set of solutions
    \[ y_1 = e^{x} \quad y_2 = e^{2x} \]
  2. Now we will try to find a particular solution for the nonhomogeneous equation by guessing a solution based on the forcing function \[ \frac{d^2y}{dx^2}-3\frac{dy}{dx}+2y = \underset{f(x)}{\underbrace{(x^2+2x-1)e^{3x}}} \]
    Since the forcing function \( f(x) = (x^2+2x-1)e^{3x} \) is an exponential multiplied by a polynomial, we will seek solutions of the form \( y = u e^{3x}\) where \(u\) is some function of \(x\) to be determined.
    Differentiate \( y= ue^{3x} \) twice
    Differentiating we obtain: \begin{align*} \frac{dy}{dx} =& \frac{du}{dx}e^{3x} + 3ue^{3x} \\ \\ \frac{d^2y}{dx^2} =& \frac{d^2u}{dx^2}e^{3x} + 6\frac{du}{dx}e^{3x} + 9ue^{3x} \\ \end{align*}
    Substituting into the nonhomogeneous equation (5.4.10) \begin{array}{rrrrr} \displaystyle \frac{d^2y}{dx^2} &=& \displaystyle\frac{d^2u}{dx^2}e^{3x} + 6\displaystyle\frac{du}{dx}e^{3x}& + 9\,ue^{3x}& \\ \displaystyle -3\frac{dy}{dx} &=& -3\displaystyle\frac{du}{dx}e^{3x}& - 9\,ue^{3x}& \\ + \quad\quad\quad\quad\quad 2y &=& & +2\,ue^{3x}& \\ \hline \underbrace{ \displaystyle \frac{d^2y}{dx^2} -3\frac{dy}{dx} + 2y} &=& \\ (x^2+2x-1)e^{3x} &=& \displaystyle\frac{d^2u}{dx^2}e^{3x} + \,3\,\displaystyle\frac{du}{dx}e^{3x}& + \,2\, ue^{3x}& \\ \end{array} Cancelling out the \( e^{3x}\) we obtain a new equation \begin{align} \label {eq:5.4.11} \frac{d^2u}{dx^2} + \,3\frac{du}{dx} + \,2u = & \, x^2+2x-1 \end{align} Now to solve this equation we will again guess! Since the forcing function \(f(x)=x^2+2x-1\) is a quadratic polynomial we will guess an arbitrary quadratic polynomial \( u = Ax^2 + Bx + C \) like we did in Example 5.3.2
    Differentiate \( u = Ax^2 + Bx + C\) twice
    After differentiating we obtain \( du/dx = 2Ax + B\) and \(d^2u/dx^2 = 2A \) which we substitute into the nonhomogeneous equation in \(u\) (5.4.11) \begin{align*} \frac{d^2u}{dx^2} + \,3\frac{du}{dx} + \,2u = & \, x^2+2x-1 \\ \overset{\frac{d^2u}{dx^2} }{\overbrace{\Big( 2A \Big)}} + 3 \overset{\frac{du}{dx} }{\overbrace{\Big( 2Ax + B \Big)}} + 2 \overset{u }{\overbrace{\Big( Ax^2 + Bx + C \Big)}} = & x^2+2x-1 \\ \end{align*}
    Distribute and collect all the like terms
    Expanding out all the terms, and rearranging them we obtain: \begin{align*} \big( 2A \big)x^2 + \big( 6A + 2B \big)x + \big( 2A + 3 B + 2 C \big) = & x^2 + 2x - 1 \\ \end{align*}
    Equating the coefficients on both sides of the equation to obtain a system of equations: \begin{align*} \left\{ \begin{array}{r r r} 2A + 3 B + 2C &=& -1 \\ 6A + 2B &=& 2 \\ 2A &=& 1 \end{array} \right. \end{align*}
    Solve the system for \(A,B,\) and \(C\)
    Substituting \(2A=1\) into the first two equations we obtain
    \begin{align*} \left\{ \begin{array}{r l} 3B + 2C =&-2 \\ 2B =&-1 \\ \end{array} \right. \end{align*}
    Substituting \(B=-1/2\) we obtain \(C=-1/4.\) Now we can substitute the values we obtained for \(A,B,\) and \(C\) in the arbitrary quadratic polynomial \( u = Ax^2 + Bx + C\) to obtain the particular solution \[ u = \frac{1}{2} x^2 - \frac{1}{2} x - \frac{1}{4} \]
    Verify \( u = \frac{1}{2} x^2 - \frac{1}{2} x - \frac{1}{4} \) is a solution of (5.4.11)
    Factoring \(u\) we obtain: \[ u = \frac{1}{4} (2 x^2 - 2 x - 1) \] And finally substituting \(u\) to obtain our particular solution \( y = u e ^{3x} \) \[ y = \frac{1}{4} (2 x^2 - 2 x - 1) e ^{3x} \]
    Verify \( y = \frac{1}{4} (2 x^2 - 2 x - 1) e ^{3x} \) is a solution of (5.4.10)
  3. Now that we have a fundamental set of solutions to the complementary equation \( \{ y_1, y_2\} = \{ e^{x}, e^{2x} \}\) and a particular solution to the nonhomogeneous equation \( y_p = \frac{1}{4} (2 x^2 - 2 x - 1) e ^{3x} \), then we can use Theorem 5.3.2 to find the general solution \( y = y_p + c_1 y_1 + c_2 y_2 \) \[ y = \frac{1}{4} (2 x^2 - 2 x - 1) e ^{3x} + c_1 e^x + c_2 e^{2x} \]
    Verify \( y = \frac{1}{4} (2 x^2 - 2 x - 1) e ^{3x} + c_1 e^x + c_2 e^{2x} \) is a solution of (5.4.10)
Given the differential equation \begin {equation} \label {eq:5.4.12} \frac{d^2y}{dx^2} - 4 \frac{dy}{dx} + 3y = (12x^2 + 8x + 6) e^{3x} \end {equation}
  1. Solve the complementary equation
  2. Find a particular solution
  3. Find the general solution
Solution
  1. The complementary equation for (5.4.12) is \begin {equation*} \frac{d^2y}{dx^2} - 4 \frac{dy}{dx} + 3y = 0 \end {equation*}
    Differentiate and substitute \( y = e^{rx} \) to find and solve the characteristic equation
    We obtain the characteristic equation \[ r^2 -4r + 3 = 0 \]
    Use the method in Example 5.2.2 to find a fundamental set of solutions
    \[ y_1 = e^{3x} \quad y_2 = e^{x} \]
  2. Now we will try to find a particular solution for the nonhomogeneous equation by guessing a solution based on the forcing function \[ \frac{d^2y}{dx^2} - 4 \frac{dy}{dx} + 3y = \underset{f(x)}{\underbrace{(12x^2 + 8x + 6) e^{3x}}} \]
    Now if we approach this problem the same way we did in Example 5.4.4, we might just notice that since the forcing function is \( f(x) = (12x^2+8x+6)e^{3x} \) then a reasonable guess for a solution might be \( y = u e^{3x}\), however, this is where we run into an issue, just like we experienced in Example 5.4.2. While guessing like this worked in the past, it won't work here, since \( e^{3x}\) is actually a solution of the complementary equation. Let's try it to find out what happens if we were to use \( y = u e^{3x}\)
    Differentiate \( y = u e^{3x}\) twice
    Differentiating \( y = u e^{3x}\) we obtain \begin{align*} \frac{dy}{dx} = & \frac{du}{dx} e^{3x} + 3u e^{3x} \\ \\ \frac{d^2y}{dx^2} = & \frac{d^2u}{dx^2} e^{3x} + 6\, \frac{du}{dx} e^{3x} + 9u e^{3x} \\ \end{align*}
    Substituting into the nonhomogeneous equation (5.4.12) \begin{array}{rrrrr} \displaystyle \frac{d^2y}{dx^2} &=& \displaystyle\frac{d^2u}{dx^2}e^{3x} + 6\displaystyle\frac{du}{dx}e^{3x}& + 9\,ue^{3x}& \\ \displaystyle -4\frac{dy}{dx} &=& -4\displaystyle\frac{du}{dx}e^{3x}& - 12\,ue^{3x}& \\ + \quad\quad\quad\quad\quad 3y &=& & +3\,ue^{3x}& \\ \hline \underbrace{ \displaystyle \frac{d^2y}{dx^2} -4\frac{dy}{dx} + 3y} &=& \displaystyle\frac{d^2u}{dx^2}e^{3x} + \,2\,\displaystyle\frac{du}{dx}e^{3x}& + \,0\, ue^{3x}& \\ (12x^2+8x+6)e^{3x} &=& \displaystyle\frac{d^2u}{dx^2}e^{3x} + \,2\,\displaystyle\frac{du}{dx}e^{3x}& & \\ \end{array} Cancelling out the \( e^{3x}\) we obtain a new equation \begin{align} \frac{d^2u}{dx^2} + \,2\,\displaystyle\frac{du}{dx} = & \, \underset{f(x)}{\underbrace{12x^2+8x+6}} \end{align} So far so good... just as before we now have an equation in \(u\) and we will again guess! Since the forcing function \(f(x)=12x^2+8x+6\) is a quadratic polynomial we will guess an arbitrary quadratic polynomial \( u = Ax^2 + Bx + C \) like we did in Example 5.3.2
    Differentiate \( u = Ax^2 + Bx + C\) twice
    After differentiating we obtain \( du/dx = 2Ax + B\) and \(d^2u/dx^2 = 2A \) which we substitute into the nonhomogeneous equation in \(u\) (5.4.13) \begin{align*} \frac{d^2u}{dx^2} + \,2\,\displaystyle\frac{du}{dx} = & \, 12x^2+8x+6 \\ \overset{\frac{d^2u}{dx^2} }{\overbrace{\Big( 2A \Big)}} + 2 \overset{\frac{du}{dx} }{\overbrace{\Big( 2Ax + B \Big)}} = & \, 12x^2+8x+6 \\ \end{align*}
    Distribute and collect all the like terms
    Expanding out all the terms, and rearranging them we obtain: \begin{align*} \big( 4A \big)x + \big( 2A + 2B \big) = & 12x^2+8x+6 \\ \end{align*}
    Normally at this point we would simply equate the coefficients to obtain a system of equations but since there are no \(x^2\) terms present on the left side, then there is no choice for \(A\) and \(B\) that would create a polynomial equal to the right side. This is actually due to the fact that our guess original was \(y=ue^{3x}\) while \(e^{3x}\) was already a solution to the complementary equation. Although at first it appeared to work out, once we got the to the \(u\) equation that is where re ran into trouble.
    With the \(u\) equation we guessed a quadratic polynomial \( u = Ax^2 + Bx + C\) but differentiating and substituting into (5.4.13) resulted in no \(x^2\) terms so if instead we guess a cubic polynomial then that should work so we will guess \( u = Ax^3 + Bx^2 + Cx + D\) instead
    Differentiate \( u = Ax^3 + Bx^2 + Cx + D\) twice
    After differentiating we obtain \( du/dx = 3A x^2 + 2B x + C \) and \(d^2u/dx^2 = 6A x + 2B \) which we substitute into the nonhomogeneous equation in \(u\) (5.4.13) \begin{align*} \frac{d^2u}{dx^2} + \,2\,\displaystyle\frac{du}{dx} = & \, 12x^2+8x+6 \\ \overset{\frac{d^2u}{dx^2} }{\overbrace{\Big( 6A x + 2B \Big)}} + 2 \overset{\frac{du}{dx} }{\overbrace{\Big( 3A x^2 + 2B x + C \Big)}} = & \, 12x^2+8x+6 \\ \end{align*}
    Distribute and collect all the like terms
    Expanding out all the terms, and rearranging them we obtain: \begin{align*} \big( 6A \big)x^2 + \big( 6A + 4B \big)x + \big( 2B + 2C \big) = & 12x^2+8x+6 \\ \end{align*}
    Equating the coefficients we obtain the system of equations
    \begin{align*} \left\{ \begin{array}{r r r} 2B + 2C &=& 6 \\ 6A + 4 B &=& 8 \\ 6A &=& 12 \end{array} \right. \end{align*}
    Solve the system for \(A,B,\) and \(C\)
    The last equation yields \(A=2\) and substituting that into the second equation we obtain
    \begin{align*} \left\{ \begin{array}{r r} 2B + 2C &=& 6 \\ 4 B &=& -4 \\ \end{array} \right. \end{align*}
    Resulting in \(B=-1\) and substituting that into the first equation, we obtain \(C = 4.\) Now we can substitute the values we obtained for \(A,B,\) and \(C\) in the arbitrary cubic polynomial \( u = Ax^3 + Bx^2 + Cx + D\) to obtain a particular solution \[ u = 2 x^3 - x^2 + 4x + D \]
    Verify \( u = 2 x^3 - x^2 + 4x + D \) is a solution of \( \frac{d^2u}{dx^2} + 2 \frac{du}{dx} = 12x^2+8x+6\)
    Since \(D\) is still arbitrary and we only need one particular solution, we'll select \(D = 0\) so we have \[ u = 2 x^3 - x^2 + 4x \]
    Verify \( u = 2 x^3 - x^2 + 4x \) is a solution of \( \frac{d^2u}{dx^2} + 2 \frac{du}{dx} = 12x^2+8x+6\)
    Finally substituting back to get our particular solution \( y = u e^{3x} \) \[ y = x (2 x^2 - x + 4) e^{3x} \]
    Verify \( y = x (2 x^2 - x + 4) e^{3x} \) is a solution of (5.4.12)
  3. Now that we have a fundamental set of solutions to the complementary equation \( \{ y_1, y_2\} = \{ e^{3x}, e^{x} \}\) and a particular solution to the nonhomogeneous equation \( y_p = x (2 x^2 - x + 4) e^{3x} \), then we can use Theorem 5.3.2 to find the general solution \( y = y_p + c_1 y_1 + c_2 y_2 \) \[ y = \big[ x (2 x^2 - x + 4) + c_1 \big] e^{3x} + c_2 e^x \]
    Verify \( y = \big[ x (2 x^2 - x + 4) + c_1 \big] e^{3x} + c_2 e^x \) is a solution of (5.4.12)
Given the differential equation \begin {equation} \label {eq:5.4.14} 4\frac{d^2y}{dx^2} + 4\frac{dy}{dx} + y = ( 144x^2 + 48x -8) e^{-\frac{1}{2}x} \end {equation}
  1. Solve the complementary equation
  2. Find a particular solution
  3. Find the general solution
Solution
  1. The complementary equation for (5.4.14) is \begin {equation*} 4\frac{d^2y}{dx^2} + 4\frac{dy}{dx} + y = 0 \end {equation*}
    Differentiate and substitute \( y = e^{rx} \) to find and solve the characteristic equation
    We obtain the characteristic equation \[ 4r^2 -4r + 1 = 0 \]
    Use the method in Example 5.2.2 to find a fundamental set of solutions
    \[ y_1 = x e^{-\frac{1}{2}x} \quad y_2 = e^{-\frac{1}{2}x} \]
  2. Now we will try to find a particular solution for the nonhomogeneous equation by guessing a solution based on the forcing function \[ 4\frac{d^2y}{dx^2} + 4\frac{dy}{dx} + y = \underset{f(x)}{\underbrace{( 144x^2 + 48x -8) e^{-\frac{1}{2}x}}} \]
    Now since the forcing function is \( f(x) = ( 144x^2 + 48x -8)e^{-\frac{1}{2}x} \) then a reasonable guess for a solution might be \( y = u e^{-\frac{1}{2}x}\), however, this is where we run into an issue, just like we experienced in Example 5.4.2. While guessing like this worked in the past, it won't work here, since \( e^{-\frac{1}{2}x}\) is actually a solution of the complementary equation. Let's try it to find out what happens if we were to use \( y = u e^{-\frac{1}{2}x}\)
    Differentiate \( y = u e^{-\frac{1}{2}x}\) twice
    Differentiating \( y = u e^{-\frac{1}{2}x}\) we obtain \begin{align*} \frac{dy}{dx} = & \frac{du}{dx} e^{-\frac{1}{2}x} -\frac{1}{2} u e^{-\frac{1}{2}x} \\ \\ \frac{d^2y}{dx^2} = & \frac{d^2u}{dx^2} e^{-\frac{1}{2}x} - \frac{du}{dx} e^{-\frac{1}{2}x} + \frac{1}{4}u e^{-\frac{1}{2}x} \\ \end{align*}
    Substituting into the nonhomogeneous equation (5.4.14) \begin{array}{rrrrr} \displaystyle 4\frac{d^2y}{dx^2} &=& 4\displaystyle\frac{d^2u}{dx^2}e^{-\frac{1}{2}x} - 4\displaystyle\frac{du}{dx}e^{-\frac{1}{2}x}& + \,ue^{-\frac{1}{2}x}& \\ \displaystyle +4\frac{dy}{dx} &=& 4\displaystyle\frac{du}{dx}e^{-\frac{1}{2}x}& - 2\,ue^{-\frac{1}{2}x}& \\ + \quad\quad\quad\quad\quad y &=& & +\,ue^{-\frac{1}{2}x}& \\ \hline \underbrace{ \displaystyle 4\frac{d^2y}{dx^2} +4\frac{dy}{dx} + y} &=& 4 \displaystyle\frac{d^2u}{dx^2}e^{-\frac{1}{2}x} + \,0\,\displaystyle\frac{du}{dx}e^{-\frac{1}{2}x}& + \,0\, ue^{-\frac{1}{2}x}& \\ (144x^2 + 48x -8)e^{-\frac{1}{2}x} &=& 4 \displaystyle\frac{d^2u}{dx^2}e^{-\frac{1}{2}x} \quad\quad\quad\quad\quad \, \, & & \\ \end{array} Cancelling out the \( 4e^{-\frac{1}{2}x}\) we obtain a new equation \begin{align} \frac{d^2u}{dx^2} = & \, 36x^2 + 12x -2 \end{align} Now this equation is integrable! So we can just find the antiderivative twice.
    Antidifferentiate \( \frac{d^2u}{dx^2} = \, 36x^2 + 12x -2 \) twice
    So we obtain \(u = 3x^4 + 2x^3 - x^2 + c_1x + c_2\) and select a simple \(u\) with \(c_1=0\) and \(c_2=0\) and use \(u = 3x^4 + 2x^3 - x^2\) therefore we have our particular solution \(y=ue^{-\frac{1}{2}x}\) \begin{align*} y= x^2 (3x^2 + 2x - 1) e^{-\frac{1}{2}x} \end{align*}
    Verify \( x^2 (3x^2 + 2x - 1) e^{-\frac{1}{2}x} \) is a particular solution of (5.4.14)
  3. Now that we have a fundamental set of solutions to the complementary equation \( \{ y_1, y_2\} = \{ x e^{-\frac{1}{2}x}, e^{-\frac{1}{2}x} \}\) and a particular solution to the nonhomogeneous equation \( y_p = x^2 (3x^2 + 2x - 1) e^{-\frac{1}{2}x} \), then we can use Theorem 5.3.2 to find the general solution \( y = y_p + c_1 y_1 + c_2 y_2 \) \[ y = \big[ x^2 (3x^2 + 2x - 1) + c_1 x + c_2 \big] e^{-\frac{1}{2}x} \]
    Verify \( y = \big[ x^2 (3x^2 + 2x - 1) + c_1 x + c_2 \big] e^{-\frac{1}{2}x} \) is a solution of (5.4.12)



Summary
Examples 5.4.4, 5.4.5, and 5.4.6 illustrate the form of a particular solution \(y_p\) of a constant coefficient equation \begin {equation} a\frac{d^2y}{dx^2}+b\frac{dy}{dx}+cy= \, e^{r x} \, P(x) \end {equation} With complementary equation \begin {equation} a\frac{d^2y}{dx^2}+b\frac{dy}{dx}+cy=0 \end {equation}
  1. If \(e^{r x}\) isn’t a solution of the complementary equation (5.4.17) then \(y_p= G(x) \, e^{r x} \)
  2. If \(e^{r x}\) is a solution of (5.4.16) but \(xe^{r x}\) is not, then \(y_p= x \, G(x) \, e^{r x} \)
  3. If both \(e^{r x}\) and \(xe^{r x}\) are solutions of (5.4.16), then \(y_p= x^2 \, G(x) \, e^{r x} \)
where \(G(x)\) is a polynomial of the same degree as \(P(x)\). Note that we won't apply these as formulas but instead always show the work as we did in the examples



Note that in all three cases, we sought solutions of the form \(y=ue^{rx}\) but which then reduced the differential equation in \(y\) to another differential equation in \(u\) where we could then guess a polynomial function. However, you could also solve them by substituting the appropriate form for \(y_p\) and its derivatives directly into \[ a\frac{d^2y}{dx^2}+b\frac{dy}{dx}+cy=e^{r x} \, P(x)\] However, if you try this method you will find out that the computations are more tedious than those we encountered in the examples.



In Case a.) if \(e^{r x}\) isn’t a solution of the complementary equation the equation for \(u\) will be of the form \[ a\frac{d^2u}{dx^2}+ p'(r) \frac{du}{dx}+p(r )u= \, P(x)\] with a particular solution of the form \(u_p=G(x)\), a polynomial of the same degree as \(P(x)\), whose coefficients can be found by the method used in Example 5.4.4.



In Case b.) the equation for \(u\) will be of the form \[ a\frac{d^2u}{dx^2}+ p'(r) \frac{du}{dx}= \, P(x) \] (no \(u\) term on the left), with a particular solution of the form \(u_p=xG(x)\), where \(G(x)\) is a polynomial of the same degree as \(P(x)\) whose coefficients can be found by the method used in Example 5.4.5.
In Case c.) the equation for \(u\) will be of the form \[ a\frac{d^2u}{dx^2}=\, P(x) \] with a particular solution of the form \(u_p=x^2 G(x)\) that can be obtained by integrating \(P(x)/a\) twice and taking the constants of integration to be zero, as in Example 5.4.6.
Using the Principle of Superposition
The next example shows how to combine the method of undetermined coefficients and Theorem 5.3.3, the principle of superposition.
Find a particular solution of \begin {equation} \label {eq:5.4.17} \frac{d^2y}{dx^2}-7\frac{dy}{dx}+12y=4e^{2x}+5e^{4x} \end {equation}
Solution In Example 5.4.1we found that \(y_{p_1}=2e^{2x}\) is a particular solution of \[ \frac{d^2y}{dx^2}-7\frac{dy}{dx}+12y=4e^{2x}\] and in Example 5.4.2we found that \(y_{p_2}=5xe^{4x}\) is a particular solution of \[ \frac{d^2y}{dx^2}-7\frac{dy}{dx}+12y=5e^{4x}\] Therefore the principle of superposition implies that \(y_p=2e^{2x}+5xe^{4x}\) is a particular solution of (5.4.17).
