5.6 Reduction of Order
Reduction of Order
In this section we give a method for finding the general solution of \begin {equation} \label {eq:5.6.1} P_0 \frac{d^2y}{dx^2}+P_1 \frac{dy}{dx} +P_2 y=F(x) \end {equation} If we know a nontrivial solution \(y_1\) of the complementary equation \begin {equation} \label {eq:5.6.2} P_0 \frac{d^2y}{dx^2}+P_1 \frac{dy}{dx} +P_2 y=0 \end {equation} The method is called reduction of order because it reduces the task of solving (5.6.1) to solving a first order equation. Unlike the method of undetermined coefficients, it does not require \(P_0 \), \(P_1 \), and \(P_2 \) to be constants, or \(F(x)\) to be of any special form.



By now you shouldnâ€™t be surprised that we look for solutions of (5.6.1) in the form \begin {equation} \label {eq:5.6.3} y=uy_1 \end {equation} where \(u\) is some function of \(x\) to be determined and \(y_1\) satisfies the complementary equation (5.6.2) so we have \begin {equation*} P_0 \frac{d^2y_1}{dx^2}+P_1 \frac{dy_1}{dx} +P_2 y_1=0 \end {equation*}
Differentiate \( y=uy_1 \) twice
Differentiating \( y=uy_1 \) we obtain: \begin{align*} \frac{dy}{dx} = & \frac{du}{dx} y_1+u \frac{dy_1}{dx} \\ \\ \frac{d^2y}{dx^2} = & \frac{d^2u}{dx^2} y_1+2 \frac{du}{dx} \frac{dy_1}{dx} +u \frac{d^2y_1}{dx^2} \end{align*}
Substituting into (5.6.1) \begin{array}{rrrrrr} P_0 \displaystyle\frac{d^2y}{dx^2} &=& {\small P_0 y_1} \displaystyle\frac{d^2u}{dx^2} &+& {\small 2P_0 \displaystyle\frac{dy_1}{dx} } \, \displaystyle\frac{du}{dx} &+& P_0 \displaystyle\frac{d^2y_1}{dx^2} \, u \\ P_1 \frac{dy}{dx} &=& & & {\small P_1 y_1} \displaystyle\frac{du}{dx} &+& P_1 \displaystyle \frac{dy_1}{dx} \, u \\ + \quad\quad\quad\quad\quad\quad\quad\quad P_2 y &=& & & & & P_2 y_1 \, u \\ \hline \underset{F(x) }{\underbrace{{\small P_0 \displaystyle \frac{d^2y}{dx^2} + P_1 \frac{dy}{dx} + P_2 y}}} &=& \Big({\small P_0 y_1}\Big) \displaystyle\frac{d^2u}{dx^2} &+& \left( {\small 2P_0 \displaystyle\frac{dy_1}{dx} + P_1 y_1} \right) \, \displaystyle \frac{du}{dx} &+& \underset{0}{\underbrace{{\small \Big(P_0 \displaystyle\frac{d^2y_1}{dx^2} + P_1 \frac{dy_1}{dx} + P_2 y_1 \Big)}}} u \\ \end{array} We obtain the equation: \begin{align} \label {eq:5.6.4} \underset{Q_0(x)}{\underbrace{\Big({\small P_0 y_1}\Big)}} \frac{d^2u}{dx^2} + \underset{Q_1(x)}{\underbrace{\left( {\small 2P_0 \frac{dy_1}{dx}+P_1 y_1} \right)}}\,\frac{du}{dx} = F(x) \\ \end{align} Since the coefficients of \(\displaystyle \frac{d^2u}{dx^2}\) and \(\displaystyle \frac{u}{dx}\) were both functions of \(x\), we'll replace them with \(Q_0(x)\) and \(Q_1(x)\) to simplify the expression but we will not memorize these as formulas. \begin{align} \label {eq:5.6.5} Q_0(x) \frac{d^2u}{dx^2} + Q_1(x) \,\frac{du}{dx} = F(x) \\ \end{align} In this simplified form, it may appear that (5.6.5) is still second order, however, as only the first and second derivatives of \(u\) are present, and not \(u\) itself, this equation can be recast as a first order linear equation using a change of variables.
Letting \(z=\displaystyle\frac{du}{dx}\) and \(\displaystyle\frac{dz}{dx}=\displaystyle\frac{d^2u}{dx^2}\) then we can write (5.6.5) as a linear differential equation \begin{align*} Q_0(x) \frac{dz}{dx} + Q_1(x) \,z = F(x) \\ \end{align*} Therefore we can solve it using the techniques from Section 1.2 and antidifferentiation.
Reduction of Order Examples
Consider the differential equation \begin {equation} \label {eq:5.6.6} x\frac{d^2y}{dx^2}-(2x+1) \frac{dy}{dx} +(x+1)y=x^2 \end {equation}
  1. Verify that \(y_1=e^x\) is a solution of the complementary equation
  2. Use reduction of order to simplify (5.6.6) into a linear first order equation in \({du}/{dx}\)
  3. Convert the equation in \({du}/{dx}\) to a first order equation in \(z\) using the substitution \(z = {du}/{dx} \) then solve for \(z\)
  4. Substitute back to solve for \(u\) then substitute back again to solve for \(y\) in general
  5. Find 4 simple particular solutions of (5.6.6)
  6. Find a fundamental set of solutions of the complementary equation
Solution
  1. We wish to verify that \(y_1=e^x\) is a solution to the complementary equation: \begin {equation} \label {eq:5.6.7} x\frac{d^2y}{dx^2}-(2x+1) \frac{dy}{dx} +(x+1)y=0 \end {equation}
    Differentiate \(y_1=e^x\) twice
    Differentiating \(y_1=e^x\) we obtain \[ \frac{d y_1}{dx} = e^x \quad \text{and} \quad \frac{d^2 y_1}{dx^2} = e^x \]
    Substituting \(y_1=e^x\) and its derivatives in to (5.6.7) \begin{align*} x\frac{d^2y_1}{dx^2}-(2x+1) \frac{dy_1}{dx} +(x+1)y_1 \overset{?}{=} & 0 \\ xe^x-(2x+1) e^x +(x+1)e^x \overset{?}{=} & 0 \\ \Big( x-(2x+1) +(x+1) \Big) e^x \overset{?}{=} & 0 \\ \underset{0}{\underbrace{\Big( x-2x-1 + x+1 \Big)}} e^x {=} & 0 \quad \checkmark \\ \end{align*} Therefore \(y_1=e^x\) is a solution to the complementary equation (5.6.7)



  2. To use reduction of order we will seek solutions of (5.6.6) of the form \(y = uy_1\) where \(y_1\) is a solution to the complementary equation. Since \(y_1=e^x\) is a solution to the complementary equation (5.6.7) we seek solutions of the form \[y = ue^x\]
    Differentiate \(y = ue^x\) twice
    Differentiating \(y=ue^x\) we obtain \[ \frac{dy}{dx} = \Big(\frac{du}{dx} +u \Big)e^x \quad \text{and} \quad \frac{d^2y}{dx^2}= \Big(\frac{d^2u}{dx^2} + 2 \frac{du}{dx} + u \Big) e^x \]
    Substitute \(y = ue^x\) and its derivatives in to (5.6.6)
    Substituting \(y = ue^x\) and its derivatives in to (5.6.6) \begin{array}{rrrrrr} x \displaystyle\frac{d^2y}{dx^2} &=& {\small x e^x} \displaystyle\frac{d^2u}{dx^2} &+& {\small 2 x \, e^x } \, \displaystyle\frac{du}{dx} &+& x \, ue^x \\ -(2x+1) \displaystyle\frac{dy}{dx} &=& & & {\small -(2x+1) \, e^x} \displaystyle\frac{du}{dx} &+& -(2x+1) \, ue^x \\ + \quad\quad\quad (x+1) y &=& & & & & (x+1) \, ue^x \\ \hline \underset{x^2 }{\underbrace{{\tiny x \displaystyle \frac{d^2y}{dx^2} -(2x+1) \frac{dy}{dx} + (x+1) y}}} &=& \Big({\small x e^x}\Big) \displaystyle\frac{d^2u}{dx^2} &+& \underset{-1}{\underbrace{ {\small \Big( 2 x -(2x+1) \Big)e^x } }} \, \displaystyle \frac{du}{dx} &+& \underset{0}{\underbrace{ {\small \Big(x -(2x+1) + (x+1) \Big)} }} ue^x \\ \end{array}
    Simplify \(\Big( 2 x -(2x+1) \Big)\) and \( \Big(x -(2x+1) + (x+1) \Big) \)
    Thus we obtain the equation \begin{align*} x e^x \frac{d^2u}{dx^2} - e^x \frac{du}{dx} = x^2 \end{align*}
    Rewrite \( x e^x \frac{d^2u}{dx^2} - e^x \frac{du}{dx} = x^2 \) in standard linear form and identify \(p(x)\) and \(f(x)\)
    Divding by \(x e^x\) to get it into standard linear form (2.1.1) \begin{align} \label {eq:5.6.8} \frac{d^2u}{dx^2} - \frac{1}{x}\frac{du}{dx} = xe^{-x} \end{align}
  3. Although (5.6.8) appears to be a second order equation, since the dependent varible \(u\) is not present in the equation, we can actually use the substitution \(z = {du}/{dx} \) to rewrite it more clearly as a first order equation with only the first derivative present.
    Letting \(z = \displaystyle \frac{du}{dx}\) and differentiating both sides \begin{align*} \frac{dz}{dx} = \frac{d}{dx}\left(\frac{du}{dx}\right) = \frac{d^2u}{dx^2} \end{align*} Now substituting into (5.6.8) \begin{align} \label {eq:5.6.9} \underset{dz/dx}{\underbrace{\frac{d^2u}{dx^2}}} - \frac{1}{x} \underset{z}{\underbrace{\frac{du}{dx}}} = & xe^{-x} \nonumber \\ \frac{dz}{dx} - \frac{1}{x} z = & xe^{-x} \end{align}
    To solve (5.6.9) we will first solve the complementary equation \begin{align*} \frac{dz}{dx} - \frac{1}{x} z = & 0 \end{align*}
    Find the general solution of \( \frac{dz}{dx} - \frac{1}{x} z = 0 \) using separation of variables. Review Sections 2.1 and 2.1 for examples.
    Using separation of variables, we find the general solution \(z = cx\) and now we apply variation of parameters to solve (5.6.9)
    To implement variation of parameters we choose a particlar solution so letting \(c=1\) we select \(z_1 = x\) as the particlar solution of the complementary equation. Now we seek solutions of the nonhomogeneous equation (5.6.9) of the form \(z = vz_1 = vx\) where \(v\) is some function of \(x\) to be determined.
    Differentiate \(z = vx\) then substitute into (5.6.9) and simplify into an integrable differential equation
    After differentiating \(z = vx\) and substitute into (5.6.9) we obtain: \begin{align*} \frac{d}{dx}\Big( vx \Big) - \frac{1}{x} \Big( vx \Big) = & xe^{-x} \Longrightarrow \frac{dv}{dx} = e^{-x} \end{align*}
    Antdifferentiate \(e^{-x}\)
    So we can find \(v\) by finding the antiderivative: \begin{align*} v = c-e^{-x} \end{align*} Substituting back into \(z = vx\) we obtain the one-parameter family that is the solution of (5.6.9) \begin{align*} z = x\Big(c-e^{-x} \Big) \end{align*}
  4. We found the general solution \(z\) but now we recall the substitution we made in the beginning \(z = {du}/{dx}\) so we can substitute back: \begin{align*} \frac{du}{dx} = x\Big(c-e^{-x} \Big) \end{align*} And now we can find \(u\) again by finding the antiderivative.
    Antdifferentiate \( xe^{-x} \) using IBP
    Antdifferentiate \( cx \) using the power rule
    Finding the antiderivative and simplifying arbitrary constants we have \(u\) \begin{align*} u = c_1 + c_2x^2 + (x + 1)e^{-x} \end{align*}
    Finally substituting back to \( y = ue^{x}\) we have \begin{align*} y = \Big( c_1 + c_2x^2 + (x + 1)e^{-x} \Big) e^x \end{align*} Which we will distribute to simplify: \begin{align} \label {eq:5.6.10} y = x + 1 + (c_1 + c_2x^2)e^x \end{align} So for any arbitrary constants \(c_1\) and \(c_2\) (5.6.10) is a solution of (5.6.6)
    Verify (5.6.10) is a solution of (5.6.6)
  5. From (5.6.10) we have a general form of the solution \(y\) so we select constants to obtain a simple particular solution. Letting \(c_1=0\) and \(c_2=0\) we obtain a particular solution \[ y_{p_1} = x + 1 \]
    Verify \(y_{p_1} = x + 1 \) is a solution of (5.6.6)
    Letting \(c_1=1\) and \(c_2=0\) we obtain another particular solution \[ y_{p_2} = x + 1 + e^x \]
    Verify \( y_{p_2} = x + 1 + e^x \) is a solution of (5.6.6)
    Letting \(c_1=0\) and \(c_2=1\) we obtain another particular solution \[ y_{p_3} = x + 1 + x^2 e^x \]
    Verify \( y_{p_3} = x + 1 + x^2 e^x \) is a solution of (5.6.6)
    Letting \(c_1=1\) and \(c_2=1\) we obtain another particular solution \[ y_{p_4} = x + 1 + e^x + x^2 e^x \]
    Verify \( y_{p_4} = x + 1 + e^x + x^2 e^x \) is a solution of (5.6.6)
  6. We can use the principle of superposition to find a fundamental set of solutions to the complementary equation (5.6.7). Recall our nonhomogeneous equation (5.6.6) with forcing function \(f(x) = x^2\) \begin {equation*} x\frac{d^2y}{dx^2}-(2x+1) \frac{dy}{dx} +(x+1)y=\underset{f(x)}{\underbrace{x^2}} \end {equation*}
    We can always obtain zero by subtracting a forcing function from itself \[ f(x) - f(x) = x^2 - x^2 = 0 \] Therefore we can rewrite the right side of the complementary equation as this \begin {equation*} x\frac{d^2y}{dx^2}-(2x+1) \frac{dy}{dx} +(x+1)y=\underset{x^2 - x^2}{\underbrace{0}} \end {equation*} Thus by the principle of superposition subtracting any two solutions of (5.6.6) yields a solution of the complementary equation (5.6.7)



    For instance subtracting \(y_{p_1}\) from \(y_{p_2}\) yields the solution we were given to begin with: \[ y_{p_2} - y_{p_1} = (x + 1 + e^x) - (x+1) = e^x \]
    Verify \( e^x \) is a solution of (5.6.7)



    Subtracting any other two solutions works as well, so we just need to obtain two linearly independent solutions. Subtracting \(y_{p_1}\) from \(y_{p_3}\) works \[ y_{p_3} - y_{p_1} = (x + 1 + x^2e^x) - (x+1) = x^2 e^x \]
    Verify \( x^2 e^x \) is a solution of (5.6.7)
    Find the Wronskian of the two solutions \(W[e^x, x^2 e^x](x)\)
    Since the ratio of the solutions \(x^2 e^x / e^x = x^2\) is nonconstant, then they are linearly independent. Therefore from Theorem 5.1.6 we have found a fundamental set of solutions of (5.6.7) \[ \{ e^x, x^2 e^x\} \]
Consider the differential equation \begin {equation*} x^2\frac{d^2y}{dx^2}+x \frac{dy}{dx} -y =x^2+1 \end {equation*}
  1. Verify that \(y_1=x\) is a solution of the complementary equation
  2. Use reduction of order to simplify into a linear first order equation in \({du}/{dx}\)
  3. Convert the equation in \({du}/{dx}\) to a first order equation in \(z\) using the substitution \(z = {du}/{dx} \) then solve for \(z\)
  4. Substitute back to solve for \(u\) then substitute back again to solve for \(y\) in general
  5. Find 4 simple particular solutions of the nonhomogeneous equation
  6. Find a fundamental set of solutions of the complementary equation
  7. Solve the IVP \begin {equation} \label {eq:5.6.12} x^2\frac{d^2y}{dx^2}+x \frac{dy}{dx} -y=x^2+1 \quad y(1)=2 \quad \left. \frac{dy}{dx} \right| _{1}=-3 \end {equation}
Solution
  1. We wish to verify that \(y_1=x\) is a solution to the complementary equation: \begin {equation} \label {eq:5.6.11} x^2\frac{d^2y}{dx^2}+x \frac{dy}{dx} -y=0 \end {equation}
    Differentiate \(y_1=x\) twice
    Differentiating \(y_1=x\) we obtain \[ \frac{d y_1}{dx} = 1 \quad \text{and} \quad \frac{d^2 y_1}{dx^2} = 0 \]
    Substituting \(y_1=x\) and its derivatives in to our equation \begin{align*} x^2\frac{d^2y_1}{dx^2}+x \frac{dy_1}{dx} -y_1 \overset{?}{=} & 0 \\ x^2\cdot 0+x \cdot 1 - x \overset{?}{=} & 0 \\ x - x = & 0 \quad \checkmark \\ \end{align*} Therefore \(y_1=x\) is a solution to the complementary equation (5.6.12)



  2. To use reduction of order we will seek solutions of the form \(y = uy_1\) where \(y_1\) is a solution to the complementary equation. Since \(y_1=x\) is a solution to the complementary equation (5.6.12) we seek solutions of the form \[y = ux\]
    Differentiate \(y = ux\) twice
    Differentiating \(y=ux\) we obtain \[ \frac{dy}{dx} = x\frac{du}{dx} +u \quad \text{and} \quad \frac{d^2y}{dx^2}= x\frac{d^2u}{dx^2} + 2 \frac{du}{dx} \]
    Substitute \(y = ue^x\) and its derivatives in to \( x^2\frac{d^2y}{dx^2}+x \frac{dy}{dx} -y=x^2+1 \)
    Substituting \(y = ux\) and its derivatives in to our equation \begin{array}{rrrrrr} x^2 \displaystyle\frac{d^2y}{dx^2} &=& {\small x^3 } \displaystyle\frac{d^2u}{dx^2} &+& {\small 2x^2 } \, \displaystyle\frac{du}{dx} & & \\ x \displaystyle\frac{dy}{dx} &=& & & {\small x^2 } \displaystyle\frac{du}{dx} &+& ux \\ + \quad\quad\quad -y &=& & & & & -ux \\ \hline \underset{x^2+1}{\underbrace{{\small x^2 \displaystyle\frac{d^2y}{dx^2} + x \frac{dy}{dx} - y}}} &=& \Big({\small x^3}\Big) \displaystyle\frac{d^2u}{dx^2} &+& \underset{x^2}{\underbrace{ {\small \Big( 2x^2+x^2 \Big)} }} \, \displaystyle \frac{du}{dx} &+& \underset{0}{\underbrace{ {\small ux-ux } }} \\ \end{array}
    Thus we obtain the equation \begin{align*} x^3 \frac{d^2u}{dx^2} + 3x^2 \frac{du}{dx} = x^2 + 1 \end{align*}
    Rewrite \( x^3 \frac{d^2u}{dx^2} + 3x^2 \frac{du}{dx} = x^2 + 1 \) in standard linear form and identify \(p(x)\) and \(f(x)\)
    Divding by \(x^3\) to get it into standard linear form (2.1.1) \begin{align} \label {eq:5.6.13} \frac{d^2u}{dx^2} + \frac{3}{x} \frac{du}{dx} = \frac{x^2 + 1}{x^3} \end{align}
  3. Although (5.6.13) appears to be a second order equation, since the dependent varible \(u\) is not present in the equation, we can actually use the substitution \(z = {du}/{dx} \) to rewrite it more clearly as a first order equation with only the first derivative present.
    Let \(z = \displaystyle \frac{du}{dx}\) differentiate both sides \( \displaystyle \frac{dz}{dx} = \frac{d}{dx}\left(\frac{du}{dx}\right) = \frac{d^2u}{dx^2} \) and substitute into (5.6.13) \begin{align} \label {eq:5.6.14} \underset{dz/dx}{\underbrace{\frac{d^2u}{dx^2}}} + \frac{3}{x} \underset{z}{\underbrace{\frac{du}{dx}}} =& \frac{x^2 + 1}{x^3} \nonumber \\ \frac{dz}{dx} + \frac{3}{x} z = & \frac{x^2 + 1}{x^3} \end{align}
    To solve (5.6.14) we will first solve the complementary equation \begin{align*} \frac{dz}{dx} + \frac{3}{x} z = & 0 \end{align*}
    Find the general solution of \( \frac{dz}{dx} + \frac{3}{x} z = 0 \) using separation of variables. Review Sections 2.1 and 2.1 for examples.
    Using separation of variables, we find the general solution \(z = c/x^3\) and now we apply variation of parameters to solve (5.6.14)
    To implement variation of parameters we choose a particlar solution so letting \(c=1\) we select \(z_1 = 1/x^3\) as the particlar solution of the complementary equation. Now we seek solutions of the nonhomogeneous equation (5.6.14) of the form \(z = vz_1 = v/x^3\) where \(v\) is some function of \(x\) to be determined.
    Differentiate \(z = v/x^3\) then substitute into (5.6.14) and simplify into an integrable differential equation
    After differentiating \(z = v/x^3\) and substitute into (5.6.14) we obtain: \begin{align*} \frac{d}{dx}\Big( \frac{v}{x^3} \Big) + \frac{3}{x} \Big( \frac{v}{x^3} \Big) = & \frac{x^2+1}{x^3} \Longrightarrow \frac{dv}{dx} = x^2 + 1 \end{align*}
    Antdifferentiate \(x^2 + 1\)
    So we can find \(v\) by finding the antiderivative: \begin{align*} v = \frac{1}{3}x^3 + x + c \end{align*} Substituting back into \(z = v/x^3\) we obtain the one-parameter family that is the solution of \begin{align*} z = \frac{1}{3} + \frac{1}{x^2} + \frac{c}{x^3} \end{align*}
  4. We found the general solution \(z\) but now we recall the substitution we made in the beginning \(z = {du}/{dx}\) so we can substitute back: \begin{align*} \frac{du}{dx} = \frac{1}{3} + \frac{1}{x^2} + \frac{c}{x^3} \end{align*} And now we can find \(u\) again by finding the antiderivative.
    Antdifferentiate \(\frac{1}{3} + \frac{1}{x^2} + \frac{c}{x^3} \) using the power rule
    Finding the antiderivative and simplifying arbitrary constants we have \(u\) \begin{align*} u = \frac{1}{3}x - \frac{1}{x} + \frac{c_1}{x^2} + c_2 \end{align*}
    Finally substituting back to \( y = ux\) we have \begin{align} \label {eq:5.6.15} y = \frac{1}{3}x^2 - 1 + \frac{c_1}{x} + c_2x \end{align} So for any arbitrary constants \(c_1\) and \(c_2\) (5.6.15) is a solution of our original differential equation
    Verify (5.6.15) is a solution of \( x^2\frac{d^2y}{dx^2}+x \frac{dy}{dx} -y=x^2+1 \)
  5. From (5.6.15) we have a general form of the solution \(y\) so we select constants to obtain a simple particular solution. Letting \(c_1=0\) and \(c_2=0\) we obtain a particular solution \[ y_{p_1} = \frac{1}{3}x^2 - 1 \]
    Verify \(y_{p_1} = \frac{1}{3}x^2 - 1\) is a solution of \( x^2\frac{d^2y}{dx^2}+x \frac{dy}{dx} -y=x^2+1 \)
    Letting \(c_1=1\) and \(c_2=0\) we obtain another particular solution \[ y_{p_2} = \frac{1}{3}x^2 - 1 + \frac{1}{x} \]
    Verify \( y_{p_2} = \frac{1}{3}x^2 - 1 + \frac{1}{x} \) is a solution of\( x^2\frac{d^2y}{dx^2}+x \frac{dy}{dx} -y=x^2+1 \)
    Letting \(c_1=0\) and \(c_2=1\) we obtain another particular solution \[ y_{p_3} = \frac{1}{3}x^2 - 1 + x \]
    Verify \( y_{p_3} = \frac{1}{3}x^2 - 1 + x \) is a solution of \( x^2\frac{d^2y}{dx^2}+x \frac{dy}{dx} -y=x^2+1 \)
    Letting \(c_1=1\) and \(c_2=1\) we obtain another particular solution \[ y_{p_4} = \frac{1}{3}x^2 - 1 + \frac{1}{x} + x \]
    Verify \( y_{p_4} = \frac{1}{3}x^2 - 1 + \frac{1}{x} + x \) is a solution of \( x^2\frac{d^2y}{dx^2}+x \frac{dy}{dx} -y=x^2+1 \)
  6. We can use the principle of superposition to find a fundamental set of solutions to the complementary equation (5.6.12). Recall our nonhomogeneous equation with forcing function \(f(x) = x^2 + 1\) \begin {equation*} x^2\frac{d^2y}{dx^2}+x \frac{dy}{dx} -y =\underset{f(x)}{\underbrace{x^2+1}} \end {equation*}
    We can always obtain zero by subtracting a forcing function from itself \[ f(x) - f(x) = (x^2+1) - (x^2+1) = 0 \] Therefore we can rewrite the right side of the complementary equation as this \begin {equation*} x\frac{d^2y}{dx^2}-(2x+1) \frac{dy}{dx} +(x+1)y=\underset{x^2 - x^2}{\underbrace{0}} \end {equation*} Thus by the principle of superposition subtracting any two particular solutions of our differential equation yields a solution of the complementary equation (5.6.12)



    For instance subtracting \(y_{p_1}\) from \(y_{p_2}\) yields the solution we were given to begin with: \[ y_{p_2} - y_{p_1} = \left(\frac{1}{3}x^2 - 1 + \frac{1}{x}\right) - \left(\frac{1}{3}x^2 - 1\right) = \frac{1}{x} \]
    Verify \( 1/x \) is a solution of (5.6.12)



    Subtracting any other two solutions works as well, so we just need to obtain two linearly independent solutions. Subtracting \(y_{p_1}\) from \(y_{p_3}\) works \[ y_{p_3} - y_{p_1} = \left(\frac{1}{3}x^2 - 1 + x\right) - \left(\frac{1}{3}x^2 - 1\right) = x \]
    Verify \( x \) is a solution of (5.6.12)
    Find the Wronskian of the two solutions \(W[x, 1/x ](x)\)
    Since the ratio of the solutions \(x / (1/x) = x^2\) is nonconstant, then they are linearly independent. Therefore from Theorem 5.1.6 we have found a fundamental set of solutions of (5.6.12) \[ \left\{ x, \frac{1}{x}\right\} \]
  7. We will not solve the IVP (5.6.11) \begin {equation*} x^2\frac{d^2y}{dx^2}+x \frac{dy}{dx} -y=x^2+1 \quad y(1)=2 \quad \left. \frac{dy}{dx} \right| _{1}=-3 \end {equation*} Since we already found the solution of the differential equation, we just need to apply the initial conditions so we start by differentiating (5.6.15)
    Differentiate \(y = \frac{1}{3}x^2 - 1 + \frac{c_1}{x} + c_2x\)
    Differentiating (5.6.15) we obtain \begin{align} y = \frac{1}{3}x^2 - 1 + \frac{c_1}{x} + c_2x \Longrightarrow \frac{dy}{dx} = \frac{2}{3}x + \frac{c_1}{x^2} + c_2 \end{align}
    Evaluate \(y = \frac{1}{3}x^2 - 1 + \frac{c_1}{x} + c_2x\) at the initial condition \(y(1)=2\)
    Evaluate \(\frac{dy}{dx} = \frac{2}{3}x + \frac{c_1}{x^2} + c_2\) at the initial condition \(\left. \frac{dy}{dx} \right| _{1}=-3\)
    Evaluating \(y\) and its derivative at the initial conditions, we obtain the system of equations \begin{align} \left\{ \begin{array}{rrr} -c_1 + c_2 &=& \displaystyle\frac{8}{3} \\ c_1 + c_2 &=& -\displaystyle\frac{11}{3} \\ \end{array} \right. \end{align}
    Solve for \(c_1\) and \(c_2\) \( \left\{ \begin{array}{rrr} -c_1 + c_2 &=& \displaystyle\frac{8}{3} \\ c_1 + c_2 &=& -\displaystyle\frac{11}{3} \\ \end{array} \right. \)
    Adding the equations together, we find \(c_2 = -1/2\) and substituting back \(c_1 = -19/6\) so we have the particular solution to the IVP (5.6.11) \[ y = \frac{1}{3}x^2 - 1 -\frac{19}{6x} -\frac{1}{2}x \]
    Verify \(y = \frac{1}{3}x^2 - 1 -\frac{19}{6x} -\frac{1}{2}x\) is a solution of \( x^2\frac{d^2y}{dx^2}+x \frac{dy}{dx} -y=x^2+1 \)
Consider the differential equation \begin {equation} \label {eq:5.6.18} x^2\frac{d^2y}{dx^2}-3x \frac{dy}{dx} +3y=0 \end {equation}
  1. Verify that \(y_1=x\) is a solution of (5.6.18)
  2. Use reduction of order to simplify (5.6.18) into a linear first order equation in \({du}/{dx}\)
  3. Convert the equation in \({du}/{dx}\) to a first order equation in \(z\) using the substitution \(z = {du}/{dx} \) then solve for \(z\)
  4. Substitute back to solve for \(u\) then substitute back again to solve for \(y\) in general
  5. Find a fundamental set of solutions
Solution
  1. We wish to verify that \(y_1=x\) is a solution to (5.6.18)
    Differentiate \(y_1=x\) twice
    Differentiating \(y_1=x\) we obtain \[ \frac{d y_1}{dx} = 1 \quad \text{and} \quad \frac{d^2 y_1}{dx^2} = 0 \]
    Substituting \(y_1=x\) and its derivatives in to our equation \begin{align*} x^2\frac{d^2y_1}{dx^2} -3x \frac{dy_1}{dx} + 3y_1 \overset{?}{=} & 0 \\ x^2\cdot 0 - 3x \cdot 1 +3 x \overset{?}{=} & 0 \\ -3x + 3x = & 0 \quad \checkmark \\ \end{align*} Therefore \(y_1=x\) is a solution to (5.6.18)



  2. To use reduction of order we will seek solutions of (5.6.18) of the form \(y = uy_1\) where \(y_1\) is a solution to the complementary equation, which is the same as (5.6.18) in this case so we seek solutions of the form \[y = ux\]
    Differentiating \(y=ux\) we obtain \[ \frac{dy}{dx} = x\frac{du}{dx} +u \quad \text{and} \quad \frac{d^2y}{dx^2}= x\frac{d^2u}{dx^2} + 2 \frac{du}{dx} \]
    Substitute \(y = ux\) and its derivatives in to (5.6.18)
    Substituting \(y = ux\) and its derivatives in to our equation \begin{array}{rrrrrr} x^2 \displaystyle\frac{d^2y}{dx^2} &=& {\small x^3 } \displaystyle\frac{d^2u}{dx^2} &+& {\small 2x^2 } \, \displaystyle\frac{du}{dx} & & \\ -3x \displaystyle\frac{dy}{dx} &=& & & {\small -3x^2 } \displaystyle\frac{du}{dx} &+& -3ux \\ + \quad\quad\quad 3y &=& & & & & 3ux \\ \hline \underset{0}{\underbrace{{\small x^2 \displaystyle\frac{d^2y}{dx^2} - 3x \frac{dy}{dx} + 3y}}} &=& \Big({\small x^3}\Big) \displaystyle\frac{d^2u}{dx^2} &+& \underset{-x^2}{\underbrace{ {\small \Big( 2x^2-3x^2 \Big)} }} \, \displaystyle \frac{du}{dx} &+& \underset{0}{\underbrace{ {\small 3ux-3ux } }} \\ \end{array}
    Thus we obtain the equation \begin{align*} x^3 \frac{d^2u}{dx^2} - x^2 \frac{du}{dx} = 0 \end{align*}
    Rewrite \( x^3 \frac{d^2u}{dx^2} - x^2 \frac{du}{dx} = 0 \) in standard linear form and identify \(p(x)\) and \(f(x)\)
    Divding by \(x^3\) to get it into standard linear form (2.1.1) \begin{align*} \frac{d^2u}{dx^2} - \frac{1}{x} \frac{du}{dx} = 0 \end{align*}
  3. Although the equation appears to be a second order equation, since the dependent varible \(u\) is not present in the equation, we can actually use the substitution \(z = {du}/{dx} \) to rewrite it more clearly as a first order equation with only the first derivative present.
    Leting \(z = \displaystyle \frac{du}{dx}\) and differentiating both sides \begin{align*} \frac{dz}{dx} = \frac{d}{dx}\left(\frac{du}{dx}\right) = \frac{d^2u}{dx^2} \end{align*} Now substituting into our equation \begin{align*} \underset{dz/dx}{\underbrace{\frac{d^2u}{dx^2}}} - \frac{1}{x} \underset{z}{\underbrace{\frac{du}{dx}}} = & 0 \nonumber \\ \frac{dz}{dx} - \frac{1}{x} z = & 0 \end{align*}
    Find the general solution of \( \frac{dz}{dx} - \frac{1}{x} z = 0 \) using separation of variables. Review Sections 2.1 and 2.1 for examples.
    Using separation of variables, we find the general solution \(z = cx\)
  4. We found the general solution \(z\) but now we recall the substitution we made in the beginning \(z = {du}/{dx}\) so we can sbstitute back: \begin{align*} \frac{du}{dx} = cx \end{align*} And now we can find \(u\) again by finding the antiderivative.
    Antdifferentiate \( cx \) using the power rule
    Finding the antiderivative we have \(u\) \begin{align*} u = c_1 + c_2x^2 \end{align*}
    Finally substituting back to \( y = ux\) we have \begin{align*} y = c_1x + c_2x^3 \end{align*} So for any arbitrary constants \(c_1\) and \(c_2\) then \( y = c_1x + c_2x^3 \) is a solution of (5.6.18)
    Verify \( y = c_1x + c_2x^3 \) is a solution of (5.6.18)
  5. We have a general form of the solution \(y\) so we select constants to obtain a simple particular solution. Letting \(c_1=1\) and \(c_2=0\) we obtain a particular solution \[ y_{p_1} = x \]
    Verify \(y_{p_1} = x \) is a solution of (5.6.18)
    Letting \(c_1=0\) and \(c_2=1\) we obtain another particular solution \[ y_{p_2} = x^3 \]
    Verify \( y_{p_2} = x^3 \) is a solution of (5.6.18)
    Find the Wronskian of the two solutions \(W[x, x^3](x)\)
    Since the ratio of the solutions \(x^3 / x = x^2\) is nonconstant, then they are linearly independent. Therefore from Theorem 5.1.6 we have found a fundamental set of solutions of (5.6.18) \[ \{ x, x^3 \} \]
