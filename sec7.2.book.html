7.2 Series Solutions Near an Ordinary Point
Ordinary Points
Many physical applications give rise to second order homogeneous linear differential equations of the form \begin {equation} \label {eq:7.2.1} P_0(x)\dfrac{d^2y}{dx^2}+P_1(x)\dfrac{dy}{dx}+P_2(x)y=0 \end {equation} where \(P_0(x)\), \(P_1(x)\), and \(P_2(x)\) are polynomials. Usually the solutions of these equations can’t be expressed in terms of familiar elementary functions. Therefore we’ll consider the problem of representing solutions of (7.2.1) with series. We assume throughout that \(P_0(x)\), \(P_1(x)\) and \(P_2(x)\) have no common factors. Then we say that \(x_0\) is an ordinary point of (7.2.1) if \(P_0(x_0)\ne 0\), or a singular point if \(P_0(x_0)=0\).




We'll consider Legendre’s equation \begin {equation} \label {eq:7.2.2} \overset{P_0(x)}{\overbrace{ (1-x^2) }} \dfrac{d^2y}{dx^2} - \overset{P_1(x)}{\overbrace{ 2x }} \dfrac{dy}{dx} + \overset{P_2(x)}{\overbrace{ \ell (\ell +1) }} y = 0 \end {equation} We have \(P_0(x) = 1-x^2 \) and we will find singular points \begin {align*} 1-x^2 = & 0 \\ (1-x)(1+x) = & 0 \\ x = 1 \quad x = -1 & \end {align*} So we have \(x_0=1\) and \(x_0=-1\) singular points and all other points are ordinary points.
Now let's consider Bessel’s equation \[ \overset{P_0(x)}{\overbrace{ x^2 }} \dfrac{d^2y}{dx^2} + \overset{P_1(x)}{\overbrace{ x }} \dfrac{dy}{dx} + \overset{P_2(x)}{\overbrace{ (x^2 - v ^2) }} y = 0 \] With \(P_0(x) = x^2 \) we have \(x_0=0\) as a singular points and all other points are ordinary points.
Finally consider Airy’s equation, \begin {equation} \label {eq:7.2.3} \dfrac{d^2y}{dx^2}-xy=0 \end {equation} Where we have \(P_0(x) = 1 \) a constant function. In this case since \(P_0(x) \) is never zero, then every point is an ordinary point.




Since polynomials are continuous everywhere, \(P_1(x)/P_0(x)\) and \(P_2(x)/P_0(x)\) are continuous at any point \(x_0\) that isn’t a zero of \(P_0(x)\). Therefore, if \(x_0\) is an ordinary points of (7.2.1) and \(c_0\) and \(c_1\) are arbitrary real numbers, then the IVP (7.2.4) has a unique solution on the largest open interval that contains \(x_0\) and does not contain any zeros of \(P_0(x)\). \begin {equation} \label {eq:7.2.4} P_0(x)\dfrac{d^2y}{dx^2}+P_1(x)\dfrac{dy}{dx}+P_2(x)y=0 \quad y(x_0)=c_0 \quad \left. \dfrac{dy}{dx} \right| _{x_0}=c_1 \end {equation} To observe this, we rewrite the differential equation as \[ \dfrac{d^2y}{dx^2}+{P_1(x)\over P_0(x)}\dfrac{dy}{dx}+{P_2(x)\over P_0(x)}y=0 \] and apply Theorem 5.1.1 with \(p(x)=P_1(x)/P_0(x)\) and \(q(x)=P_2(x)/P_0(x)\). We now consider the problem of representing solutions of (7.2.1) by power series that converge for values of \(x\) near an ordinary point.
Series Solutions
Theorem 7.2.1 Suppose \(P_0(x)\), \(P_1(x)\), and \(P_2(x)\) are polynomials with no common factor and \(P_0(x)\) isn’t identically zero. Let \(x_0\) be a point such that \(P_0(x_0)\ne 0,\) and let \(\rho \) be the distance from \(x_0\) to the nearest zero of \(P_0(x)\) in the complex plane. \((\)If \(P_0(x)\) is constant, then \(\rho =\infty \).\()\) Then every solution of (7.2.5) can be represented by a power series (7.2.6) \begin {equation} \label {eq:7.2.5} P_0(x)\dfrac{d^2y}{dx^2}+P_1(x)\dfrac{dy}{dx}+P_2(x)y=0 \end {equation} \begin {equation} \label {eq:7.2.6} y=\sum _{n=0}^\infty c_n(x-x_0)^n \end {equation} That converges at least on the open interval \((x_0-\rho ,x_0+\rho )\). If \(P_0(x)\) is nonconstant, so that \(\rho \) is necessarily finite, then the open interval of convergence of (7.2.6) may be larger than \((x_0-\rho ,x_0+\rho )\). If \(P_0(x)\) is constant then \(\rho =\infty \) and the open interval of convergence is \((x_0-\rho ,x_0+\rho )=(-\infty ,\infty )\)
We call (7.2.6) a power series solution in \(x-x_0\) of (7.2.5). We’ll now develop a method for finding power series solutions of (7.2.5). Theorem 7.2.1 implies that every solution of (7.2.5) on \((x_0-\rho ,x_0+\rho )\) can be written as \begin {equation} \label {eq:7.2.7} y=\sum _{n=0}^\infty c_n(x-x_0)^n = c_0 + c_1(x-x_0) + c_2(x-x_0)^2 + \cdots \end {equation} And we can find the derivative of (7.2.7) using Theorem 7.1.4. \begin {equation} \label {eq:7.2.8} \frac{dy}{dx} = \sum _{n=1}^\infty n \, c_n \, (x-x_0)^{n-1} = c_1 + 2c_2(x-x_0) + 3c_3(x-x_0)^2 + \cdots \end {equation} Evaluating (7.2.7) and its derivative at the initial point \(x_0\) yields the initial conditions \begin {align*} y(x_0) = & \, c_0 + c_1(x_0-x_0) + c_2(x_0-x_0)^2 + \cdots = \, c_0 \\ \\ \left. \frac{dy}{dx}\right|_{x_0} = & \, c_1 + 2c_2(x_0-x_0) + 3c_3(x_0-x_0)^2 + \cdots = \, c_1 \end {align*}
Since every IVP (7.2.4) has a unique solution, this means that \(c_0\) and \(c_1\) can be chosen arbitrarily, and that \(c_2, c_3, \dots \, ,\) are uniquely determined by them.



To find \(c_2, c_3, \dots \, ,\) we represent \(y\) as a power series then substitute (7.2.7) and its derivatives into (7.2.5) then simplify the series so that \(P_0(x)\), \(P_1(x)\), and \(P_2(x)\) are all in terms of powers of \((x-x_0)^n\) then collect coefficients and equate them to zero.
For simplicity, we call the power series obtained this way the power series in \(x-x_0\) for the general solution of \(Ly=0\), without explicitly identifying the open interval of convergence of the series.



Let \(x_0\) be an arbitrary real number. Find the power series in \(x-x_0\) for the general solution of \begin {equation} \label {eq:7.2.9} \dfrac{d^2y}{dx^2}+ y=0 \end {equation}
Solution
  1. First we represent \(y\) as a power series as in \((x-x_0)\) and differentiate it twice
    Differentiate \(\displaystyle y = \sum _{n=0}^\infty c_n(x-x_0)^n\) twice
    \begin {align*} y = & \sum _{n=0}^\infty c_n(x-x_0)^n \\ \\ \frac{dy}{dx} = & \sum _{n=1}^\infty n \, c_n \, (x-x_0)^{n-1} \\ \\ \frac{d^2y}{dx^2} = & \sum _{n=2}^\infty n(n-1) \, c_n \, (x-x_0)^{n-2} \\ \\ \end {align*}
    Next we rewrite the second derivative in terms of powers of \((x-x_0)^n\) by shifting the index \begin {align*} \frac{d^2y}{dx^2} = & \sum _{n+2=2}^\infty (n+2)(n+2-1) \, c_{n+2} \, (x-x_0)^{n+2-2} \\ = & \sum _{n=0}^\infty (n+2)(n+1) \, c_{n+2} \, (x-x_0)^{n} \\ \end {align*} Now we substitute the power series expressions for \(y\) and \(d^2 y/dx^2 \) into (7.2.9) \begin{align*} \begin{array} {rrrrr} \dfrac{d^2y}{dx^2} &=& &\displaystyle\sum _{n=0}^\infty& (n+2)(n+1) \, c_{n+2} \, &(x-x_0)^{n}& \\ + \quad\quad\quad y &=& &\displaystyle\sum _{n=0}^\infty& c_n &(x-x_0)^n& \\ \hline \underset{0}{ \underbrace{\dfrac{d^2y}{dx^2} + y}} &=& &\displaystyle\sum _{n=0}^\infty& \Big\{ \quad (n+2)(n+1) \, c_{n+2} + c_{n} \, & \Big \} \, (x-x_0)^{n}& \\ \end{array} \end{align*} Now equating the coefficience on both sides yields the following recurrence relation, where we define the coefficients in the sequence \(\{c_n\}\) in terms of one or more other coefficients with lesser indices \begin {align*} (n+2)(n+1) \, c_{n+2} + c_{n} = 0 \end {align*} Then solving for \( c_{n+2} \) we obtain \begin {equation} \label {eq:7.2.10} c_{n+2}={-c_n\over (n+2)(n+1)} \end {equation} Where \(c_0\) and \(c_1\) are arbitrary and then we will use (7.2.10) to calculate the coefficients
    \begin{align*} c_2 & = c_{0+2} = {-c_0\over (0+2)(0+1)} = -{c_0\over (2)(1)} = -{c_0\over 2!} \\ c_3 & = c_{1+2} = {-c_1\over (1+2)(1+1)} = -{c_1\over (3)(2)} = -{c_1\over 3!} \\ c_4 & = c_{2+2} = {-c_2\over (2+2)(2+1)} = -{c_2\over (4)(3)} = -{1\over 4\cdot3}\cdot -{c_0\over 2} = {c_0\over 4!} \\ c_5 & = c_{3+2} = {-c_3\over (3+2)(3+1)} = -{c_3\over (5)(4)} = -{1\over 5\cdot4}\cdot -{c_1\over 3!} = {c_1\over 5!} \\ c_6 & = c_{4+2} = {-c_4\over (4+2)(4+1)} = -{c_4\over (6)(5)} = -{1\over 6\cdot5}\cdot {c_0\over 4!} = -{c_0\over 6!} \\ c_7 & = c_{5+2} = {-c_5\over (5+2)(5+1)} = -{c_5\over (7)(6)} = -{1\over 7\cdot6}\cdot {c_1\over 5!} = -{c_1\over 7!} \\ \end{align*} We notice that the even indexed constants all depend on \(c_0\) and the odd indexed constants depend on \(c_1\) so we group them accordingly \begin{array}{rrrrr} c_0 & & &\quad& c_1 & & \\ c_2 &=& -\displaystyle{c_0\over 2!} &\quad& c_3 &=& -\displaystyle{c_1\over 3!} \\ c_4 &=& \displaystyle{c_0\over 4!} &\quad& c_5 &=& \displaystyle{c_1\over 5!} \\ c_6 &=& -\displaystyle{c_0\over 6!} &\quad& c_7 &=& -\displaystyle{c_1\over 7!} \\ \end{array} In fact we could have figured out that this would be the case to begin with based on the recurrence formula for the constants (7.2.10) since indices on the left and right sides differ by two so we will calculate the even and odd cases separately next time to help simplify the calculations.
    Substituting the coefficients back into our power series representation of \(y\) \begin{align} y = & \sum _{n=0}^\infty c_n(x-x_0)^n \\ \nonumber \\ = & c_0 + c_1(x-x_0) + c_2(x-x_0)^2 + c_3(x-x_0)^3 + c_4(x-x_0)^4 + \cdots \nonumber \\ \nonumber \\ = & \Big( c_0 + c_2(x-x_0)^2 + c_4(x-x_0)^4 + c_6(x-x_0)^6 + \cdots \Big) \\ & \quad + \Big( c_1(x-x_0) + c_3(x-x_0)^3 + c_5(x-x_0)^5 + c_7(x-x_0)^7 + \cdots \Big) \nonumber \\ \nonumber \\ = & \Bigg( c_0 - \frac{c_0}{2!}(x-x_0)^2 + \frac{c_0}{4!}(x-x_0)^4 - \frac{c_0}{6!}(x-x_0)^6 + \cdots \Bigg) \label {eq:7.2.13} \\ & \quad + \Bigg( c_1(x-x_0) - \frac{c_1}{3!}(x-x_0)^3 + \frac{c_1}{5!}(x-x_0)^5 - \frac{c_1}{7!}(x-x_0)^7 + \cdots \Bigg) \nonumber \\ \nonumber \\ = & c_0 \, \Bigg( 1 - \frac{1}{2!}(x-x_0)^2 + \frac{1}{4!}(x-x_0)^4 - \frac{1}{6!}(x-x_0)^6 + \cdots \Bigg) \label {eq:7.2.14} \\ & \quad + c_1 \, \Bigg( \, (x-x_0) - \frac{1}{3!}(x-x_0)^3 + \frac{1}{5!}(x-x_0)^5 - \frac{1}{7!}(x-x_0)^7 + \cdots \Bigg) \nonumber \\ \nonumber \\ = & c_0 \, \sum_{m=0}^{\infty} \frac{(-1)^m}{(2m)!}(x-x_0)^{2m} + c_1 \, \sum_{m=0}^{\infty} \frac{(-1)^m}{(2m+1)!}(x-x_0)^{2m+1} \label {eq:7.2.15} \\ \nonumber \\ = & c_0 \, \cos(x-x_0) + c_1 \, \sin (x-x_0) \nonumber \end{align}
    Although in this case we were able to write the answer in terms of familiar elementary functions, this is a special case. Most solutions cannot be expressed in terms of elementary functions and once we find the recurrence relation (7.2.10) for the coefficients we are able to calculate as many as we would like to get our solution as accurate as we need. This may sound daunting to do by hand, and practically, this would be programed into a computer. By hand we will only expand these series out to the first five to ten terms.



Legendre Airy Chebyshev Hermite
In the remainder of this section we consider the problem of finding power series solutions in \((x-x_0)\) for equations of the form \begin {equation} \label {eq:7.2.16} \overset{P_0(x)}{\overbrace{\left (1+\alpha (x-x_0)^2\right )}} \dfrac{d^2y}{dx^2} + \overset{P_1(x)}{\overbrace{\beta (x-x_0)}} \dfrac{dy}{dx} + \overset{P_2(x)}{\overbrace{\gamma}} y = 0 \end {equation} Many important equations that arise in applications are of this form with \(x_0=0\), including Legendre’s equation (7.2.2), Airy’s equation (7.2.3), Chebyshev’s equation \[ (1-x^2)\dfrac{d^2y}{dx^2}-x\dfrac{dy}{dx}+\alpha ^2 y=0 \] and Hermite’s equation \[ \dfrac{d^2y}{dx^2}-2x\dfrac{dy}{dx}+2\alpha y=0 \] Note that the point \(x_0\) is an ordinary point of (7.2.16) since \(P_0(x_0) = 1+\alpha (x_0-x_0)^2 = 1 \ne 0 \) and Theorem 7.2.1 indicates that the solutions of (7.2.16) can be written as power series in \(x-x_0\) that converge on the interval \[ \left( x_0 - \frac{1}{\sqrt{|\alpha|}} , x_0 + \frac{1}{\sqrt{|\alpha|}} \right) \] if \(\alpha \ne 0\), or on \((-\infty ,\infty )\) if \(\alpha =0\). We’ll find that the coefficients in these power series can be obtained by methods similar to the one used in Example 7.2.1



To simplify finding the coefficients, we introduce some notation for products: \[ \prod ^s_{j=r}b_j=b_rb_{r+1}\cdots b_s\quad \mbox {if} \quad s\ge r\] Thus, \[ \prod ^7_{j=2}b_j=b_2b_3b_4b_5b_6b_7\] \[ \prod ^4_{j=0}(2j+1)=(1)(3)(5)(7)(9)=945\] and \[ \prod ^2_{j=2}j^2=2^2=4\] We define \[ \prod ^s_{j=r}b_j=1\quad \mbox {if}\quad s < r\] no matter what the form of \(b_j\).



Find the power series in \(x\) for the general solution of \begin {equation} \label {eq:7.2.17} (1+2x^2)\dfrac{d^2y}{dx^2}+6x\dfrac{dy}{dx}+2y=0 \end {equation}
Solution
  1. Here \[ Ly=(1+2x^2)\dfrac{d^2y}{dx^2}+6x\dfrac{dy}{dx}+2y\] If \[ y=\sum _{n=0}^\infty c_nx^n \] then \[ \dfrac{dy}{dx}=\sum _{n=1}^\infty na_nx^{n-1}\quad \mbox { and }\quad \dfrac{d^2y}{dx^2}=\sum _{n=2}^\infty n(n-1)a_nx^{n-2}\] so
    \begin {eqnarray*} Ly&=&(1+2x^2) \sum ^\infty _{n=2}n(n-1)a_nx^{n-2}+ 6x \sum ^\infty _{n=1}na_nx^{n-1} +2 \sum ^\infty _{n=0}a_nx^n\\ &=&\sum _{n=2}^\infty n(n-1)a_nx^{n-2}+\sum _{n=0}^\infty \left [2n(n-1)+6n+2\right ]a_nx^n\\ &=&\sum _{n=2}^\infty n(n-1)a_nx^{n-2}+2\sum _{n=0}^\infty (n+1)^2a_nx^n \end {eqnarray*}
    To collect coefficients of \(x^n\), we shift the summation index in the first sum. This yields \[ Ly=\sum _{n=0}^\infty (n+2)(n+1)a_{n+2}x^n+2\sum _{n=0}^\infty (n+1)^2a_nx^n =\sum _{n=0}^\infty b_nx^n\] with \[ b_n=(n+2)(n+1)a_{n+2}+2(n+1)^2a_n,\quad n\ge 0\]
  2. To obtain solutions of (7.2.17), we set \(b_n=0\) for \(n\ge 0\). This is equivalent to the recurrence relation \begin {equation} \label {eq:7.2.18} a_{n+2}=-2{n+1\over n+2}a_n,\quad n\ge 0 \end {equation} Since the indices on the left and right differ by two, we write (7.2.18) separately for \(n=2m\) and \(n=2m+1\), as in Example 7.2.1. This yields
    \begin {eqnarray} a_{2m+2}&=&-2 {2m+1\over 2m+2}a_{2m}=-{2m+1\over m+1}a_{2m},\quad m \ge 0,\label {eq:7.2.19}\\ {\mbox {and}}\nonumber \\ a_{2m+3}&=&-2{2m+2\over 2m+3}a_{2m+1}=-4{m+1\over 2m+3}a_{2m+1},\quad m\ge 0. \label {eq:7.2.20} \end {eqnarray}
    Computing the coefficients of even powers of \(x\) from (7.2.19) yields
    \begin {eqnarray*} c_2&=&-{1\over 1}c_0,\\[5pt] c_4&=&-{3\over 2}c_2=\left (-{3\over 2}\right )\left (-{1\over 1}\right )c_0 ={1\cdot 3\over 1\cdot 2}c_0, \\[5pt] c_6&=&-{5\over 3}c_4= -{5\over 3}\left (1\cdot 3\over 1\cdot 2\right )c_0 =-{1\cdot 3\cdot 5\over 1\cdot 2\cdot 3}c_0, \\[5pt] c_8&=&-{7\over 4}c_6=-{7\over 4} \left (-{1\cdot 3\cdot 5\over 1\cdot 2\cdot 3}\right )c_0= {1\cdot 3\cdot 5\cdot 7\over 1\cdot 2\cdot 3\cdot 4}c_0.\\ \end {eqnarray*}
    In general, \begin {equation} \label {eq:7.2.21} a_{2m}=(-1)^m{\prod _{j=1}^m(2j-1)\over m!}c_0,\quad m\ge 0 \end {equation} (Note that (7.2.21) is correct for \(m=0\) because we defined \(\prod _{j=1}^0b_j=1\) for any \(b_j\).)
  3. Computing the coefficients of odd powers of \(x\) from (7.2.20) yields
    \begin {eqnarray*} c_3&=&-4\,{1\over 3}c_1, \\[5pt] c_5&=&-4\,{2\over 5}c_3=-4\,{2\over 5}\left (-4{1\over 3}\right )c_1 =4^2{1\cdot 2\over 3\cdot 5}c_1, \\[5pt] c_7&=&-4\,{3\over 7}c_5=-4\,{3\over 7}\left ( 4^2{1\cdot 2\over 3\cdot 5}\right )c_1= -4^3{1\cdot 2\cdot 3\over 3\cdot 5\cdot 7}c_1,\\[5pt] c_9&=&-4\, {4\over 9}c_7=-4\, {4\over 9}\left ( 4^3{1\cdot 2\cdot 3\over 3\cdot 5\cdot 7}\right )c_1= 4^4{1\cdot 2\cdot 3\cdot 4\over 3\cdot 5\cdot 7\cdot 9}c_1 \end {eqnarray*}
    In general, \begin {equation} \label {eq:7.2.22} a_{2m+1}={(-1)^m4^m m!\over \prod _{j=1}^m(2j+1)}c_1,\quad m\ge 0 \end {equation} From (7.2.21) and (7.2.22), \[ y=c_0 \sum ^\infty _{m=0}(-1)^m {\prod _{j=1}^m(2j-1)\over m!}x^{2m} +c_1 \sum ^\infty _{m=0}(-1)^m {4^mm!\over \prod _{j=1}^m(2j+1)} x^{2m+1}\] is the power series in \(x\) for the general solution of (7.2.17). Since \(P_0(x)=1+2x^2\) has no real zeros, Theorem 5.1.1 implies that every solution of (7.2.17) is defined on \((-\infty ,\infty )\). However, since \(P_0(\pm i/\sqrt 2)=0\), Theorem 7.2.1 implies only that the power series converges in \((-1/\sqrt 2,1/\sqrt 2)\) for any choice of \(c_0\) and \(c_1\).
    The results in Examples 7.2.1 and 7.2.2 are consequences of the following general theorem.
Theorem 7.2.2 The coefficients \(\{a_n\}\) in any solution \(y=\sum _{n=0}^\infty c_n(x-x_0)^n\) of \begin {equation} \label {eq:7.2.23} \left (1+\alpha (x-x_0)^2\right )\dfrac{d^2y}{dx^2}+\beta (x-x_0) \dfrac{dy}{dx}+\gamma y=0 \end {equation} satisfy the recurrence relation \begin {equation} \label {eq:7.2.24} a_{n+2}=-{p(n) \over (n+2)(n+1)}a_n,\quad n\ge 0 \end {equation} where \begin {equation} \label {eq:7.2.25} p(n)=\alpha n(n-1) +\beta n+\gamma \end {equation} Moreover, the coefficients of the even and odd powers of \(x-x_0\) can be computed separately as
\begin {eqnarray} a_{2m+2}&=&-{p(2m)\over (2m+2)(2m+1)}a_{2m},\quad m\ge 0\label {eq:7.2.26}\\ {\mbox {and}}\nonumber \\ a_{2m+3}&=&-{p(2m+1)\over (2m+3)(2m+2)}a_{2m+1},\quad m\ge 0, \label {eq:7.2.27} \end {eqnarray}
where \(c_0\) and \(c_1\) are arbitrary.
Proof Here \[ Ly=\left (1+\alpha (x-x_0\right )^2)\dfrac{d^2y}{dx^2}+\beta (x-x_0) \dfrac{dy}{dx}+\gamma y\] If \[ y=\sum _{n=0}^\infty c_n(x-x_0)^n\] then \[ \dfrac{dy}{dx}=\sum _{n=1}^\infty na_n(x-x_0)^{n-1} \quad \mbox { and }\quad \dfrac{d^2y}{dx^2}=\sum _{n=2}^\infty n(n-1)a_n(x-x_0)^{n-2}\] Hence, \[ \begin {array}{ccl} Ly&=&\displaystyle {\sum _{n=2}^\infty n(n-1)a_n(x-x_0)^{n-2}+ \sum _{n=0}^\infty \left [\alpha n(n-1) +\beta n+\gamma \right ]a_n(x-x_0)^n}\\ &=&\displaystyle {\sum _{n=2}^\infty n(n-1)a_n(x-x_0)^{n-2}+\sum _{n=0}^\infty p(n)a_n(x-x_0)^n} \end {array} \] from (7.2.25). To collect coefficients of powers of \(x-x_0\), we shift the summation index in the first sum. This yields \[ Ly=\sum _{n=0}^\infty \left [(n+2)(n+1)a_{n+2}+p(n)a_n\right ](x-x_0)^n\] Thus, \(Ly=0\) if and only if \[ (n+2)(n+1)a_{n+2}+p(n)a_n=0,\quad n\ge 0\] which is equivalent to (7.2.24). Writing (7.2.24) separately for the cases where \(n=2m\) and \(n=2m+1\) yields (7.2.26) and (7.2.27).
Series Solutions
Find the power series in \(x-1\) for the general solution of \begin {equation} \label {eq:7.2.28} (2+4x-2x^2)\dfrac{d^2y}{dx^2}-12(x-1)\dfrac{dy}{dx}-12y=0 \end {equation}
Solution We must first write the coefficient \(P_0(x)=2+4x-x^2\) in powers of \(x-1\). To do this, we write \(x=(x-1)+1\) in \(P_0(x)\) and then expand the terms, collecting powers of \(x-1\); thus,
\begin {eqnarray*} 2+4x-2x^2&=&2+4[(x-1)+1]-2[(x-1)+1]^2\\ &=&4-2(x-1)^2 \end {eqnarray*}
Therefore we can rewrite (7.2.28) as \[ \left (4-2(x-1)^2\right )\dfrac{d^2y}{dx^2}-12(x-1)\dfrac{dy}{dx}-12y=0\] or, equivalently, \[ \left (1-{1\over 2}(x-1)^2\right )\dfrac{d^2y}{dx^2}-3(x-1)\dfrac{dy}{dx}-3y=0\] This is of the form (7.2.23) with \(\alpha =-1/2\), \(\beta =-3\), and \(\gamma =-3\). Therefore, from (7.2.25) \[ p(n)=-{n(n-1)\over 2}-3n-3=-{(n+2)(n+3)\over 2}\] Hence, Theorem 7.2.2 implies that
\begin {eqnarray*} a_{2m+2}&=&-{p(2m)\over (2m+2)(2m+1)}a_{2m}\\&=& {(2m+2)(2m+3)\over 2(2m+2)(2m+1)} a_{2m}={2m+3\over 2(2m+1)}a_{2m},\quad m\ge 0\\ {\mbox {and}}\\ a_{2m+3}&=&-{p(2m+1)\over (2m+3)(2m+2)}a_{2m+1}\\&=& {(2m+3)(2m+4)\over 2 (2m+3)(2m+2)}a_{2m+1}={m+2\over 2(m+1)}a_{2m+1},\quad m\ge 0 \end {eqnarray*}
We leave it to you to show that \[ a_{2m}={2m+1\over 2^m}c_0\quad \mbox { and }\quad a_{2m+1}={m+1\over 2^m}c_1,\quad m\ge 0\] which implies that the power series in \(x-1\) for the general solution of (7.2.28) is \[ y=c_0\sum _{m=0}^\infty {2m+1\over 2^m}(x-1)^{2m}+c_1\sum _{m=0}^\infty {m+1\over 2^m}(x-1)^{2m+1}\]
In the examples considered so far we were able to obtain closed formulas for coefficients in the power series solutions. In some cases this is impossible, and we must settle for computing a finite number of terms in the series. The next example illustrates this with an initial value problem.
Compute \(c_0\), \(c_1\), …, \(c_7\) in the series solution \(y=\sum _{n=0}^\infty c_nx^n\) of the initial value problem \begin {equation} \label {eq:7.2.29} (1+2x^2)\dfrac{d^2y}{dx^2}+10x\dfrac{dy}{dx}+8y=0,\quad y(0)=2,\quad \left. \dfrac{dy}{dx} \right| _{0}=-3 \end {equation}
Solution Since \(\alpha =2\), \(\beta =10\), and \(\gamma =8\) in (7.2.29), \[ p(n)=2n(n-1)+10n+8=2(n+2)^2\] Therefore \[ a_{n+2}=-2{(n+2)^2\over (n+2)(n+1)}a_n=-2{n+2\over n+1}a_n,\quad n\ge 0\] Writing this equation separately for \(n=2m\) and \(n=2m+1\) yields
\begin {eqnarray} a_{2m+2}&=&-2{(2m+2)\over 2m+1}a_{2m}=-4{m+1\over 2m+1}a_{2m},\quad m\ge 0\label {eq:7.2.30}\\ {\mbox {and}}\nonumber \\ a_{2m+3}&=&-2{2m+3\over 2m+2}a_{2m+1}=-{2m+3\over m+1}a_{2m+1},\quad m\ge 0. \label {eq:7.2.31} \end {eqnarray}
Starting with \(c_0=y(0)=2\), we compute \(c_2, c_4\), and \(c_6\) from (7.2.30):
\begin {eqnarray*} c_2&=&-4\,{1\over 1}2=-8,\\ c_4&=&-4\,{2\over 3}(-8)={64\over 3},\\ c_6&=&-4\,{3\over 5}\left (64\over 3\right )=-{256\over 5} \end {eqnarray*}
Starting with \(c_1=\left. \dfrac{dy}{dx} \right| _{0}=-3\), we compute \(c_3,c_5\) and \(c_7\) from (7.2.31):
\begin {eqnarray*} c_3&=&-{3\over 1}(-3)=9,\\ c_5&=&-{5\over 2}9=-{45\over 2},\\ c_7&=&-{7\over 3}\left (-{45\over 2}\right )={105\over 2} \end {eqnarray*}
Therefore the solution of (7.2.29) is \[ y=2-3x-8x^2+9x^3+{64 \over 3}x^4-{45 \over 2}x^5-{256 \over 5} x^6+{105 \over 2}x^7+\cdots \]




Technology and Computational Methods
Computing coefficients recursively as in the Examples can be tedious. In practicality, solving differential equations using this technique is done computationally using a computer. As our course is not a computational class, we will calculate by hand coefficients to five to ten terms of the series solution.
If you’re interested in actually using series to compute numerical approximations to solutions of a differential equation, then whether or not there’s a simple closed form for the coefficients is essentially irrelevant. For computational purposes it’s usually more efficient to start with the given coefficients \(c_0=y(x_0)\) and \(c_1= dy/dx \left. \right| _{x_0}\), compute \(c_2, c_3, \dots, c_N \) recursively, and then compute approximate values of the solution from the Taylor polynomial \[ T_N(x)=\sum _{n=0}^N c_n \, (x-x_0)^n \] The trick is to decide how to choose \(N\) so the approximation \(y\approx T_N(x)\) is sufficiently accurate on the subinterval of the interval of convergence that you’re interested in. In real life, you would choose one or the other of the two methods (numerical integration or series solution). If you choose the method of series solution, then a practical procedure for determining a suitable value of \(N\) is to continue increasing \(N\) until the maximum of \(|T_N-T_{N-1}|\) on the interval of interest is within the margin of error that you’re willing to accept.
In doing computational problems that call for numerical solution of differential equations you should choose the most accurate numerical integration procedure your software supports, and experiment with the step size until you’re confident that the numerical results are sufficiently accurate for the problem at hand.
