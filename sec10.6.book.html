10.6 Constant Coefficient Homogeneous Systems III
Complex Eigenvalues
We now consider the system \(\dfrac{d{\bf \vec{y}}}{dt}={\bf A}{\bf \vec{y}}\), where \({\bf A}\) has complex eigenvalues \({r}_1 =\alpha +i\beta \) and \({r}_2 =\alpha -i\beta \). We continue to assume that \({\bf A}\) has real entries, so the characteristic polynomial of \({\bf A}\) has real coefficients.
An eigenvector \({\bf \vec{u}}\) of \({\bf A}\) associated with \({r}_1 =\alpha +i\beta \) will have complex entries, so we’ll write the eigenvector in real and imaginary components as the following \[ {\bf \vec{u}}={\bf \vec{a}}+i{\bf \vec{b}} \] where \({\bf \vec{a}}\) and \({\bf \vec{b}}\) have real entries; that is, \({\bf \vec{a}}\) is the real part and \({\bf \vec{b}}\) is the imaginary part of \({\bf \vec{u}}\). Since \({\bf A}{\bf \vec{u}}={r} {\bf \vec{u}}\), \begin {equation} \label {eq:10.6.1} {\bf A}({\bf \vec{a}}+i{\bf \vec{b}})=(\alpha +i\beta )({\bf \vec{a}}+i{\bf \vec{b}}) \end {equation} Taking complex conjugates here and recalling that \(A\) has real entries yields \[ {\bf A}({\bf \vec{a}}-i{\bf \vec{b}})=(\alpha -i\beta )({\bf \vec{a}}-i{\bf \vec{b}})\] which shows that \({\bf \vec{u}}={\bf \vec{a}}-i{\bf \vec{b}}\) is an eigenvector associated with \({r}_2 =\alpha -i\beta \). The complex conjugate eigenvalues \({r}_1\) and \({r}_2\) can be separately associated with linearly independent solutions of \(\dfrac{d{\bf \vec{y}}}{dt}={\bf A}{\bf \vec{y}}\); however, we won’t pursue this approach, since solutions obtained in this way turn out to be complex–valued. Instead, we’ll obtain solutions of \(\dfrac{d{\bf \vec{y}}}{dt}={\bf A}{\bf \vec{y}}\) in the form \begin {equation} \label {eq:10.6.2} {\bf \vec{y}}=y_1{\bf \vec{a}}+y_2{\bf \vec{b}} \end {equation} where \(y_1\) and \(y_2\) are real–valued scalar functions. The next theorem shows how to do this.
Theorem 10.6.1 Let \({\bf A}\) be an \(n\times n\) matrix with real entries. Let \({r}_1 =\alpha +i\beta \) be a complex eigenvalue of \({\bf A}\) and let \({\bf \vec{u}}={\bf \vec{a}}+i{\bf \vec{b}}\) be an associated eigenvector, where \({\bf \vec{a}}\) and \({\bf \vec{b}}\) have real components. Then \({\bf \vec{a}}\) and \({\bf \vec{b}}\) are both nonzero and \[ {\bf \vec{y}}_1=e^{\alpha t}({\bf \vec{a}}\cos \beta t-{\bf \vec{b}}\sin \beta t) \quad \mbox{ and} \quad {\bf \vec{y}}_2=e^{\alpha t}({\bf \vec{a}}\sin \beta t+{\bf \vec{b}}\cos \beta t) \] which are the real and imaginary parts of \begin {equation} \label {eq:10.6.3} e^{\alpha t}(\cos \beta t+i\sin \beta t)({\bf \vec{a}}+i{\bf \vec{b}}) \end {equation} are linearly independent solutions of \(\dfrac{d{\bf \vec{y}}}{dt}=A{\bf \vec{y}}\).
Proof A function of the form (10.6.2) is a solution of \(\dfrac{d{\bf \vec{y}}}{dt}=A{\bf \vec{y}}\) if and only if \begin {equation} \label {eq:10.6.4} \dfrac{df_1}{dt}{\bf \vec{a}}+\dfrac{df_2}{dt}{\bf \vec{b}}=f_1A{\bf \vec{a}}+f_2A{\bf \vec{b}} \end {equation} Carrying out the multiplication indicated on the right side of (10.6.1) and collecting the real and imaginary parts of the result yields \[ A({\bf \vec{a}}+i{\bf \vec{b}})=(\alpha {\bf \vec{a}}-\beta {\bf \vec{b}})+i(\alpha {\bf \vec{b}}+\beta {\bf \vec{a}})\] Equating real and imaginary parts on the two sides of this equation yields \[ \begin {array}{rcl} A{\bf \vec{a}}&=&\alpha {\bf \vec{a}}-\beta {\bf \vec{b}}\\ A{\bf \vec{b}}&=&\alpha {\bf \vec{b}}+\beta {\bf \vec{a}} \end {array} \] We leave it to you (Exercise 25) to show from this that \({\bf \vec{a}}\) and \({\bf \vec{b}}\) are both nonzero. Substituting from these equations into (10.6.4) yields
\begin {eqnarray*} \dfrac{df_1}{dt}{\bf \vec{a}}+\dfrac{df_2}{dt}{\bf \vec{b}} &=&f_1(\alpha {\bf \vec{a}}-\beta {\bf \vec{b}})+f_2(\alpha {\bf \vec{b}}+\beta {\bf \vec{a}})\\ &=&(\alpha f_1+\beta f_2){\bf \vec{a}}+(-\beta f_1+\alpha f_2){\bf \vec{b}} \end {eqnarray*}
This is true if \[ \begin {array}{rcr} \dfrac{df_1}{dt}&=&\alpha f_1+\beta f_2\phantom {,}\\ \dfrac{df_2}{dt}&=&-\beta f_1+\alpha f_2 \end {array} \quad \mbox{ or, equivalently,} \quad \begin {array}{rcr} \dfrac{df_1}{dt}-\alpha f_1&=&\phantom {-}\beta f_2\phantom {.}\\ \dfrac{df_2}{dt}-\alpha f_2&=&-\beta f_1 \end {array} \] If we let \(f_1=g_1e^{\alpha t}\) and \(f_2=g_2e^{\alpha t}\), where \(g_1\) and \(g_2\) are to be determined, then the last two equations become \[ \begin {array}{rcr} \dfrac{dg_1}{dt}&=&\beta g_2\phantom {.}\\ \dfrac{dg_2}{dt}&=&-\beta g_1 \end {array} \] which implies that \[ \dfrac{d^2g_1}{dt^2}=\beta \dfrac{dg_2}{dt}=-\beta ^2 g_1\] so \[ \dfrac{d^2g_1}{dt^2}+\beta ^2 g_1=0\] The general solution of this equation is \[ g_1=c_1\cos \beta t+c_2\sin \beta t\] Moreover, since \(g_2=\dfrac{dg_1}{dt}/\beta \), \[ g_2=-c_1\sin \beta t+c_2\cos \beta t\] Multiplying \(g_1\) and \(g_2\) by \(e^{\alpha t}\) shows that
\begin {eqnarray*} f_1&=&e^{\alpha t}(\phantom {-}c_1\cos \beta t+c_2\sin \beta t ),\\ f_2&=&e^{\alpha t}(-c_1\sin \beta t+c_2\cos \beta t) \end {eqnarray*}
Substituting these into (10.6.2) shows that \begin {equation} \label {eq:10.6.5} \begin {array}{rcl} {\bf \vec{y}}&=&e^{\alpha t}\left [(c_1\cos \beta t+c_2\sin \beta t){\bf \vec{a}} +(-c_1\sin \beta t+c_2\cos \beta t){\bf \vec{b}}\right ]\\ &=&c_1e^{\alpha t}({\bf \vec{a}}\cos \beta t-{\bf \vec{b}}\sin \beta t) +c_2e^{\alpha t}({\bf \vec{a}}\sin \beta t+{\bf \vec{b}}\cos \beta t) \end {array} \end {equation} is a solution of \(\dfrac{d{\bf \vec{y}}}{dt}=A{\bf \vec{y}}\) for any choice of the constants \(c_1\) and \(c_2\). In particular, by first taking \(c_1=1\) and \(c_2=0\) and then taking \(c_1=0\) and \(c_2=1\), we see that \({\bf \vec{y}}_1\) and \({\bf \vec{y}}_2\) are solutions of \( \dfrac{d{\bf \vec{y}}}{dt}=A{\bf \vec{y}}\). We leave it to you to verify that they are, respectively, the real and imaginary parts of (10.6.3) (Exercise 26), and that they are linearly independent (Exercise 27).


Find the general solution of \begin {equation} \label {eq:10.6.6} \dfrac{d{\bf \vec{y}}}{dt}=\twobytwo 4{-5}5{-2}{\bf \vec{y}} \end {equation}
Solution
  1. To solve (10.6.6) we will seek solutions of the form \[ {\bf \vec{y}} = {\bf \vec{u}} e^{rt} \] Where \({\bf \vec{u}} \) is some constant vector to be determined \[ {\bf \vec{u}} =\twocol {u_1}{u_2} \] Now differentiating \({\bf \vec{y}} = {\bf \vec{u}} e^{rt}\) we obtain \[ \frac{d{\bf \vec{y}}}{dt} = r{\bf \vec{u}} e^{rt} \] Now substituting \({\bf \vec{y}}\) and \(d{\bf \vec{y}}/dt\) into (10.6.6) \begin{align*} \dfrac{d{\bf \vec{y}}}{dt} & =\twobytwo {4}{-5}{5}{-2} {\bf \vec{y}} \\ \\ r{\bf \vec{u}} e^{rt} & =\twobytwo {4}{-5}{5}{-2}{\bf \vec{u}} e^{rt} \\ \\ r{\bf \vec{u}} & =\twobytwo {4}{-5}{5}{-2}{\bf \vec{u}} \\ \\ {\bf \vec{0}} & =\twobytwo {4}{-5}{5}{-2}{\bf \vec{u}} - r{\bf \vec{u}} \\ \\ {\bf \vec{0}} & =\left[ \twobytwo {4}{-5}{5}{-2} - r \right]{\bf \vec{u}} \\ \\ \end{align*}
  2. Rewriting \(r\) in matrix form \(r{\bf \vec{I}}\) multiplied by the identity matrix: \begin{align} {\bf \vec{0}} & =\left[ \twobytwo {4}{-5}{5}{-2} - r {\bf {I}} \right]{\bf \vec{u}}\nonumber \\ \nonumber\\ {\bf \vec{0}} & =\left[\twobytwo {4}{-5}{5}{-2} - r \twobytwo 1001 \right] {\bf \vec{u}} \nonumber \\ \nonumber\\ {\bf \vec{0}} & =\left[\twobytwo {4}{-5}{5}{-2} - \twobytwo r00r \right] {\bf \vec{u}} \nonumber\\ \nonumber\\ {\bf \vec{0}} & =\twobytwo {4-r}{-5}{5}{-2-r} {\bf \vec{u}} \\ \nonumber\\ \end{align} The eigenvalue equation (10.6.7) shows the relationship between the eigenvalue \(r\) and associated eigenvector \({\bf \vec{u}} \) for the system of differential equations (10.6.6) and since we'll be using it to solve for the eigenvector components \(u_1\) and \(u_2\), we'll write it in augmented matrix form as the following \begin{align} \twobytwo {4-r}{-5}{5}{-2-r} {\bf \vec{u}} & = {\bf \vec{0}} \nonumber \\ \nonumber \\ \twobytwo {4-r}{-5}{5}{-2-r} \twocol{u_1}{u_2} & = \twocol{0}{0} \nonumber \\ \nonumber \\ \left(\begin{array}{cc|c} 4-r & -5 & 0 \\ 5 & -2-r & 0 \end{array}\right) \end{align}
  3. We can show that a system of equations has only the trivial solution by showing that the determinant of the coefficient matrix is nonzero. As we are only interested in nontrivial solutions we will require that the determinant of the coefficient matrix equals zero: \begin{align*} \det \left( \begin{array}{ccc} 4-r & -5 \\ 5 & -2-r \\ \end{array} \right) & =0 \\ \\ (4-r)(-2-r)+5\cdot5 & =0 \\ \big( r^2-2r-8 \big) +25 & =0 \\ \underset{p(r)}{\underbrace{r^2-2r+17}} & =0 \\ \end{align*} We have found the characteristic polynomial \(p(r)=r^2-2r+17\) and we will now solve the characteristic equation \(r^2-2r+17 =0 \) using the quadratic formula: \begin{align*} r = & \frac{-(-2) \pm \sqrt{(-2)^2-4(1)(17)}}{2(1)} \\ r = & \frac{2 \pm \sqrt{4(1-17)}}{2} \\ r = & \frac{2 \pm \sqrt{4(-16)}}{2} \\ r = & \frac{2 \pm 2\cdot4i}{2} \\ r = & 1 \pm 4i \\ r_1 = 1 + 4i & \quad r_2 = 1 - 4i \\ \end{align*}
  4. Thus we have complex conjugate eigenvalues \(r_1= 1 + 4i\) and \(r_2 = 1 - 4i\) and thus two solutions of the system are \begin{align*} {\bf \vec{y}_1} & = {\bf \vec{u}_1} e^{r_1t}= {\bf \vec{u}_1} e^{(1+4i)t} \quad\text{and}\quad {\bf \vec{y}_2} = {\bf \vec{u}_2} e^{r_2t} ={\bf \vec{u}_2} e^{(1-4i)t} \\ \end{align*} We already have the eigenvalues and now we need to find the associated eigenvectors \({\bf \vec{u}_1}\) and \({\bf \vec{u}_2}\) using row reduction on the augmented matrix form of the eigenvalue equation (10.6.7) \begin{align*} {\bf \vec{0}} & =\twobytwo {4-r_1}{-5}{5}{-2-r_1} {\bf \vec{u}}_1 \\ \nonumber\\ & \left(\begin{array}{cc|c} 4-(1+4i) & -5 & 0 \\ 5 & -2-(1+4i) & 0 \end{array}\right) \\ \\ & \left(\begin{array}{cc|c} 3-4i & -5 & 0 \\ 5 & -3-4i & 0 \end{array}\right) \\ \\ \end{align*} Now we'll get one in the first row and column by dividing the first row by a complex number, which requires multiplying by the conjugate in the numerator and denominator of the second entry. \begin{align*} & \left(\begin{array}{cc|c} 3-4i & -5 & 0 \\ 5 & -3-4i & 0 \end{array}\right) \\ \\ \overset{\frac{1}{3-4i}R_1}{\longrightarrow} & \left(\begin{array}{cc|c} \frac{3-4i}{3-4i} & \frac{-5}{3-4i}\cdot\frac{3+4i}{3+4i} & 0 \\ 5 & -3-4i & 0 \end{array}\right) \\ \\ & \left(\begin{array}{cc|c} 1 & \frac{-5(3+4i)}{25} & 0 \\ 5 & -3-4i & 0 \end{array}\right) \\ \\ \overset{5R_1}{\longrightarrow} & \left(\begin{array}{cc|c} 5 & -3-4i & 0 \\ 5 & -3-4i & 0 \end{array}\right) \\ \\ \overset{R_2-R_1}{\longrightarrow} & \left(\begin{array}{cc|c} 5 & -3-4i & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & 0 \end{array}\right) \\ \\ \end{align*}
  5. The system simplifies down to a single equation for the components of the eigenvector \(u_1\) and \(u_2\) \[5u_1 - (3+4i)u_2 = 0 \] Since we have only 1 equation with 2 unknowns, we have 1 free variable, so we'll let \(u_2=c\) where \(c\) is any number. Then we can solve for \(u_1\) \begin{align*} 5u_1 - (3+4i)u_2 & = 0 \\ 5u_1 - (3+4i)c & = 0 \\ 5u_1 & = (3+4i)c \\ u_1 & = \frac{1}{5}(3+4i)c \\ \end{align*} Therefore we have determined the eigenvector \({\bf \vec{u}_1}\) \[ {\bf \vec{u}_1} =\twocol {u_1}{u_2} =\twocol {\frac{1}{5}(3+4i)c}{c} =c\twocol {\frac{1}{5}(3+4i)}{1} \] Since \(c\) can be any number we'll select \(c=5\) for a simple eigenvector \begin{align*} {\bf \vec{u}_1}=\twocol {3+4i}{5} =\twocol {3}{5} + i\twocol {4}{0} \end{align*} With the eigenvalue-eigenvector pair \(r_1\) and \({\bf \vec{u}_1}\) we have one solution of our original equation \begin{align*} {\bf \vec{y}_1} = {\bf \vec{u}_1}e^{r_1 t} = \left[\twocol {3}{5} + i\twocol {4}{0}\right]e^{(1+4i)t} \end{align*}
  6. Our characteristic equation yielded complex conjugate eigenvalues \(r_1= 1 + 4i\) and \(r_2 = 1 - 4i\) and we already found an eigenvector associated with \(r_1= 1 + 4i\) so now we'll find an eigenvector for \(r_2 = 1 - 4i\) again using row reduction on the augmented matrix form of the eigenvalue equation (10.6.7) \begin{align*} {\bf \vec{0}} & =\twobytwo {4-r_2}{-5}{5}{-2-r_2} {\bf \vec{u}}_1 \\ \nonumber\\ & \left(\begin{array}{cc|c} 4-(1-4i) & -5 & 0 \\ 5 & -2-(1-4i) & 0 \end{array}\right) \\ \\ & \left(\begin{array}{cc|c} 3+4i & -5 & 0 \\ 5 & -3+4i & 0 \end{array}\right) \\ \\ \end{align*} Again we'll get one in the first row and column by dividing the first row by a complex number, which requires multiplying by the conjugate in the numerator and denominator of the second entry. \begin{align*} & \left(\begin{array}{cc|c} 3+4i & -5 & 0 \\ 5 & -3+4i & 0 \end{array}\right) \\ \\ \overset{\frac{1}{3+4i}R_1}{\longrightarrow} & \left(\begin{array}{cc|c} \frac{3+4i}{3+4i} & \frac{-5}{3+4i}\cdot\frac{3-4i}{3-4i} & 0 \\ 5 & -3+4i & 0 \end{array}\right) \\ \\ & \left(\begin{array}{cc|c} 1 & \frac{-5(3-4i)}{25} & 0 \\ 5 & -3+4i & 0 \end{array}\right) \\ \\ \overset{5R_1}{\longrightarrow} & \left(\begin{array}{cc|c} 5 & -3+4i & 0 \\ 5 & -3+4i & 0 \end{array}\right) \\ \\ \overset{R_2-R_1}{\longrightarrow} & \left(\begin{array}{cc|c} 5 & -3+4i & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & 0 \end{array}\right) \\ \\ \end{align*}
    The system simplifies down to a single equation for the components of the eigenvector \(u_1\) and \(u_2\) \[5u_1 - (3-4i)u_2 = 0 \] Since we have only 1 equation with 2 unknowns, we have 1 free variable, so we'll let \(u_2=c\) where \(c\) is any number. Then we can solve for \(u_1\) \begin{align*} 5u_1 - (3-4i)u_2 & = 0 \\ 5u_1 - (3-4i)c & = 0 \\ 5u_1 & = (3-4i)c \\ u_1 & = \frac{1}{5}(3-4i)c \\ \end{align*}
  7. Therefore we have determined the eigenvector \({\bf \vec{u}}_2\) \[ {\bf \vec{u}}_2 =\twocol {u_1}{u_2} =\twocol {\frac{1}{5}(3-4i)c}{c} =c\twocol {\frac{1}{5}(3-4i)}{1} \] Since \(c\) can be any number we'll select \(c=5\) for a simple eigenvector \begin{align*} {\bf \vec{u}}_2=\twocol {3-4i}{5} =\twocol {3}{5} - i\twocol {4}{0} \end{align*} With the eigenvalue-eigenvector pair \(r_2\) and \({\bf \vec{u}_2}\) we have a second solution of our original equation \begin{align*} {\bf \vec{y}_2} = {\bf \vec{u}_2}e^{r_2 t} = \left[\twocol {3}{5} - i\twocol {4}{0}\right]e^{(1-4i)t} \end{align*}
    You may notice that the eigenvector and eigenvalues obtained this way are complex conjugates of each other, meaning in the future we can calculate just one of them and then have both to obtain a fundamental set of solutions in exponential form \begin{align*} \{{\bf \vec{y}_1},{\bf \vec{y}_2}\} = & \left\{ \left[{\bf \vec{a}} + i{\bf \vec{b}}\right]e^{(\alpha + i\beta)t} , \left[{\bf \vec{a}} - i{\bf \vec{b}}\right]e^{(\alpha - i\beta)t} \right\} \\ \\ \{{\bf \vec{y}_1},{\bf \vec{y}_2}\} = & \left\{ \left[\twocol {3}{5} + i\twocol {4}{0}\right]e^{(1+4i)t} , \left[\twocol {3}{5} - i\twocol {4}{0}\right]e^{(1-4i)t} \right\} \\ \\ \end{align*} As we encountered in Ch 5.2, there is a small issue with this solution set: that it includes complex valued functions! So we will again use Euler's formula to rewrite them in terms of real-valued functions, starting with \({\bf \vec{y}_1}\) \begin{align*} {\bf \vec{y}_1} = \, & \left[\twocol {3}{5} + i\twocol {4}{0}\right]\underset{e^{t} \, e^{4it} }{\underbrace{e^{(1+4i)t}}} \\ = \, & e^{t}\left[\twocol {3}{5} + i\twocol {4}{0}\right] e^{4it} \\ = \, & e^{t}\left[\twocol {3}{5} + i\twocol {4}{0}\right] \Big( \cos 4t + i \sin 4t\Big) \\ = \, & e^{t} \Bigg[ \left\{ \twocol {3}{5} \cos 4t - \twocol {4}{0} \sin 4t \right\} + i \left\{ \twocol {4}{0} \cos 4t + \twocol {3}{5} \sin 4t \right\} \Bigg] \\ = \, & \left[ \twocol {3}{5} \cos 4t - \twocol {4}{0} \sin 4t \right] e^{t} + i \left[ \twocol {4}{0} \cos 4t + \twocol {3}{5} \sin 4t \right] e^{t} \\ = \, & \underset{{\bf \vec{a}}_1}{ \underbrace{ \twocol {3\cos 4t - 4 \sin 4t}{5\cos 4t } e^{t} }} + i \underset{{\bf \vec{b}}_1}{ \underbrace{ \twocol {4 \cos 4t + 3 \sin 4t}{5 \sin 4t} e^{t} }} \\ \end{align*} We have rewritten our solution in real and imaginary components as \[ {\bf \vec{y}}_1 = {\bf \vec{a}}_1 + i{\bf \vec{b}}_1 \]
  8. Since \( {\bf \vec{y}}_1 \) is a solution of our original system, then it turns out that its component parts are also solutions \begin{align*} \frac{d {\bf \vec{y}}_1 }{dt} = & {\bf A} {\bf \vec{y}}_1 \\ \frac{d }{dt} \Big( {\bf \vec{a}}_1 + i{\bf \vec{b}}_1 \Big) = & {\bf A} \Big( {\bf \vec{a}}_1 + i{\bf \vec{b}}_1 \Big) \\ \frac{d {\bf \vec{a}}_1 }{dt} + i\frac{d {\bf \vec{b}}_1 }{dt} = & {\bf A} {\bf \vec{a}}_1 + i{\bf A} {\bf \vec{b}}_1 \\ \end{align*} Equating the real parts and the imaginary parts, we obtain \[ \frac{d {\bf \vec{a}}_1 }{dt} = {\bf A} {\bf \vec{a}}_1 \quad \quad \frac{d {\bf \vec{b}}_1 }{dt} = {\bf A} {\bf \vec{b}}_1 \] Which indicates that \( {\bf \vec{a}}_1 \) and \( {\bf \vec{b}}_1 \) are both real-valued solutions of the equation. Applying this to our solution: \begin{align*} {\bf \vec{y}_1} = \, & \underset{{\bf \vec{a}}_1}{ \underbrace{ \twocol {3\cos 4t - 4 \sin 4t}{5\cos 4t } e^{t} }} + i \underset{{\bf \vec{b}}_1}{ \underbrace{ \twocol {4 \cos 4t + 3 \sin 4t}{5 \sin 4t} e^{t} }} \end{align*} We have the following two solutions \begin{align*} {\bf \vec{a}}_1 = \twocol {3\cos 4t - 4 \sin 4t}{5\cos 4t } e^{t} \quad\quad {\bf \vec{b}}_1= \twocol {4 \cos 4t + 3 \sin 4t}{5 \sin 4t} e^{t} \end{align*} We can also show that the two solutions \({\bf \vec{a}}_1\) and \({\bf \vec{b}}_1\) are linearly independent using the Wronskian determinant: \begin{align*} W\big[{\bf \vec{a}}_1, {\bf \vec{b}}_1\big] = \det \left( \begin{array}{ccc} {\bf \vec{a}}_1 & {\bf \vec{b}}_1 \\ \dfrac{d{\bf \vec{a}}_1}{dt} & \dfrac{d{\bf \vec{b}}_1}{dt} \\ \end{array} \right) \end{align*}
    Calculate \( W\big[{\bf \vec{a}}_1, {\bf \vec{b}}_1\big] = \det \left( \begin{array}{ccc} {\bf \vec{a}}_1 & {\bf \vec{b}}_1 \\ \dfrac{d{\bf \vec{a}}_1}{dt} & \dfrac{d{\bf \vec{b}}_1}{dt} \\ \end{array} \right) \)

Remember both complex conjugate solutions will yield different fundamental sets so for consistency we will use the fundamental set formed from the positive eigenvalue \(i\) \begin{align*} {\bf \vec{y}_2} = \, & \left[\threecol {0}{0}{1} + i\threecol {1}{-1}{0}\right]\underset{e^{2t} \, e^{2it}}{\underbrace{e^{(2+2i)t}}} \\ = \, & e^{2t} \left[\threecol {0}{0}{1} + i\threecol {1}{-1}{0}\right] \Big( \cos 2t + i \sin 2t\Big) \\ = \, & e^{2t} \left[ \left\{ \threecol {0}{0}{1} \cos 2t - \threecol {1}{-1}{0} \sin 2t \right\} + i \left\{ \threecol {1}{-1}{0} \cos 2t + \threecol {0}{0}{1} \sin 2t \right\} \right] \\ = \, & \left[ \threecol {0}{0}{1} \cos 2t - \threecol {1}{-1}{0} \sin 2t \right] e^{2t} + i \left[ \threecol {1}{-1}{0} \cos 2t + \threecol {0}{0}{1} \sin 2t \right] e^{2t} \\ = \, & \underset{{\bf \vec{a}}_2}{ \underbrace{ \threecol { -\sin 2t}{\sin 2t }{\cos 2t} }} e^{2t} + i \underset{{\bf \vec{b}}_2}{ \underbrace{ \threecol {\cos 2t }{-\cos 2t}{\sin 2t} }} e^{2t} \\ \end{align*} Now we have rewritten our solution in real and imaginary components and we know that the components of \( {\bf \vec{y}}_2 = {\bf \vec{a}}_2 + i {\bf \vec{b}}_2\) form two of the solutions in our fundamental set \[ \left\{ {\bf \vec{y}}_1,{\bf \vec{a}}_2, {\bf \vec{b}}_2 \right\} = \left\{ \threecol {1}{1}{-1} e^{2t} , \threecol {- \sin 2t}{\sin 2t }{\cos 2t} e^{2t} , \threecol {\cos 2t}{-\cos 2t}{\sin 2t} e^{2t} \right\} \]
    Therefore we can represent the general solution as \({\bf \vec{y}} = c_1 {\bf \vec{y}}_1 + c_2 {\bf \vec{a}}_2 + c_3 {\bf \vec{b}}_2\) \begin{align*} {\bf \vec{y}} = \left[ c_1 \threecol {1}{1}{-1} + c_2 \threecol {- \sin 2t}{\sin 2t }{\cos 2t} + c_3 \threecol {\cos 2t}{-\cos 2t}{\sin 2t} \right] e^{2t} \end{align*}
Geometric Properties of Solutions when \(n=2\)
We’ll now consider the geometric properties of solutions of a \(2\times 2\) constant coefficient system \begin {equation} \label {eq:10.6.10} \twocol {\dfrac{dy_1}{dt}}{\dfrac{dy_2}{dt}}=\left [ \begin {array}{cc} a_{11}&a_{12}\\ a_{21}&a_{22} \end {array} \right ]\twocol {y_1}{y_2} \end {equation} under the assumptions of this section; that is, when the matrix \[ A=\left [ \begin {array}{cc} a_{11}&a_{12}\\ a_{21}&a_{22} \end {array} \right ] \] has a complex eigenvalue \({r} =\alpha +i\beta \) (\(\beta \ne 0\)) and \({\bf \vec{u}}={\bf \vec{a}}+i{\bf \vec{b}}\) is an associated eigenvector, where \({\bf \vec{a}}\) and \({\bf \vec{b}}\) have real components. To describe the trajectories accurately it’s necessary to introduce a new rectangular coordinate system in the \(y_1\)-\(y_2\) plane. This raises a point that hasn’t come up before: It is always possible to choose \(\bf x\) so that \(({\bf \vec{a}},{\bf \vec{b}})=0\). A special effort is required to do this, since not every eigenvector has this property. However, if we know an eigenvector that doesn’t, we can multiply it by a suitable complex constant to obtain one that does. To see this, note that if \(\bf x\) is a \({r} \)-eigenvector of \(A\) and \(k\) is an arbitrary real number, then \[ {\bf \vec{u}}_1=(1+ik){\bf \vec{u}}=(1+ik)({\bf \vec{a}}+i{\bf \vec{b}}) =({\bf \vec{a}}-k{\bf \vec{b}})+i({\bf \vec{b}}+k{\bf \vec{a}}) \] is also a \({r} \)-eigenvector of \(A\), since \[ A{\bf \vec{u}}_1= A((1+ik){\bf \vec{u}})=(1+ik)A{\bf \vec{u}}=(1+ik){r} {\bf \vec{u}}= {r} ((1+ik){\bf \vec{u}})={r} {\bf \vec{u}}_1\] The real and imaginary parts of \({\bf \vec{u}}_1\) are \begin {equation} \label {eq:10.6.11} {\bf \vec{a}}_1={\bf \vec{a}}-k{\bf \vec{b}} \quad \mbox{ and } \quad {\bf \vec{b}}_1={\bf \vec{b}}+k{\bf \vec{a}} \end {equation} so \[ ({\bf \vec{a}}_1,{\bf \vec{b}}_1)=({\bf \vec{a}}-k{\bf \vec{b}},{\bf \vec{b}}+k{\bf \vec{a}}) =-\left [({\bf \vec{a}},{\bf \vec{b}})k^2+(\|{\bf \vec{b}}\|^2-\|{\bf \vec{a}}\|^2)k -({\bf \vec{a}},{\bf \vec{b}})\right ]\] Therefore \(({\bf \vec{a}}_1,{\bf \vec{b}}_1)=0\) if \begin {equation} \label {eq:10.6.12} ({\bf \vec{a}},{\bf \vec{b}})k^2+(\|{\bf \vec{b}}\|^2-\|{\bf \vec{a}}\|^2)k-({\bf \vec{a}},{\bf \vec{b}})=0 \end {equation} If \(({\bf \vec{a}},{\bf \vec{b}})\ne 0\) we can use the quadratic formula to find two real values of \(k\) such that \(({\bf \vec{a}}_1,{\bf \vec{b}}_1)=0\) (Exercise 28).
In Example 10.6.1 we found the eigenvector \[ {\bf \vec{u}}=\ctwocol {3+4i}5=\twocol 35+i\twocol 40 \] for the matrix of the system (10.6.6). Here \({\bf \vec{a}}=\displaystyle {\twocol 35}\) and \({\bf \vec{b}}=\twocol 40\) are not orthogonal, since \(({\bf \vec{a}},{\bf \vec{b}})=12\). Since \(\|{\bf \vec{b}}\|^2-\|{\bf \vec{a}}\|^2=-18\), (10.6.12) is equivalent to \[ 2k^2-3k-2=0\] The zeros of this equation are \(k_1=2\) and \(k_2=-1/2\). Letting \(k=2\) in (10.6.11) yields \[ {\bf \vec{a}}_1={\bf \vec{a}}-2{\bf \vec{b}}=\twocol {-5}{\phantom {-}5}\quad \mbox{ and } \quad {\bf \vec{b}}_1={\bf \vec{b}}+2{\bf \vec{a}}=\twocol {10}{10}\] and \(({\bf \vec{a}}_1,{\bf \vec{b}}_1)=0\). Letting \(k=-1/2\) in (10.6.11) yields \[ {\bf \vec{a}}_1={\bf \vec{a}}+{{\bf \vec{b}}\over 2}=\twocol {5}5\quad \mbox{ and } \quad {\bf \vec{b}}_1={\bf \vec{b}}-{{\bf \vec{a}}\over 2}={1\over 2}\twocol {-5}{\phantom {-}5}\] and again \(({\bf \vec{a}}_1,{\bf \vec{b}}_1)=0\).
(The numbers don’t always work out as nicely as in this example. You’ll need a calculator or computer to do Exercises 29-40.)
Henceforth, we’ll assume that \(({\bf \vec{a}},{\bf \vec{b}})=0\). Let \({\bf \vec{a}}\) and \({\bf \vec{b}}\) be unit vectors in the directions of \({\bf \vec{a}}\) and \({\bf \vec{b}}\), respectively; that is, \({\bf \vec{a}}={\bf \vec{a}}/\|{\bf \vec{a}}\|\) and \({\bf \vec{b}}={\bf \vec{b}}/\|{\bf \vec{b}}\|\). The new rectangular coordinate system will have the same origin as the \(y_1\)-\(y_2\) system. The coordinates of a point in this system will be denoted by \((z_1,z_2)\), where \(z_1\) and \(z_2\) are the displacements in the directions of \({\bf \vec{a}}\) and \({\bf \vec{b}}\), respectively.
From (10.6.5), the solutions of (10.6.10) are given by \begin {equation} \label {eq:10.6.13} {\bf \vec{y}}=e^{\alpha t}\left [(c_1\cos \beta t+c_2\sin \beta t){\bf \vec{a}} +(-c_1\sin \beta t+c_2\cos \beta t){\bf \vec{b}}\right ] \end {equation} For convenience, let’s call the curve traversed by \(e^{-\alpha t}{\bf \vec{y}}(t)\) a shadow trajectory of (10.6.10). Multiplying (10.6.13) by \(e^{-\alpha t}\) yields \[ e^{-\alpha t}{\bf \vec{y}}(t)=z_1(t){\bf \vec{a}}+z_2(t){\bf \vec{b}}\] where
\begin {eqnarray*} z_1(t)&=&\|{\bf \vec{a}}\|(c_1\cos \beta t+c_2\sin \beta t)\\ z_2(t)&=&\|{\bf \vec{b}}\|(-c_1\sin \beta t+c_2\cos \beta t) \end {eqnarray*}
Therefore \[ {(z_1(t))^2\over \|{\bf \vec{a}}\|^2}+{(z_2(t))^2\over \|{\bf \vec{b}}\|^2} =c_1^2+c_2^2 \] (verify!), which means that the shadow trajectories of (10.6.10) are ellipses centered at the origin, with axes of symmetry parallel to \({\bf \vec{a}}\) and \({\bf \vec{b}}\). Since \[ \dfrac{dz_1}{dt}={\beta \|{\bf \vec{a}}\|\over \|{\bf \vec{b}}\|} z_2\quad \mbox{ and } \quad \dfrac{dz_2}{dt}=-{\beta \|{\bf \vec{b}}\|\over \|{\bf \vec{a}}\|} z_1\] the vector from the origin to a point on the shadow ellipse rotates in the same direction that \(\bf V\) would have to be rotated by \(\pi /2\) radians to bring it into coincidence with \(\bf U\) (Figures 10.6.1 and 10.6.2).
If \(\alpha =0\), then any trajectory of (10.6.10) is a shadow trajectory of (10.6.10); therefore, if \({r} \) is purely imaginary, then the trajectories of (10.6.10) are ellipses traversed periodically as indicated in Figures 10.6.1 and 10.6.2.
If \(\alpha >0\), then \[ \lim _{t\to \infty }\|{\bf \vec{y}}(t)\|=\infty \quad \mbox{ and } \quad \lim _{t\to -\infty }{\bf \vec{y}}(t)=0\] so the trajectory spirals away from the origin as \(t\) varies from \(-\infty \) to \(\infty \). The direction of the spiral depends upon the relative orientation of \({\bf \vec{a}}\) and \({\bf \vec{b}}\), as shown in Figures 10.6.3 and 10.6.4.
If \(\alpha <0\), then \[ \lim _{t\to -\infty }\|{\bf \vec{y}}(t)\|=\infty \quad \mbox{ and } \quad \lim _{t\to \infty }{\bf \vec{y}}(t)=0\] so the trajectory spirals toward the origin as \(t\) varies from \(-\infty \) to \(\infty \). Again, the direction of the spiral depends upon the relative orientation of \({\bf \vec{a}}\) and \({\bf \vec{b}}\), as shown in Figures 10.6.5 and 10.6.6.
