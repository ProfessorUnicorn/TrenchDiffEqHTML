4.2 Cooling and Mixing
Newton’s Law of Cooling
Newton’s law of cooling states that the temperature of an object changes at a rate proportional to the difference between the temperature of the object and the temperature of the surroundings. Thus, if \(A=A(t)\) is the temperature of the surroundings and \(T=T(t)\) is the temperature of the object at time \(t\), then \begin{equation} \label{eq:4.2.1} \dot{T} = -k(T-A) \end{equation} where \(k > 0\) is a positive constant and the minus sign indicates that the temperature of the object increases with time if it's less than the temperature of the surroundings \(T < A\) and the temperature of the object decreases with time if it's greater than the temperature of the surroundings \(T > A\). We'll call \(k\) the temperature decay constant.
For simplicity, in this section we'll assume that the surroundings is maintained at a constant temperature \( A\). This is another example of building a simple mathematical model for a physical phenomenon. Like most mathematical models it has its limitations. For example, it's reasonable to assume that the temperature of a room remains approximately constant if the cooling object is a cup of coffee, but perhaps not if it's cooling a huge cauldron of molten metal. (More on this in Exercise 17.)
Notice the constant solution for (4.2.1) which is \( T=A\), or the temperature of the object is the same temperature as the surroundings. Well assume this is not the case. With \(T \ne A\) then (4.2.1) is separable \begin{align*} \frac{dT}{dt} = & -k(T-A) \\ \frac{1}{T-A}\cdot \frac{dT}{dt} = & -k \\ \int \left( \frac{1}{T-A} \cdot \frac{dT}{dt} \right) dt = & \int \Big( -k \Big) dt \\ \int \frac{1}{\underset{u}{\underbrace{T-A}}} \underset{du}{\underbrace{dT}} = & -k \int dt \\ \int \frac{1}{u} du = & -k \int dt \\ \ln \left| u \right| = & -kt + c \\ \ln \left| T-A \right| = & -kt + c \\ e^{\ln \left| T-A \right|} = & e^{-kt + c} \\ \left| T-A \right| = & ce^{-kt} \\ T-A = & ce^{-kt} \\ T = & A + ce^{-kt} \\ \end{align*} Notice our one-parameter family \( T = A + ce^{-kt} \) includes the constant solution \( T=A\) when we set \(c=0\), therefore our general solution is just the one-parameter family
Explain how \( \left| T-A \right| \) simplified to \( T-A \)
Verify \( T = A \) is a solution of (4.2.1)
Verify \( T = ce^{-kt} \) is a solution of (4.2.1)
We'll now solve for our constant \(c\) by applying the initial condition that the initial temperature is \(T_0\) at time \(t=0\), i.e. \(T(0)=T_0\): \begin{align*} T(t) = & A + ce^{-kt} \\ \underset{T_0}{\underbrace{T(0)}} = & A + ce^{-k(0)} \\ T_0 = & A + ce^0 \Longrightarrow c = T_0 - A \\ \end{align*} Substituting in our constant \(c = T_0 - A\), we obtain our particular solution \begin{equation} \label{eq:4.2.2} T = A + (T_0 - A) e^{-kt} \end{equation} Subtracting \(A\) on both sides gives \(T - A=(T_0 - A) e^{-kt}\), so the expression \(T-A\) decays exponentially, with initial value \(T_0-A\) and decay constant \(k\).

  2. Calculate the decay constant and substitute it in to the particular solution to the IVP
  3. Use the result from (Part 2) to solve the original problem
Solution fixme

Newton’s law of cooling states that the temperature of an object changes at a rate proportional to the difference between the temperature of the object, \( T \) and the temperature of the surroundings \(25^\circ \)C and using \(k\) as our decay constant, we have the differential equation \begin{align*} \frac{dT}{dt} = & -k(T-25^\circ\text{C}) \end{align*} But setting up an IVP requires an initial condition as well, which is given in the problem statement as when it says the ceramic insulator is baked at \(400^\circ \)C and that is the initial temperature, which we can write as \( T(0) = 400^\circ\text{C} \) so we have our IVP \begin{align*} \frac{dT}{dt} = & -k(T-25^\circ\text{C}) \quad T(0) = 400^\circ\text{C} \end{align*} First identify the constant solution \(T=25^\circ\text{C}\)
    Verify \(T=25^\circ\text{C}\) is a solution of \( \dot{T} = -k(T-25^\circ\text{C}) \)
    Assuming \(T \ne 25\), the equation can be solved using separation of variables: \begin{align*} \frac{dT}{dt} = & -k(T-25) \\ \frac{1}{T-25} \cdot \frac{dT}{dt} = & -k \\ \int \left( \frac{1}{T-25} \cdot \frac{dT}{dt} \right) dt = & \int \Big( -k \Big) dt \\ \int \frac{1}{\underset{u}{\underbrace{T-25}}} \underset{du}{\underbrace{dT}} = & -k \int dt \\ \int \frac{1}{u} du = & -k \int dt \\ \ln \left| u \right| = & -kt + c \\ \ln \left| T-25 \right| = & -kt + c \\ e^{\ln \left| T-25 \right|} = & e^{-kt + c} \\ \left| T-25 \right| = & ce^{-kt} \\ T-25 = & ce^{-kt} \\ T = & 25 + ce^{-kt} \\ \end{align*} Then applying our initial condition, we find \( c \) \begin{align*} T(t) = & 25 + ce^{-kt} \\ \underset{400}{\underbrace{T(0)}} = & 25 + ce^{-k(0)} \\ 400 = & 25 + c \\ c = & 375 \\ \end{align*} And finally substitute it back in to obtain \begin{equation} \label{eq:4.2.3} T = 25 + 375e^{-kt} \end{equation}
    Verify \( T = 25 + 375e^{-kt} \) is a solution of \( \dot{T} = -k(T-25^\circ\text{C}) \)
  2. After 4 minutes the temperature of the insulator is \( 200^\circ\)C means \(T(4) = 200\), substituting this in we can obtain the temperature decay constant value \begin{align*} T(t) = & 25 + 375e^{-kt} \\ \underset{200}{\underbrace{T(4)}} = & 25 + 375e^{-4k} \\ 200 = & 25 + 375e^{-4k} \\ 175 = & 375e^{-4k} \\ \frac{175}{375} = & e^{-4k} \\ \ln \left( \frac{175/25}{375/25} \right) = & \ln e^{-4k} \\ \ln \left( \frac{7}{15} \right) = & -4k \\ k = & - \frac{1}{4} \ln \left( \frac{7}{15} \right) \\ k = & \frac{1}{4} \ln \left( \frac{15}{7} \right) \\ \end{align*} Substituting in the the temperature decay constant \( k\) \begin{equation*} T = 25 + 375e^{-\frac{t}{4} \ln \left( \frac{15}{7} \right)} \end{equation*}
    Verify \( T = 25 + 375e^{-\frac{t}{4} \ln \left( \frac{15}{7} \right)} \) is a solution of \( \dot{T} = -k(T-25^\circ\text{C}) \)
  3. We want to find the temperature of the insulator after 8 minutes, i.e. evaluate \( T(8) \) \begin{align*} T(t) = & 25 + 375e^{-\frac{t}{4} \ln \left( \frac{15}{7} \right)} \\ T(8) = & 25 + 375e^{-\frac{8}{4} \ln \left( \frac{15}{7} \right)} \\ = & 25 + 375e^{-2 \ln \left( \frac{15}{7} \right)} \\ = & 25 + 375e^{ \ln \left( \frac{15}{7} \right)^{-2}} \\ = & 25 + 375 \left( \frac{7}{15} \right)^{2} \\ = & 25 + 375 \left( \frac{49}{225} \right) \\ = & 25 + 15 \left( \frac{49}{9} \right) \\ = & 25 + \frac{245}{3} = \frac{320}{3} \approx 107^\circ\text{C} \end{align*}
An object with temperature \(72^\circ \)F is placed outside, where the temperature is \(-20^\circ \)F. At 11:05 the temperature of the object is \(60^\circ \)F and at 11:07 its temperature is \(50^\circ \)F. At what time was the object placed outside?
  1. Set up and solve an IVP using Newton's Law of cooling
  2. Calculate the decay constant and substitute it in to the particular solution to the IVP
  3. Use the result from (Part 2) to solve the original problem
Solution
  1. Newton’s law of cooling states that the temperature of an object changes at a rate proportional to the difference between the temperature of the object, \( T \) and the temperature of the surroundings \(-20^\circ \)F and using \(k\) as our decay constant, we have the differential equation \begin{align*} \frac{dT}{dt} = & -k(T+20^\circ\text{F}) \end{align*} But setting up an IVP requires an initial condition as well, which is given in the problem statement as when it says the temperature of the object is \(60^\circ \)F and that is the initial temperature, which we can write as \( T(0) = 60^\circ\text{F} \) so we have our IVP \begin{align*} \frac{dT}{dt} = & -k(T+20^\circ\text{F}) \quad T(0) = 60^\circ\text{F} \end{align*} First identify the constant solution \(T=-20^\circ\text{F}\)
    Verify \(T=-20^\circ\text{F}\) is a solution of \( \dot{T} = -k(T+20) \)
    Solve \( \dot{T} = -k(T+20^\circ\text{F}) \) using separation of variables
    Assuming \( T \ne -20 \) and using separation of variables, we obtain \begin{align*} T = & -20 + ce^{-kt} \\ \end{align*}
    Verify \(T= -20 + ce^{-kt} \) is a solution of \( \dot{T} = -k(T+20) \)
    Apply the initial condition \( T(0) = 60^\circ\text{F} \) to \(T= -20 + ce^{-kt} \) and solve for \( c\)
    Applying the initial condition \( T(0) = 60^\circ\text{F} \) we can solve for \( c \) \begin{align*} \underset{60}{\underbrace{T(0)}} = & -20 + ce^{-k(0)} \Longrightarrow c = 80 \\ \end{align*} So we finally obtain our particular solution \begin{equation} \label{eq:4.2.4} T = -20 + 80 e^{-kt} \end{equation}
    Verify \(T= -20 + 80 e^{-kt} \) is a solution of \( \dot{T} = -k(T+20) \)
  2. At 11:05 the temperature of the object is \( 60^\circ\)F and at 11:07 its temperature is \(50^\circ\)F since we took 11:05 to be time \(t=0\), then 11:07 would be time \(t=2\), so we have \(T(2) = 50\), and, substituting this in we can obtain the temperature decay constant value: \begin{align*} T(t) = & -20 + 80 e^{-kt} \\ \underset{50}{\underbrace{T(2)}} = & -20 + 80 e^{-k\cdot (2)} \\ 50 = & -20 + 80 e^{-2k} \\ 70 = & 80 e^{-2k} \\ \frac{70}{80} = & e^{-2k} \\ \ln \left( \frac{7}{8} \right) = & \ln e^{-2k} \\ \ln \left( \frac{7}{8} \right) = & -2k \\ k = & - \frac{1}{2} \ln \left( \frac{7}{8} \right) =\frac{1}{2} \ln \left( \frac{8}{7} \right) \\ \end{align*} Substituting in the the temperature decay constant \(k\) \begin{equation*} T = -20 + 80 e^{-\frac{t}{2} \ln \left( \frac{8}{7} \right)} \end{equation*}
    Verify \(T= -20 + 80 e^{-\frac{t}{2} \ln \left( \frac{8}{7} \right)} \) is a solution of \( \dot{T} = -k(T+20) \)
  3. The object was originally \(72^\circ\)F and placed outside some time in the past. At 11:05 the temperature of the object is \(60^\circ\)F, which we wrote as \(T(0)=60\). At 11:07 its temperature is \(50^\circ\)F, which we wrote as \(T(2)=50\). We want to find out what time the object was placed outside. In other words, we need to find the time \(t\) when the temperature is \(72^\circ\)F, so substitute in \(T(t)=72\) and solve for \(t\): \begin{align*} T(t) = & -20 + 80 e^{-\frac{t}{2} \ln \left( \frac{8}{7} \right)} \\ 72 = & -20 + 80 e^{-\frac{t}{2} \ln \left( \frac{8}{7} \right)} \\ 92 = & 80 e^{-\frac{t}{2} \ln \left( \frac{8}{7} \right)} \\ \frac{92}{80} = & e^{-\frac{t}{2} \ln \left( \frac{8}{7} \right)} \\ \ln \left( \frac{46}{40} \right) = & \ln e^{-\frac{t}{2} \ln \left( \frac{8}{7} \right)} \\ \ln \left( \frac{23}{20} \right) = & -\frac{t}{2} \ln \left( \frac{8}{7} \right) \\ -\frac{t}{2} = & \frac{ \ln \left( \frac{23}{20} \right) }{ \ln \left( \frac{8}{7} \right) } \\ t = & -2 \frac{ \ln \left( \frac{23}{20} \right) }{ \ln \left( \frac{8}{7} \right) } \approx -2.09 \end{align*} As our initial time at \(t=0\) was taken as 11:05, \(t \approx -2.09\) represents about 2 minutes earlier, so the object was placed outside at about about 11:03.
Mixing Problems
In the next two examples a saltwater solution with a given concentration (weight of salt per unit volume of solution) is added at a specified rate to a tank that initially contains saltwater with a different concentration. The problem is to determine the quantity of salt in the tank as a function of time. This is an example of a mixing problem. To construct a tractable mathematical model for mixing problems we assume in our examples (and most exercises) that the mixture is stirred instantly so that the salt is always uniformly distributed throughout the mixture. (Exercises 22 and 23 deal with situations where this isn’t so, but the distribution of salt becomes approximately uniform as time approaches infinity.)
A tank initially contains 40 pounds of salt dissolved in 600 gallons of water. Starting at \(t_0 = 0\), water that contains 1/2 pound of salt per gallon is poured into the tank at the rate of 4 gal/min and the mixture is drained from the tank at the same rate (Figure 4.2.3). Find the quantity \(Q=Q(t)\) of salt in the tank at time \(t\) and determine the \(\lim_{t\to\infty}Q\)
  1. Set up and solve an IVP to model the mixing
  2. Use the result from (Part 1) to solve the original problem
Solution
  1. There are two competing rates here: the rate in which is increasing of the quantity of salt \(Q\) from the solution that is being poured into the tank and the rate out which is decreasing the quantity of salt from the draining of the tank. Our differential equation must account for both of these rates \begin{align} \label{eq:4.2.5} \dot{Q} = & \mbox{ rate in } - \mbox{ rate out } \end{align}
    We'll analyze each of these rates separately to determine how to set up our differential equation. We'll start with the rate in which is increasing of the quantity of salt from the solution that is being poured into the tank. The solution being poured into the tank contains \({1}/{2}\) pound of salt per gallon and is flowing in at \(4\) gallons/minute: \begin{align*} \mbox{ rate in } = & \Bigg( \frac{1}{2} \,\, \frac{ \text{lb} }{ \text{gal} } \Bigg) \Bigg( 4 \,\, \frac{ \text{gal} }{ \text{min} } \Bigg) = 2 \,\, \frac{ \text{lb} }{ \text{min} } \end{align*}
    So the rate in is pretty straightforward and is just a constant rate. As for the rate out we will need to do a little more work. We know \(2\) pounds of salt are being added per minute to the tank. We need to find out how many pounds of salt are being drained out of the tank each minute. The total tank is \(600\) gallons and assuming the solution being drained out of the tank is always uniformly mixed it contains a total of \(Q\) pounds of salt, or taken as a ratio of the entire tank \({ Q }/{ 600 } \) pounds of salt per gallon and is flowing out at \(4\) gallons/minute: \begin{align*} \mbox{ rate out } = & \Bigg( \frac{ Q }{ 600 } \,\, \frac{ \text{lb} }{ \text{gal} } \Bigg) \Bigg( 4 \,\, \frac{ \text{gal} }{ \text{min} } \Bigg) = \frac{4 Q}{600} \,\, \frac{ \text{lb} }{ \text{min} } = \frac{Q}{150} \,\, \frac{ \text{lb} }{ \text{min} } \end{align*}
    With both the rate in (quantity of salt increasing) and the rate out (quantity of salt decreasing), we can substitute into (4.2.5) \begin{align*} \dot{Q} = & \mbox{ rate in } - \mbox{ rate out} \\ \dot{Q} = & 2 \,\, \frac{ \text{lb} }{ \text{min} } - \frac{Q}{150} \,\, \frac{ \text{lb} }{ \text{min} } \\ \\ \dot{Q} = & 2 - \frac{Q}{150} \end{align*}
    Rewriting our first order linear equation in standard form and pairing this with our initial condition, that the tank initially contains 40 pounds of salt, i.e. \(Q(0)=40\), we have our IVP \begin{equation*} \dot{Q} + \frac{1}{150}Q = 2 \quad Q(0)=40 \end{equation*}
    Solve \( \dot{Q} + \frac{1}{150}Q = 2 \) using separation of variables
    Separating variables, we find \begin{align*} \frac{dQ}{dt} = & \frac{300-Q}{150} \\ \end{align*} So we take note of the constant solution \( Q=300\)
    Verify \(Q=300\) is a solution of \( \dot{Q} + \frac{1}{150}Q = 2 \)
    Assuming \(Q\ne300\) and factoring out the negative, we can separate variables and solve \begin{align} \frac{dQ}{dt} = & -\frac{Q-300}{150} \nonumber \\ \frac{1}{Q-300} \cdot \frac{dQ}{dt} = & -\frac{1}{150} \nonumber \\ \int \left( \frac{1}{Q-300} \cdot \frac{dQ}{dt} \right) dt = & \int \Big( -\frac{1}{150} \Big) dt \nonumber \\ \int \frac{1}{Q-300} dQ = & -\frac{1}{150} \int dt \nonumber \\ \int \frac{1}{u} du = & -\frac{1}{150} \int dt \nonumber \\ \ln \left| u \right| = & -\frac{1}{150} t + c \nonumber \\ \ln \left| Q-300 \right| = & -\frac{1}{150} t + c \nonumber \\ e^{\ln \left| Q-300 \right|} = & e^{-\frac{1}{150} t + c} \nonumber \\ \left| Q-300 \right| = & ce^{-\frac{1}{150} t} \nonumber \\ Q-300 = & ce^{-\frac{1}{150} t} \nonumber \\ Q = & 300 + ce^{-\frac{t}{150}} \label{eq:4.2.6} \end{align}
    Verify (4.2.6) is a solution of \( \dot{Q} + \frac{1}{150}Q = 2 \)
    Applying the initial condition, \(Q(0)=40\) we can solve for \(c\) \begin{align*} Q(t) = & 300 + ce^{-\frac{t}{150}} \\ Q(0) = & 300 + ce^{-\frac{0}{150}} \\ 40 = & 300 + c \\ c = & -260 \\ \end{align*} So we have the particular solution to our IVP: \begin{equation*} Q = 300 - 260 e^{-\frac{t}{150}} \end{equation*}
    Verify \( Q = 300 - 260 e^{-\frac{t}{150}} \) is a solution of \( \dot{Q} + \frac{1}{150}Q = 2 \)
  2. Taking the limit of our solution: \begin{align*} \lim_{x\to \infty} Q = & \lim_{x\to \infty} \Bigg( 300 - 260 e^{-\frac{t}{150}} \Bigg) \\ = & \lim_{x\to \infty} \Big( 300 \Big) - 260 \lim_{x\to \infty} \Big( e^{-\frac{t}{150}} \Big) \\ = & 300 - 260 e^{-\infty} \\ = & 300 \\ \end{align*}
    From (4.2.6) we find that that \(\lim _{t \to \infty }Q=300\) for any value of \(Q(0)\). This is intuitively reasonable, since the incoming solution contains 1/2 pound of salt per gallon and there are always 600 gallons of water in the tank.
A 500-liter tank initially contains 10 g of salt dissolved in 200 liters of water. Starting at \(t_0=0\), water that contains 1/4 g of salt per liter is poured into the tank at the rate of 4 liters/min and the mixture is drained from the tank at the rate of 2 liters/min (Figure 4.2.5). Find the quantity \(Q=Q(t)\) of salt in the tank at time \(t\) prior to the time when the tank overflows. Represent the concentration \(K = K(t)\) (g/liter) of salt in the tank at any time \( t \).
  1. Set up and solve an IVP to model the mixing
  2. Use the result from (Part 1) to solve the original problem
Solution
  1. There are two competing rates here: the rate of increase of the quantity of salt $Q$ from the solution that is being poured into the tank and the rate of decrease of the quantity of salt $Q$ from the draining of the tank. Our differential equation must account for both of these rates. \begin{align} \label{eq:4.2.7} \dot{Q} = & \mbox{ rate in } - \mbox{ rate out } \end{align}
    Again we'll analyze each of these rates separately to determine how to set up our differential equation. We'll start with the rate of increase of the quantity of salt from the solution that is being poured into the tank. The solution being poured into the tank contains \({1}/{4}\) gram of salt per liter and is flowing in at \(4\) liters/minute: \begin{align} \label{eq:4.2.8} \mbox{ rate in} = & \Bigg( \frac{1}{4} \,\, \frac{ \text{gm} }{ \text{L} } \Bigg) \Bigg( 4 \,\, \frac{ \text{L} }{ \text{min} } \Bigg) = 1 \,\, \frac{ \text{gm} }{ \text{min} } \end{align}
    We know \(1\) gram of salt is being added per minute to the tank. We need to find out how many grams of salt are being drained out of the tank each minute. Recall that the volume of solution in our tank is not constant as it was in the previous example. In this case, we start out with 200 liters of solution and 4 liters per minute are being added to the tank while 2 liters per minute are being drained from the tank. That means the volume \(V\) of solution in the tank is increasing at 2 liters per minute: \begin{align*} V = & \overset{ \text{initial volume} }{\overbrace{200} } + \overset{ \text{solution added} }{\overbrace{4t} } - \overset{ \text{solution drained} }{\overbrace{2t} } \\ V = & \quad 200 \quad + \quad 2t \\ V = & \quad 2t + 200 \end{align*}
    So at time \(t=0\), the volume of our tank is \(V(0)=200\) and the volume of fluid is increasing until the time when our tank gets full at 500 liters, or when \(V(t)=500\) which is after 150 minutes \begin{align*} V(t) = & 2t + 200 \\ 500 = & 2t + 200 \\ 300 = & 2t \\ t = & 150 \\ \end{align*}
    The total tank is \( 2t + 200 \) liters and assuming the solution being drained out of the tank is always uniformly mixed it contains a total of \( Q\) grams of salt, or taken as a ratio of the entire tank \({ Q }/{ (2t + 200) } \) grams of salt per liter and is flowing out at \(2\) liters/minute \begin{align} \mbox{ rate out } = & \Bigg( \frac{ Q }{ (2t + 200) } \,\, \frac{ \text{gm} }{ \text{L} } \Bigg) \Bigg( 2 \,\, \frac{ \text{L} }{ \text{min} } \Bigg) \nonumber = \frac{2 Q}{2t + 200} \,\, \frac{ \text{L} }{ \text{min} } \nonumber \\ \end{align} Simplifying we obtain the rate out: \begin{align} \label{eq:4.2.9} \mbox{ rate out } = & \frac{Q}{t + 100} \,\, \frac{ \text{L} }{ \text{min} } \end{align}
    With both the rate in and the rate out, we can substitute into (4.2.7) \begin{align*} \dot{Q} = & \mbox{ rate in } - \mbox{ rate out} \\ \dot{Q} = & 1 \,\, \frac{ \text{gm} }{ \text{min} } \ - \frac{Q}{t + 100} \,\, \frac{ \text{L} }{ \text{min} } \\ \dot{Q} = & 1 - \frac{Q}{t + 100} \end{align*}
    Rewriting our 1st order linear equation in standard form and pairing this with our initial condition, that the tank initially contains 10 grams of salt, i.e. \(Q(0)=10\), we have our IVP: \begin{equation}\label{eq:4.2.10} \dot{Q} + \left( \frac{1}{t + 100} \right) Q = 1 \quad Q(0)=10 \end{equation}
    Although our IVP (4.2.10) is not separable, since it is linear first order, we can start by solving the complementary equation which is separable \begin{align*} \dot{Q} + & \left( \frac{1}{t+100} \right) Q = 0 \\ \end{align*}
    Separate the variables \( \dot{Q} + \left( \frac{1}{t+100} \right) Q = 0 \)
    After separating the variables we get: \begin{align*} \int \frac{1}{Q} dQ = & - \int \frac{1}{t+100} dt \\ \end{align*}
    Find the antiderivative \( \int \frac{1}{t+100} dt \) using substitution
    Then we find the antiderivatives on both sides and solve for \(Q \) to obtain the solution to our complementary equation. \begin{align*} Q = & \frac{c}{t+100} \\ \end{align*} We will use variation of parameters with and now select a simple particular solution so using \(c=1\) we will select \( Q_1 = \frac{1}{t+100} \) and seek solutions of the form \(Q=uQ_1 = u \cdot \left(\frac{1}{t+100} \right) = \frac{u}{t+100} \) where \( u = u(t) \) is some unknown function of \(t\)
    Differentiate \( \frac{u}{t+100} \) using the quotient rule
    and then substitute back into the nonhomogeneous equation (4.2.10) and separate variables \begin{align} \frac{dQ}{dt} + \frac{1}{t+100}Q = & 1 \nonumber \\ \Bigg(\frac{1}{t+100}\frac{du}{dt} - \frac{u}{\left(t+100\right)^2} \Bigg) +\frac{1}{t+100} \Bigg( \frac{u}{t+100} \Bigg) = & 1 \nonumber \\ \frac{1}{t+100}\frac{du}{dt} - \frac{u}{\left(t+100\right)^2} +\frac{u}{\left(t+100\right)^2} = & 1 \nonumber \\ \frac{1}{t+100}\frac{du}{dt}= & 1 \nonumber \\ \frac{du}{dt} = & t+100 \nonumber \\ \end{align} Now we find the antiderivative on both sides \begin{align} \int \left( \frac{du}{dt} \right) dt = & \int \overset{v}{\overbrace{\left( t+100 \right)}} \overset{dv}{\overbrace{dt}} \nonumber \\ \int du = & \int v dv \nonumber \\ u = & \frac{1}{2}v^2 + c \nonumber \\ u = & \frac{1}{2}\left( t+100 \right)^2 + c \label{eq:4.2.11} \\ \end{align} Then multiplying both sides by \(Q_1\)
    \begin{align} \underset{Q}{\underbrace{u \cdot \left(\frac{1}{t+100} \right)}} = & \left( \frac{1}{2}\left( t+100 \right)^2 + c \right) \cdot \left(\frac{1}{t+100} \right) \nonumber \\ Q = & \frac{1}{2}\left(\frac{\left( t+100 \right)^2}{t+100} \right) + \frac{c}{t+100} \nonumber \\ Q(t) = & \frac{t+100}{2} + \frac{c}{t+100} \nonumber \end{align}
    Verify \( Q = \frac{t+100}{2} - \frac{c}{t+100}\) is a solution of \( \dot{Q} + \left( \frac{1}{t+100} \right) Q = 1 \)
    Applying our initial condition that the tank initially contains 10 g of salt, i.e. \(Q(0)=10\), we can find \(c\) \begin{align*} Q(t) = & \frac{t+100}{2} + \frac{c}{t+100} \\ Q(0) = & \frac{0+100}{2} + \frac{c}{0+100} \\ 10 = & 50 + \frac{c}{100} \\ -40 = & \frac{c}{100} \Longrightarrow c = -4000 \end{align*} Substituting back in \(c = -4000\), we have an equation that represents the weight of salt at any time \(t\) \begin{equation*} Q = \frac{t+100}{2} - \frac{4000}{t+100} \end{equation*}
    Verify \( Q = \frac{t+100}{2} - \frac{4000}{t+100}\) is a solution of \( \dot{Q} + \left( \frac{1}{t+100} \right) Q = 1 \)
  2. We want to represent the concentration \(K(t)\) (grams/liter) of salt in the tank at any time \(t\). From earlier, we have the equation that represents the volume of solution: \begin{align*} V(t) = & 200 + 2t \\ \end{align*}
    The concentration \(K(t)\) of salt is the weight of salt per unit volume of solution: \begin{align*} K = & \frac{\text{Weight of Salt}}{\text{Volume of Solution}} \\ \\ K = & \frac{Q(t)}{V(t)} \\ \\ K = & \frac{\frac{t+100}{2} - \frac{4000}{t+100}}{2t + 200} \\ K = & \frac{1}{2(t + 100)} \left( \frac{t+100}{2} - \frac{4000}{t+100} \right) \\ K = & \frac{t+100}{2\cdot 2(t + 100)} - \frac{4000}{2(t + 100)(t + 100)} \\ K = & \frac{1}{4} - \frac{2000}{(t + 100)^2} \\ \end{align*}
