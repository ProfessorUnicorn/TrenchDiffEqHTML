10.2 Linear Systems of Differential Equations
A first order system of differential equations that can be written in the form \begin {equation} \label {eq:10.2.1} \begin {array}{ccl} \dfrac{dy_1}{dt} &=&a_{11}(t)y_1+a_{12}(t)y_2+\cdots +a_{1n}(t)y_n+f_1(t)\\ \dfrac{dy_2}{dt} &=&a_{21}(t)y_1+a_{22}(t)y_2+\cdots +a_{2n}(t)y_n+f_2(t)\\ &\vdots \\ \dfrac{dy_n}{dt} & =&a_{n1}(t)y_1+a_{n2}(t)y_2+\cdots +a_{nn}(t)y_n+f_n(t) \end {array} \end {equation} is called a linear system.
The linear system (10.2.1) can be written in matrix form as \[ \col { \dfrac{dy}{dt} }n=\matfunc ann\col yn+\colfunc fn\] or more briefly as \begin {equation} \label {eq:10.2.2} \dfrac{d{\bf \vec{y}}}{dt}={\bf{A}}(t)\, {\bf \vec{y}}+{\bf \vec{f}}(t) \end {equation} where \[ {\bf \vec{y}}=\col yn,\quad {\bf{A}}(t)=\matfunc ann,\quad \mbox{ and } \quad {\bf \vec{f}}(t)=\colfunc fn\] We call \({\bf{A}}(t)\) the coefficient matrix of (10.2.2) and \({\bf \vec{f}}(t)\) the forcing function. Weâ€™ll say that \({\bf{A}}(t)\) and \({\bf \vec{f}}(t)\) are continuous if their entries are continuous. If \({{\bf \vec{f}}(t)}\equiv{\bf \vec{0}}\), then the equation (10.2.2) is homogeneous otherwise it's nonhomogeneous.
An IVP for (10.2.2) consists of finding a solution of (10.2.2) that equals a given constant vector at some initial point \(t_0\) \[ {\bf \vec{k}} =\col kn\] We write this IVP as \[ \dfrac{d{\bf \vec{y}}}{dt}={\bf{A}}(t) \,{\bf \vec{y}}+{\bf \vec{f}}(t) \quad {\bf \vec{y}}(t_0)={\bf \vec{k}}\]
The next theorem gives sufficient conditions for the existence of solutions of IVPs for (10.2.2).
Theorem 10.2.1 Suppose the coefficient matrix \({\bf{A}}(t)\) and the forcing function \({\bf \vec{f}}(t)\) are continuous on \((a,b)\), let \(t_0\) be in \((a,b)\), and let \(\bf \vec{k}\) be an arbitrary constant \(n\)-vector. Then the following IVP has a unique solution on \((a,b)\) \[ \dfrac{d{\bf \vec{y}}}{dt}={\bf{A}}(t)\,{\bf \vec{y}}+{\bf \vec{f}}(t) \quad {\bf \vec{y}}(t_0)={\bf \vec{k}} \]
  1. Write the system in matrix form and conclude from Theorem 10.2.1 that every IVP for (10.2.3) has a unique solution on \((-\infty ,\infty )\) \begin {equation} \label {eq:10.2.3} \begin {array}{rcl} \dfrac{dy_1}{dt}&=&\phantom {2}y_1+2y_2+2e^{4t} \\ \dfrac{dy_2}{dt}&=&2y_1+\phantom {2}y_2+\phantom {2}e^{4t} \end {array} \end {equation}
  2. Verify that (10.2.4) is a solution of (10.2.3) for all values of the constants \(c_1\) and \(c_2\) \begin {equation} \label {eq:10.2.4} {\bf \vec{y}}= {1\over 5}\twocol 87e^{4t}+c_1\twocol 11e^{3t}+c_2\twocol 1{-1}e^{-t} \end {equation}
  3. Find the solution of the IVP \begin {equation} \label {eq:10.2.5} \dfrac{d{\bf \vec{y}}}{dt}=\twobytwo 1221{\bf \vec{y}}+\twocol 21e^{4t}\quad {\bf \vec{y}}(0)={1\over 5}\twocol 3{22} \end {equation}
Solution
  1. The system (10.2.3) can be written in matrix form as \begin {align*} & \left\{ \begin {array}{rcl} \dfrac{dy_1}{dt}&=&\phantom {2}y_1+2y_2+2e^{4t} \\ \dfrac{dy_2}{dt}&=&2y_1+\phantom {2}y_2+\phantom {2}e^{4t} \end {array} \right. \\ \\ & \dfrac{d}{dt}\twocol {y_1}{y_2} =\twobytwo 1221\twocol {y_1}{y_2}+\twocol {2e^{4t}}{e^{4t}} \\ \\ \quad & \quad\quad \dfrac{d{\bf \vec{y}}}{dt} =\twobytwo 1221{\bf \vec{y}}+\twocol 21e^{4t} \end {align*} An IVP for (10.2.3) can be written as \[ \dfrac{d{\bf \vec{y}}}{dt}=\twobytwo 1221{\bf \vec{y}}+\twocol 21e^{4t} \quad y(t_0)=\twocol {k_1}{k_2}\] Since the coefficient matrix and the forcing function are both continuous on \((-\infty ,\infty )\), Theorem 10.2.1 implies that this problem has a unique solution on \((-\infty ,\infty )\).

  2. We can verify (10.2.4) is a solution to the system of differential equations (10.2.3) differentiating and substituting. The left side of the equation is just the derivative so we differentiate: \begin {align*} {\bf \vec{y}} & = {1\over 5}\twocol 87e^{4t}+c_1\twocol 11e^{3t}+c_2\twocol 1{-1}e^{-t} \\ \frac{d{\bf \vec{y}}}{dt} & = \frac{d}{dt} \Bigg[ {1\over 5}\twocol 87e^{4t}+c_1\twocol 11e^{3t}+c_2\twocol 1{-1}e^{-t} \Bigg] \\ & = {1\over 5}\twocol 87 \frac{d}{dt} \big(e^{4t}\big) +c_1\twocol 11\frac{d}{dt} \big(e^{3t}\big)+c_2\twocol 1{-1}\frac{d}{dt} \big(e^{-t}\big) \\ & = {4\over 5}\twocol 87 e^{4t} + 3c_1\twocol 11 e^{3t} -c_2\twocol 1{-1} e^{-t} \\ & = {4\over 5}\twocol 87 e^{4t} + 3c_1\twocol 11 e^{3t} +c_2\twocol {-1}{1} e^{-t} \\ \end {align*} Now we'll substitute (10.2.4) into the right side of the equation (10.2.3) to confirm it matches. \begin {align*} & \twobytwo 1221{\bf \vec{y}}+\twocol 21e^{4t} \\ = & \twobytwo 1221\Bigg[ {1\over 5}\twocol 87e^{4t}+c_1\twocol 11e^{3t}+c_2\twocol 1{-1}e^{-t} \Bigg]+\twocol 21e^{4t} \\ = & \Bigg[ {1\over 5}\twobytwo 1221\twocol 87e^{4t}+c_1\twobytwo 1221\twocol 11e^{3t}+c_2\twobytwo 1221\twocol 1{-1}e^{-t} \Bigg]+\twocol 21e^{4t} \\ = & \Bigg[ {1\over 5}\twocol {8 + 14}{16 + 7}e^{4t}+c_1\twocol {1 + 2 }{2 + 1 }e^{3t}+c_2\twocol {1 - 2 }{2 - 1 }e^{-t} \Bigg]+\twocol 21e^{4t} \\ = & \Bigg[ {1\over 5}\twocol {22}{23}e^{4t}+c_1\twocol {3}{3}e^{3t}+c_2\twocol {-1}{1}e^{-t} \Bigg]+\twocol 21e^{4t} \\ = & \Bigg[ {1\over 5}\twocol {22}{23}e^{4t} +{1\over 5}\twocol {10}{5}e^{4t}\Bigg] +c_1\twocol {3}{3}e^{3t}+c_2\twocol {-1}{1}e^{-t}\\ = & {1\over 5}\twocol {32}{28}e^{4t} +c_1\twocol {3}{3}e^{3t}+c_2\twocol {-1}{1}e^{-t}\\ = & \underset{{\dfrac{d{\bf \vec{y}}}{dt}}}{\underbrace{{4\over 5}\twocol {8}{7}e^{4t} +c_1\twocol {3}{3}e^{3t}+c_2\twocol {-1}{1}e^{-t}}}\\ \end {align*}

  3. We already verified the solution \begin {align*} {\bf \vec{y}} & = {1\over 5}\twocol 87e^{4t}+c_1\twocol 11e^{3t}+c_2\twocol 1{-1}e^{-t} \end {align*} No we'll apply the initial condition \[ {\bf \vec{y}}(0)={1\over 5}\twocol 3{22} \] \begin {align*} {\bf \vec{y}}(0) & = {1\over 5}\twocol 87e^{0}+c_1\twocol 11e^{0}+c_2\twocol 1{-1}e^{0} \\ {1\over 5}\twocol 3{22} & = {1\over 5}\twocol 87+ \twobytwo 111{-1} \twocol {c_1}{c_2} \\ \twocol 3{22} & = \twocol 87+ 5\twobytwo 111{-1} \twocol {c_1}{c_2} \\ \twocol {-5}{15} & = 5\twobytwo 111{-1} \twocol {c_1}{c_2} \\ \twocol {-1}{3} & = \twobytwo 111{-1} \twocol {c_1}{c_2} \\ \end {align*} The system of equations can be written in the form of an augmented matrix and we can solve using row operations \begin {align*} \left( \begin{array}{rr|r} 1 & 1 & -1 \\ 1 & -1 & 3 \\ \end{array} \right) & \overset{R_2-R_1}{ \longrightarrow} \left( \begin{array}{rr|r} 1 & 1 & -1 \\ 0 & -2 & 4 \\ \end{array} \right) \\ & \overset{-\frac{1}{2}R_2}{ \longrightarrow} \left( \begin{array}{rr|r} 1 & 1 & -1 \\ 0 & 1 & -2 \\ \end{array} \right) \\ & \overset{R_1-R_2}{ \longrightarrow} \left( \begin{array}{rr|r} 1 & 0 & 1 \\ 0 & 1 & -2 \\ \end{array} \right) \\ \end {align*} Solving this system yields \(c_1=1\), \(c_2=-2\), and substituting in we obtain the solution of the IVP (10.2.5) \[ {\bf \vec{y}}={1\over 5}\twocol 87e^{4t}+\twocol 11e^{3t}-2\twocol 1{-1}e^{-t} \]
Remark: The theory of \(n\times n\) linear systems of differential equations is analogous to the theory of the scalar \(n\)-th order equation \begin {equation} \label {eq:10.2.6} P_0(t)\frac{d^n y}{dt^n} + P_1(t) \frac{d^{n-1} y}{dt^{n-1}} + \cdots + P_{n-1}(t)\frac{dy}{dt} + P_n(t)y=F(t) \end {equation} as developed in Sections 9.1. For example, by rewriting (10.2.6) as an equivalent linear system it can be shown that Theorem 10.2.1 implies Theorem 9.1.1
