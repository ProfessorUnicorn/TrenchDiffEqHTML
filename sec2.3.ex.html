Part 2: IVPs
17.
Consider the IVP \[ \frac{dy}{dx} = 3x(y-1)^{1/3} \quad y(x_0) = y_0 {\tag A} \]
  1. For what points \((x_0,y_0)\) does Theorem  2.3.1 imply that (A) has a solution?
  2. For what points \((x_0,y_0)\) does Theorem  2.3.1 imply that (A) has a unique solution on some open interval that contains \(x_0\)?
18.
Find nine solutions of the IVP \[ \frac{dy}{dx} = 3x(y-1)^{1/3} \quad y(0)=1 \] that are all defined on \((-\infty ,\infty )\) and differ from each other for values of \(x\) in every open interval that contains \(x_0=0\).
19.
From Theorem 2.3.1, the IVP \[ \frac{dy}{dx} = 3x(y-1)^{1/3} \quad y(0)=9 \] has a unique solution on an open interval that contains \(x_0=0\). Find the solution and determine the largest open interval on which it’s unique.
20.
  1. From Theorem 2.3.1, the IVP \[ \frac{dy}{dx} = 3x(y-1)^{1/3} \quad y(3)=-7 {\tag A} \] has a unique solution on some open interval that contains \(x_0=3\). Determine the largest such open interval, and find the solution on this interval.
  2. Find infinitely many solutions of (A) all defined on \((-\infty ,\infty )\)
21.
Prove:
  1. If \[ f(x,y_0) = 0,\quad a<x<b, {\tag A} \] and \(x_0\) is in \((a,b)\), then \(y\equiv y_0\) is a solution of \[ \frac{dy}{dx} = f(x,y) \quad y(x_0)=y_0 \] on \((a,b)\)
  2. If \(f\) and \(f_y\) are continuous on an open rectangle that contains \((x_0,y_0)\) and (A) holds, no solution of \( \displaystyle \frac{dy}{dx} = f(x,y)\) other than \(y\equiv y_0\) can equal \(y_0\) at any point in \((a,b)\)
