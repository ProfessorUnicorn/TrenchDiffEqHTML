2.1 Linear First Order Equations
Linear Standard Form
A first order differential equation is said to be linear if it can be written in standard form as
\begin {equation} \label {eq:2.1.1} \frac{dy}{dx} + p(x)y = f(x) \end {equation} Any other first order differential equation that can’t be written in this form is nonlinear.
We say that a first order linear equation (2.1.1) is homogeneous if \(f(x) \equiv 0\): \[ \frac{dy}{dx} + p(x)y = 0\] Otherwise for any \( f(x) \not\equiv 0 \) (2.1.1) is nonhomogeneous.
Since \(y(x) \equiv 0\) is obviously a solution of the homgeneous equation we call it the trivial solution. \[ \frac{d}{dx}\Big(0\Big)+p(x)\cdot\Big(0\Big) = 0 \quad \checkmark \] Any other solution is nontrivial.
Rewrite the linear first order differential equations in standard form, idenitfy \( p(x) \) and \( f(x) \), and state whether the equation is homogeneous or nonhomogeneous
\begin {eqnarray*} x^2 \frac{dy}{dx} + 3y& = &x^2 \\ x \frac{dy}{dx} -8x^2y& = &\sin x \\ x \frac{dy}{dx} + (\ln x)y& = &0 \\ \frac{dy}{dx} & = &x^2y - 2 \end {eqnarray*}
Solution
Rearranging the equations into standard form (2.1.1), we can readily identify \( p(x) \) and \( f(x)\): \begin{align*} \frac{dy}{dx} + \overset{p(x)=\frac{3}{x^2}}{\overbrace{\frac{3}{x^2} }}y = & \overset{f(x)=1}{\overbrace{1}} \\ \\ \frac{dy}{dx} - \overset{p(x)=-8x}{\overbrace{8x }}y = & \overset{f(x)=\frac{\sin x}{x}}{\overbrace{\frac{\sin x}{x}}} \\ \\ \frac{dy}{dx} + \overset{p(x)=\frac{1}{x}\ln x}{\overbrace{\frac{1}{x}\ln x }}y = & \overset{f(x)=0}{\overbrace{0}} \\ \\ \frac{dy}{dx} - \overset{p(x)=-x^2}{\overbrace{x^2 }}y = & \overset{f(x)=-2}{\overbrace{-2}} \\ \\ \end{align*} The third equation \( \displaystyle \frac{dy}{dx} + \frac{1}{x}\ln x = 0 \) is homogeneous since \(f(x) \equiv 0\) and all others are nonhomogeneous
Rewrite \( 5 \frac{dy}{dx} = 10\frac{y}{x} - 7x^{12} \) in standard form (2.1.1) and identify \( p(x) \) and \( f(x)\)
Rewrite \( 2x \frac{dy}{dx} - 4x^3y + e^x = 6e^x \) in standard form (2.1.1) and identify \( p(x) \) and \( f(x)\)

The following equations cannot be written in this form. \begin{align*} x\frac{dy}{dx} + 3y^2 = 2x & \quad \text{is nonlinear as $y$ is squared} \\ y\frac{dy}{dx} = 3 & \quad \text{is nonlinear as there is a product of $y$ and its derivative $\frac{dy}{dx}$} \\ \frac{dy}{dx} + xe^y = 12 & \quad \text{is nonlinear as $e^y$ is a non-linear function of $y$} \\ \end{align*}

Intervals of Validity of One–Parameter Families
Consider the simple linear first order equation: \begin {equation} \label {eq:2.1.2} \frac{dy}{dx} = {1\over x^2} \end {equation}
Show the work to find the antiderivative of (2.1.2) using integral notation
Since (2.1.2) is integrable we can immediately find the antiderivative \begin {equation} \label {eq:2.1.3} y = -{1\over x} + c \end {equation}
We call the arbitrary constant of integration \(c\) a parameter and we say that (2.1.3) defines a one–parameter family of functions. Since (2.1.3) is undefined at \( x=0 \) then the only open intervals where the solution is valid are \( (-\infty,0) \) and \( (0,\infty). \) Every function defined by (2.1.3) is a solution of (2.1.2) and valid on the intervals \( (-\infty,0) \) and \( (0,\infty) \) and in this case where every solution of (2.1.2) is of the form of (2.1.3) for some choice of \(c\), we say that (2.1.3) is the general solution. (When there are other solutions to the differential equation that are not part of the one-parameter family, then those solutions are called singular solutions.)
Although (2.1.2) was a simple differential equation, the same is true for all first order linear differential equations \begin {equation} \label {eq:2.1.4} \frac{dy}{dx} + p(x)y = f(x) \end {equation} That is, if \(p(x)\) and \(f(x)\) are continuous on some open interval \((a,b)\) then there’s a unique formula \(y = y(x,c)\) analogous to (2.1.3) that defines a one–parameter family of functions as a function of \(x\) and a parameter \(c\) that has the these properties:
  • For each value of \(c\) in the open interval \((a,b),\) the resulting function \( y=y(x) \) is a solution of (2.1.4) on \((a,b),\) referred to as the interval of validity (IOV)
  • If \( y = y(x) \) is a solution of (2.1.4) on \((a,b)\) then it can be obtained from the equation representing the one-parameter family by choosing \( c \) appropriately
  • We call \( y = y(x,c) \) the general solution and for a particular value of \( c \) substituted in then \( y=y(x) \) is a particular solution
So far we have only considered first order linear differential equations in the form: \begin{equation*} \frac{dy}{dx} + p(x)y = f(x) \end{equation*} However, first order linear differential equations could also be of the form: \begin {equation} \label {eq:2.1.5} P_0(x) \frac{dy}{dx} + P_1(x)y = F(x) \end{equation} which has a general solution provided \(P_0(x)\), \(P_1(x)\), and \(F(x)\) are all continuous on an open interval \((a,b)\) on which and \(P_0(x) \) has no zeros, since in this case we can rewrite it in the form of (2.1.4) by dividing by \( P_0(x) \): \begin {equation*} \frac{dy}{dx} + \underset{p(x)}{\underbrace{\frac{P_1(x)}{P_0(x)}}}y = \underset{f(x)}{\underbrace{\frac{F(x)}{P_0(x)}}} \end{equation*} However, take note of the fact that the IOVs might be impacted when dividing if \( P_0(x) \) has any zero in the open interval (Exercise 44 for an example of this), and whenever we find a solution, we should include every open interval for which the solution is defined.
Homogeneous Linear First Order Equations
We begin with the problem of finding the general solution of a homogeneous linear first order equation. The next example recalls a familiar result from calculus.
  1. Let \(a\) be a constant. Find the general solution: \begin {equation} \label{eq:2.1.6} \frac{dy}{dx} - ay = 0 \end {equation}
  2. Solve the initial value problem \[ \frac{dy}{dx} -ay = 0 \quad y(x_0) = y_0\]
Solution
  1. First notice (2.1.6) has a trivial solution \( y(x) \equiv 0 \) which we can verify by substitution: \begin{align*} \frac{dy}{dx} - ay = & 0 \\ \frac{d}{dx} \Big( 0 \Big) - a \Big( 0 \Big) = & 0 \quad \checkmark \\ \end{align*}
    Now we seek nontrivial solution where \( y \ne 0 \) by rearranging the equation: \begin{align*} \frac{dy}{dx} - ay = & 0 \\ \frac{dy}{dx} = & ay \\ \frac{1}{y}\frac{dy}{dx} = & a \\ \int \Bigg( \frac{1}{y}\frac{dy}{dx} \Bigg) dx = & \int \Big( a \Big) dx \\ \int \frac{1}{y} dy = & a\int dx \\ \end{align*}
    Notice how the equation has the variables separated so that the left side has only \( y \) and the right side has only \( x \). This strategy is called separation of variables. Now find the antiderivative on both sides. Technically each side has an arbitrary constant but we combine them into one constant \( k \). \begin{align*} \int \frac{1}{y} dy = & a\int dx \\ \ln \left| y \right| = & ax + k \end{align*}
    Solving for \( y \) requires exponeniating both sides resulting in \( e^k \) in the expression, which is just another arbitrary constant so we can simplify it to just \( K \) \begin{align*} \ln \left| y \right| = & ax + k \\ e^{\ln \left| y \right|} = & e^{ax + k} \\ \left| y \right| = & e^{ax} \underset{c}{\underbrace{e^{k}}} \\ \left| y \right| = & ce^{ax} \\ \end{align*} The function \( e^{ax} \) is exponential with horizontal asymptote at \(y=0\) so for a given and \(a\) the function is always positive or always negative, so we can further simplify our result to \begin {equation} \label {eq:2.1.7} y = ce^{ax} \end{equation}
    This shows us that every nontrivial solution of (2.1.6) is of the form of (2.1.7) for some nonzero constant \(c\) and since selecting \(c=0\) yields the trivial solution, then all solutions of (2.1.6), both trivial and nontrivial, are of the form of (2.1.7), meaning it is our general solution.
    Differentiate (2.1.7) with respect to \( x \)
    Differentiating (2.1.7) we obtain \begin{align*} \frac{dy}{dx} = a ce^{ax} \end{align*}
    Substituting into (2.1.6) we obtain \begin{align*} \frac{dy}{dx} - ay = & 0 \\ \overset{\frac{dy}{dx}}{\overbrace{ a ce^{ax} }} - a \overset{y}{\overbrace{\left(ce^{ax}\right)}} \overset{?}{=} & 0 \\ 0 = & 0 \quad \checkmark \end{align*}
  2. We already have the general solution (2.1.7) and we evaluate it at our initial condition \(y(x_0) = y_0\) and solve for the constant \begin{align*} y(x) = & ce^{ax} \\ \underset{y_0}{\underbrace{y(x_0)}} = & ce^{ax_0} \\ y_0 = & ce^{ax_0} \Longrightarrow c = \frac{y_0}{e^{ax_0}} = y_0 e^{-ax_0} \\ \end{align*}
    Substitute \( c = y_0 e^{-ax_0} \) into (2.1.7) and simplify
    Substituting \( c = y_0 e^{-ax_0} \) and simplifying yields the particular solution of our IVP \begin{align*} y = & y_0 e^{a\left(x-x_0\right)} \\ \end{align*} Which is defined on all \(x\) so the IOV is \((-\infty,\infty)\)
    Verify \( y = y_0 e^{a\left(x-x_0\right)} \) is a solution of (2.1.6) by differentiating and substituting

  1. Find the general solution of \begin {equation} \label{eq:2.1.8} x \frac{dy}{dx} + y = 0 \end {equation}
  2. Solve the initial value problem \begin {equation} \label {eq:2.1.9} x \frac{dy}{dx} + y = 0\quad y(1) = 3 \end {equation}
Solution
  1. First notice (2.1.8) has a trivial solution \( y(x) \equiv 0 \) which we can verify by substitution: \begin{align*} x\frac{dy}{dx} + y = & 0 \\ x\frac{d}{dx} \Big( 0 \Big) + \Big( 0 \Big) = & 0 \quad \checkmark \\ \end{align*}
    Next divide (2.1.8) by \( x \) to rewrite in standard form (2.1.1) \begin {equation} \label {eq:2.1.10} \frac{dy}{dx} + \frac{1}{x} y = 0 \end {equation} Now \( x \ne 0 \) since we divided by it, that limits our intervals to either \((-\infty ,0)\) or \((0,\infty )\)
    Since we have already accounted for the trivial solution, we now seek nontrivial solution where \( y \ne 0 \) by rearranging the equation using separation of variables which requires dividing by \( y \) which is fine in this case since we have already assumed it is not zero \begin{align*} \frac{dy}{dx} + \frac{1}{x}y = & 0 \nonumber \\ \frac{dy}{dx} = & -\frac{1}{x}y \nonumber \\ \frac{1}{y}\frac{dy}{dx} = & -\frac{1}{x} \nonumber \\ \int \Bigg( \frac{1}{y}\frac{dy}{dx} \Bigg) dx = & \int \Bigg( -\frac{1}{x} \Bigg) dx \nonumber \\ \int \frac{1}{y} dy = & - \int \frac{1}{x} dx \nonumber \\ \end{align*} Finding the antiderivatives and simplifying, \begin{align*} \ln \left| y \right| = & - \ln \left| x \right| + k \nonumber \\ \ln \left| y \right| + \ln \left| x \right| = & k \nonumber \\ \ln \Big( \left| yx \right| \Big) = & k \nonumber \\ e^{\ln \big( \left| yx \right| \big)} = & \underset{c}{\underbrace{e^{k}}} \nonumber \\ \left| yx \right| = & c \nonumber \\ \left| y \right| = & \frac{c}{\left| x \right|} \nonumber \\ \end{align*}
    Since \( x \) is never zero on our intervals \((-\infty ,0)\) and \((0,\infty )\), and \( y \) is never zero since we assumed it, this expression is either always positive or always negative so we can further simplify \begin {equation} \label {eq:2.1.11} y = \frac{c}{x} \end{equation}
    This shows us that every nontrivial solution of (2.1.10) is of the form of (2.1.11) for some nonzero constant \(c\) and since selecting \(c=0\) yields the trivial solution, then all solutions of (2.1.10), both trivial and nontrivial, are of the form of (2.1.11), meaning it is our general solution.
    Additionally, we can check our answer simplify by differentiating \begin{align*} \frac{dy}{dx} = & \frac{d}{dx} \Bigg( \frac{c}{x} \Bigg) = c\frac{d}{dx} \Big( x^{-1} \Big) = -cx^{-2} = -\frac{c}{x^2} \\ \end{align*} And substituting the general solution (2.1.11) and its derivative into (2.1.10). \begin{align*} \frac{dy}{dx} + \frac{1}{x} y = & 0 \\ -\frac{c}{x^2} + \frac{1}{x} \Bigg( \frac{c}{x} \Bigg) \overset{?}{=} & 0 \\ -\frac{c}{x^2} + \frac{c}{x^2} = & 0 \quad \checkmark \end{align*}
  2. We already have the general solution (2.1.11) and we evaluate it at our initial condition \(y(1) = 3\) to solve for the constant \begin{align*} y(x) = & \frac{c}{x} \\ \underset{3}{\underbrace{y(1)}} = & \frac{c}{1} \\ c = & 3 \\ \end{align*} Substituting \( c = 3 \) into (2.1.11) yields the particular solution of our IVP (2.1.9) \begin{align*} y = \frac{3}{x} \\ \end{align*} With \( x = 1 \) as our initial value then the interval of validity (IOV) for this solution is \((0,\infty )\) since it is the largest interval on which (2.1.11) is defined that contains our initial \( x \) value
    Verify \( y = 3/x \) is a solution of (2.1.8) by differentiating and substituting

A note on simplifying arbitrary constants. As a reminder, they are arbitrary, and every opportunity where we can simplify them, we should. It is common to use \( c, C, k, K \) to represent arbitrary constants but other letters may be used and subscripts may be used, such as \( k_1, k_2, k_3, \dots \)
We have made efforts to distinguish between one constant and another, such as replacing \(e^k\) with \(c\), but this becomes tedious, and once you are more practiced in the simplifying of arbitrary constants it is common to just simplify them to the same letter, so instance, replacing \(e^k\) with \(k\) as acceptable. In general, we will try to stick to using \( c \) for the arbitrary constant in the final simplified general solution.

Nonhomogeneous Linear First Order Equations
In Examples 2.1.3 and 2.1.4, we rewrote homogeneous linear equations so that one side depended on only \( y \) and its derivative and the other side only depended on \(x\) and then found the antiderivative on both sides. This method is called separation of variables and can be used to solve all homogeneous linear equations (2.1.2) but now we would like to solve nonhomogeneous equations: \begin {equation} \label{eq:2.1.15} \frac{dy}{dx} + p(x)y = f(x) \end {equation} To solve nonhomogeneous equations (2.1.15) we will first solve the corresponding homogeneous equation, which we call the complementary equation \[ \frac{dy}{dx} + p(x)y = 0 \] We then use a method called variation of parameters to seek solutions of the form \( y = u y_1 \) where \( y_1 = y_1 (x) \) is a nontrivial solution of the complementary equation and \( u = u(x) \) is some function of \( x \) to be determined. Since \( y_1 \) is a solution of the complementary equation, then we know and will use the fact that \begin{equation} \label{eq:2.1.16} \frac{dy_1}{dx} + p(x)y_1 = 0 \end{equation}
To implement variation of parameters, we will differentiate \( y = uy_1 \) and then substitutute back into our nonhomogeneous equation (2.1.15) to solve. We differente using the product rule to get an expression for the derivative in terms of \( u \) and \( y_1 \): \begin{align*} \frac{dy}{dx} = & \frac{d}{dx} \Big( u \cdot y_1 \Big) = y_1\frac{du}{dx} + u\frac{dy_1}{dx} \end{align*}
Substituting \( y = uy_1 \) and its derivative into (2.1.15) then simplifying the equation it transforms into an integrable equation (2.1.17): \begin{align} \label{eq:2.1.17} \frac{dy}{dx} + p(x)y = & f(x) \nonumber \\ \overset{\frac{dy}{dx}}{\overbrace{y_1\frac{du}{dx} + u\frac{dy_1}{dx}}} + p(x) \overset{y}{\overbrace{u \cdot y_1}} = & f(x) \nonumber \\ y_1\frac{du}{dx} + u \underset{0}{ \underbrace{ \left( \frac{dy_1}{dx} + p(x)y_1 \right)} } = & f(x) \nonumber \\ y_1\frac{du}{dx} + u \cdot 0 = & f(x) \nonumber \\ y_1\frac{du}{dx} = & f(x) \nonumber \\ \frac{du}{dx} = & \frac{f(x)}{y_1(x)} \end{align}
Now we simply find the antiderivative to obtain \( u \), which introduces a arbitrary constant of integration. Finally, multiply the result \( u \) by \(y_1\) to get \( y = uy_1 \): \begin{align*} \frac{du}{dx} = & \frac{f(x)}{y_1(x)} \\ u = & c + \int \frac{f(x)}{y_1(x)} dx \\ y = & y_1 \left( c + \int \frac{f(x)}{y_1(x)} dx \right) \\ \end{align*}
  1. Find the general solution \begin {equation} \label {eq:2.1.18} \frac{dy}{dx} + 2y = x^3e^{-2x} \end {equation}
  2. Solve the IVP \begin {equation*} \frac{dy}{dx} + 2y = x^3e^{-2x} \quad\quad y(0)=\frac{3}{4} \end {equation*}
Solution
  1. To solve the nonhomogeneous (2.1.18), we will always first solve the complementary homogeneous equation: \begin{align*} \frac{dy}{dx} + 2y = & 0 \end{align*}
    Solve \( \frac{dy}{dx} + 2y = 0 \) using separation of variables as in Examples 2.1.3 and 2.1.4
    Using separation of variables we find \( y = ce^{-2x} \) as the general solution of the complementary equation. Now when using variation of parameters, we will select \( y_1 \) as any simple particular solution of the complementary equation, so we choose \( c = 1 \) and use \( y_1 = e^{-2x} \) to seek solutions of (2.1.18) of the form: \[ y = u \cdot y_1 = u \cdot e^{-2x} \]
    Differentiate \( u \cdot e^{-2x} \) with respect to \( x \) using the product and chain rules
    Differentiating using the product rule and chain rule yields \begin{align*} \frac{dy}{dx} = & \left( \frac{du}{dx} - 2 u \right) e^{-2x} \\ \end{align*}
    We now substitute this into (2.1.18) and simplify the equation which transforms it into an integrable equation \begin{align}\label{eq:2.1.19} \frac{dy}{dx} + 2y = & x^3 e^{-2x} \nonumber \\ \overset{\frac{dy}{dx}}{\overbrace{ \left( \frac{du}{dx} - 2 u \right) e^{-2x} }} + 2\overset{y}{\overbrace{ue^{-2x}}} = & x^3 e^{-2x} \nonumber \\ \frac{du}{dx}e^{-2x} \underset{0}{\underbrace{- 2 u e^{-2x} + 2ue^{-2x}}} = & x^3 e^{-2x} \nonumber \\ \frac{du}{dx}e^{-2x} = & x^3 e^{-2x} \nonumber \\ \frac{du}{dx} = & x^3 \end{align} Now we simply find the antiderivative of (2.1.19) to obtain \( u \) \begin{align*} \int du = & \int x^3 dx \Longrightarrow u = k + \frac{1}{4} x^4 \\ \end{align*} Then multiply the result by \(y_1 = e^{-2x} \) to get the general solution of (2.1.18) \begin{align*} y = ue^{-2x} = & \left( k + \frac{1}{4} x^4 \right)e^{-2x} \\ \end{align*} Now we can simplify further by factoring out the \( 1/4 \) and simplifying our constant \begin{align*} y = \left( k + \frac{1}{4} x^4 \right)e^{-2x} = & \frac{1}{4} \left( \underset{c}{\underbrace{4k}} + x^4 \right)e^{-2x} \\ \end{align*} Obtaining our final simplified particular solution \begin{align*} y = & \frac{1}{4} \left( c + x^4 \right)e^{-2x} \\ \end{align*}
    Verify \( y = \frac{1}{4} \left( c + x^4 \right)e^{-2x} \) is a solution of (2.1.18) by differentiating and substituting
  2. We already have the general solution and just evaluate it at our initial condition \( y(0)= 3/4 \) \begin{align*} y(x) = & \frac{1}{4} \left( c + x^4 \right)e^{-2x}\\ y(0) = & \frac{1}{4} \left( c + 0^4 \right)e^{0}\\ 12 = & \frac{1}{4} c \Longrightarrow c = 3 \\ \end{align*} Substituting it back in we obtain our particular solution \begin{align*} y = \frac{1}{4} \left( 3 + x^4 \right)e^{-2x}\\ \end{align*} Which is defined for all \( x \) so the IOV is \((-\infty ,\infty)\)
    Verify \( y = \frac{1}{4} \left( 3 + x^4 \right)e^{-2x} \) is a solution of (2.1.18) by differentiating and substituting

    Differentiate \( \frac{u}{\sin x} \) with respect to \( x \) using the quotient rule
    Differentiating \( y = \frac{u}{\sin x} \) using the quotient rule gives us: \begin{align} \label{eq:2.1.23} \frac{dy}{dx} = & \frac{1}{\sin^2 x} \left( \sin x \frac{du}{dx} - u \cos x\right) \\ \end{align}
    We now substitute into (2.1.20) and simplify the equation which transforms it into an integrable equation \begin{align}\label{eq:2.1.24} \frac{dy}{dx} + 2y = & x^3 e^{-2x} \nonumber \\ \overset{ \frac{dy}{dx} }{\overbrace{ \frac{1}{\sin^2 x} \left( \sin x \frac{du}{dx} - u \cos x\right) }} + \left( \frac{\cos x}{\sin x} \right) \overset{y}{\overbrace{ \frac{u}{\sin x} }} = & x \csc x \nonumber \\ \frac{u}{\sin x}\frac{du}{dx} - \underset{0}{\underbrace{ \frac{u \cos x}{\sin^2 x} + \frac{u \cos x}{\sin^2 x} }} = & x \csc x \nonumber \\ \frac{u}{\sin x}\frac{du}{dx} = & \frac{x}{\sin x} \nonumber \\ \frac{du}{dx} = & x \end{align} Now we simply find the antiderivative of (2.1.24) to obtain \( u \): \begin{align*} \int du = & \int x dx \Longrightarrow u = k + \frac{1}{2} x^2 \\ \end{align*} Then multiply the result by \(y_1 = \displaystyle \frac{1}{\sin x} = \csc x \) to get the general solution of (2.1.20) \begin{align} \label{eq:2.1.25} y = \frac{u}{\sin x} = u \csc x = & \left( k + \frac{1}{2} x^2 \right) \csc x = \frac{1}{2} \left( c + x^2 \right) \csc x \\ \end{align} with which is defined on open intervals \( \Big(k\pi,(k+1)\pi \Big) \) where \( k \) is any integer.
    Verify \( y = \frac{1}{2} \left( c + x^2 \right) \csc x \) is a solution of (2.1.20) by differentiating and substituting
  2. We already found the general solution (2.1.25) now we evaluate it at our initial condition \( y\left( \frac{\pi}{2} \right) = 1\) to solve for the constant \( c \) \begin{align*} y(x) =& \frac{1}{2} \left( c + x^2 \right) \csc x \\ \underset{1}{\underbrace{ y\left(\frac{\pi}{2}\right) }} = & \frac{1}{2} \Big( c + \underset{\frac{\pi^2}{4}}{\underbrace{ \left( \frac{\pi}{2} \right)^2}} \Big) \underset{\frac{1}{\sin \left( \frac{\pi}{2} \right) }=1}{\underbrace{\csc \left( \frac{\pi}{2} \right)}} \\ 1 = & \frac{1}{2} \left(c + \frac{\pi^2}{4} \right) \\ 2 = & c + \frac{\pi^2}{4} \Longrightarrow c = 2 - \frac{\pi^2}{4} \\ \end{align*}
    Substituting \( c = \frac{8-\pi^2}{8} \) yields the particular solution of our IVP (2.1.21) \begin{align*} y(x) = \frac{1}{2} \left( 2 - \frac{\pi^2}{4} + x^2 \right) \csc x = \frac{1}{8} \left( 8 - \pi^2 + 4x^2 \right) \csc x \\ \end{align*} Which is defined on open intervals \( \Big(k\pi,(k+1)\pi \Big) \)
    Since our initial condition is \( x = \frac{\pi}{2} \) then the IOV is \((0,\pi )\)
    Verify \( y = \frac{1}{8} \left( 8 - \pi^2 + 4x^2 \right) \csc x \) is a solution of (2.1.20) by differentiating and substituting

Variation of Parameters for First Order Linear Equations \begin {equation} \label {eq:2.1.26} \frac{dy}{dx} + p(x)y = f(x) \end {equation}
Summary of the Steps To Solve (2.1.26)
  1. Solve the complementary homogeneous equation \( \frac{dy}{dx} + p(x)y = 0 \) and select a simple particular solution as \( y_1 \)
  2. Express \( y \) as a product \( y = u \cdot y_1 \) and calculate its derivative \( \frac{d}{dx} \Big( u \cdot y_1 \Big) \) using product, quotient, and/or chain rules
  3. Substitute \( uy_1 \) and \( \frac{d}{dx} \Big( u \cdot y_1 \Big) \) into the nonhomogeneous (2.1.26) and simplify to transform into an integrable equation
  4. Find the antiderivative \( u \) including the constant of integration, and multiply result by \( y_1 \) to get \( y = uy_1 \)
Solutions in Integral Form
Sometimes the integrals that arise in solving a linear first order equation can’t be evaluated in terms of elementary functions. In this case the solution must be left in terms of an integral.
  1. Find the general solution of \begin {equation} \label {eq:2.1.27} \frac{dy}{dx} -2xy = 1 \end {equation}
  2. Solve the initial value problem \begin {equation} \label {eq:2.1.28} \frac{dy}{dx} - 2xy = 1 \quad y(0) = y_0 \end {equation}
Solution
  1. 1. Solve the complementary homogeneous equation \( \displaystyle \frac{dy}{dx} + p(x)y = 0 \) and select a simple particular solution as \( y_1 \)
    To solve the nonhomogeneous (2.1.27), we will always first solve the complementary homogeneous equation: \begin{align*} \frac{dy}{dx} - 2xy = & 0 \end{align*}
    Solve \( \frac{dy}{dx} - 2xy = 0 \) using separation of variables as in Examples 2.1.3 and 2.1.4
    Using separation of variables we find \( y = ce^{x^2} \) as the general solution of the complementary equation. Now we select \( y_1 \) as any simple particular solution of the complementary equation, so we choose \( c = 1 \) and use \[ y_1 = e^{x^2} \]
    2. Express \( y \) as a product \( y = u \cdot y_1 \) and calculate its derivative \( \frac{d}{dx} \Big( u \cdot y_1 \Big) \) using product, quotient, and/or chain rules
    We seek solutions of (2.1.18) of the form: \[ y = u \cdot y_1 = u \cdot e^{x^2} \]
    Differentiate \( u \cdot e^{x^2} \) with respect to \( x \) using the product and chain rules
    Differentiating \( y = u \cdot e^{x^2} \) using the product rule and chain rule gives us: \begin{align*} \frac{dy}{dx} = & \left( \frac{du}{dx} + 2 x u \right) e^{x^2} \\ \end{align*}
    3. Substitute \( uy_1 \) and \( \frac{d}{dx} \Big( u \cdot y_1 \Big) \) into the nonhomogeneous and simplify to transform into an integrable equation
    \begin{align*} \frac{dy}{dx} - 2 x y = & 1 \nonumber \\ \overset{\frac{dy}{dx}}{\overbrace{ \left( \frac{du}{dx} + 2 x u \right) e^{x^2} }} - 2x\overset{y}{\overbrace{ue^{x^2}}} = & 1 \nonumber \\ \frac{du}{dx}e^{x^2} - \underset{0}{\underbrace{ 2 x u e^{x^2} + 2xue^{x^2}}} = & 1 \nonumber \\ \frac{du}{dx}e^{x^2} = & 1 \nonumber \\ \frac{du}{dx} = & e^{-x^2} \end{align*}
    4. Find the antiderivative \( u \) including the constant of integration, and multiply result by \( y_1 \) to get \( y = uy_1 \)
    Normally we simplfy find the antiderivative of \( \displaystyle \frac{du}{dx} \) but this time it is a little tricky \begin{align*} u = \int du = & \int e^{-x^2} dx = ? \end{align*} There are no techniques we have from calculus to find the antiderivative of this function and in fact, it cannot even be expressed in terms of the elementary functions we have studied in math classes. This is no problem though, because using Fundamental Theorem of Calculus we can actually write any antiderivative as a definite integral \begin{align*} u = & \int e^{-x^2} dx =c + \int_{x_0}^x e^{-t^2} dt =c + \int_{0}^x e^{-t^2} dt \end{align*} where \( x_0 \) is some initial \(x-\)value which we can choose conveniently to evaluate the definite integral to zero in the case of an IVP, so we use \(x_0 = 0\) from (2.1.28) in this case. We explicitly write out the "\( + c \)" here in front of the integral to make it more clear that we are "done" simplifying the integral for now as it will stay in this form for our general solution.
    Differentiate \( u = c + \displaystyle \int_{0}^x e^{-t^2} dt\) using the Fundamental Theorem of Calculus to verify \( \displaystyle \frac{du}{dx} = e^{-x^2} \)
    Then multiply the result by \(y_1 = e^{x^2} \) to get the general solution \( y \) of (2.1.27) \begin{align} \label {eq:2.1.29} y = ue^{x^2} = & e^{x^2}\left( c + \int_{0}^x e^{-t^2} dt \right) \\ \end{align}
    Verify (2.1.29) is a solution of (2.1.27) by differentiating and substituting
  2. We already found the general solution (2.1.29) now we evaluate it at our initial condition \( y(0) = y_0 \) to solve for the constant \( c \) \begin{align*} y(x) = & e^{x^2}\left( c + \int_{0}^x e^{-t^2} dt \right) \\ \underset{y_0}{\underbrace{y(0)}} = & e^{\left(0^2\right)}\Bigg( c + \underset{0}{\underbrace{\int_{0}^0 e^{-t^2} dt}} \Bigg) \\ y_0 = & 1 \left( c + 0 \right) = c \end{align*} Substituting \( c = y_0 \) yields the particular solution of our IVP (2.1.28): \begin{align} \label {eq:2.1.30} y(x) = & e^{x^2}\left( y_0 + \int_{0}^x e^{-t^2} dt \right) \end{align}
Figure 2.1.5 shows graphs of (2.1.30) for several values of \(y_0\).
A note about solutions in the form of an integral. Comparing to our experience in calculus, a general solution in the form of an integral (2.1.30) may seem incomplete, almost like we still have more steps to do; while this may have been true in calculus class, it is important to realize that a solution in the form or a definite integral is perfectly valid and can be easily analyzed using numerical methods. For instance, for any specific value of \( x \) this definite integral will evaluate to a number \( y(x) \) forming an ordered pair \( \big( x, y(x) \big) \) and we could always use a calculator or spreadsheet to make a table of those ordered pairs and simply plot the points on a graph which would be a graph of the solution. While we won’t be studying numerical methods in this class, hopefully you could imagine someone doing so and can be okay with sometimes having answers in the form of an integral.

Existence and Uniqueness
We already know from Theorem 2.1.1 that there exists a unique solution (2.1.13) for every homogeneous first order linear differential equation (2.1.12). Theorem 2.1.2 completes this section with the guarantees the existence and uniqueness of solutions for every nonhomogeneous first order linear differential equations.
Theorem 2.1.2 Suppose \(p(x)\) and \(f(x)\) are continuous on an open interval \((a,b)\) and let \(y_1 = y_1(x) \) be any nontrivial particular solution of the complementary homogeneous equation. Then:
  1. The general solution of the nonhomogeneous (2.1.31) on \((a,b)\) is given by (2.1.32) \begin {equation} \label {eq:2.1.31} \frac{dy}{dx} + p(x)y = f(x) \end {equation} \begin {equation} \label {eq:2.1.32} y = y_1 \left (c + \int \frac{f(x)}{y_1(x)} dx \right ) \end {equation}
  2. If \(x_0\) is an arbitrary point in \((a,b)\) and \(y_0 = y(x_0)\) is an arbitrary real number, then the IVP (2.1.33) has the unique solution (2.1.34) on \((a,b)\) \begin {equation} \label {eq:2.1.33} \frac{dy}{dx} + p(x)y = f(x) \quad y(x_0) = y_0 \end {equation} \begin {equation} \label {eq:2.1.34} y = y_1 \left( {y_0\over y_1(x_0)} + \int^x_{x_0} {f(t)\over{y_1(t)}} dt \right) \end {equation}
Use the Fundamental Theorem of Calculus to differentiate (2.1.32) and use the solution to the complementary equation \( y_1 \) to substitute and show that (2.1.32) is a solution of (2.1.31)
Differentiate \( u = \frac{y}{y_1} \) using the quotient rule and use the solution to the complementary equation \( y_1 \) and (2.1.31) to substitute to show that \( \frac{du}{dx} = \frac{f(x)}{y_1(x)} \)
Evaluate (2.1.34) at \( x = x_0 \) to verify \( y( x_0 ) = y_0 \). Next evaluate (2.1.32) at \( x = x_0 \) and solve for \( c \) to show that \( c = \frac{y(x_0)}{y_1(x_0)} \)
