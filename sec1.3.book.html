1.3 Direction Fields for First Order Equations
Direction Fields
It is impossible to find explicit formulas for solutions of many differential equations. Even if there are formulas, they may be so complicated that they’re useless. In this case we may resort to graphical or numerical methods to get some idea of how the solutions of the given equation behave.
In Section 2.3 we’ll take up the question of existence of solutions of a first order differential equation but in this section we’ll simply assume that solutions exist and discuss a graphical method for approximating them.
Recall that a solution of (1.3.1) is a function \(y=y(x)\) such that \begin {equation} \label {eq:1.3.1} \frac{dy}{dx}=f\Big(x,y(x)\Big) \end {equation} for all values of \(x\) in some interval, and an integral curve is either the graph of a solution or is made up of segments that are graphs of solutions. Therefore, not being able to solve (1.3.1) is equivalent to not knowing the equations of integral curves of (1.3.1)
While it may be difficult to solve a differential equation, it is easy to calculate the slopes of the curves. To be specific, the slope of an integral curve of (1.3.1) through a given point \((x_0,y_0)\) is given by the number \(f(x_0,y_0) = \left.\frac{dy}{dx}\right|_{(x_0,y_0)} \)
If \(f(x,y)\) is defined on a set \(R\), we can construct a direction field (slope field) for (1.3.1) in \(R\) by drawing a short line segment through each point \((x,y)\) in \(R\) with slope \(f(x,y) = dy/dx \). Of course, as a practical matter, we can’t actually draw line segments through every point in \(R\) so we must select a finite set of points in the set.
For example, suppose \(f(x,y)\) is defined on the closed rectangular region \( R:\{a\le x\le b, c\le y\le d\}\)Let \( a= x_0< x_1< \cdots < x_m=b \) be equally spaced points in \([a,b]\) and \( c=y_0<y_1<\cdots <y_n=d \) be equally spaced points in \([c,d]\). We say that the points \[ (x_i,y_j) \quad 0\le i\le m \quad 0\le j\le n\] form a rectangular grid
Through each point in the grid we draw a short line segment with slope \(f(x_i,y_j)\). The result is an approximation to a direction field for (1.3.1) in \(R\). If the grid points are sufficiently numerous and close together, we can draw approximate integral curves of (1.3.1) by drawing curves through points in the grid tangent to the line segments associated with the points in the grid.
Unfortunately, approximating a direction field and graphing integral curves in this way is very tedious when done by hand. However, there is software for doing this and using the direction fields and integral curves gives useful insights into the behavior of the solutions of the differential equation even if we can’t obtain exact solutions.

Parametric Representation
Constructing a direction field (slope field) for some differential equations can be challenging due to undefined values. For instance, consider the Equation: \begin {equation} \label {eq:1.3.2} \frac{dy}{dx} = \overset{f(x,y)}{\overbrace{-\frac{x}{y}}} \end {equation} The function \( \displaystyle f(x,y)= -\frac{x}{y} \) is undefined when \( y=0 \) which accounts for the entire \( x-\)axis. So if you are trying to construct your direction field (slope field) on any rectangle that contains part of the \( x-\)axis there will be undefined values in the calculations.
Similarly, consider the differential equation: \begin {equation} \label {eq:1.3.3} \frac{dy}{dx} = \overset{f(x,y)}{\overbrace{{x^2\over1-x^2-y^2}}} \end {equation} The function \( \displaystyle f(x,y)= {x^2\over1-x^2-y^2} \) is undefined when \( 1-x^2-y^2 \) or when \[ x^2+y^2 = 1 \] which is the equation of the unit circle. So if you are trying to construct your direction field (slope field) on any rectangle that contains part of the unit circle you will get undefined values there.
Because of the challenges that arise with these undefined values, it is common to write differential equations in parametric form where \( x = x(t) \) and \( x = y(t) \) are regarded as functions of the parameter \(t\) and then we can calculate the derivative using the parametric equations instead. Recall parametric derivatives: \begin {equation} \label {eq:1.3.4} \frac{dy}{dx} = \frac{ \displaystyle\frac{dy}{dt}}{ \displaystyle\frac{dx}{dt}} = \frac{A(x,y)}{B(x,y)} \end {equation} In this method both \( A(x,y) \) and \( B(x,y) \) are continuous on any rectangle \(R\) so we can construct direction fields on any rectangle \begin {equation} \label {eq:1.3.5} {dx\over dt}=B(x,y) \quad {dy\over dt}=A(x,y) \end {equation}
  1. Write in parametric form \begin {equation} \frac{dy}{dx} = -\frac{x}{y} \end {equation}
  2. Sketch some integral curves on the direction field
Solution
  1. Equation (1.3.2) can be rewritten in parametric form as \begin{equation*} \frac{dy}{dx} = \frac{ \displaystyle\frac{dy}{dt}}{ \displaystyle\frac{dx}{dt}} = -\frac{x}{y} = {A(x,y)\over B(x,y)} \end{equation*} with \( A(x,y) = -x \) and \( B(x,y)=y \), so we have \[ {dx\over dt}=-y \quad {dy\over dt}=x \]
  2. Figure 1.3.5 shows a direction field and some integral curves for (1.3.2). As shown in Example 1.2.1 and will verify again in Section 2.2, the integral curves of (1.3.2) are circles centered at the origin
  1. Write in parametric form \begin {equation} \frac{dy}{dx} ={x^2\over1-x^2-y^2} \end {equation}
  2. Sketch some integral curves on the direction field
Solution
  1. Equation (1.3.3) can also be rewritten in this form as \begin{equation*} \frac{dy}{dx} = \frac{ \displaystyle\frac{dy}{dt}}{ \displaystyle\frac{dx}{dt}} = {x^2\over1-x^2-y^2} = {A(x,y)\over B(x,y)} \end{equation*} with \( A(x,y) = x^2 \) and \( B(x,y)= 1-x^2-y^2 \), so we have \[ {dx\over dt}=1-x^2-y^2 \quad {dy\over dt}=x^2\]
  2. Figure 1.3.6 shows a direction field and some integral curves for (1.3.3). The integral curves near the top and bottom are solution curves. However, the integral curves near the middle are more complicated. For example, Figure 1.3.7 shows the integral curve through the origin. The vertices of the dashed rectangle are on the circle \(x^2+y^2=1\) (with \(a\approx 0.846\),\(b\approx 0.533\)), where all integral curves of (1.3.3) have infinite slope because the equation is undefined on the unit circle.
There are three separate solution curves of (1.3.3) on the integral curve in the Figure 1.3.7:
  1. the segment above the level \(y=b\) is the graph of a solution on \((-\infty ,a)\)
  2. the segment below the level \(y=-b\) is the graph of a solution on \((-a,\infty )\)
  3. the segment between these two levels is the graph of a solution on \((-a,a)\)
Note that even if \(f(x,y)\) is continuous and otherwise “nice” throughout the rectangle \(R\), some software for producing directions fields may require the differential equation to be rewritten in parametric form, but even \(\displaystyle \frac{dy}{dx}=f(x,y)\) can be written in this way as \[ {dx\over dt}=1 \quad {dy\over dt}=f(x,y)\] with \(A(x,y)=f(x,y)\) and \(B(x,y)=1\)
