7.1 Review of Power Series
Interval of Convergence
Many applications give rise to differential equations with solutions that can’t be expressed in terms of elementary functions such as polynomials, rational functions, exponential and logarithmic functions, and trigonometric functions. The solutions of some of the most important of these equations can be expressed in terms of power series. We’ll study such equations in this chapter. In this section we review relevant properties of power series. We’ll omit proofs, which can be found in any standard calculus text.
Definition 7.1.1  An infinite series of the form \begin {equation} \label {eq:7.1.1} \sum _{n=0}^\infty a_n(x-x_0)^n \end {equation} where \(x_0\) and \(a_0\), \(a_1,\) …, \(a_n,\) …are constants\(,\) is called a power series in \(x-x_0.\) We say that the power series (7.1.1) converges for a given \(x\) if the limit \[ \lim _{N\to \infty } \sum _{n=0}^Na_n(x-x_0)^n \] exists\(; \) otherwise, we say that the power series diverges for the given \(x.\)
A power series in \(x-x_0\) must converge if \(x=x_0\), since the positive powers of \(x-x_0\) are all zero in this case. This may be the only value of \(x\) for which the power series converges. However, the next theorem shows that if the power series converges for some \(x\ne x_0\) then the set of all values of \(x\) for which it converges forms an interval.
Theorem 7.1.2 \[ \sum _{n=0}^\infty a_n(x-x_0)^n\] For any power series exactly one of the these statements is true:
  1. The power series converges only for \(x=x_0\)
  2. The power series converges for all values of \(x\)
  3. There’s a positive number \(R\) such that the power series converges if \(|x-x_0|<R\) and diverges if \(|x-x_0|>R\)
In case (i) we say that the radius is zero, \(R=0,\) and there is no interval of convergence as the series converges only at the single point \(x=x_0\)
In case (ii) we say that the radius is infinity, \(R=\infty,\) and the open interval of convergence is the entire real number line \[(-\infty, \infty ) \]
In case (iii) we say that \(R\) is the radius of convergence and the open interval of convergence is \[(x_0 - R, x_0 + R ) \] Note that no general statement can be made concerning convergence at the endpoints \(x=x_0\pm R\) of the open interval of convergence; the series may converge at one or both points, or diverge at both.
Recall from calculus that a series of constants \(\sum _{n=0}^\infty \alpha _n\) is said to converge absolutely if the series of absolute values \(\sum _{n=0}^\infty |\alpha _n|\) converges. It can be shown that a power series \(\sum _{n=0}^\infty a_n(x-x_0)^n\) with a positive radius of convergence \(R\) converges absolutely in its open interval of convergence; that is, the series \[ \sum _{n=0}^\infty |a_n||x-x_0|^n \] of absolute values converges if \(|x-x_0|<R\). However, if \(R<\infty \), the series may fail to converge absolutely at an endpoint \(x_0\pm R\), even if it converges there.
The next theorem provides a useful method for determining the radius of convergence of a power series. It’s derived in calculus by applying the ratio test to the corresponding series of absolute values.
Theorem 7.1.3 Ratio Test Suppose there’s an integer \(N\) such that \(a_n\ne 0\) if \(n\ge N\) and \[ \lim _{n\to \infty }\left |a_{n+1}\over a_n\right |=L\] where \(0\le L\le \infty\). Then the radius of convergence of \(\sum _{n=0}^\infty a_n(x-x_0)^n\) is \(R=1/ L,\) which should be interpreted to mean that \(R=0\) if \(L=\infty\), or \(R=\infty \) if \(L=0\).



Find the radius and interval of convergence of the series:
  1. \[ \sum _{n=0}^\infty n!x^n \]
  2. \[ \sum _{n=10}^\infty (-1)^n {x^n\over n!} \]
  3. \[ \sum _{n=0}^\infty 2^nn^2 (x-1)^n \]
Solution
  1. Comparing our series with the definition (7.1.1) \begin{align*} \sum _{n=0}^\infty a_n(x-x_0)^n = \sum _{n=0}^\infty\underset{a_n}{\underbrace{n!}} \underset{x-x_0}{\underbrace{(x-0)}}^n = \sum _{n=0}^\infty {n!}x^n \end{align*} We can identify \(a_n=n!\) and \( x_0 = 0 \) \begin{align*} L = & \lim _{n\to \infty }\left |a_{n+1}\over a_n\right | \\ = & \lim _{n\to \infty }\left |(n+1)!\over n!\right | \\ = & \lim _{n\to \infty } { (n+1)!\over n! } \\ = & \lim _{n\to \infty } {(n+1) \cdot n!\over n!} \\ = & \lim _{n\to \infty } (n+1) \\ = & \infty \end{align*} \(L=\infty\) so \(R=\dfrac{1}{L}=\dfrac{1}{\infty}=0\)
    Since the radius is \(0\) there is no interval of convergence, so this series \(\sum _{n=0}^\infty n!x^n\) converges only at the single point \(x=0\)
  2. Comparing our series with the definition (7.1.1) \begin{align*} \sum _{n=0}^\infty a_n(x-x_0)^n = \sum _{n=0}^\infty\underset{a_n}{\underbrace{\frac{(-1)^n}{n!}}} \underset{x-x_0}{\underbrace{(x-0)}}^n = \sum _{n=10}^\infty \frac{(-1)^n}{n!} {x^n} \end{align*} We can identify \(\displaystyle a_n=\frac{(-1)^n}{n!}\) and \( x_0 = 0 \) \begin{align*} L = & \lim _{n\to \infty }\left |a_{n+1}\over a_n\right | \\ = & \lim _{n\to \infty }\left | \frac{(-1)^{n+1}}{(n+1)!} \div \frac{(-1)^n}{n!} \right| \\ = & \lim _{n\to \infty }\left | \frac{(-1)^{n}(-1)^{1}}{(n+1)\cdot n!} \cdot \frac{n!}{(-1)^n} \right| \\ = & \lim _{n\to \infty }\left | \frac{-1}{(n+1)} \right| \\ = & \lim _{n\to \infty } \frac{1}{(n+1)} \\ = & \lim _{n\to \infty } \frac{1}{(n+1)} \cdot \frac{1/n}{1/n} \\ = & \lim _{n\to \infty } \frac{1/n}{(1+1/n)} \\ = & \frac{0}{(1+0)} = \frac{0}{(1)} = 0 \\ \end{align*} \(L=0 \) so \(R=\dfrac{1}{L}=\dfrac{1}{0}=\infty\)
    Since the radius is \(\infty\) then the interval of convergence is the entire number line \((-\infty,\infty)\)
  3. Comparing our series with the definition (7.1.1) \begin{align*} \sum _{n=0}^\infty a_n(x-x_0)^n = \sum _{n=0}^\infty\underset{a_n}{\underbrace{2^n n^2}} \underset{x-x_0}{\underbrace{(x-1)}}^n = \sum _{n=0}^\infty 2^n n^2 (x-1)^n \end{align*} We can identify \(a_n=2^n n^2\) and \( x_0 = 1 \) \begin{align*} L = & \lim _{n\to \infty }\left |a_{n+1}\over a_n\right | \\ = & \lim _{n\to \infty }\left | 2^{n+1} (n+1)^2\over 2^n \, n^2 \right | \\ = & \lim _{n\to \infty }{ 2^{n+1} (n+1)^2\over 2^n \, n^2 } \\ = & \lim _{n\to \infty }{ 2^{n} \, 2^{1} (n+1)^2\over 2^n \, n^2 } \\ = & 2 \lim _{n\to \infty }{ \left( {n+1\over n} \right)^2 } \\ = & 2 \left( \lim _{n\to \infty} \Big( 1 + {1\over n} \Big)\right)^2 \\ = & 2 \big( 1 + 0 \big)^2 \\ = & 2 \big( 1 \big)^2 = 2\\ \end{align*} \(L=2\) so \(R=\dfrac{1}{L}=\dfrac{1}{2}\)
    Since the radius is \(1/2\) and \(x_0 = 1\) then the interval of convergence is \((x_0-R,x_0+R)\) \begin{align*} & \left(1-\dfrac{1}{2},1+\dfrac{1}{2}\right) = \left(\dfrac{1}{2},\dfrac{3}{2}\right) \\ \end{align*}

Power Series Representations
A power series with a positive radius of convergence defines a function \[ f(x)=\sum _{n=0}^\infty a_n(x-x_0)^n \] on its open interval of convergence. We say that the series represents \(f\) on the open interval of convergence. A function \(f\) represented by a power series may be a familiar elementary function, however, it often happens that \(f\) isn’t a familiar function, so the series actually defines \(f\).
The next theorem shows that a function represented by a power series has derivatives of all orders on the open interval of convergence of the power series, and provides power series representations of the derivatives.
Theorem 7.1.4 A power series \[ f(x)=\sum _{n=0}^\infty a_n(x-x_0)^n \] with positive radius of convergence \(R\) has derivatives of all orders in its open interval of convergence\(,\) and successive derivatives can be obtained by repeatedly differentiating term by term and simplifying out the zero terms by changing the starting index \begin{align} \label {eq:7.1.6} f^{'}(x) = & \sum _{n=0}^\infty a_n \frac{d}{dx} \Big( (x-x_0)^n \Big) \nonumber \\ = & \sum _{n=0}^\infty a_n \Big( n (x-x_0)^{n-1} \Big) \nonumber \\ = & {\sum _{n=0}^\infty n \, a_n(x-x_0)^{n-1}} \nonumber \\ = & {\sum _{n=1}^\infty n \, a_n(x-x_0)^{n-1}} \\ \nonumber \\ f^{''}(x) = & {\sum _{n=1}^\infty n \, a_n \frac{d}{dx} \Big( (x-x_0)^{n-1} \Big) } \nonumber \\ = & {\sum _{n=1}^\infty n \, a_n \Big( (n-1) (x-x_0)^{n-2} \Big) } \nonumber \\ = & {\sum _{n=1}^\infty n (n-1) a_n (x-x_0)^{n-2} } \nonumber \\ = & {\sum _{n=2}^\infty n (n-1) a_n (x-x_0)^{n-2} } \\ \nonumber \\ f^{'''}(x) = & {\sum _{n=2}^\infty n (n-1) a_n \frac{d}{dx} \Big( (x-x_0)^{n-2} \Big)}\nonumber \\ = & {\sum _{n=2}^\infty n (n-1) a_n \Big( (n-2)(x-x_0)^{n-3} \Big)} \nonumber \\ = & {\sum _{n=2}^\infty n (n-1) (n-2) a_n (x-x_0)^{n-3} } \nonumber \\ = & {\sum _{n=3}^\infty n (n-1) (n-2) a_n (x-x_0)^{n-3} } \nonumber \\ \nonumber \\ \vdots \, & \nonumber \nonumber \\ \nonumber \\ f^{^{(k)}}(x) = & \sum _{n=k}^\infty n(n-1)\cdots (n-k+1)a_n(x-x_0)^{n-k} \end{align}
Moreover, all of these series have the same radius of convergence \(R\).




  1. Find the Maclaurin series representation for \(\sin x\)
  2. Differentiate the Maclaurin series for \(\sin x\) term-by-term
  3. Find the Maclaurin series representation for \(\cos x\) and verify it is the same
Solution
  1. We can express \(f(x)=\sin x\) as a Maclaurin series by finding the first several derivatives and evaluating them at \(x=0\) \begin{array}{rrrlrrr} f(x) &=& \sin x &\quad& f(0) &=& \sin 0 &=& 0 \\ f^{'}(x) &=& \cos x &\quad& f^{'}(0) &=& \cos 0 &=& 1 \\ f^{''}(x) &=& -\sin x &\quad& f^{''}(0) &=& -\sin 0 &=& 0 \\ f^{'''}(x) &=& -\cos x &\quad& f^{'''}(0) &=& -\cos 0 &=& -1 \\ f^{''''}(x) &=& \sin x &\quad& f^{''''}(0) &=& \sin 0 &=& 0 \\ f^{^{V}}(x) &=& \cos x &\quad& f^{^{V}}(0) &=& \cos 0 &=& 1 \\ f^{^{VI}}(x) &=& -\sin x &\quad& f^{^{VI}}(0) &=& -\sin 0 &=& 0 \\ f^{^{VII}}(x) &=& -\cos x &\quad& f^{^{VII}}(0) &=& -\cos 0 &=& -1 \\ \end{array} And substituting them into the formula (7.1.3) \begin{align*} & \sum _{n=0}^\infty {f^{(n)}(0)\over n!} x^n \\ = & \underset{0}{\underbrace{{f(0)}}} + {f^{'}(0)}x + \underset{0}{\underbrace{ {f^{''}(0)\over 2!}}} x^2 + {f^{'''}(0)\over 3!}x^3 + \underset{0}{\underbrace{{f^{^{IV}}(0)\over 4!}}}x^4 + \cdots \\ = & {f^{'}(0)}x + {f^{'''}(0)\over 3!}x^3 + {f^{^{V}}(0)\over 5!}x^5 + {f^{^{VII}}(0)\over 7!}x^7 + \cdots \\ = & x - {1\over 3!}x^3 + {1\over 5!}x^5 - {1\over 7!}x^7 + \cdots \\ \end{align*} Notice that terms of the Maclaurin series expansion of sine are alternating so we will include a \((-1)^n\) factor and since all the even derivatives of sine evaluated to zero, the Maclaurin series expansion contains only the odd terms, and we can write it in sigma notation iterating through the odd values using \(2n+1\) \begin{align*} x - {1\over 3!}x^3 + {1\over 5!}x^5 - {1\over 7!}x^7 + \cdots = \sum_{n=0}^{\infty} \frac{(-1)^n \, x^{2n+1}}{(2n+1)!} \\ \end{align*} Thus we have the Maclaurin series form for \(\sin x\) \begin{align*} \sin x = \sum_{n=0}^{\infty} \frac{(-1)^n \, x^{2n+1}}{(2n+1)!} \end{align*}
  2. We will now differentiate it term-by-term: \begin{align*} \sin x = & \sum_{n=0}^{\infty} \frac{(-1)^n \, x^{2n+1}}{(2n+1)!} \\ \frac{d}{dx} \Big( \sin x \Big) = & \sum_{n=0}^{\infty} \frac{(-1)^n }{(2n+1)!} \frac{d}{dx} \Big( x^{2n+1} \Big)\\ \cos x = & \sum_{n=0}^{\infty} \frac{(-1)^n }{(2n+1)!} (2n+1 )\Big( x^{2n} \Big)\\ \cos x = & \sum_{n=0}^{\infty} \frac{(-1)^n \cdot (2n+1 )\cdot x^{2n} }{(2n+1) \cdot (2n)!} \\ \cos x = & \sum_{n=0}^{\infty} \frac{(-1)^n \, x^{2n} }{ (2n)!} \\ \end{align*}
  3. We will now find the Maclaurin series representation for \(\cos x\) and verify it is the same result. Let \(g(x)=\cos x\) and now we will find the first several derivatives and evaluate them at \(x=0\) \begin{array}{rrrlrrr} g(x) &=& \cos x &\quad& g(0) &=& \cos 0 &=& 1 \\ g^{'}(x) &=& -\sin x &\quad& g^{'}(0) &=& -\sin 0 &=& 0 \\ g^{''}(x) &=& -\cos x &\quad& g^{''}(0) &=& -\cos 0 &=& -1 \\ g^{'''}(x) &=& \sin x &\quad& g^{'''}(0) &=& \sin 0 &=& 0 \\ g^{''''}(x) &=& \cos x &\quad& g^{''''}(0) &=& \cos 0 &=& 1 \\ g^{^{V}}(x) &=& -\sin x &\quad& g^{^{V}}(0) &=& -\sin 0 &=& 0 \\ g^{^{VI}}(x) &=& -\cos x &\quad& g^{^{VI}}(0) &=& -\cos 0 &=& -1 \\ g^{^{VII}}(x) &=& \sin x &\quad& f^{^{VII}}(0) &=& \sin 0 &=& 0 \\ \end{array} And substituting them into the formula (7.1.3) \begin{align*} & \sum _{n=0}^\infty {f^{(n)}(0)\over n!} x^n \\ = & {f(0)} + \underset{0}{\underbrace{ {f^{'}(0)} }} x + {f^{''}(0)\over 2!} x^2 + \underset{0}{\underbrace{ {f^{'''}(0)\over 3!} }} x^3 + {f^{^{IV}}(0)\over 4!} x^4 + \cdots \\ = & {f(0)}x + {f^{''}(0)\over 2!}x^2 + {f^{''''}(0)\over 4!}x^4 + {f^{^{VI}}(0)\over 6!}x^6 + \cdots \\ = & 1 - {1\over 2!}x^2 + {1\over 4!}x^4 - {1\over 6!}x^6 + \cdots \\ \end{align*} Notice again that terms of the Maclaurin series expansion of sine are alternating so we will include a \((-1)^n\) factor and since all the odd derivatives of cosine evaluated to zero, the Maclaurin series expansion contains only the even terms, and we can write it in sigma notation iterating through the even values using \(2n\) \begin{align*} 1 - {1\over 2!}x^2 + {1\over 4!}x^4 - {1\over 6!}x^6 + \cdots = \sum_{n=0}^{\infty} \frac{(-1)^n \, x^{2n}}{(2n)!} \\ \end{align*} Thus we have the Maclaurin series form for \(\cos x\) \begin{align*} \cos x = \sum_{n=0}^{\infty} \frac{(-1)^n \, x^{2n}}{(2n)!} \end{align*} Which matches our term-by-term differentiation exactly




The next theorem shows that if \(f\) is defined by a power series in \(x-x_0\) with a positive radius of convergence, then the power series is the Taylor series of \(f\) about \(x_0\)
Theorem 7.1.5 If the power series \[ f(x)=\sum _{n=0}^\infty a_n(x-x_0)^n \] has a positive radius of convergence, then \begin {equation} \label {eq:7.1.9} a_n={f^{(n)}(x_0)\over n!} \end {equation} that is, \(\sum _{n=0}^\infty a_n(x-x_0)^n\) is the Taylor series of \(f\) about \(x_0\)
This result can be obtained by setting \(x=x_0\) in (7.1.8), which yields \begin{align*} f^{(k)}(x) = & \sum _{n=k}^\infty n(n-1)\cdots (n-k+1) \, a_n(x-x_0)^{n-k} \\ f^{(k)}(x_0) = & \sum _{n=k}^\infty n(n-1)\cdots (n-k+1) \, a_n\underset{0}{\underbrace{(x_0-x_0)}}^{n-k} \\ = & k(k-1)\cdots (k-k+1) \, a_k \\ = & k(k-1)\cdots 1 \, a_k \\ = & k! \, a_k \end{align*} Solving for \(a_k\) we obtain \[ a_k={f^{(k)}(x_0)\over k!}\] Which is the same expression as (7.1.9)


The next theorem lists two important properties of power series that follow from Theorem 7.1.5.
Theorem 7.1.6
  1. If two series are equal for all \(x\) in an open interval that contains \(x_0,\) \[ \sum _{n=0}^\infty a_n(x-x_0)^n=\sum _{n=0}^\infty b_n(x-x_0)^n \] Then \(a_n=b_n\) for \(n=0\), \(1\), \(2\), \(\dots\)
  2. If a series is equal to zero for all \(x\) in an open interval that contains \(x_0,\) \[ \sum _{n=0}^\infty a_n(x-x_0)^n=0 \] Then \(a_n=0\) for \(n=0\), \(1\), \(2\), \(\dots\)
To obtain (a) we observe that the two series represent the same function \(f\) on the open interval; hence, Theorem 7.1.5 implies that \[ a_n=b_n={f^{(n)}(x_0)\over n!},\quad n=0,1,2, \dots \](b) can be obtained from (a) by taking \(b_n=0\) for \(n=0\), \(1\), \(2\), ….




If \(f\) has \(N\) derivatives at a point \(x_0\), we say that \[ T_N(x)=\sum _{n=0}^N{f^{(n)}(x_0)\over n!}(x-x_0)^n \] is the \(N\)-th Taylor polynomial of \(f\) about \(x_0\). This definition and Theorem 7.1.5 imply that if \[ f(x)=\sum _{n=0}^\infty a_n(x-x_0)^n\] where the power series has a positive radius of convergence\(,\) then the Taylor polynomials of \(f\) about \(x_0\) are given by \[ T_N(x)=\sum _{n=0}^N a_n(x-x_0)^n\] In numerical applications, we use the Taylor polynomials to approximate \(f\) on subintervals of the open interval of convergence of the power series. For example, the the Taylor series formula (7.1.2) implies that the Taylor polynomial \(T_N (x) \) of \(f(x)=e^x\) is \begin{align*} T_N(x) = & \sum _{n=0}^N{x^n\over n!} \\ T_1(x) = & \sum _{n=0}^1{x^n\over n!} = {x^0\over 0!} + {x^1\over 1!} = 1 + x \\ T_2(x) = & \sum _{n=0}^2{x^n\over n!} = 1 + x + {x^2\over 2!} \\ T_3(x) = & \sum _{n=0}^3{x^n\over n!} = 1 + x + {x^2\over 2!} + {x^3\over 3!} \\ T_4(x) = & \sum _{n=0}^4{x^n\over n!} = 1 + x + {x^2\over 2!} + {x^3\over 3!} + {x^4\over 4!} \\ T_5(x) = & \sum _{n=0}^5{x^n\over n!} = 1 + x + {x^2\over 2!} + {x^3\over 3!} + {x^4\over 4!} + {x^5\over 5!} \\ \end{align*} The solid curve in Figure 7.1.1 is the graph of \(y=e^x\) on the interval \([0,5]\). The dotted curves in Figure 7.1.1 are the graphs of the Taylor polynomials \(T_1 (x) \), \(T_2 (x) ,\) \(T_3 (x) ,\) \(T_4 (x) ,\) \(T_5 (x) ,\) and \(T_6 (x) \) of \(y=e^x\) about \(x_0=0\). From this figure, we conclude that the accuracy of the approximation of \(y=e^x\) by its Taylor polynomial \(T_N (x) \) improves as \(N\) increases.
In Definition 7.1.1 of a power series in \(x-x_0\), the \(n\)-th term is a constant multiple of \((x-x_0)^n\). This isn’t true in the expressions for the derivatives of the power series in (7.1.6), (7.1.7) and (7.1.8) where the general terms are constant multiples of \((x-x_0)^{n-1}\), \((x-x_0)^{n-2}\) and \((x-x_0)^{n-k}\) respectively, and shown below for reference. \begin{align*} f^{'}(x) = & {\sum _{n=1}^\infty n \, a_n(x-x_0)^{n-1}} \tag{6} \\ \\ f^{''}(x) = & {\sum _{n=2}^\infty n \, (n-1) \, a_n (x-x_0)^{n-2} } \tag{7} \\ \\ f^{^{(k)}}(x) = & \sum _{n=k}^\infty n \, (n-1) \, \cdots (n-k+1)a_n(x-x_0)^{n-k} \tag{8} \\ \\ \end{align*} However, these series can all be rewritten so that their \(n\)-th terms are constant multiples of \((x-x_0)^n\). For example, letting \(n=k+1\) in the series in (7.1.6) yields \begin {align} \label {eq:7.1.10} f'(x) = & {\sum _{n=1}^\infty n \, a_n(x-x_0)^{n-1}} \nonumber \tag{6} \\ = & {\sum _{k+1=1}^\infty \overbrace{(k+1)} \, a_{\underbrace{k+1}}(x-x_0)^{\overbrace{k+1}-1}} \nonumber \\ = & \sum _{k=0}^\infty (k+1) \, a_{k+1}(x-x_0)^k \end {align} where we start the new summation index \(k\) from zero so that the first term in (7.1.10) (obtained by setting \(k=0\)) is the same as the first term in (7.1.6) (obtained by setting \(n=1\) ). However, the sum of a series is independent of the symbol used to denote the summation index, just as the value of a definite integral is independent of the symbol used to denote the variable of integration. Therefore we can replace \(k\) by \(n\) in (7.1.10) to obtain an expression for (7.1.6) where the general term is a constant multiple of \((x-x_0)^n\) \begin{align} \label {eq:7.1.11} f'(x)= & \sum _{n=0}^\infty (n+1) \, a_{n+1}(x-x_0)^n \end{align}
It isn’t really necessary to introduce the intermediate summation index \(k\). We can obtain (7.1.11) directly from (7.1.6) by replacing \(n\) by \(n+1\) in the general term of (7.1.6) and subtracting \(1\) from the lower limit of (7.1.6).
