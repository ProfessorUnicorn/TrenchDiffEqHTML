2.2 Separable Equations
Separable Equations
A first order differential equation is separable if it can be written as \begin {equation} \label {eq:2.2.1} h(y) \frac{dy}{dx} = g(x) \end {equation} where the left side is a product of the derivative \( \frac{dy}{dx}\) and a function of the dependent variable \(y\) and the right side is a function of the independent variable \(x\). Rewriting a separable differential equation in this form is called separation of variables and can be used to solve linear and nonlinear equations.
To understand how to solve (2.2.1), we'll first assume that \(y = y(x)\) is a solution. Let \(G(x)\) be any antiderivative of \(g(x)\) and let \(H(y)\) be any antiderivative of \(h(y)\) \begin {equation*} G(x) = \int_{x_0}^x g(t) dt \quad\text{and}\quad H(y) = \int_{y_0}^y h(t) dt \end{equation*}
Calculate \( G'(x) \) and \( H'(y) \) using the Fundamental Theorem of Calculus
Rewriting them as derivatives using Differential (Leibniz) notation, we have: \begin {equation} \label {eq:2.2.2} \frac{dG}{dx} = g(x) \quad\text{and}\quad \frac{dH}{dy} = h(y) \end{equation} Now (2.2.1) can be rewritten using (2.2.2) \begin {align*} \underbrace{h(y)} \frac{dy}{dx} = & \underbrace{g(x)} \\ \frac{dH}{dy} \cdot \frac{dy}{dx} = & \frac{dG}{dx} \\ \end {align*} Differentiating \( H \big( y \big) = H \Big( y(x) \Big) \) using the chain rule we get the same expression as the left side \begin{align*} {d\over dx} \Bigg[ H \Big( y(x) \Big) \Bigg] = H' \Big( y(x) \Big) \cdot y'(x) = \frac{dH}{dy} \cdot \frac{dy}{dx} \end{align*} Further modifying (2.2.1) we can replace the left side with the expression from the chain rule and then find the antiderivative of both sides \begin {align} \label{eq:2.2.3} \underbrace{h(y)} \frac{dy}{dx} = & \underbrace{g(x)} \nonumber \\ \underbrace{ \frac{dH}{dy} \cdot \frac{dy}{dx} } = & \frac{dG}{dx} \nonumber \\ {d\over dx} \Big[ H (y) \Big] = & {d\over dx} \Big[ G (x) \Big] \nonumber \\ \int {d\over dx} \Big[ H(y) \Big] dx = & \int \frac{d}{dx} \Big[ G(x) \Big] dx \nonumber \\ H(y) = & G(x) + c \end {align}
The result is that when \(y = y(x)\) is a solution of (2.2.1) then we have (2.2.3) but now we can take a different perspective: any differentiable functions \(y = y(x)\) that satisfies (2.2.3) for some constant \(c\) is a solution of (2.2.1). To verify this, we differentiate both sides of (2.2.3), using the chain rule on the left, to obtain \begin{align*} H\Big(y(x)\Big) = & G(x) + c \\ \frac{d}{dx} \Bigg[ H(y) \Bigg] = & \frac{d}{dx} \Bigg[ G(x) + c \Bigg] \\ \frac{dH}{dy} \cdot \frac{dy}{dx} = & \frac{dG}{dx} + 0 \\ h(y) \frac{dy}{dx} = & g(x) \end{align*}
In conclusion, to solve (2.2.1) it suffices to find functions \(G=G(x)\) and \(H=H(y)\) that satisfy (2.2.2). Then any differentiable function \(y=y(x)\) that satisfies (2.2.3) is a solution of (2.2.1) \begin{align*} h(y) \frac{dy}{dx} = & g(x) \\ \int \Bigg( h(y) \frac{dy}{dx} \Bigg) dx = & \int g(x) dx \\ \int h(y) dy = & \int g(x) dx \\ H(y) = & G(x) + c \end{align*}
Solve the equation \[ \frac{dy}{dx} =x(1+y^2)\]
Solution
 Separating variables then finding the antiderivative: \begin{align*} \frac{dy}{dx} = & x(1+y^2) \\ \frac{1}{1+y^2} \cdot \frac{dy}{dx} = & x \\ \int \Bigg( \frac{1}{1+y^2} \cdot \frac{dy}{dx} \Bigg) dx = & \int \Big( x \Big) dx \\ \int \frac{1}{1+\underset{y = \tan \theta}{\underbrace{y^2}} } \underset{\sec^2 \theta}{\underbrace{dy}} = & \int x dx \\ \int \frac{\sec^2 \theta}{1+\tan^2 \theta} d\theta = & \int x dx \\ \int d\theta = & \int x dx \\ \theta = & \frac{1}{2}x^2 + c \\ \tan^{-1} y = & \frac{1}{2}x^2 + c \\ y = & \tan \left(\frac{1}{2}x^2 + c\right) \\ \end{align*}
Differentiate \( \tan x \) by rewriting as \( \displaystyle \frac{\sin x}{ \cos x} \) and using the quotient rule
Divide the pythagorean identity \( \cos^2 x + \sin^2 x = 1\) by \( \cos^2 x \) to obtain the form of the pythagorean identity used in the Example 2.2.1
  1. Find the general solution \begin {equation} \label {eq:2.2.4} \frac{dy}{dx}=-{x\over y}\end {equation}
  2. Solve the initial value problem (IVP) \begin {equation} \label {eq:2.2.5} \frac{dy}{dx}=-{x\over y} \quad\quad y(1)=1 \end {equation}
  3. Solve the initial value problem (IVP) \begin {equation} \label {eq:2.2.6} \frac{dy}{dx}=-{x\over y} \quad\quad y(1)=-2 \end {equation}
Solution
  1.  Separating variables in (2.2.4) then finding the antiderivative: \begin{align*} \frac{dy}{dx} = & -\frac{x}{y} \\ y \cdot \frac{dy}{dx} = & -x \\ \int \Bigg( y \cdot \frac{dy}{dx} \Bigg) dx = & \int -x dx \\ \int y dy = & - \int x dx \\ \frac{1}{2}y^2 = & -\frac{1}{2}x^2 + k \\ \frac{1}{2}y^2 + \frac{1}{2}x^2 = & + k \\ y^2 + x^2 = & \underset{c}{\underbrace{2 k}} \\ y^2 + x^2 = & c \\ \end{align*} The arbitrary constant is a positive value since it is the sum of squares, so we can replace it with an expression that is always positive. We'll use \( a^2 \) as our arbitrary constant to obtain the implicit solution \begin{align} \label {eq:2.2.7} y^2 + x^2 = r^2 \end{align} Which we can recognize as the equation of a circle with radius \( r \) centered at the origin
    Solve (2.2.7) explicitly for \(y\)
    Solving explicitly for \( y \) results in two different differentiable functions \begin{equation}\label {eq:2.2.8} y(x) = \sqrt{r^2-x^2} \quad\text{and}\quad y(x) = -\sqrt{r^2-x^2} \end{equation} Where \( y(x) = \sqrt{r^2-x^2} \) is the top half of the circle above the \(x\)-axis and \( y(x) = -\sqrt{r^2-x^2} \) is the bottom half blow the \(x\)-axis (Figure 2.2.1). Each of these semicircles are solution curves of (2.2.4) while the circle itself is an integral curve.
    Both of these functions are only defined when \( r^2-x^2 \ge 0 \) yields the same interval of validity (IOV) for both \( (-r,r) \)
    Find the domain of (2.2.8)
  2. To solve (2.2.5) notice the initial condition \(y(1)=1\) indicates a positive value, so the function \( y(x) = \sqrt{r^2-x^2} \) will be used. Substituting the initial condition and solving for the arbitrary constant: \begin{align*} y(x) = \sqrt{r^2-x^2} \Longrightarrow \underset{1}{\underbrace{y(1)}} = & \sqrt{r^2-1^2} \\ 1 = & \sqrt{r^2-1} \\ 1^2 = & r^2-1 \Longrightarrow r^2 = 2 \end{align*} Substituting \( r^2 = 2 \) into (2.2.8), we obtain the solution to the IVP and its IOV \[ y=\sqrt {2-x^2} \quad\quad \left(-\sqrt{2},\sqrt{2}\right) \]
  3. To solve (2.2.6) notice the initial condition \(y(1)=-2\) indicates a negative value, so the function \( y(x) = -\sqrt{r^2-x^2} \) will be used. Substituting the initial condition and solving for the arbitrary constant: \begin{align*} y(x) = -\sqrt{r^2-x^2} \Longrightarrow \underset{-2}{\underbrace{y(1)}} = & \sqrt{r^2-1^2} \\ -2 = & -\sqrt{r^2-1} \\ 4 = & r^2-1 \Longrightarrow r^2 = 5 \end{align*} Substituting \( r^2 = 5 \) into (2.2.8), we obtain the solution to the IVP and its IOV \[ y=-\sqrt {5-x^2} \quad\quad \left(-\sqrt{5},\sqrt{5}\right) \]
Implicit Solutions
In Examples 2.2.1 and 2.2.2we were able to solve the equation explicitly for \( y \) for our general solution. However, sometimes we are not able to explicitly solve for \( y \) and when this happens we will leave the general solution in implicit form.
Theorem 2.2.1
Suppose \(g=g(x)\) is continous on \((a,b)\) with antiderivative \(G\) and \(h=h(y)\) is continous on \((c,d)\) with antiderivative \(H.\) Let \(x_0\) be an arbitrary point in \((a,b)\) and \(y_0\) in \((c,d)\) such that \(y(x_0)=y_0\) and \(h(y_0)\ne 0.\) Then the separable differential equation (2.2.9) has a unique represented as an implicit solution (2.2.10) where the function \(y=y(x,c)\) itself is the actual solution. \begin {equation} \label {eq:2.2.9} h(y) \frac{dy}{dx} =g(x) \end {equation} \begin {equation} \label {eq:2.2.10} H(y)=G(x)+c \end {equation} And the IVP (2.2.11) has a unique implicit solution (2.2.12) where the function \(y=y(x)\) itself is the actual solution. \begin {equation} \label {eq:2.2.11} h(y) \frac{dy}{dx} =g(x) \quad \quad y(x_0) = y_0 \end {equation} \begin {equation} \label {eq:2.2.12} H(y)=G(x) + \Big( H(y_0) - G(x_0) \Big) \end {equation}
We call (2.2.10) with arbitrary constant \(c\) an implicit solution of (2.2.9) which might be written in different forms including \( H(y)=G(x)+c \) or \( H(y) - G(x) = c \) but either way it is important to remember that the function \(y=y(x)\) itself is the actual solution. For instance, curves defined by (2.2.10) are integral curves or (2.2.9), whereas the curves defined by \(y=y(x)\) are the actual solution curves of (2.2.9). Evaluating (2.2.10) at the initial point \( \left( x_0, y_0 \right) \) we obtain \( H(y_0)=G(x_0)+c \) such that the arbitrary constant in our IVP evaluates to \( c = H(y_0) - G(x_0)\) and when \(c\) exists that satisfies this expression we’ll say that (2.2.12) is an implicit solution of the IVP (2.2.11) however it is important to realize that for some choices of \( c\) there may not be any differentiable functions \( y = y(x) \) that satisfy (2.2.10).
  1. Find implicit solutions of \begin {equation} \label {eq:2.2.13} \frac{dy}{dx} = {2x+1\over 5y^4+1}\end {equation}
  2. Find an implicit solution of \begin {equation} \label {eq:2.2.14} \frac{dy}{dx} = {2x+1\over 5y^4+1},\quad y(2)=1\end {equation}
Solution
  1. Separating variables in (2.2.13) yields \begin{align}\label {eq:2.2.15} \frac{dy}{dx} = & \frac{2x+1}{5y^4+1} \nonumber \\ (5y^4+1)\frac{dy}{dx} = & 2x+1 \nonumber \\ \int (5y^4+1)\frac{dy}{dx} dx = & \int \left(2x+1\right) dx \nonumber \\ 5\int y^4 dy + \int dy = & 2\int x dx + \int dx \nonumber \\ 5\left(\frac{1}{5}y^5\right) + y = & 2\left(\frac{1}{2}x^2\right) + x + c \nonumber \\ \underset{H(y)}{\underbrace{y^5 + y}} = & \underset{G(x)}{\underbrace{x^2 + x}}+ c \end{align} We call this the implicit solution of (2.2.13) and notice in this case it is not possible to solve explicitly for \( y \)
  2. We already have our implicit solution (2.2.15) so we just substitute in our initial condition \( y(2)=1 \) to solve for the constant \( c \) \begin{align*} y^5 + y = & x^2 + x + c \\ \Big[ y(x) \Big]^5 + y(x) = & x^2 + x + c \\ \Big[ \underset{1}{\underbrace{y(2)}} \Big]^5 + y(2) = & (2)^2 + (2) + c \\ \big[ 1 \big]^5 + 1 = & 4 + 2 + c \\ 2 = & 6 + c \\ c = -4 \end{align*} Substituting in \( c = -4 \) into (2.2.15) we have the implicit solution for our IVP (2.2.14) \begin{equation*} y^5 + y = x^2 + x -4 \end{equation*}

Constant Solutions
Both linear and nonlinear equations of the form \( \displaystyle \frac{dy}{dx}=g(x)p(y) \) are always separable. Simply divide by \( p(y) \) and find the antiderivative of both sides: \begin{align*} \frac{dy}{dx} = & g(x)p(y) \\ \frac{1}{p(y)}\frac{dy}{dx} = & g(x) \\ \int \Bigg( \frac{1}{p(y)}\frac{dy}{dx} \Bigg) dx = & \int g(x) dx \\ \int \frac{1}{p(y)} dy = & \int g(x) dx \\ \end{align*} However, the division by \( p(y) \) will only work if \( p(y) \) has no zeros. When \( p(y) \) has a zero, we treat it similarly to how we treated trivial solutions, first considering the trivial solution case individually, and then assuming \( y \) is not equal to the individual values. We will do the same for constant solutions (equilibrium solutions)
Find the general solution \begin {equation} \label {eq:2.2.16} \frac{dy}{dx} = 2xy^2 \end{equation}
Solution
First notice (2.2.16) has a trivial solution \( y(x) \equiv 0 \) which we can verify by substitution: \begin{align*} \frac{dy}{dx} = & 2xy^2 \\ \frac{d}{dx} \Big( 0 \Big) = & 2x \Big(0\Big)^2 \quad \checkmark \\ \end{align*}
Now we seek nontrivial solutions where \( y\ne0 \) and divide by \( y^2 \) to separate the variables \begin{align} \frac{dy}{dx} = & 2xy^2 \nonumber \\ \frac{1}{y^2} \cdot \frac{dy}{dx} = & 2x \nonumber \\ \int \Big( \frac{1}{y^2}\frac{dy}{dx} \Big) dx = & \int \Big( 2x \Big) dx \nonumber \\ \int y^{-2} dy = & 2\int x dx \nonumber \\ -1y^{-1} = & 2\left(\frac{1}{2}x^2\right) + c \nonumber \\ -\frac{1}{y} = & x^2 + k \nonumber \\ \frac{1}{y} = & - x^2 \underset{c}{\underbrace{-k}} \nonumber \\ \frac{1}{y} = & c - x^2 \nonumber \\ 1 = & y \left( c - x^2 \right) \nonumber \\ y = & \frac{1}{c - x^2} \end{align}
We’ve shown that if \(y\) is a solution of (2.2.16) that is not identically zero, then \(y\) must be of the form of the one-parameter family given by (2.2.17). Notice that regardless of any choice of \( c \), there is no way to obtain the trivial solution. When a solution exists that is not from the one-parameter family. we call it a singular solution. Thus our general solution includes both the trivial solution and the one-parameter family: \begin{equation*} y(x) \equiv 0 \quad \quad y(x) = \frac{1}{c - x^2} \end{equation*}

Find the general solution \begin {equation} \label {eq:2.2.18} \frac{dy}{dx} = {1\over2}x(1-y^2) \end {equation}
Solution
Although there is no trivial solution for (2.2.18), there are other constant solutions. Notice that \( y=1 \) and \( y=-1 \) are both solutions: \begin{align*} \frac{d}{dx} \Big( 1 \Big) = {1\over2}x \Big( 1- (1)^2 \Big) \Longrightarrow 0 = & {1\over2}x \Big( 0 \Big) \quad \checkmark \\ \end{align*} and \begin{align*} \frac{d}{dx} \Big( -1 \Big) = {1\over2}x \Big( 1- (-1)^2 \Big) \Longrightarrow 0 = & {1\over2}x \Big( 0 \Big) \quad \checkmark \\ \end{align*} These constant solutions are called equilibrium solutions and separating variables requires dividing by \( 1-y^2 = (1-y)(1+y) \), which is perfectly fine to do as long as we first assume \( y(x) \ne 1 \) and \( y(x) \ne -1 \) and we'll start by factoring out the negative: \begin{align*} \frac{dy}{dx} = & -\frac{1}{2}x(y^2-1) \\ \frac{1}{y^2-1}\frac{dy}{dx} = & -\frac{1}{2}x \\ \int \Bigg( \frac{1}{y^2-1}\frac{dy}{dx} \Bigg) dx = & -\int \frac{1}{2}x dx \\ \int \frac{1}{(y-1)(y+1)}dy = & - \frac{1}{2}\int x dx \\ \end{align*} Finding the antiderivative on the right is simple enough using the power rule but the antiderivative on the left is more complicated, requiring partial fraction decomposition (PFD): \begin{align*} \frac{1}{(y-1)(y+1)} = \frac{A}{y-1}+\frac{B}{y+1} = \frac{(A+B)y+(A-B)}{(y-1)(y+1)} \\ \end{align*} To make the numerators \( 1 = (A+B)y+(A-B) \) equal, equate the coefficients to obtain the system of equations which we can add to find \(A\) \begin{align*} \left\{ \begin{array}{r l} A + B = & 0 \\ A - B = & 1 \\ \end{array} \right. = \left\{ \begin{array}{r l} B = & -A \\ 2A = & 1 \\ \end{array} \right. = \left\{ \begin{array}{r l} B = & -\frac{1}{2} \\ A = & \frac{1}{2} \\ \end{array} \right. \end{align*} Substituting in these values we can decompose the fraction on the left: \begin{align*} \int \frac{1}{(y-1)(y+1)}dy = & - \frac{1}{2}\int x dx \\ \frac{1}{2}\int \frac{1}{y-1}dy - \frac{1}{2}\int \frac{1}{y+1}dy = & -\frac{1}{2}\int x dx \\ \int \frac{1}{\underset{u}{\underbrace{y-1}}} \underset{du}{\underbrace{dy}} -\int \frac{1}{\underset{v}{\underbrace{y+1}}} \underset{dv}{\underbrace{dy}} = & -\int x dx \\ \int \frac{1}{u}du - \int \frac{1}{v}dv = & -\int x dx \\ \ln \left| u \right| - \ln \left| v \right| = & -\frac{1}{2} x^2 + k \\ \ln \left| \frac{y-1}{y+1} \right| = & -\frac{1}{2}x^2 + k \\ \left| \frac{y-1}{y+1} \right| = & e^k e^{-\frac{1}{2}x^2} \end{align*} Recall that we assumed \( y(x) \ne 1 \) and \( y(x) \ne -1 \) so the quantity \( (y-1)/(y+1) \) is always positive or always negative in any given interval we are on, therefore we can further simplify and represent this one-parameter family by: \begin{align*} \frac{y-1}{y+1} =& ce^{-\frac{1}{2}x^2} \end{align*} Although this representation is currently implicit, we can solve explicitly for \( y \) \begin{align} y-1 = & ce^{-\frac{1}{2}x^2} (y+1) \nonumber \\ y-1 = & cye^{-\frac{1}{2}x^2}+ce^{-\frac{1}{2}x^2} \nonumber \\ y-cye^{-\frac{1}{2}x^2} = & 1 + ce^{-\frac{1}{2}x^2} \nonumber \\ y\left(1-ce^{-\frac{1}{2}x^2}\right) = & 1 + ce^{-\frac{1}{2}x^2} \nonumber \\ y(x) = & \frac{1 + ce^{-\frac{1}{2}x^2}}{1-ce^{-\frac{1}{2}x^2}} \end{align}
By first identifying the constant solutions (equilibrium solutions) and then assuming \( y \) was explicitly not equal to those values, we were able to find this one-parameter family (2.2.19) to represent all solutions of (2.2.18) which are not identically equal to one or negative one. It turns out that one of the equilibrium solutions, \( y(x)=1 \) is also represented in the one-parameter family when c = 0: \begin{align*} y(0) = & \frac{1 + 0\cdot e^{-\frac{1}{2}x^2}}{1-0\cdot e^{-\frac{1}{2}x^2}} = 1 \\ \end{align*} While one of the equilibrium solutions is part of the family, the other equilibrium solution is not. That is, regardless of choice of \( c \) we can never obtain the equilibrium solution \( y(x)=-1 \) from the family, so we call it a singular solution and it must be included in the general solution: \begin{align*} y(x) = -1 \quad \quad y(x) = \frac{1 + ce^{-\frac{1}{2}x^2}}{1-ce^{-\frac{1}{2}x^2}} \\ \end{align*}

Linear v. Nonlinear
Theorem 2.1.2 shows us that there exists a unique solution for all linear first order differential equations and IVPs: \[ \frac{dy}{dx} + p(x)y=f(x) \] \[ \frac{dy}{dx} + p(x)y=f(x) \quad\quad y(x_0)=y_0 \]
The not true for nonlinear equations: first order nonlinear differential equations may or may not have a solution.
While solutions of linear equations all come from a single one-parameter family, solutions of nonlinear equations may be a singular equilibrium solution (Examples 2.2.4 and 2.2.5).
Intervals of validity (IOV) are also different. For linear equations IOVs can be determined directly from the equation of the solution by determining undefined values whereas with nonlinear equations the equation does not provide enough information as the IOV may also depend on the initial condition (Example 2.2.2).
Solve the initial value problem \[ \frac{dy}{dx} = 2xy^2 \quad\quad y(0)=y_0 \] and determine the interval of validity of the solution.
Solution
From Example 2.2.4, we know that other than the trivial solution, \(y\) must be of the form of the one-parameter family: \begin {equation} \label{eq:2.2.20} y={1\over c-x^2} \end {equation} Applying the initial condition, we can solve for a specific \( c\) \begin{align*} y(x) = & {1\over c-x^2} \\ \underset{y_0}{\underbrace{y(0)}} = & {1\over c-0^2} \\ y_0 = & {1\over c} \Longrightarrow c =\frac{1}{y_0} \\ \end{align*} Substituting into (2.2.20) we obtain the particular solution to the IVP: \begin{align*} y(x) = & \frac{1}{\frac{1}{y_0}-x^2} \\ \end{align*} Simplifying by multiplying \( y_0 \) on top and bottom, we obtain: \begin{align*} y(x) = & \frac{y_0}{1-y_0x^2} \\ \end{align*}
So we have our particular solution and now we need to determine the IOVs. We will have to consider cases for different values of \( y_0 \) so we will consider \( y_0 = 0 ,\) \( y_0 < 0 ,\) and \( y_0 > 0 \) separately:
  • When \( y_0 = 0 \) then our solution is the trivial solution \( y(x) = \displaystyle \frac{0}{1-0\cdot x^2} = \frac{0}{1} = 0 \) which is valid for all values of \( x \) so the IOV is \( (-\infty, \infty) \)
  • When \( y_0 < 0 \) since this would result in the sum of two positive quantities in the denominator, guaranteeing that the solution is always defined, which again is valid for all values of \( x \) so the IOV is \( (-\infty, \infty) \)
  • When \( y_0 > 0 \) it is a little trickier since the denominator could evaluate to zero making it undefined. So we simply find the undefined points by setting the denominator to zero and solve: \begin{align*} 1-y_0x^2 = & 0 \\ 1 = & y_0x^2 \\ \frac{1}{y_0} = & x^2 \Longrightarrow x = \pm \frac{1}{\sqrt{y_0}} \end{align*} Meaning the expression is undefined at \( x = 1 / \sqrt{y_0} \) and \( x = -1 / \sqrt{y_0} \) so possible IOVs are \begin{align*} \left(-\infty, -\frac{1}{\sqrt{y_0}}\right) \quad \text{or} \quad \left(-\frac{1}{\sqrt{y_0}}, \frac{1}{\sqrt{y_0}}\right) \quad \text{or} \quad \left(\frac{1}{\sqrt{y_0}}, \infty\right) \end{align*} The initial condition of \( y(0) = y_0 \) then the \(x\) value of 0 places us directly in the IOV \[ \left(-\frac{1}{\sqrt{y_0}}, \frac{1}{\sqrt{y_0}}\right) \]
