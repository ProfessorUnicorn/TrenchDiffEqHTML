Section 2.2 Separable Equations
2.2   SEPARABLE EQUATIONS
Separable Equations
A first order differential equation is separable if it can be written as \begin {equation} \label {eq:2.2.1} h(y) \frac{dy}{dx} = g(x) \end {equation} where the left side is a product of the derivative \( \frac{dy}{dx}\) and a function of the dependent variable \(y\) and the right side is a function of the independent variable \(x\). Rewriting a separable differential equation in this form is called separation of variables and can be used to solve linear and nonlinear equations.
To understand how to solve (2.2.1), we'll first assume that \(y\) is a solution. Let \(G(x)\) be antiderivative of \(g(x)\) and let \(H(y)\) be antiderivative of \(h(y)\) then rewriting them as derivatives using Leibniz notation, we have: \begin {equation} \label {eq:2.2.2} \frac{dH}{dy} = h(y) \quad\text{and}\quad \frac{dG}{dx} = g(x) \end{equation} Then, from the chain rule \[ {d\over dx} \Bigg[ H \Big( y(x) \Big) \Bigg] = \frac{dH}{dy} \cdot \frac{dy}{dx} = h(y) \frac{dy}{dx} \] Therefore (2.2.1) is equivalent to \[ h(y) \frac{dy}{dx} = g(x) \Longrightarrow \frac{dH}{dy} \cdot \frac{dy}{dx} = \frac{dG}{dx} \Longrightarrow {d\over dx} \Big[ H(y) \Big] = \frac{d}{dx} \Big[ G(x) \Big] \] Finding the antiderivative of both sides yields \begin{align} \label{eq:2.2.3} {d\over dx} \Big[ H(y) \Big] = & \frac{d}{dx} \Big[ G(x) \Big] \nonumber \\ \int {d\over dx} \Big[ H(y) \Big] dx = & \int \frac{d}{dx} \Big[ G(x) \Big] dx \nonumber \\ H(y) = & G(x) + c \\ \end{align}
Although we derived this equation on the assumption that \(y\) is a solution of (2.2.1), but now we can take a different perspective: Ant differentiable functions \(y\) that satisfies (2.2.3) for some constant \(c\) is a solution of (2.2.1). To verify this, we differentiate both sides of (2.2.3), using the chain rule on the left, to obtain \begin{align*} H\Big(y(x)\Big) = & G(x) + c \\ \frac{d}{dx} \Bigg[ H(y) \Bigg] = & \frac{d}{dx} \Bigg[ G(x) + c \Bigg] \\ \frac{dH}{dy} \cdot \frac{dy}{dx} = & \frac{dG}{dx} + 0 \\ h(y) \frac{dy}{dx} = & g(x) \end{align*}
In conclusion, to solve (2.2.1) it suffices to find functions \(G=G(x)\) and \(H=H(y)\) that satisfy (2.2.2). Then any differentiable function \(y=y(x)\) that satisfies (2.2.3) is a solution of (2.2.1).
Solve the equation \[ \frac{dy}{dx} =x(1+y^2)\]
Solution
 Separating variables then finding the antiderivative: \begin{align*} \frac{dy}{dx} = & x(1+y^2) \\ \frac{1}{1+y^2}\frac{dy}{dx} = & x \\ \int \Bigg( \frac{1}{1+y^2}\frac{dy}{dx} \Bigg) dx = & \int \Big( x \Big) dx \\ \int \frac{1}{1+\underset{\tan \theta}{\underbrace{y}}^2} \underset{\sec^2 \theta}{\underbrace{dy}} = & \int x dx \\ \int \frac{\sec^2 \theta}{1+\tan^2 \theta} d\theta = & \int x dx \\ \int d\theta = & \int x dx \\ \theta = & \frac{1}{2}x^2 + c \\ \tan^{-1} y = & \frac{1}{2}x^2 + c \\ y = & \tan \left(\frac{1}{2}x^2 + c\right) \\ \end{align*}
  1. Find the general solution \begin {equation} \label {eq:2.2.4} \frac{dy}{dx}=-{x\over y}\end {equation}
  2. Solve the initial value problem (IVP) \begin {equation} \label {eq:2.2.5} \frac{dy}{dx}=-{x\over y} \quad\quad y(1)=1 \end {equation}
  3. Solve the initial value problem (IVP) \begin {equation} \label {eq:2.2.6} \frac{dy}{dx}=-{x\over y} \quad\quad y(1)=-2 \end {equation}
Solution
  1.  Separating variables in (2.2.4) then finding the antiderivative: \begin{align*} \frac{dy}{dx} = & -\frac{x}{y} \\ y\frac{dy}{dx} = & -x \\ \int \Bigg( y\frac{dy}{dx} \Bigg) dx = & \int -x dx \\ \int y dy = & - \int x dx \\ \frac{1}{2}y^2 = & -\frac{1}{2}x^2 + k \\ \frac{1}{2}y^2 + \frac{1}{2}x^2 = & + k \\ y^2 + x^2 = & 2 k \\ y^2 + x^2 = & c \\ \end{align*} Since the arbitrary constant is equal to positive quantities (squares being added), then the constant itself represents a positive value so we can replace it with an expression that is always positive. We'll use \( a^2 \) as our arbitrary constant, so we obtain: \begin{align} \label {eq:2.2.7} y^2 + x^2 = & c \Longrightarrow y^2 + x^2 = a^2 \end{align} This is an implicit representation of our solution \( y \) but we can solve it explicitly which results in 2 different differentiable functions: \begin{equation}\label {eq:2.2.8} y(x) = \sqrt{a^2-x^2} \quad\text{or}\quad y(x) = -\sqrt{a^2-x^2} \end{equation} The solution curves defined by \( y(x) = \sqrt{a^2-x^2} \) are semicircles above the \(x\)-axis and those defined by \( y(x) = -\sqrt{a^2-x^2} \) are semicircles below the \(x\)-axis (Figure 2.2.1). The interval of validity for both is \begin{equation}\label {eq:2.2.9} (-a,a) \end{equation}
    Solve (2.2.7) explicitly for \(y\) to get (2.2.8)
    Find the domain of (2.2.8)
  2. To solve (2.2.5) notice the initial condition \(y(1)=1\) indicates a positive value, so the function \( y(x) = \sqrt{a^2-x^2} \) will be used. Substituting the initial condition and solving for the arbitrary constant: \begin{align*} y(x) = \sqrt{a^2-x^2} \Longrightarrow \underset{1}{\underbrace{y(1)}} = & \sqrt{a^2-1^2} \\ 1 = & \sqrt{a^2-1} \\ 1^2 = & a^2-1 \Longrightarrow a^2 = 2 \end{align*} Substituting \( a^2 = 2 \) into (2.2.8), we obtain the solution to the IVP: \[ y=\sqrt {2-x^2}\] With interval of validity \((-\sqrt{2},\sqrt{2})\)
  3. To solve (2.2.6) notice the initial condition \(y(1)=-2\) indicates a negative value, so the function \( y(x) = -\sqrt{a^2-x^2} \) will be used. Substituting the initial condition and solving for the arbitrary constant: \begin{align*} y(x) = -\sqrt{a^2-x^2} \Longrightarrow \underset{-2}{\underbrace{y(1)}} = & \sqrt{a^2-1^2} \\ -2 = & -\sqrt{a^2-1} \\ 4 = & a^2-1 \Longrightarrow a^2 = 5 \end{align*} Substituting \( a^2 = 5 \) into (2.2.8), we obtain the solution to the IVP: \[ y=-\sqrt {5-x^2}\] With interval of validity \((-\sqrt{5},\sqrt{5})\)
Implicit Solutions of Separable Equations
In Examples  2.2.1 and 2.2.2 we were able to solve the equation \(H(y)=G(x)+c\) to obtain explicit formulas for solutions of the given separable differential equations. As we’ll see in the next example, this isn’t always possible. In this situation we must broaden our definition of a solution of a separable equation. The next theorem provides the basis for this modification. We omit the proof, which requires a result from advanced calculus called as the implicit function theorem.
Theorem 2.2.1
Suppose \(g=g(x)\) is continous on \((a,b)\) and \(h=h(y)\) are continuous on \((c,d)\). Let \(G\) be an antiderivative of \(g\) on \((a,b)\) and let \(H\) be an antiderivative of \(h\) on \((c,d)\). Let \(x_0\) be an arbitrary point in \((a,b)\), let \(y_0\) be a point in \((c,d)\) such that \(h(y_0)\ne 0\), and define \begin {equation} \label {eq:2.2.10} c=H(y_0)-G(x_0)\end {equation} Then there’s a function \(y=y(x)\) defined on some open interval \((a_1,b_1),\) where \(a\le a_1<x_0<b_1\le b\), such that \(y(x_0)=y_0\) and \begin {equation} \label {eq:2.2.11} H(y)=G(x)+c \end {equation} for \(a_1<x<b_1\). Therefore \(y(x) \) is a solution of the IVP \begin {equation} \label {eq:2.2.12} h(y) \frac{dy}{dx} =g(x),\quad y(x_0)=x_0\end {equation}
It’s convenient to say that (2.2.11) with arbitrary constant \(c\) is an implicit solution of \(h(y) \frac{dy}{dx} = g(x)\). Curves defined by (2.2.11) are integral curves of \(h(y) \frac{dy}{dx} = g(x)\). If \(c\) satisfies (2.2.10), we’ll say that (2.2.11) is an implicit solution of the IVP (2.2.12). However, keep these points in mind:
  • For some choices of \(c\) there may not be any differentiable functions \(y\) that satisfy (2.2.11)
  • The function \(y=y(x)\) in (2.2.11) is a solution of \(\displaystyle h(y) \frac{dy}{dx} =g(x)\)
  1. Find implicit solutions of \begin {equation} \label {eq:2.2.13} \frac{dy}{dx} = {2x+1\over 5y^4+1}\end {equation}
  2. Find an implicit solution of \begin {equation} \label {eq:2.2.14} \frac{dy}{dx} = {2x+1\over 5y^4+1},\quad y(2)=1\end {equation}
Solution
  1. Separating variables in (2.2.13) yields \begin{align}\label {eq:2.2.15} \frac{dy}{dx} = & \frac{2x+1}{5y^4+1} \nonumber \\ (5y^4+1)\frac{dy}{dx} = & 2x+1 \nonumber \\ \int (5y^4+1)\frac{dy}{dx} dx = & \int \left(2x+1\right) dx \nonumber \\ 5\int y^4 dy + \int dy = & 2\int x dx + \int dx \nonumber \\ 5\left(\frac{1}{5}y^5\right) + y = & 2\left(\frac{1}{2}x^2\right) + x + c \nonumber \\ \underset{H(y)}{\underbrace{y^5 + y}} = & \underset{G(x)}{\underbrace{x^2 + x}}+ c \end{align} We call this the implicit solution and notice in this case it is not possible to solve explicitly for \( y \)
  2. We already have our implicit solution (2.2.15) so we just substitute in our initial condition \( y(2)=1 \) to solve for the constant \( c \) \begin{align*} y^5 + y = & x^2 + x + c \\ \Big[ y(x) \Big]^5 + y(x) = & x^2 + x + c \\ \Big[ \underset{1}{\underbrace{y(2)}} \Big]^5 + y(2) = & (2)^2 + (2) + c \\ \big[ 1 \big]^5 + 1 = & 4 + 2 + c \\ 2 = & 6 + c \\ c = -4 \end{align*} Substituting in \( c = -4 \) into (2.2.15) \begin{equation*} y^5 + y = x^2 + x -4 \end{equation*}

Constant Solutions of Separable Equations
Both linear and nonlinear equations of the form \( \frac{dy}{dx}=g(x)p(y) \) are always separable. Simply divide by \( p(y) \) and find the antiderivative of both sides: \begin{align*} \frac{dy}{dx} = & g(x)p(y) \\ \frac{1}{p(y)}\frac{dy}{dx} = & g(x) \\ \int \Bigg( \frac{1}{p(y)}\frac{dy}{dx} \Bigg) dx = & \int g(x) dx \\ \end{align*} However, the division by \( p(y) \) will only work if \( p(y) \) has no zeros. When \( p(y) \) has a zero, we treat it similarly to how we treated trivial solutions, first considering the trivial solution case individually, and then assuming \( y \) is not equal to the individual values. We will do the same for constant solutions (equilibrium solutions)
Find the general solution \begin {equation} \label {eq:2.2.16} \frac{dy}{dx} = 2xy^2 \end{equation}
Solution
First notice (2.1.16) has a trivial solution \( y(x) \equiv 0 \) which we can verify by substitution: \begin{align*} \frac{dy}{dx} = & 2xy^2 \\ \frac{d}{dx} \Big( 0 \Big) = & 2x \Big(0\Big)^2 \quad \checkmark \\ \end{align*}
Now we seek nontrivial solution where \( y\ne0 \) and divide by \( y^2 \) to separate the variables \begin{align} \frac{dy}{dx} = & 2xy^2 \nonumber \\ \frac{1}{y^2}\frac{dy}{dx} = & 2x \nonumber \\ \int \Big( \frac{1}{y^2}\frac{dy}{dx} \Big) dx = & \int \Big( 2x \Big) dx \nonumber \\ \int y^{-2} dy = & 2\int x dx \nonumber \\ -1y^{-1} = & 2\left(\frac{1}{2}x^2\right) + c \nonumber \\ -\frac{1}{y} = & x^2 + k \nonumber \\ \frac{1}{y} = & - x^2 \underset{c}{\underbrace{-k}} \nonumber \\ \frac{1}{y} = & c - x^2 \nonumber \\ 1 = & y \left( c - x^2 \right) \nonumber \\ y = & \frac{1}{c - x^2} \end{align}
We’ve shown that if \(y\) is a solution of (2.2.16) that is not identically zero, then \(y\) must be of the form of the one-parameter family given by (2.2.17). Notice that regardless of any choice of \( c \), there is no way to obtain the trivial solution. When a solution exists that is not from the one-parameter family. we call it a singular solution. Thus our general solution includes both: \begin{equation*} y(x)=0 \quad \quad y(x) = \frac{1}{c - x^2} \end{equation*}

Find the general solution \begin {equation} \label {eq:2.2.18} \frac{dy}{dx} = {1\over2}x(1-y^2) \end {equation}
Solution
Although there is no trivial solution for (2.2.18), there are other constant solutions. Notice that \( y=1 \) and \( y=-1 \) are both solutions: \begin{align*} \frac{d}{dx} \Big( 1 \Big) = {1\over2}x \Big( 1- (1)^2 \Big) \Longrightarrow 0 = & {1\over2}x \Big( 0 \Big) \quad \checkmark \\ \end{align*} and \begin{align*} \frac{d}{dx} \Big( -1 \Big) = {1\over2}x \Big( 1- (-1)^2 \Big) \Longrightarrow 0 = & {1\over2}x \Big( 0 \Big) \quad \checkmark \\ \end{align*} These constant solutions are nontrivial and called equilibrium solutions. Now we can proceed to separate variables here, which requires dividing by \( (1-y^2) \), which is fine to do so long as we assume \( y \ne 1 \) and ( y \ne -1 \) \begin{align*} \frac{dy}{dx} = & \frac{1}{2}x(1-y^2) \\ \frac{dy}{dx} = & -\frac{1}{2}x(y^2-1) \\ \frac{1}{y^2-1}\frac{dy}{dx} = & \frac{1}{2}x \\ \int \Bigg( \frac{1}{y^2-1}\frac{dy}{dx} \Bigg) dx = &\int \frac{1}{2}x dx \\ \int \frac{1}{(y-1)(y+1)}dy = & \frac{1}{2}\int x dx \\ \end{align*} Finding the antiderivative on the right is simple enough using the power rule but the antiderivative on the left is more complicated, requiring partial fraction decomposition: \begin{align*} \frac{1}{(y-1)(y+1)} = \frac{A}{y-1}+\frac{B}{y+1} = \frac{(A+B)y+(A-B)}{(y-1)(y+1)} \\ \end{align*} To make the numerators \( 1 = (A+B)y+(A-B) \) equal, equate the coefficients to obtain the system of equations which we can add to find \(A\) \begin{align*} \left\{ \begin{array}{r l} A + B = & 0 \\ A - B = & 1 \\ \end{array} \right. = \left\{ \begin{array}{r l} B = & -A \\ 2A = & 1 \\ \end{array} \right. = \left\{ \begin{array}{r l} B = & -\frac{1}{2} \\ A = & \frac{1}{2} \\ \end{array} \right. \end{align*} Substituting in these values we can decompose the fraction on the left: \begin{align*} \int \frac{1}{(y-1)(y+1)}dy = & \frac{1}{2}\int x dx \\ \frac{1}{2}\int \frac{1}{y-1}dy - \frac{1}{2}\int \frac{1}{y+1}dy = & \frac{1}{2}\int x dx \\ \int \frac{1}{\underset{u}{\underbrace{y-1}}} \underset{du}{\underbrace{dy}} -\int \frac{1}{\underset{v}{\underbrace{y+1}}} \underset{dv}{\underbrace{dy}} = & -\int x dx \\ \int \frac{1}{u}du - \int \frac{1}{v}dv = & -\int x dx \\ \ln \left| u \right| - \ln \left| v \right| = & -\frac{1}{2} x^2 + k \\ \ln \left| \frac{y-1}{y+1} \right| = & -\frac{1}{2}x^2 + k \\ \left| \frac{y-1}{y+1} \right| = & e^{-\frac{1}{2}x^2} e^{k} = Ke^{-\frac{1}{2}x^2}\\ \frac{y-1}{y+1} = Ke^{-\frac{1}{2}x^2} \quad & \text{or} \quad \frac{y-1}{y+1} = -Ke^{-\frac{1}{2}x^2} \\ \frac{y-1}{y+1} =& ce^{-\frac{1}{2}x^2} \end{align*} So we can represent this one-parameter family by simply: \begin{align*} \frac{y-1}{y+1} =& ce^{-\frac{1}{2}x^2} \end{align*} Although this representation is currently implicit, we can solve explicitly for \( y \) \begin {equation} \label {eq:2.2.19} y={1+ce^{-x^2/2}\over 1-ce^{-x^2/2}}\end {equation}
We’ve now shown that if \(y\) is a solution of (2.2.18) that is not identically equal to \(\pm 1\), then \(y\) must be as in (2.2.19). By substituting (2.2.19) into (2.2.18) you can verify that (2.2.19) is a solution of (2.2.18). Thus, the solutions of (2.2.18) are \(y\equiv 1\), \(y\equiv -1\) and the functions of the form (2.2.19). Note that the constant solution \(y \equiv 1\) can be obtained from this formula by taking \(c=0\); however, the other constant solution, \(y \equiv -1\), can’t be obtained in this way.

Differences Between Linear and Nonlinear Equations
Theorem  2.1.2 states that if \(p\) and \(f\) are continuous on \((a,b)\) then every solution of \[ \frac{dy}{dx} + p(x)y=f(x) \] on \((a,b)\) can be obtained by choosing a value for the constant \(c\) in the general solution, and if \(x_0\) is any point in \((a,b)\) and \(y_0\) is arbitrary, then the initial value problem \[ \frac{dy}{dx} + p(x)y=f(x),\quad y(x_0)=y_0 \] has a solution on \((a,b)\).
The not true for nonlinear equations. First, we saw in Examples  2.2.4 and 2.2.5 that a nonlinear equation may have solutions that can’t be obtained by choosing a specific value of a constant appearing in a one-parameter family of solutions. Second, it is in general impossible to determine the interval of validity of a solution to an initial value problem for a nonlinear equation by simply examining the equation, since the interval of validity may depend on the initial condition. For instance, in Example  2.2.2 we saw that the solution of \[ {dy\over dx}=-{x\over y},\quad y(x_0)=y_0 \] is valid on \((-a,a)\), where \(a=\sqrt {x_0^2+y_0^2}\).
Solve the initial value problem \[ \frac{dy}{dx} = 2xy^2, \quad y(0)=y_0 \] and determine the interval of validity of the solution.
Solution
First suppose \(y_0\ne 0\). From Example  2.2.4 , we know that \(y\) must be of the form \begin {equation} \label {eq:2.2.20} y=-{1\over x^2+c}\end {equation} Imposing the initial condition shows that \(c=-1/y_0\). Substituting this into (2.2.20) and rearranging terms yields the solution \[ y= {y_0\over 1-y_0x^2}\] This is also the solution if \(y_0=0\). If \(y_0<0\), the denominator isn’t zero for any value of \(x\), so the the solution is valid on \((-\infty ,\infty )\). If \(y_0>0\), the solution is valid only on \((-1/\sqrt {y_0},1/\sqrt {y_0})\).
