8.5 Discontinuous Forcing Functions
Discontinuous Forcing Functions without Laplace
We’ll now consider IVPs where \(a\), \(b\), and \(c\) are constants (\(a\ne 0\)) and \(f(t)\) is piecewise continuous on \([0,\infty )\) \begin {equation} \label {eq:8.5.1} a\ddot{y}+b\dot{y}+cy=f(t) \quad y(0)=k_0 \quad \dot{y}(0)=k_1 \end {equation} Problems of this kind occur in situations where the input to a physical system undergoes instantaneous changes, as when a switch is turned on or off or the forces acting on the system change abruptly.
It can be shown (Exercises 23 and 24) that the differential equation in (8.5.1) has no solutions on an open interval that contains a jump discontinuity of \(f(t)\). Therefore we must define what we mean by a solution of (8.5.1) on \([0,\infty )\) in the case where \(f(t)\) has jump discontinuities. The next theorem motivates our definition.
Theorem 8.5.1 Suppose \(a,b\), and \(c\) are constants \((a\ne 0),\) and \(f(t)\) is piecewise continuous on \([0,\infty ).\) with jump discontinuities at \(t_1,\) …, \(t_n,\) where \[ 0<t_1<\cdots <t_n\] Let \(k_0\) and \(k_1\) be arbitrary real numbers. Then there is a unique function \(y\) defined on \([0,\infty )\) with these properties:
  1.   \(y(0)=k_0\) and \(\dot{y}(0)=k_1\)
  2.   \(y\) and \(\dot{y}\) are continuous on \([0,\infty )\)
  3.   \(\ddot{y}\) is defined on every open subinterval of \([0,\infty)\) that does not contain \(t_1,\) …, \(t_n\)
  4.   \( a\ddot{y}+b\dot{y}+cy=f(t) \) on every such subinterval
  5.   \(\ddot{y}\) has limits from the right and left at \(t_1,\) …\(,\) \(t_n\)
We define the function \(y\) of Theorem 8.5.1 to be the solution of the IVP (8.5.1)
We begin by considering IVPs where the forcing function has a single jump discontinuity at \(a_1\). \begin {equation} \label {eq:8.5.2} a\ddot{y}+b\dot{y}+cy=\left \{ \begin {array}{cl} f_0(t) &0\le t<a_1\\ f_1(t) &t\ge a_1 \end {array} \right . \quad y(0)=k_0 \quad \dot{y}(0)=k_1 \end {equation}
We can solve (8.5.2) by these steps:
  1. Find the solution \(y_0\) of the IVP \[ a\ddot{y}+b\dot{y}+cy=f_0(t) \quad y(0)=k_0\quad \dot{y}(0)=k_1\]
  2. Compute \(c_0=y_0(t_1)\) and \(c_1=\dot{y_0}(t_1)\)
  3. Find the solution \(y_1\) of the IVP \[ a\ddot{y}+b\dot{y}+cy=f_1(t) \quad y(t_1)=c_0 \quad \dot{y}(t_1)=c_1\]
  4. Obtain the solution \(y\) of (8.5.2) as \[ y=\left \{ \begin {array}{cl} y_0(t) &0\le t<a_1\\ y_1(t) &t\ge a_1 \end {array} \right. \]
It is shown in Exercise 23 that \(\dot{y}\) exists and is continuous at \(a_1\). The next example illustrates this procedure.



Solve the IVP without using Laplace transform \begin {equation} \label {eq:8.5.3} \ddot{y}+y=f(t) \quad y(0)=2 \quad \dot{y}(0)=-1 \end {equation} \[ f(t)=\left \{ \begin {array}{rl} 1 &0\le t<\displaystyle {\pi \over 2}\\ -1 &t\ge \displaystyle {{\pi \over 2}} \end {array} \right. \]
Solution
  1. We'll start by solving the complementary homogeneous equation \[ \ddot{y}+y=0 \]
    Just as in Section 5.2 we seek solutions of the form \(y=e^{rt}\)
    Solve \(\ddot{y}+y=0 \) to obtain exponential solutions
    The characteristic equation yields imaginary roots \(r=\pm i\) so we obtain the solutions \[ y_1=e^{it} \quad y_2=e^{-it} \]
    Use Euler's formula to write the solutions \(y_1=e^{it} \quad y_2=e^{-it}\) in terms of sine and cosine
    From Euler's formula we have the solutions \[ y_1= \cos t + i \sin t \quad y_2= \cos t - i \sin t \]
    Use linear combinations to write the solutions \(y_1=e^{it} \quad y_2=e^{-it}\) without \(i\)
    Finally we use a linear combination of the solutions to eliminate the imaginary number to obtain a fundamental set of solutions \[ y_1= \cos t \quad y_2= \sin t \]
  2. Now we will solve the IVP using only the first piece of the forcing function when \(0 \le t < \frac{\pi}{2} \) \begin {equation*} \ddot{y}+y=1 \quad y(0)=2 \quad \dot{y}(0)=-1 \end {equation*} We already found a fundamental set so now we need to find a particular solution. Using the Method of Undetermined Coefficients, we seek a particular solution of the same form as the forcing function, which is just a constant \[ y_p = A \] Differentiating an arbitrary constant \(A\) twice we obtain \[ \dot{y}_p = 0 \quad \text{and} \quad \ddot{y}_p = 0 \] Substituting it back in to our IVP we have \begin{align*} \ddot{y}_p+y_p = & \, 1 \\ 0+A = & \, 1 \\ A = & \, 1 \\ \end{align*} So we obtain our particular solution \(y_p=1\) so we can finally write our general solution of the IVP \[ y = 1 + c_1 \cos t + c_2 \sin t \]
  3. We'll now apply our initial conditions to find our constants \(\dot{y}(0)=-1 \) yields \begin{align*} y(0) = & \, 1 + c_1 \cos 0 + c_2 \sin 0 \\ 2 = & \, 1 + c_1 \Longrightarrow c_1 = 1 \\ \end{align*} Substituting in \(c_1=1\) we have \[ y = 1 + \cos t + c_2 \sin t \] And differentiating we obtain \[ \dot{y} = -\sin t + c_2 \cos t \] Applying the initial condition \(\dot{y}(0)=-1 \) yields \begin{align*} \dot{y}(0) = & \, -\sin 0 + c_2 \cos 0 \Longrightarrow c_2 = -1 \\ \end{align*} So our solution on \(0 \le t < \frac{\pi}{2} \) is given by \[ y = 1 + \cos t - \sin t \]
  4. But we are not done solving the IVP because we have only solved it on the first interval. To solve it for the second interval, we first need to obtain appropriate initial conditions, so we will evaluate this solution and its derivative at the point where the step occurs, \(t=\frac{\pi}{2}\) to get initial conditions for the second piece of the forcing function \begin{align*} y(\textstyle{\frac{\pi}{2}}) = & \, 1 + \cos \textstyle{\frac{\pi}{2}} - \sin \textstyle{\frac{\pi}{2}} \\ = & \, 1 + 0 - 1 \\ = & \, 0 \\ \end{align*} And the derivative of our solution is \(\dot{y} = -\sin t - \cos t\) and evaluating it at the step \(t=\frac{\pi}{2}\) we obtain \begin{align*} \dot{y}(\textstyle{\frac{\pi}{2}}) = & \, -\sin \textstyle{\frac{\pi}{2}} - \cos \textstyle{\frac{\pi}{2}} \\ = & \, 0 - 1 \\ = & \, -1 \\ \end{align*} Thus the initial conditions for our second IVP are \[ y(\textstyle{\frac{\pi}{2}}) = 0 \quad \dot{y}(\textstyle{\frac{\pi}{2}}) =-1 \] And applying the second piece of the forcing function we obtain the second IVP \begin {equation*} \ddot{y}+y=-1 \quad y(\textstyle{\frac{\pi}{2}})=0 \quad \dot{y}(\textstyle{\frac{\pi}{2}})=-1 \end {equation*}
  5. We already solved the complementary solution above and found the fundamental set \[ \left\{ y_1 , y_2 \right\} = \left\{ \cos t , \sin t \right\} \] So now we seek a particular solution again using the Method of Undetermined Coefficients, of the same form as the forcing function, which is just a constant \[ y_p = A \] Differentiating an arbitrary constant \(A\) twice we obtain \[ \dot{y}_p = 0 \quad \text{and} \quad \ddot{y}_p = 0 \] Substituting it back in to our IVP we have \begin{align*} \ddot{y}_p+y_p = & \, -1 \\ 0+A = & \, -1 \\ A = & \, -1 \\ \end{align*} So we obtain our particular solution \(y_p=-1\) so we can finally write our general solution of the IVP \[ y = -1 + c_1 \cos t + c_2 \sin t \]
  6. We'll now apply our initial conditions to find our constants \(\dot{y}(\textstyle{\frac{\pi}{2}})=0 \) yields \begin{align*} y(\textstyle{\frac{\pi}{2}}) = & \, -1 + c_1 \cos \textstyle{\frac{\pi}{2}} + c_2 \sin \textstyle{\frac{\pi}{2}} \\ 0 = & \, -1 + 0 + c_2 \Longrightarrow c_2 = 1 \\ \end{align*} Substituting in \(c_2=1\) we have \[ y = -1 + c_1\cos t + \sin t \] And differentiating we obtain \[ \dot{y} = -c_1\sin t + \cos t \] Applying the initial condition \(\dot{y}(\textstyle{\frac{\pi}{2}})=-1 \) yields \begin{align*} \dot{y}(\textstyle{\frac{\pi}{2}}) = & \, -c_1\sin \textstyle{\frac{\pi}{2}} + \cos \textstyle{\frac{\pi}{2}} \\ -1 = & \, -c_1 + 0 \Longrightarrow c_1 = 1 \\ \end{align*} So our solution on \(t \ge \frac{\pi}{2} \) is given by \[ y = -1 + \cos t + \sin t \]
  7. Finally putting the pieces together we obtain the piecewise solution of (8.5.3) \begin {equation} \label {eq:8.5.4} y=\left \{ \begin {array}{rl} 1+\cos t-\sin t &0\le t<\displaystyle {{\pi \over 2}} \\ -1+\cos t+\sin t &t\ge \displaystyle {{\pi \over 2}} \end {array} \right. \end {equation}
Discontinuous Forcing Functions with Laplace
In Example 8.5.1 we solved an equation of the form of (8.5.2) without the use of Laplace transforms. \begin {equation*} a\ddot{y}+b\dot{y}+cy=\left \{ \begin {array}{cl} f_0(t) &0\le t<a_1\\ f_1(t) &t\ge a_1 \end {array} \right . \quad y(0)=k_0 \quad \dot{y}(0)=k_1 \tag{2} \end {equation*} It works out to essentially solving one separate IVP for each separate piece of the forcing function. In this case it was simple enough since the forcing function was pretty simple, just constants. You can imagine how much longer this solution process could get if our forcing function contains more complicated terms like in the later sections where we studied the Method of Undetermined Coefficients (Sections 5.4 and 5.5). However, if we apply Laplace transforms instead we can solve equations of this form more directly.
Recall from Section 8.4 that if \(f_0(t)\) and \(f_1(t)\) are defined on \([0,\infty )\), we can rewrite the forcing function in (8.5.2) in terms of the Unit Step Function as \[ a\ddot{y}+b\dot{y}+cy=f_0(t)+\Big (f_1(t)-f_0(t)\Big ) \, u(t-a_1)\] Thus rewriting the IVP in (8.5.2) in a form where we can apply the method of Laplace transforms. \[ a\ddot{y}+b\dot{y}+cy=f_0(t)+\Big (f_1(t)-f_0(t)\Big ) \, u(t-a_1) \quad y(0)=k_0\quad \dot{y}(0)=k_1\] We’ll now solve the same problem considered in Example 8.5.1 by this method.



Solve the IVP using Laplace transform \begin {equation} \label {eq:8.5.5} \ddot{y}+y=f(t) \quad y(0)=2 \quad\dot{y}(0)=-1 \end {equation} \[ f(t)=\left \{ \begin {array}{cl} \phantom {-}1 &0\le t<\displaystyle {{\pi \over 2}} \\ -1 &t\ge \displaystyle {{\pi \over 2}} \end {array} \right. \]
Solution
  1. We'll start by rewriting the forcing function \(f(t)\) in terms of the unit step function \begin{align*} f(t) = & \, 1 + (-1-1) \cdot u(t-\textstyle{\frac{\pi}{2}}) \\ = & \, 1 -2 u(t-\textstyle{\frac{\pi}{2}}) \end{align*} So we can rewrite our IVP (8.5.5) as \begin {equation*} \ddot{y}+y=1-2 u(t-\textstyle{\frac{\pi}{2}}) \quad y(0)=2 \quad\dot{y}(0)=-1 \end {equation*} Now we can solve by taking the Laplace transform of both sides of the equation \begin {align} \label {eq:8.5.6} \ddot{y}+y= & \,1-2 u(t-\textstyle{\frac{\pi}{2}}) \nonumber\\ {\cal L} \Big\{ \ddot{y}+y \Big\} = & \, {\cal L} \Big\{ 1-2 u(t-\textstyle{\frac{\pi}{2}})\Big\}\nonumber\\ {\cal L} \big\{ \ddot{y} \big\} + {\cal L} \big\{ y \big\} = & \, {\cal L} \big\{ 1\big\} - \, {\cal L} \Big\{ 2u(t-\textstyle{\frac{\pi}{2}})\Big\}\nonumber\\ \overbrace{s^2{\cal L} \big\{ {y} \big\} - s \, y(0) - \dot{y}(0)} + {\cal L} \big\{ y \big\} = & \, {\cal L} \big\{ 1\big\} - 2 \, {\cal L} \Big\{ u(t-\textstyle{\frac{\pi}{2}})\Big\}\nonumber\\ s^2 Y(s) - 2s +1 + Y(s) = & \, \frac{1}{s} - 2 \cdot \frac{e^{-\frac{\pi}{2}s}}{s}\nonumber\\ (s^2 + 1 )Y(s) - (2s -1) = & \, \frac{1-2e^{-\frac{\pi}{2}s}}{s}\nonumber\\ (s^2 + 1 )Y(s) = & \, \frac{1-2e^{-\frac{\pi}{2}s}}{s} + (2s -1)\nonumber\\ Y(s) = & \, \frac{1-2e^{-\frac{\pi}{2}s}+ s(2s -1)}{s(s^2 + 1 )} \end {align} Now to find \(y(t)\) we take the inverse Laplace transform of \(Y(s)\) \begin {align*} {\cal L}^{-1} \Big[ Y(s) \Big] = & \, {\cal L}^{-1} \Bigg[ \frac{1-2e^{-\frac{\pi}{2}s}+ s(2s -1)}{s(s^2 + 1 )} \Bigg] \\ y(t) = & \, {\cal L}^{-1} \Bigg[ \underset{G_{_1}(s)}{\underbrace{\frac{1}{s(s^2 + 1 )}}} \Bigg] -2 \, {\cal L}^{-1} \Bigg[ e^{-\frac{\pi}{2}s} \cdot \underset{G_{_1}(s)}{\underbrace{\frac{1}{s(s^2 + 1 )}}} \Bigg] + {\cal L}^{-1} \Bigg[ \underset{G_{_2}(s)}{\underbrace{\frac{2s -1}{s^2 + 1 }}} \Bigg] \\ \end {align*}
  2. Just as in Section 8.4 we divide this up into three separate problems and we will evaluate the inverse Laplace Transform of each one separately. Starting with \(G_{_1}(s)\) which is a basic one and does not require shifting but does require PFD \begin{align}\label {eq:8.5.7} g_1(t) = & \, {\cal L}^{-1}\Big [ G_{_1}(s) \Big ] \nonumber\\ = & \, {\cal L}^{-1}\left [ \frac{1}{s(s^2 + 1 )} \right ] \nonumber\\ = & \, {\cal L}^{-1}\left [ \frac{A}{s} \right ] + {\cal L}^{-1}\left [ \frac{Bs+C}{s^2 + 1} \right ] \end{align}
    Solve for \(A,\) \(B\) and \(C\) using PFD \(\, \displaystyle \frac{1}{s(s^2 + 1 )} = \frac{A}{s} + \frac{Bs+C}{s^2 + 1} \)
    Substituting \(A,\) \(B\) and \(C\) back in we obtain \begin{align*} g_1(t) = & \, {\cal L}^{-1}\left [ \frac{1}{s} \right ] - {\cal L}^{-1}\left [ \frac{s}{s^2 + 1} \right ] \\ = & \, \quad 1 \quad-\quad \cos t \\ \end{align*}



    For the next term \(e^{-\frac{\pi}{2}s} \,G_{_1}(s)\) we will have to apply the Second Shifting Theorem (Theorem 8.4.2) and we already found \(g_1(t)\) so we identify \(a=\frac{\pi}{2}\) and substitute to obtain \begin{align*} {\cal L}^{-1} \Big[ e^{-\frac{\pi}{2}s} \, G_{_1}(s) \Big] = & g_{_1}(t-\textstyle{\frac{\pi}{2}}) \, u(t-\textstyle{\frac{\pi}{2}}) \\ = & \Big( 1-\cos(t-\textstyle{\frac{\pi}{2}}) \Big) \, u(t-\textstyle{\frac{\pi}{2}}) \\ = & \Big( 1-\big( \cos t \underset{0}{\underbrace{\cos \textstyle{\frac{\pi}{2}}}} + \sin t \underset{1}{\underbrace{\sin \textstyle{\frac{\pi}{2}}}} \big) \Big) \, u(t-\textstyle{\frac{\pi}{2}}) \\ = & \big( 1-\sin t \big) \, u(t-\textstyle{\frac{\pi}{2}}) \\ \end{align*}



    The last term \(G_{_2}(s)\) does not require shifting but does require or PFD \begin{align*} g_{_2}(t) = & \, {\cal L}^{-1}\Big [ G_{_2}(s) \Big ] \nonumber\\ = & \, {\cal L}^{-1}\left [ \frac{2s-1}{s^2 + 1} \right ] \nonumber\\ = & \, 2 \, {\cal L}^{-1}\left [ \frac{s}{s^2 + 1} \right ] - {\cal L}^{-1}\left [ \frac{1}{s^2 + 1} \right ] \nonumber\\ = & \, \quad 2 \, \cos t \quad-\quad \sin t \nonumber\\ \end{align*}



  3. Finally substituting it back in \begin{array}{cccc} y(t) &=& \, {\cal L}^{-1}\Big [ G_{_1}(s) \Big ] &-& 2\, {\cal L}^{-1}\Big [ e^{-\frac{\pi}{2}s} \, G_{_1}(s) \Big ] &+& {\cal L}^{-1}\Big [ G_{_2}(s) \Big ] \\ &=& \, g_{_1}(t) &-& 2\, g_{_1}(t-\textstyle{\frac{\pi}{2}}) \, u(t-\textstyle{\frac{\pi}{2}}) &+& g_{_2}(t) \\ &=& \, 1 - \cos t &-& 2\, \big( 1-\sin t \big) \, u(t-\textstyle{\frac{\pi}{2}}) &+& 2 \cos t - \sin t \\ \end{array} Simplifying by combining like terms we obtain \begin {equation*} y(t) = 1 + \cos t - \sin t - 2 \big( 1-\sin t \big) \, u(t-\textstyle{\frac{\pi}{2}}) \end {equation*} Then to write it as a piecewise function we have \begin {equation*} \begin {array}{rl} y_0(t) &=& 1 + \cos t - \sin t \\ y_1(t) - y_0(t) &=& - 2 \big( 1-\sin t \big) = -2 + 2\sin t\\ \end {array} \end {equation*} Solving for \(y_1(t)\) we have \begin {align*} y_1(t) = & \, -2 + 2\sin t + y_0(t) \\ = & \, -2 + 2\sin t + 1 + \cos t - \sin t \\ = & \, -1 + \cos t + \sin t \\ \end {align*} Then writing in piecewise form we have \[ y(t)=\left \{ \begin {array}{rl} 1 + \cos t - \sin t &0\le t<\displaystyle {\pi \over 2}\\ -1 + \cos t + \sin t &t\ge \displaystyle {{\pi \over 2}} \end {array} \right. \] Which is the same result obtained in Example 8.5.1



Solve the IVP using Laplace transform \begin {equation} \label {eq:8.5.8} \ddot{y}-y=f(t) \quad y(0)=-1 \quad\dot{y}(0)=2 \end {equation} \[ f(t)=\left \{ \begin {array}{cl} t &0\le t<1 \\ 1 &t\ge 1 \end {array} \right. \]
Solution
  1. We'll start by rewriting the forcing function \(f(t)\) in terms of the unit step function \begin{align*} f(t) = & \, t + \big(1-t\big) u(t-1) \\ = & \, t - \big(t-1\big) u(t-1) \\ \end{align*} So we can rewrite our IVP (8.5.5) as \begin {equation*} \ddot{y}-y=t - \big(t-1\big) u(t-1) \quad y(0)=-1 \quad\dot{y}(0)=2 \end {equation*} Now we can solve by taking the Laplace transform of both sides of the equation \begin {align} \ddot{y}-y= & \,t - \big(t-1\big) u(t-1) \nonumber\\ {\cal L} \Big\{ \ddot{y}-y \Big\} = & \, {\cal L} \Big\{ t - \big(t-1\big) u(t-1) \Big\}\nonumber\\ {\cal L} \big\{ \ddot{y} \big\} - {\cal L} \big\{ y \big\} = & \, {\cal L} \big\{ t \big\} - \, {\cal L} \Big\{ \big(t-1\big) u(t-1)\Big\}\nonumber\\ \overbrace{s^2{\cal L} \big\{ {y} \big\} - s \, y(0) - \dot{y}(0)} - {\cal L} \big\{ y \big\} = & \, {\cal L} \big\{ t\big\} - e^{-s} \, {\cal L} \Big\{ (t-1)+1 \Big\} \nonumber\\ s^2 Y(s) + s -2 - Y(s) = & \, (1-e^{-s}) \, {\cal L} \big\{ t\big\} \nonumber\\ (s^2 -1 ) Y(s) + (s -2) = & \, \frac{1- e^{-s}}{s^2} \nonumber\\ (s^2 -1 ) Y(s) = & \, \frac{1- e^{-s}}{s^2} - (s -2) \nonumber\\ Y(s) = & \, \frac{1- e^{-s} + s^2(2-2)}{s^2(s^2 -1 )} \end {align} Now to find \(y(t)\) we take the inverse Laplace transform of \(Y(s)\) \begin {align*} {\cal L}^{-1} \Big[ Y(s) \Big] = & \, {\cal L}^{-1} \Bigg[ \frac{1- e^{-s} + s^2(s +2)}{s^2(s^2 -1 )} \Bigg] \\ y(t) = & \, {\cal L}^{-1} \Bigg[ \underset{G_{_1}(s)}{\underbrace{\frac{1}{s^2(s^2 - 1 )}}} \Bigg] - \, {\cal L}^{-1} \Bigg[ e^{-s} \cdot \underset{G_{_1}(s)}{\underbrace{\frac{1}{s^2(s^2 - 1 )}}} \Bigg] + {\cal L}^{-1} \Bigg[ \underset{G_{_2}(s)}{\underbrace{\frac{2-s}{s^2 - 1 }}} \Bigg] \\ \end {align*}
  2. Just as before we divide this up into three separate problems and we will evaluate the inverse Laplace Transform of each one separately. Starting with \(G_{_1}(s)\) which is a basic one and does not require shifting but does require PFD \begin{align} g_1(t) = & \, {\cal L}^{-1}\Big [ G_{_1}(s) \Big ] \nonumber\\ = & \, {\cal L}^{-1}\left [ \frac{1}{s^2(s^2 - 1 )} \right ] \nonumber\\ = & \, {\cal L}^{-1}\left [ \frac{A}{s^2} \right ] + {\cal L}^{-1}\left [ \frac{B}{s^2 - 1} \right ] \end{align}
    Solve for \(A\) and \(B\) using Heaviside's method \(\, \displaystyle \frac{1}{s^2(s^2 - 1 )} = \frac{A}{s^2} + \frac{B}{s^2 - 1} \)
    Substituting \(A\) and \(B\) back in we obtain \begin{align*} g_1(t) = & \, {\cal L}^{-1}\left [ \frac{1}{s^2-1} \right ] - {\cal L}^{-1}\left [ \frac{1}{s^2 } \right ] \\ = & \, \quad\quad \sinh t \quad-\quad t \\ \end{align*}



    For the next term \(e^{-s} \,G_{_1}(s)\) we will have to apply the Second Shifting Theorem (Theorem 8.4.2) and we already found \(g_1(t)\) so we identify \(a=1\) and substitute to obtain \begin{align*} {\cal L}^{-1} \Big[ e^{-s} \, G_{_1}(s) \Big] = & g_{_1}(t-1) \, u(t-1) \\ = & \Big(\sinh (t-1) - (t-1)\Big) \, u(t-1) \\ = & \Big(1-t + \sinh (t-1) \Big) \, u(t-1) \\ \end{align*}



    The last term \(G_{_2}(s)\) does not require shifting but does require or PFD \begin{align*} g_{_2}(t) = & \, {\cal L}^{-1}\Big [ G_{_2}(s) \Big ] \nonumber\\ = & \, {\cal L}^{-1}\left [ \frac{2-s}{s^2 - 1} \right ] \nonumber\\ = & \, 2 \, {\cal L}^{-1}\left [ \frac{1}{s^2 - 1} \right ] - {\cal L}^{-1}\left [ \frac{s}{s^2 - 1} \right ] \nonumber\\ = & \, \quad 2 \, \sinh t \quad-\quad \cosh t \nonumber\\ \end{align*}



  3. Finally substituting it back in \begin{array}{cccc} y(t) &=& \, {\cal L}^{-1}\Big [ G_{_1}(s) \Big ] &-& {\cal L}^{-1}\Big [ e^{-s} \, G_{_1}(s) \Big ] &+& {\cal L}^{-1}\Big [ G_{_2}(s) \Big ] \\ &=& \, g_{_1}(t) &-& g_{_1}(t-1) \, u(t-1) &+& g_{_2}(t) \\ &=& \, \sinh t - t &-& \Big(1-t + \sinh (t-1) \Big) \, u(t-1) &+& 2 \sinh t - \cosh t \\ \end{array} Simplifying by combining like terms we obtain \begin {equation} \label {eq:8.5.11} y(t) = - t + 3 \sinh t - \cosh t - \Big(1-t + \sinh (t-1) \Big) \, u(t-1) \end {equation} Then to write it as a piecewise function we have \begin {equation*} \begin {array}{rl} y_0(t) &=& - t + 3 \sinh t - \cosh t \\ y_1(t) - y_0(t) &=& - \Big(1-t + \sinh (t-1) \Big)\\ &=& -1+t - \sinh (t-1) \\ \end {array} \end {equation*} Solving for \(y_1(t)\) we have \begin {align*} y_1(t) = & \, -1+t - \sinh (t-1) + y_0(t) \\ = & \, -1+t - \sinh (t-1) - t + 3 \sinh t - \cosh t \\ = & \, -1 - \sinh (t-1) + 3 \sinh t - \cosh t \\ \end {align*} Then writing in piecewise form we have \[ y(t)=\left \{ \begin {array}{cl} - t + 3 \sinh t - \cosh t &0\le t< 1\\ -1 - \sinh (t-1) + 3 \sinh t - \cosh t &t\ge 1 \end {array} \right. \]



Solve the IVP using Laplace transform \begin {equation} \label {eq:8.5.12} \ddot{y}+y=f(t) \quad y(0)=0 \quad\dot{y}(0)=0 \end {equation} \[ f(t)=\left \{ \begin {array}{cl} 0 &0\le t<\displaystyle {{\pi \over 4}} \\ \cos 2t &\displaystyle {{\pi \over 4}}\le t<\pi \\ 0 &t\ge \pi \end {array} \right. \]
Solution
  1. We'll start by rewriting the forcing function \(f(t)\) in terms of the unit step function \begin{align*} f(t) = & \, 0 + \big(\cos 2t-0\big) u(t-\textstyle{\frac{\pi}{4}}) + \big(0-\cos 2t\big) u(t-\pi) \\ = & \, \cos (2t) \, u(t-\textstyle{\frac{\pi}{4}}) -\cos (2t) \, u(t-\pi) \\ \end{align*} So we can rewrite our IVP (8.5.5) as \begin {equation*} \ddot{y}+y=\cos (2t) \, u(t-\textstyle{\frac{\pi}{4}}) -\cos (2t) \, u(t-\pi) \quad y(0)=0 \quad\dot{y}(0)=0 \end {equation*} Now we can solve by taking the Laplace transform of both sides of the equation \begin {align} \label {eq:8.5.13} \ddot{y}+y= & \,\cos (2t) \, u(t-\textstyle{\frac{\pi}{4}}) -\cos (2t) \, u(t-\pi) \nonumber\\ {\cal L} \Big\{ \ddot{y}+y \Big\} = & \, {\cal L} \Big\{ \cos (2t) \, u(t-\textstyle{\frac{\pi}{4}}) -\cos (2t) \, u(t-\pi) \Big\}\nonumber\\ {\cal L} \big\{ \ddot{y} \big\} + {\cal L} \big\{ y \big\} = & \, {\cal L} \big\{ \cos (2t) \, u(t-\textstyle{\frac{\pi}{4}}) \big\} - \, {\cal L} \Big\{ \cos (2t) \, u(t-\pi) \Big\}\nonumber\\ \overbrace{s^2{\cal L} \big\{ {y} \big\} - s \, y(0) - \dot{y}(0)} + {\cal L} \big\{ y \big\} = & \, e^{-\frac{\pi}{4}s} \, {\cal L} \Big\{ \cos \big(2(t+\textstyle{\frac{\pi}{4}})\big) \Big\} - e^{-\pi s} \, {\cal L} \Big\{ \cos \big(2(t+\pi)\big)\Big\}\nonumber\\ s^2 Y(s) + 0 +0 + Y(s) = & \, e^{-\frac{\pi}{4}s} \, {\cal L} \Big\{ \cos (2t+\textstyle{\frac{\pi}{2}}) \Big\} - e^{-\pi s} \, {\cal L} \Big\{ \cos (2t+2\pi)\Big\}\nonumber\\ s^2 Y(s) + Y(s) = & \, e^{-\frac{\pi}{4}s} \, {\cal L} \Big\{ \cos (2t) \overset{0}{\overbrace{\cos (\textstyle{\frac{\pi}{2}})}} - \sin (2t) \overset{1}{\overbrace{\sin (\textstyle{\frac{\pi}{2}})}} \Big\} \nonumber\\ & \quad\quad - e^{-\pi s} \, {\cal L} \Big\{ \cos (2t) \underset{1}{\underbrace{\cos (2\pi)}} - \sin (2t) \underset{0}{\underbrace{\sin (2\pi)}} \Big\}\nonumber\\ \big( s^2 + 1 \big) \, Y(s) = & \, -e^{-\frac{\pi}{4}s} \, {\cal L} \Big\{ \sin (2t) \Big\} - e^{-\pi s} \, {\cal L} \Big\{ \cos (2t) \Big\}\nonumber\\ \big( s^2 + 1 \big) \, Y(s) = & \, -e^{-\frac{\pi}{4}s} \, \left( \frac{2}{s^2 + 4} \right) - e^{-\pi s} \left( \frac{s}{s^2 + 4} \right)\nonumber\\ Y(s) = & \, -\frac{2e^{-\frac{\pi}{4}s}}{\big( s^2 + 1 \big) (s^2 + 4)} - \frac{se^{-\pi s} }{\big( s^2 + 1 \big) (s^2 + 4)} \end {align}
  2. Now to find \(y(t)\) we take the inverse Laplace transform of (8.5.13) \begin {align*} {\cal L}^{-1} \Big[ Y(s) \Big] = & \, {\cal L}^{-1} \Bigg[ -\frac{2e^{-\frac{\pi}{4}s}}{\big( s^2 - 1 \big) (s^2 + 4)} - \frac{se^{-\pi s} }{\big( s^2 + 1 \big) (s^2 + 4)} \Bigg] \\ = y(t) = & - 2 \, {\cal L}^{-1} \Bigg[ e^{-\frac{\pi}{4}s} \cdot \underset{G_{_1}(s)}{\underbrace{\frac{1}{\big( s^2 + 1 \big) (s^2 + 4)}}} \Bigg] - \, {\cal L}^{-1} \Bigg[ e^{-\pi s} \cdot \underset{G_{_2}(s) }{\underbrace{\frac{s}{\big( s^2 + 1 \big) (s^2 + 4)}}} \Bigg] \\ \end {align*} Just as before we divide this up into separate problems and we will evaluate the inverse Laplace Transform of each one separately. We'll start by finding \(g_{_1}(t)\) using PFD \begin{align} \label {eq:8.5.14} g_1(t) = & \, {\cal L}^{-1}\Big [ G_{_1}(s) \Big ] \nonumber\\ = & \, {\cal L}^{-1}\left [ \frac{1}{\big( s^2 + 1 \big) (s^2 + 4)} \right ] \nonumber\\ = & \, {\cal L}^{-1}\left [ \frac{A}{s^2 + 1 } \right ] + {\cal L}^{-1}\left [ \frac{B}{s^2 + 4} \right ] \end{align}
    Solve for \(A\) and \(B\) using Heaviside's method \(\, \displaystyle \frac{1}{\big( s^2 - 1 \big) (s^2 + 4)} = \frac{A}{s^2 - 1 } + \frac{B}{s^2 + 4} \)
    Substituting \(A\) and \(B\) back in to (8.5.14) we obtain \begin{align*} g_1(t) = & \, \frac{1}{3} \, \Bigg( \, {\cal L}^{-1}\left [ \frac{1}{s^2+1} \right ] - {\cal L}^{-1}\left [ \frac{1}{s^2+4 } \right ] \, \Bigg)\\ = & \, \frac{1}{3} \, \Bigg( \, {\cal L}^{-1}\left [ \frac{1}{s^2+1} \right ] - \frac{1}{2} \, {\cal L}^{-1}\left [ \frac{2}{s^2+4 } \right ] \, \Bigg)\\ = & \, \frac{1}{3} \, \Bigg( \, \sin t - \frac{1}{2} \sin 2t \, \Bigg)\\ = & \, \frac{1}{6} \, \Big( \, 2\sin t - \sin 2t \, \Big)\\ \end{align*}
  3. Now to calculate the inverse Laplace transform of the first term \(e^{-\frac{\pi}{4}s} \,G_{_1}(s)\) we will apply the Second Shifting Theorem (Theorem 8.4.2) and we already found \(g_1(t)\) so we identify \(a=\frac{\pi}{4}\) and substitute to obtain \begin{align*} {\cal L}^{-1} \Big[ e^{-\frac{\pi}{4}s} \, G_{_1}(s) \Big] = & \, g_{_1}(t-\textstyle{\frac{\pi}{4}}) \, u(t-\frac {\pi}{4}) \\ = & \, \frac{1}{6} \, \Big( \, 2\sin (t-\textstyle{\frac{\pi}{4}}) - \sin \big(2(t-\textstyle{\frac{\pi}{4}})\big) \, \Big) \, u(t-\frac{\pi}{4}) \\ = & \, \frac{1}{6} \, \Big( \, 2\sin (t-\textstyle{\frac{\pi}{4}}) - \sin (2t-\textstyle{\frac{\pi}{2}}) \, \Big) \, u(t-\frac{\pi}{4}) \\ = & \, \frac{1}{6} \, \Big( \, 2\big( \sin t \, \overset{\frac{\sqrt{2}}{2}}{\overbrace{\cos \textstyle{\frac{\pi}{4}}}} - \cos t \, \overset{\frac{\sqrt{2}}{2}}{\overbrace{\sin \textstyle{\frac{\pi}{4}}}} \big) \\ & \quad\quad - \big( \sin 2t \, \underset{0}{\underbrace{\cos \textstyle{\frac{\pi}{2}}}} - \cos 2t \, \underset{1}{\underbrace{\sin \textstyle{\frac{\pi}{2}}}} \big) \, \Big) \, u(t-\textstyle{\frac{\pi}{4}}) \\ = & \, \frac{1}{6} \, \Big( \, 2\big( {\textstyle \frac{\sqrt{2}}{2}} \sin t - {\textstyle \frac{\sqrt{2}}{2} } \cos t \big) - \big( - \cos 2t \big) \, \Big) \, u(t-\textstyle{\frac{\pi}{4}}) \\ = & \, \frac{1}{6} \, \Big( \, \sqrt{2} \sin t - \sqrt{2} \cos t + \cos 2t \, \Big) \, u(t-\textstyle{\frac{\pi}{4}}) \\ \end{align*}
    Now we'll calculate the inverse Laplace transform of the second term \(e^{-\pi s} \,G_{_2}(s)\) finding \(g_{_2}(t)\) but notice that \(G_{_2}(s) = s \cdot G_{_1}(s) \) so we can apply the same PFD from before. \begin{align} g_2(t) = & \, {\cal L}^{-1}\Big [ G_{_2}(s) \Big ] \nonumber\\ = & \, {\cal L}^{-1}\Big [ s \cdot G_{_1}(s) \Big ] \nonumber\\ = & \, {\cal L}^{-1}\left [ s \cdot \frac{1}{\big( s^2 + 1 \big) (s^2 + 4)} \right ] \nonumber\\ = & \, \frac{1}{3} \, {\cal L}^{-1}\left [ s \Big( \frac{1}{ s^2 + 1 } - \frac{1}{s^2 + 4} \Big) \right ] \nonumber\\ = & \, \frac{1}{3} \, {\cal L}^{-1}\left [ \frac{s}{ s^2 + 1 } - \frac{s}{s^2 + 4} \right ] \nonumber\\ = & \, \frac{1}{3} \, \Bigg( {\cal L}^{-1}\left [ \frac{s}{ s^2 + 1 } \right ] - {\cal L}^{-1}\left [ \frac{s}{s^2 + 4} \right ] \Bigg) \nonumber\\ = & \, \frac{1}{3} \, \Big( \cos t - \cos 2t \Big) \nonumber\\ \end{align}
    Now to calculate the inverse Laplace transform of the second term \(e^{-\pi s} \,G_{_2}(s)\) we will apply the Second Shifting Theorem (Theorem 8.4.2) and we already found \(g_2(t)\) so we identify \(a=\pi\) and substitute to obtain \begin{align*} {\cal L}^{-1} \Big[ e^{-\pi s} \, G_{_2}(s) \Big] = & \, g_{_2}(t-\pi) \, u(t-\pi) \\ = & \, \frac{1}{3} \, \Big( \cos (t-\pi) - \cos \big(2(t-\pi)\big) \Big) \, u(t-\pi) \\ = & \, \frac{1}{3} \, \Big( \cos (t-\pi) - \cos (2t-2\pi)\Big) \, u(t-\pi) \\ = & \, \frac{1}{3} \, \Big( \big( \cos t \, \overset{-1}{\overbrace{\cos \pi}} + \sin t \, \overset{0}{\overbrace{\sin \pi}} \big) \\ & \quad\quad - \big( \cos 2t \, \underset{1}{\underbrace{\cos 2\pi}} + \sin 2t \, \underset{0}{\underbrace{\sin 2\pi}} \big) \Big) \, u(t-\pi) \\ = & \, \frac{1}{3} \, \Big(-\cos t- \cos 2t \Big) \, u(t-\pi) \\ = & \, -\frac{1}{3} \, \Big(\cos t+ \cos 2t \Big) \, u(t-\pi) \\ \end{align*}



  4. Finally substituting it back in \begin{array}{cccc} y(t) &=& \, -2 \, {\cal L}^{-1}\Big [ e^{-\frac{\pi}{4}s} G_{_1}(s) \Big ] &-& {\cal L}^{-1}\Big [ e^{-\pi s} \, G_{_2}(s) \Big ] \\ &=& \, -2g_{_1}(t-{\textstyle \frac{\pi}{4}}) \, u(t-{\textstyle \frac{\pi}{4}}) &-& g_{_2}(t-\pi) \, u(t-\pi) \\ &=& \, -\frac{1}{3} \, \Big( \, \sqrt{2} \sin t - \sqrt{2} \cos t + \cos 2t \, \Big) \, u(t-\textstyle{\frac{\pi}{4}}) &+& \frac{1}{3} \, \Big(\cos t+ \cos 2t \Big) \, u(t-\pi) \\ \end{array} So we have our solution \begin {equation} \label {eq:8.5.15} y(t) = -\frac{1}{3} \, \Big( \, \sqrt{2} \sin t - \sqrt{2} \cos t + \cos 2t \, \Big) \, u(t-\textstyle{\frac{\pi}{4}}) + \frac{1}{3} \, \Big(\cos t+ \cos 2t \Big) \, u(t-\pi) \end {equation} Then to write (8.5.15) as a piecewise function we have \begin {equation*} \begin {array}{rl} y_0(t) &=& 0 \\ y_1(t) - y_0(t) &=& -\frac{1}{3} \, \Big( \, \sqrt{2} \sin t - \sqrt{2} \cos t + \cos 2t \, \Big) \\ y_2(t) - y_1(t) &=& \frac{1}{3} \, \Big(\cos t+ \cos 2t \Big) \end {array} \end {equation*} Solving for \(y_1(t)\) we have \begin {align*} y_1(t) = & \, -\frac{1}{3} \, \Big( \, \sqrt{2} \sin t - \sqrt{2} \cos t + \cos 2t \, \Big) + y_0(t) \\ = & -\frac{1}{3} \, \Big( \, \sqrt{2} \sin t - \sqrt{2} \cos t + \cos 2t \, \Big) \\ = & \frac{1}{3} \, \Big( \, \sqrt{2} ( \cos t - \sin t ) - \cos 2t \, \Big) \\ = & \frac{\sqrt{2}}{3} ( \cos t - \sin t ) - \frac{1}{3} \cos 2t \\ \end {align*} Solving for \(y_2(t)\) we have \begin {align*} y_2(t) = & \, \frac{1}{3} \, \Big(\cos t+ \cos 2t \Big) + y_1(t) \\ = & \frac{1}{3} \, \Big(\cos t+ \cos 2t \Big) -\frac{1}{3} \, \Big( \, \sqrt{2} \sin t - \sqrt{2} \cos t + \cos 2t \, \Big) \\ = & \frac{1}{3} \, \Big(\cos t+ \cos 2t - \sqrt{2} \sin t + \sqrt{2} \cos t - \cos 2t \, \Big) \\ = & \frac{1}{3} \, \Big( (1+\sqrt{2})\cos t - \sqrt{2} \sin t \, \Big) \\ = & \frac{1+\sqrt{2}}{3}\cos t - \frac{\sqrt{2}}{3} \sin t \\ \end {align*} Then writing in piecewise form we have \begin {equation} \label {eq:8.5.16} y(t)=\left \{ \begin {array}{cl} 0 &0\le t< \frac{\pi}{4}\\ \frac{\sqrt{2}}{3} ( \cos t - \sin t ) - \frac{1}{3} \cos 2t &\frac{\pi}{4} \le t < \pi\\ \frac{1+\sqrt{2}}{3}\cos t - \frac{\sqrt{2}}{3} \sin t &t\ge \pi \end {array} \right. \end {equation}
