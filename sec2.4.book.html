2.4 Transformation of Nonlinear Equations into Separable Equations
Nonlinear to Separable Transformations
In Section 2.1 we found that the solutions of a linear nonhomogeneous equation \[ \frac{dy}{dx} +p(x)y=f(x) \] are of the form \(y=uy_1\), where \(y_1\) is a nontrivial solution of the complementary equation \begin {equation} \label {eq:2.4.1} \frac{dy}{dx} + p(x)y=0 \end {equation} and \(u\) is a solution of \[ \frac{du}{dx} y_1(x)=f(x)\] Note that this last equation is separable, since it can be rewritten as \[ \frac{du}{dx} = {f(x)\over y_1(x)}\] In this section we’ll consider nonlinear differential equations that are not separable to begin with, but can be solved in a similar fashion by writing their solutions in the form \(y=uy_1\), where \(y_1\) is a suitably chosen known function and \(u\) satisfies a separable equation. We’ll say in this case that we transformed the given equation into a separable equation.
Bernoulli Equations
A Bernoulli equation is an equation of the form \begin {equation} \label {eq:2.4.2} \frac{dy}{dx}+p(x)y=f(x)y^r \end {equation} where \(r\) can be any real number other than \(0\) or \(1\). (Note that (2.4.2) is linear if and only if \(r=0\) or \(r=1\).)
Substitute \(r=0\) and \(r=1\) into (2.4.2) and show the result is linear
Using variation of parameters, we will transform the Bernoulli equations into a separable equation by finding solutions of the form \( y=uy_1\) where \( y_1 \) a nontrivial solution of the "complementary" equation (2.4.1). Then, just as before, we differentiate \( y = u y_1 \) using the product rule \begin{align*} y = & u \cdot y_1 \\ \frac{d}{dx} \Big( y \Big) = & \frac{d}{dx} \Big( u \cdot y_1 \Big) \\ \frac{dy}{dx} = & y_1\frac{du}{dx} + u\frac{dy_1}{dx} \end{align*}
Now we substitute \( y = uy_1\) and \( \frac{dy}{dx} = y_1\frac{du}{dx} + u\frac{dy_1}{dx}\) into the Bernoulli equation (2.4.2) and simplify. By using the fact that \( y_1 \) is a solution to the "complementary" equation (2.4.1), we can use the fact that \( \frac{dy_1}{dx} + p(x)y_1=0\) and then transform the Bernoulli equation into a separable equation in \( u\) \begin{align*} \frac{dy}{dx} + p(x)y = & f(x)y^r \nonumber \\ \overset{\frac{dy}{dx}}{\overbrace{y_1\frac{du}{dx} + u\frac{dy_1}{dx}}} + p(x) \overset{y}{\overbrace{u \cdot y_1}} = & f(x)\overset{y^r}{\overbrace{\left(uy_1\right)^r}} \nonumber \\ y_1\frac{du}{dx} + \underbrace{u\frac{dy_1}{dx} + p(x)u \cdot y_1} = & f(x)u^ry_1^r \nonumber \\ y_1\frac{du}{dx} + u \underset{0}{\left( \underbrace{\frac{dy_1}{dx} + p(x)y_1 } \right) } = & f(x)u^ry_1^r \nonumber u^ry_1^r \\ y_1\frac{du}{dx} + u \left(0\right) = & f(x) \nonumber u^ry_1^r \\ y_1\frac{du}{dx} = & f(x) u^ry_1^r \nonumber \\ \frac{du}{dx} = & f(x) u^ry_1^{r-1}(x) \nonumber \\ \frac{1}{u^r} \cdot \frac{du}{dx} = & f(x) y_1^{r-1}(x) \nonumber \\ \int \Big( \frac{1}{u^r} \cdot \frac{du}{dx}\Big) dx = & \int \Big( f(x) y_1^{r-1}(x) \Big) dx \nonumber \\ \int \frac{1}{u^r} dxu = & \int \Big( f(x) y_1^{r-1}(x) \Big) dx \nonumber \\ \end{align*}
Solve the Bernoulli equation \begin {equation} \label {eq:2.4.3} \frac{dy}{dx} - y=xy^2 \end {equation}
Solution
  1. First notice the Bernoulli equation (2.4.3) has a trivial solution \(y(x)\equiv 0\) which we can verify by substitution: \begin{align*} \frac{dy}{dx} -y = & xy^2 \\ \frac{d}{dx}\Big(0\Big) -\Big(0\Big) \overset{?}{=} & x\Big(0\Big)^2 \quad \checkmark \\ \end{align*}
    Now we will seek nontrivial solutions \( y \ne 0 \) and start by solving the "complementary" equation: \begin {equation*} \frac{dy}{dx} - y = 0 \end {equation*}
    Solve \( \frac{dy}{dx} - y = 0 \) using separation of variables as in Examples 2.1.3 and 2.1.4
    Using separation of variables we find \( y = ce^{x} \) as the general solution "complementary" equation. Now when using variation of parameters, we will select \( y_1 \) as any simple particular solution of the complementary equation, so we choose \( c = 1 \) and use \( y_1 = e^{x} \) to seek solutions of (2.4.3) of the form: \[ y = u \cdot y_1 = u \cdot e^{x} \]
    Differentiate \( u \cdot e^{x} \) with respect to \( x \) using the product rule
    Differentiating using the product rule yields \begin{align*} \frac{dy}{dx} = & \left( \frac{du}{dx} + u \right) e^{x} \\ \end{align*}
    We now substitute this into (2.4.3) and simplify the equation which transforms it into an integrable equation \begin{align*} \frac{dy}{dx} -y = & xy^2 \\ \overset{\frac{dy}{dx}}{\overbrace{ \left( \frac{du}{dx} + u \right) e^{x} }} - \overset{y}{\overbrace{ue^{x}}} = & x\overset{y^2}{\overbrace{\left(u e^{x}\right)^2}} \\ \frac{du}{dx}e^{x} +\underset{0}{\underbrace{u e^{x} - ue^{x}}} = & xu^2 e^{2x} \\ \frac{du}{dx}e^{x} = & xu^2 e^{2x} \\ \frac{1}{u^2} \cdot \frac{du}{dx} = & x e^x\\ \int u^{-2} du = & \int x e^x dx \\ \end{align*}
  2. Finding the antiderivative on the right requires integration by parts (IBP) \(\displaystyle \int U dV = UV - \int V dU\) \begin{align*} \int u^{-2} du = & \int \overset{U}{\overbrace{x}} \underset{dV}{\underbrace{e^x dx}} \\ -u^{-1} = & \overset{UV}{\overbrace{x e^x}} - \int \overset{VdU}{\overbrace{e^x dx}} \\ -\frac{1}{u} = & x e^x - e^x + c \\ -\frac{1}{u} = & ( x - 1 )e^x + c \\ \end{align*}
    Solve \( -\frac{1}{u} = (x-1)e^x + c \) for \( u \)
    Solving for \( u \) we obtain: \begin{align*} u = \frac{1}{(1-x)e^x + c} \end{align*}
    Then multiply the result by \(y_1 = e^{x} \) to get \( y \) \begin{align*} y = ue^{x} = & \frac{e^{x}}{(1-x)e^x + c} \\ \end{align*} Simplify by multiplying by \( e^{-x} \) on top and bottom \begin{align*} \frac{e^{x}}{(1-x)e^x + c} \cdot \left( \frac{e^{-x}}{e^{-x}} \right) = & \frac{e^{x}e^{-x}}{(1-x)e^xe^{-x} + ce^{-x}} =\frac{1}{(1-x) + ce^{-x}} \\ \end{align*} Notice that our one-parameter family fails to include the trivial solution so our general solution includes the trivial solution (a singular solution) and the one-parameter family: \begin{align*} y \equiv 0 \quad\quad y =\frac{1}{(1-x) + ce^{-x}} \end{align*}
    Verify \( y =\frac{1}{(1-x) + ce^{-x}} \) is a solution to (2.4.3) by differentiating and substituting

Nonlinear Homogeneous
We showed how the nonlinear Bernoulli equation can be transformed into a separable equation by the substitution \(y=uy_1\) where \(y_1\) is a solution to the "complementary equation". Now let's discover a sufficient condition for transforming other nonlinear first order differential equations: \begin {equation} \label {eq:2.4.4} \frac{dy}{dx} = f(x,y) \end {equation}
Differentiate \( u y_1 \) where \( u = u(x) \) and \( y_1 = y_1(x) \) with respect to \( x \) using the product rule
We'll start by making a familiar substitution of \( y = u y_1 \) into (2.4.4) \begin{align} \overset{\frac{dy}{dx}}{\overbrace{\frac{du}{dx} y_1+u\frac{dy_1}{dx}}} = & f\Big(x,\overset{y}{\overbrace{uy_1}}\Big) \nonumber \\ \label {eq:2.4.5} \frac{du}{dx} y_1 = & f\Big(x,uy_1\Big) - u\frac{dy_1}{dx} \end{align}
Now if we are able to write \( f(x,y) \) as a product of a function of \( u \) and the derivative of \( y_1 \) then the nonlinear equation will transform into a separable equation. \begin{align} \frac{du}{dx} y_1 = & \overset{ f\Big(x,uy_1\Big) }{\overbrace{q(u)\frac{dy_1}{dx}}} - u\frac{dy_1}{dx} \nonumber \\ \frac{du}{dx} y_1 = & \Big( q(u) - u \Big) \frac{dy_1}{dx} \label{eq:2.4.6} \end{align}
Now (2.4.6) is separable once we first handle constant solution cases individually \begin{align*} y_1 = 0 \quad\quad q(u) - u = 0 \end{align*}
Once constant solutions are identified, we can now proceed to separate the variables in (2.4.6) and solve \begin{align*} \frac{du}{dx} y_1 = & \Big( q(u) - u \Big) \frac{dy_1}{dx} \\ \frac{1}{q(u) - u} \cdot \frac{du}{dx} = & \frac{1}{y_1} \cdot \frac{dy_1}{dx} \\ \int \Big( \frac{1}{q(u) - u} \cdot \frac{du}{dx} \Big) dx = & \Big( \frac{1}{y_1} \cdot \frac{dy_1}{dx} \Big) dx \\ \int \frac{1}{q(u) - u} du = & \int \frac{1}{y_1} dy_1 \\ \end{align*}
We will consider homogeneous nonlinear equations, the most widely studied class of equations for which this substitution works. Other types of equations appear in Exercises 44–51.
We previously defined homogeneous in terms of linear differential equations, but it is something entirely different for nonlinear differential equations. A nonlinear differential equation (2.4.4) is said to be homogeneous if \( x \) and \( y \) occur in \( f(x,y) \) in such a way that \( f(x,y) \) can be written to depend on only the ratio \( y / x \) in other words if \( f(x,y) = q( y / x) \) can be written as a function of a single variable \( u = y / x \) \begin {equation} \label {eq:2.4.7} \frac{dy}{dx}= q \left( \frac{y}{x} \right) \end {equation}
For instance, we can rewrite the following differential equations in terms of \( y / x \) \[ \frac{dy}{dx} = {y+xe^{-y/x}\over x} = \underset{ q\left(y\over x\right) }{ \underbrace{ {\left(y\over x\right)}+e^{-\left(y\over x\right)} }} \] and \[ \frac{dy}{dx} = {y^2+xy-x^2\over x^2} = \underset{ q\left(y\over x\right) }{ \underbrace{ \left (y\over x\right )^2+{\left(y\over x\right)} -1 }} \] are of the form (2.4.7), with \[ q(u)=u+e^{-u}\quad \mbox{ and } \quad q(u)=u^2+u-1 \]
The general method for transforming nonlinear differential equations into separable differential equations involves first making a substitution \( y = uy_1 \) for an appropriately chosen \( y_1 \) and then rewriting \( f(x,y) \) of (2.4.4) as a product of \( u \) and the derivative of \( y_1 \) as shown in (2.4.6) \begin{align*} f\Big(x,uy_1(x)\Big)=q(u)\frac{dy_1}{dx} \end{align*}
Homogeneous nonlinear differential equations (2.4.7) can be solved with this method using \( y_1 = x .\) Notice we are not using \(y_1\) to represent a solution to a "complementary equation" but rather we are choosing an appropriate \(y_1\) that is known to work. When we use \( y_1 = x\) then \(y = uy_1 = ux \) which we can differentiate using the product rule: \begin{align*} \frac{d}{dx} \Big( ux \Big) = & u \underset{1}{\underbrace{\frac{d}{dx} \Big( x \Big)}} + x\frac{d}{dx} \Big( u \Big) = u + x\frac{du}{dx} \end{align*} Substituting \( y = ux \) and \( \frac{dy}{dx} = u + x\frac{du}{dx} \) into (2.4.7) produces a separable equation: \begin{align*} \frac{dy}{dx} = & \overset{f(x,y)}{\overbrace{q\left(\frac{y}{x}\right)}} \\ \overset{\frac{dy}{dx}}{\overbrace{u + x\frac{du}{dx}}} = & q\overset{y=ux}{\overbrace{\left( \frac{ux}{x} \right)}} \\ u + x\frac{du}{dx} = & q(u) \\ x\frac{du}{dx} = & q(u) - u \\ \frac{1}{q(u) - u} \cdot \frac{du}{dx} = & \frac{1}{x} \\ \int \bigg( \frac{1}{q(u) - u} \cdot \frac{du}{dx} \bigg) dx = & \int \bigg( \frac{1}{x} \bigg) dx \\ \int \frac{1}{q(u) - u} du = & \int \frac{1}{x} dx \\ \end{align*}
Since \(y/x\) is in general undefined if \(x=0\), we’ll consider solutions of homogeneous nonlinear equations only on open intervals that do not contain the point \(x=0\).
Solve \begin {equation} \label {eq:2.4.8} \frac{dy}{dx} = {y+xe^{-y/x}\over x}\end {equation}
Solution
We showed earlier that we can rearrange the nonlinear differential equation (2.4.8) into homogeneous form, written as a function of \( y / x \) \begin{align*} \frac{dy}{dx} = & \frac{y+xe^{-y/x}}{x} = \left(\frac{y}{x}\right) + e^{-\left(\frac{y}{x}\right)} \end{align*}
For homogeneous nonlinear equations, we will always use the substitution \( y = u x \)
Differentiate \( u x \) where \( u = u(x) \) with respect to \( x \) using the product rule
Substituting \( y = u x \) transforms (2.4.8) into a separable equation which we can easily solve by finding the antiderivatives \begin{align*} \overset{\frac{dy}{dx}}{\overbrace{u + x\frac{du}{dx}}} = & \overset{y=ux}{\overbrace{\left(\frac{ux}{x}\right)}} + e^{-\overset{y=ux}{\overbrace{\left(\frac{ux}{x}\right)}}} \\ u + x\frac{du}{dx} & = u + e^{-u} \\ e^{u}\frac{du}{dx} & = \frac{1}{x} \\ \int e^{u} du & = \int \frac{1}{x} dx \\ e^{u} & = \ln \left| x \right| + c \\ \ln \Big( e^{u} \Big) & = \ln \Big( \ln \left| x \right| + c \Big) \\ u & = \ln \Big( \ln \left| x \right| + c \Big) \\ \end{align*} Multiplying the result by \( x \) gets us our solution \( y = ux \) \begin{align*} y & = x\ln \Big( \ln \left| x \right| + c \Big) \\ \end{align*}
Verify \( y =x\ln \Big( \ln \left| x \right| + c \Big) \) is a solution to (2.4.8) by differentiating and substituting

Nonlinear Homogeneous IVPs
  1. Solve \begin {equation} \label {eq:2.4.9} x^2 \frac{dy}{dx} = y^2+xy-x^2 \end {equation}
  3. Show that the solution to the IVP is not unique \begin {equation*} x^2\frac{dy}{dx} = y^2+xy-x^2 \quad \quad y(0)=0 \end {equation*}
  4. Solve the IVP \begin {equation} \label {eq:2.4.10} x^2\frac{dy}{dx} = y^2+xy-x^2 \quad \quad y(1)=2 \end {equation}
Solution
  1. We showed earlier that we can rearrange the nonlinear differential equation (2.4.9) into homogeneous form, written as a function of \( y / x \) \begin{align*} \frac{dy}{dx} = & \frac{y^2 +xy - x^2}{x^2} = \left(\frac{y}{x}\right)^2 + \left(\frac{y}{x}\right) - 1 \end{align*}
    For homogeneous nonlinear equations, we will always use the substitution \( y = u x \)
    Differentiate \( u x \) where \( u = u(x) \) with respect to \( x \) using the product rule
    Substituting \( y = u x \) transforms this into a separable equation \begin{align} \overset{\frac{dy}{dx}}{\overbrace{u + x\frac{du}{dx}}} = & \overset{y=ux}{\overbrace{\left(\frac{ux}{x}\right)^2}} + \overset{y=ux}{\overbrace{\left(\frac{ux}{x}\right)}} - 1 \nonumber \\ u + x\frac{du}{dx} = & u^2 + u - 1 \nonumber \\ \label{eq:2.4.11} x\frac{du}{dx} = & u^2 - 1 \end{align} Separating the variables in (2.4.11) will require dividing the expression by \(u^2-1 \) which is invalid if \((u-1)(u+1) = 0 \) so we first check these equilibrium solutions when \( u = 1 \) and \( u = -1 \)
    Recall we made the substitution \( y = ux ,\) so we get \( y = x\) and \( y = -x\) as the equilibrium solutions which we can verify by substituting into (2.4.9) \begin{align*} x^2 \frac{dy}{dx} = & y^2+xy-x^2 \\ x^2 \underset{1}{\underbrace{\frac{d}{dx} \Big( x \Big)}} \overset{?}{=} & \underset{2x^2}{\underbrace{\Big( x \Big)^2+x\Big( x \Big)}}-x^2 \\ x^2 = & 2x^2 -x^2 \quad \checkmark \\ \end{align*} and \begin{align*} x^2 \frac{dy}{dx} = & y^2+xy-x^2 \\ x^2 \underset{-1}{\underbrace{\frac{d}{dx} \Big( -x \Big)}} \overset{?}{=} & \underset{x^2}{\underbrace{\Big( -x \Big)^2 }} + \underset{-2x^2}{\underbrace{x\Big( -x \Big)-x^2}} \\ -x^2 = & x^2 - 2x^2 \quad \checkmark \\ \end{align*}
    Now that we have verified our equilibrium solutions \( y = x\) (from \( u = 1 \)) and \( y = -x\) (from \( u = -1 \)) we can assume \( u \ne 1 \) and \( u \ne -1 \) in (2.4.11) and separate variables \begin{align} x\frac{du}{dx} = & u^2 - 1 \nonumber \\ \frac{1}{u^2 - 1} \cdot \frac{du}{dx} = & \frac{1}{x} \nonumber \\ \int \Big( \frac{1}{u^2 - 1} \cdot \frac{du}{dx} \Big) dx = & \int \Big( \frac{1}{x} \Big) dx \nonumber \\ \int \frac{1}{u^2 - 1} du= & \int \frac{1}{x} dx \nonumber \\ \end{align}
    Decompose \( \frac{1}{u^2 - 1} \) using PFD as in Example 2.2.5
  2. Decomposing the left side yields: \begin{align} \int \frac{1}{2} \left( \frac{1}{u-1} \right) du - \int \frac{1}{2} \left( \frac{1}{u+1} \right) du= & \int \frac{1}{x} dx \nonumber \\ \int \frac{1}{\underset{w}{\underbrace{u-1}}} \underset{dw}{\underbrace{du}} -\int \frac{1}{\underset{v}{\underbrace{u+1}}} \underset{dv}{\underbrace{du}} = & 2\int \frac{1}{x} dx \nonumber \\ \int \frac{1}{w}dw - \int \frac{1}{v}dv = & 2\int \frac{1}{x} dx \nonumber \\ \ln \left| w \right| - \ln \left| v \right| = & 2\ln \left| x \right| + k \nonumber \\ \ln \left| \frac{w}{v} \right| = & \ln \left| x \right|^2 + k \nonumber \\ \ln \left| \frac{u-1}{u+1} \right| = & \ln x^2 + k \nonumber \\ e^{\ln \left| \frac{u-1}{u+1} \right|} = & e^{\ln x^2 + k} \nonumber \\ \left| \frac{u-1}{u+1} \right| = & e^{\ln x^2} \underset{c}{\underbrace{e^{k}}} \nonumber \\ \left| \frac{u-1}{u+1} \right| = & cx^2 \nonumber \end{align} Since we assumed \( u \ne 1 \) and \( u \ne -1 \) then the quantity \( \displaystyle \left| \frac{u-1}{u+1} \right| \) won't change signs on the interval so we can further simplify to \begin{align} \label {eq:2.4.12} \frac{u-1}{u+1} =& cx^2 \\ \end{align}
    Solve (2.4.12) for \( u \) as in Example 2.2.5
    Solving for \( u \) we obtain \begin{align*} u = & \frac{1 + cx^2}{1-cx^2} \end{align*} Multiplying the result by \( x \) gets us \( y = ux \) \begin{align} \label {eq:2.4.13} y & = \frac{x \left( 1 + cx^2 \right) }{1-cx^2} \\ \end{align}
    Differentiate \( y = \displaystyle \frac{x \left( 1 + cx^2 \right) }{1-cx^2} \) using quotient rule
    Verify \( y = \displaystyle \frac{x \left( 1 + cx^2 \right) }{1-cx^2} \) is a solution to (2.4.9)
    Choosing \( c = 0 \) in the one-parameter family (2.4.13) yields one of the equilibrium solutions \begin{align*} y & = \frac{x \left( 1 + 0 \cdot x^2 \right) }{1-0 \cdot x^2} = x \\ \end{align*} but the other equilibrium solution \( y = -x \) cannot be obtained from the family regardless of choice of \( c \) therefore it is a singular solution, so our general solution on intervals that don't contain \( x = 0 \) is \begin{align} \label {eq:2.4.14} y = -x \quad \quad y = \frac{x \left( 1 + cx^2 \right) }{1-cx^2} \\ \end{align}
  3. Recall that the entire method of solving this (and all) nonlinear homogeneous differential equation(s) relied upon \( x \) and \( y \) occurring as a ratio \( y / x\) meaning by default we are only considering intervals not containing \( x = 0 .\) However, right away we notice that both the singular solution \( y = -x \) and the one-parameter family are defined at \( x = 0 \) (which is a hint to us that perhaps it is possible to consider intervals that do contain \( x = 0 \))
    Both the singular solution \( y = -x \) and the one-parameter family are defined at \( x = 0 \) \begin{align*} y(0) = -0=0 \quad\quad y(0) & = \frac{0 \left( 1 + c\cdot0^2 \right) }{1-c\cdot0^2} = 0 \\ \end{align*} And in fact, both satisfy the initial condition \( y(0) = 0 \) so right away we have two distinct solutions that satisfy the IVP so the solution to the IVP is not unique. And actually, we left \(c\) arbitrary in that calculation, so we have already found an infinite number of solutions that satisfy the IVP. Now the IOV is another question entirely. It is fairly straightforward for the equilibrium solution \( y = -x \) which is defined everywhere so the IOV is \( (-\infty,\infty)\) but for the one-parameter family (2.4.13) it is more tricky since it is a rational function and we have to ensure the denominator is not zero.
    Assuming \( c < 0 \) find any points of discontinuity \(y = \displaystyle \frac{x \left( 1 + cx^2 \right) }{1-cx^2} \)
    Assuming \( c \le 0 \) the denominator would be all positive quantities since subtracting a negative would result in a positive and the one and square are both positive as well. Therefore there are no discontinuities in this case so the IOV would be \( (-\infty,\infty)\)
    Assuming \( c > 0 \) find any points of discontinuity \(y = \displaystyle \frac{x \left( 1 + cx^2 \right) }{1-cx^2} \)
    Assuming \( c > 0 \) and setting \( 1-cx^2 = 0\) we find the one-parameter family is discontinuous at \( x = \frac{1}{\sqrt{c}}\) and \( x = -\frac{1}{\sqrt{c}}\) and with the initial \(x\) value of \(x=0\) that makes the IOV for this solution \( \left( -\frac{1}{\sqrt{c}} , \frac{1}{\sqrt{c}} \right) \)
  4. Applying the initial condition \( y(1)=2 \) we can solve for the constant \( c \)
    Apply the initial condition \( y(1)=2 \) to (2.4.13) and solve for \( c \)
    Apply the initial condition \( y(1)=2 \) to (2.4.12) and solve for \( c \)
    Which equation was easier to solve? Do the two values for \( c \) agree?
    \begin{align*} \underset{2}{\underbrace{y (1)}} = & \frac{\left(1\right) \cdot \left(1 + c \cdot \left(1\right)^2\right)}{1-c \cdot \left(1\right)^2} \Longrightarrow c = \frac{1}{3} \end{align*} Substituting \( c = \frac{1}{3} \) back in to (2.4.13) we obtain our particular solution of the IVP \begin{align*} y = & \frac{x\left(1 + \frac{1}{3}x^2\right)}{1- \frac{1}{3}x^2} \\ \end{align*} Multiplying by \( 3 \) on top and bottom to simplify: \begin{align*} y = & \frac{x\left(3 + x^2\right)}{3- x^2} \\ \end{align*} Now to determined the IOV we start by checking for undefined values when \( 3- x^2 = 0\) which occurs when \( x = \sqrt{3}\) or \( x = -\sqrt{3}\) and with the initial \(x\) value of \(x=0\) that makes the IOV for this solution \( (-\sqrt{3}, \sqrt{3}) \)

Bernoulli IVPs
Solve the IVP \begin{equation} \label{eq:2.4.15} \frac{dy}{dx} - xy = xy^{3/2} \quad y(1)=4 \end{equation}
Solution
  1. We recognize (2.4.15) as a Bernoulli equation (2.4.2).
    Identify \( p(x) \), \(f(x)\), and \(r\)
    Solve the "complementary equation"
    The general solution is \( y = ce^{\frac{1}{2} x^2} \). Choosing \( c = 1 \) we will use \( y_1 = e^{\frac{1}{2} x^2} \) and seek solutions of the form \( y = ue^{\frac{1}{2} x^2} \).
    Differentiate \( y = ue^{\frac{1}{2} x^2} \)
    Substituting \( y = e^{\frac{1}{2} x^2} \) and its derivative into (2.4.15), we obtain \begin{equation} \frac{du}{dx} e^{\frac{1}{2} x^2} + xue^{\frac{1}{2} x^2} - xue^{\frac{1}{2} x^2} = x(ue^{\frac{1}{2} x^2})^{\frac{3}{2}} \end{equation}
    Simplify and separate the variables from (2.4.16)
    Now we antidifferentiate to find \(u\). \begin{align*} \int \frac{1}{u} du &= \int xe^{\frac{1}{4} x^2} dx \\ \ln |u| &= 2e^{\frac{1}{4} x^2} + c \\ u &= ce^{2e^{\frac{1}{4} x^2}} \end{align*} Substituting back in to \( y = ue^{\frac{1}{2} x^2} \) \begin{align*} y = ce^{2e^{\frac{1}{4} x^2}} \cdot e^{\frac{1}{2} x^2} \\ y = ce^{x^2 e^{\frac{1}{4} x^2}} \end{align*} Applying the initial condition, \begin{align*} y(1) &= ce^{1^2 \cdot e^{\frac{1}{4} \cdot 1^2}} \\ 4 &= ce^{\sqrt[4]{e}} \\ \frac{4}{e^{-\sqrt[4]{e}}} &= c \\ \end{align*} Substituting in our constant, we have our particular solution. \begin{align*} y = \frac{4}{e^{-\sqrt[4]{e}}} e^{x^2 e^{\frac{1}{4} x^2}} \\ y = 4e^{x^2 e^{\frac{1}{4} x^2} - \sqrt[4]{e}} \end{align*}

Riccati Equations
A generalized Riccati equation is of the form \[ \frac{dy}{dx} = P(x)+Q(x)y+R(x)y^2 \] Some Riccati equations can be transformed into Bernoulli equations using the substitution \(y=z+y_1\) where \(z\) is some function of \(x\) and \(y_1\) is a known particular solution to the Riccati equation.
\[ \frac{dy}{dx} = 1+x - (1+2x)y+xy^2 \quad y_1=1 \]
  1. Identify \(P(x),\) \(Q(x),\) and \(R(x)\) for the Riccati equation and plot a direction field with some integral curves
  2. Verify that \(y_1\) is a solution of the Riccati equation and identify it on the direction field
  3. Apply the substitution \(y = z + y_1\) to convert the Riccati equation to a Bernoulli equation and identity \(p(x),\) \(f(x),\) and \(r\)
  4. Solve the Bernoulli equation by first solving the "complementary" equation followed by variation of parameters then substitute back at the end to find \(y\)
Solution
  1. \[ \frac{dy}{dx} = \overset{P(x)}{\overbrace{1+x}} - \overset{Q(x)}{\overbrace{(1+2x)}}y + \overset{R(x)}{\overbrace{x}} y^2 \]
  2. Substituting in \(y_1=1\) and \(dy_1/dx=0\) into our Riccati equation \begin{align*} \frac{dy_1}{dx} \overset{?}{=} & 1+x - (1+2x)y_1+xy_1^2 \\ 0 \overset{?}{=} & 1+x - (1+2x)\cdot(1)+x\cdot(1)^2 \\ 0 \overset{?}{=} & 1+x - 1 - 2x + x \\ 0 \overset{?}{=} & \underbrace{1 - 1} + \underbrace{x - 2x + x} \\ 0 = & \quad 0 \quad + \quad 0 \quad \checkmark \end{align*}
  3. Substituting \(y_1=1\) and differentiating \(y = z + y_1\) \begin{align*} y = & \, z + 1 \\ \\ \frac{dy}{dx} = & \frac{d}{dx} \Big( z + 1 \Big) = \frac{dz}{dx} \\ \end{align*} We will now convert the Riccati equation to a Bernoulli equation in \(z\) \begin{align*} \frac{dy}{dx} = & 1+x - (1+2x)y+xy^2 \\ \frac{dz}{dx} = & 1+x - (1+2x)\cdot (z+1)+x \cdot (z+1)^2 \\ \frac{dz}{dx} = & \Big( 1 -1 \Big) + \Big( x - 2x + x \Big) + \Big( 2xz - 2xz \Big) - z + xz^2 \\ \frac{dz}{dx} = & - z + xz^2 \\ \frac{dz}{dx} \, + \, & \, z \, = \, xz^2 \\ \end{align*} Now our equation matches the Bernoulli form 2.4.2 with \(p(x)=1,\) \(f(x) = x,\) and \(r=2\) \begin{align*} \frac{dz}{dx} \, + \, & \, z \, = \, xz^2 \\ \end{align*}
  4. Now we will solve the Bernoulli equation by first solving the "complementary" equation \begin{align*} \frac{dz}{dx} \, + \, & \, z \, = \, 0 \\ \end{align*}
    Solve \( {dz}/{dx} \, + \, \, z \, = \, 0\) by separating variables
    Solving we obtain \(z_1 = e^{-x}\) and using variation of parameters we seek solutions of the Bernoulli equation in \(z\) of the form \(z = uz_1 = ue^{-x}\)
    Differentiate \(z = ue^{-x}\) using product rule
    Substituting \(z = ue^{-x}\) and its derivative into the Bernoulli equation \begin{align*} \frac{dz}{dx} \, + \, & \, z \, = \, xz^2 \\ \overbrace{e^{-x}\frac{du}{dx} - ue^{-x}} \, + \, & \, \overbrace{ ue^{-x} } \, = \, x \big(ue^{-x}\big)^2 \\ e^{-x}\frac{du}{dx} \, = \, u^2 \, x \, e^{-2x} \\ \frac{du}{dx} \, = \, u^2 \, x \, e^{-x} \\ \frac{1}{u^2} \frac{du}{dx} \, = \, x \, e^{-x} \\ \int \frac{1}{u^2} du \, = \, \int x \, e^{-x} \, dx \\ \int u^{-2} du \, = \, \int (-x) \, e^{-x} \, d(-x) \\ \int u^{-2} du \, = \, \int w \, e^{w} \, dw \\ -u^{-1} \, = \, w \, e^{w} - \int e^{w} dw \\ -\frac{1}{u} \, = \, ( w -1 ) e^{w} + c \\ u \, = \, \frac{-1}{( w -1 ) e^{w} + c} \\ u \, = \, \frac{-1}{( -x -1 ) e^{-x} + c} \\ u \, = \, \frac{1}{(x +1 ) e^{-x} + c} \\ ue^{-x} \, = \, \frac{e^{-x}}{(x +1 ) e^{-x} + c} \\ z \, = \, \frac{1}{(x +1 ) + ce^{x}} \\ z + 1\, = \, \frac{1}{(x +1 ) + ce^{x}} + 1 \\ y \, = \, \frac{(x +2 ) + ce^{x}}{(x +1 ) + ce^{x}} \\ \end{align*}
