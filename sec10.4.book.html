10.4 Constant Coefficient Homogeneous Systems I
Real Eigenvalues
We’ll now begin our study of the homogeneous system (10.4.1) where \({\bf A}\) is an \(n\times n\) constant matrix \begin {equation} \label {eq:10.4.1} \dfrac{d{\bf \vec{y}}}{dt}={\bf A}{\bf \vec{y}} \end {equation} Since \({\bf A}\) is continuous on \((-\infty ,\infty )\), Theorem 10.2.1 implies that all solutions of (10.4.1) are defined on \((-\infty ,\infty )\). Therefore, when we refer to solutions of \(\dfrac{d{\bf \vec{y}}}{dt}={\bf A}{\bf \vec{y}}\), we’ll mean solutions on \((-\infty ,\infty )\).
In this section we assume that all the eigenvalues of \(A\) are real and that \(A\) has a set of \(n\) linearly independent eigenvectors. In the next two sections we consider the cases where some of the eigenvalues of \(A\) are complex, or where \(A\) does not have \(n\) linearly independent eigenvectors.



In Example 10.3.2 we showed that the vector functions \(\{\bf \vec{y}_1 , \vec{y}_2\}\) form a fundamental set of solutions of the system (10.4.2) \[ {\bf \vec{y}}_1=\twocol {-e^{2t}}{2e^{2t}}\quad \mbox{ and } \quad {\bf \vec{y}}_2=\twocol {-e^{-t}}{e^{-t}} \] \begin {equation} \label {eq:10.4.2} \dfrac{d{\bf \vec{y}}}{dt}=\twobytwo {-4}{-3}65 {\bf \vec{y}} \end {equation} But we did not show how we obtained \({\bf \vec{y}}_1\) and \({\bf \vec{y}}_2\) in the first place. To solve we will seek solutions of the form \[ {\bf \vec{y}} = {\bf \vec{u}} e^{rt} \] Where \({\bf \vec{u}} \) is some constant vector to be determined and \(r\) will be determined by the characteristic equation. Now differentiating \({\bf \vec{y}}\) we obtain \[ \frac{d{\bf \vec{y}}}{dt} = r{\bf \vec{u}} e^{rt} \] Substituting into (10.4.2) \begin{align*} \dfrac{d{\bf \vec{y}}}{dt} & =\twobytwo {-4}{-3}65 {\bf \vec{y}} \\ \\ r{\bf \vec{u}} e^{rt} & =\twobytwo {-4}{-3}65 {\bf \vec{u}} e^{rt} \\ \\ {\bf \vec{0}} & =\twobytwo {-4}{-3}65 {\bf \vec{u}} e^{rt} - r{\bf \vec{u}} e^{rt} \\ \\ \end{align*} Now to factor out the vector expression \({\bf \vec{u}} e^{rt}\) we have to write a factor of the identity matrix \({\bf I} \) \begin{align*} {\bf \vec{0}} & =\twobytwo {-4}{-3}65 {\bf \vec{u}} e^{rt} - r{\bf I} \cdot {\bf \vec{u}} e^{rt} \\ \\ {\bf \vec{0}} & =\left[\twobytwo {-4}{-3}65 - r{\bf \vec{I}} \right] {\bf \vec{u}} e^{rt} \\ \\ \end{align*} Substituting in \({\bf \vec{I}} = \twobytwo 1001\) the definition of the identity matrix we obtain \begin{align*} {\bf \vec{0}} & =\left[\twobytwo {-4}{-3}65 - r \twobytwo 1001 \right] {\bf \vec{u}} \\ \\ {\bf \vec{0}} & =\twobytwo {-4-r}{-3}6{5-r} {\bf \vec{u}} \\ \\ \twocol 00 & =\twobytwo {-4-r}{-3}6{5-r} \twocol {u_1}{u_2} \\ \\ \end{align*} Which can be written as a system of equations \begin{align} \label {eq:10.4.3} \left\{ \begin{array}{ccc} (-4-r)u_1 &-& 3u_2 &=& 0 \\ 6u_1 &+& (5-r)u_2 &=& 0 \\ \end{array} \right. \end{align} Recall that if the determinant of the coefficient matrix of a system is nonzero then the system has only the trivial solution. Similarly if the determinant is zero then the system has a nontrivial solution. Therefore to seek nontrivial solutions we must guarantee that the determinant of the coefficient matrix is zero \begin{align*} \det \left( \begin{array}{ccc} -4-r & -3 \\ 6 & 5-r \\ \end{array} \right) & =0 \\ \\ (-4-r)(5-r)+3\cdot 6 & =0 \\ (r^2-r-20)+18 & =0 \\ \underset{p(r)}{\underbrace{r^2-r-2}} & =0 \\ \end{align*} So we have our characteristic polynomial \(p(r)=r^2-r-2\) and characteristic equation \(p(r)=0\) which we can solve by factoring in this case \begin{align*} r^2-r-2 & =0 \\ (r+1)(r-2) & =0 \\ r+1= 0 \quad r-2 & =0 \\ r= -1 \quad r =& \,2 \\ r_1= -1 \quad r_2 =& \,2 \\ \end{align*} We'll call the roots of the characteristic polynomial eigenvalues and the solutions to the system are \begin{align*} {\bf \vec{y}_1} & = {\bf \vec{u}_1} e^{r_1t}= {\bf \vec{u}_1} e^{-t} \quad\text{and}\quad {\bf \vec{y}_2} = {\bf \vec{u}_2} e^{r_2t} ={\bf \vec{u}_2} e^{2t} \\ \end{align*} But we are not done solving since we still need to determine \({\bf \vec{u}_1}\) and \({\bf \vec{u}_2}\) the unknown vectors. For each eigenvalue, the associated vector is called an eigenvector, so \({\bf \vec{u}_1}\) is the eigenvector for \(r_1=-1\) and \({\bf \vec{u}_2}\) is the eigenvector for \(r_2=2\) and we'll determine them using the system of equations (10.4.3) \begin{align*} \left\{ \begin{array}{ccc} (-4-r)u_1 &-& 3u_2 &=& 0 \\ 6u_1 &+& (5-r)u_2 &=& 0 \\ \end{array} \right. \tag{3} \end{align*} Substituting \(r_1=-1\) we have \begin{align*} \left\{ \begin{array}{ccc} (-4-r_1)u_1 &-& 3u_2 &=& 0 \\ 6u_1 &+& (5-r_1)u_2 &=& 0 \\ \end{array} \right. \\ \\ \left\{ \begin{array}{ccc} (-4+1)u_1 &-& 3u_2 &=& 0 \\ 6u_1 &+& (5+1)u_2 &=& 0 \\ \end{array} \right. \\ \\ \left\{ \begin{array}{ccc} -3u_1 &-& 3u_2 &=& 0 \\ 6u_1 &+& 6u_2 &=& 0 \\ \end{array} \right. \\ \\ \left\{ \begin{array}{ccc} u_1 &+& u_2 &=& 0 \\ u_1 &+& u_2 &=& 0 \\ \end{array} \right. \\ \\ \end{align*} The system simplifies down to the single equation \[u_1 + u_2 = 0 \] Since we have only 1 equation with 2 unknowns then we have a free variable, so we'll let \(u_1=c\) where \(c\) is any number. Then we can solve for \(u_2\) in terms of \(c\) \begin{align*} u_1 + u_2 & = 0 \\ c + u_2 & = 0 \\ u_2 & = -c \\ \end{align*} Therefore we have determined the eigenvector \({\bf \vec{u}_1}\) \[ {\bf \vec{u}_1} =\twocol {u_1}{u_2} =\twocol {c}{-c} =c\twocol {1}{-1} \] Since \(c\) can be any number we'll select \(c=1\) for a simple eigenvector \begin{align} \label {eq:10.4.4} {\bf \vec{u}_1}=\twocol {1}{-1} \end{align} With the eigenvalue-eigenvector pair \(r_1\) and \({\bf \vec{u}_1}\) we have one of the solutions to our original equation \begin{align} \label {eq:10.4.5} {\bf \vec{y}_1} = {\bf \vec{u}_1}e^{r_1 t} = \twocol {1}{-1}e^{-t} \end{align} Now we'll substitute \(r_2=2\) into the system (10.4.3) to find \({\bf \vec{u}_2}\) \begin{align*} \left\{ \begin{array}{ccc} (-4-r_2)u_1 &-& 3u_2 &=& 0 \\ 6u_1 &+& (5-r_2)u_2 &=& 0 \\ \end{array} \right. \\ \\ \left\{ \begin{array}{ccc} (-4-2)u_1 &-& 3u_2 &=& 0 \\ 6u_1 &+& (5-2)u_2 &=& 0 \\ \end{array} \right. \\ \\ \left\{ \begin{array}{ccc} -6u_1 &-& 3u_2 &=& 0 \\ 6u_1 &+& 3u_2 &=& 0 \\ \end{array} \right. \\ \\ \left\{ \begin{array}{ccc} 2u_1 &+& u_2 &=& 0 \\ 2u_1 &+& u_2 &=& 0 \\ \end{array} \right. \\ \\ \end{align*} The system simplifies down to the single equation \[2u_1 + u_2 = 0 \] So again we have a free variable, so we'll let \(u_1=c\) where \(c\) is any number. Then we can solve for \(u_2\) in terms of \(c\) \begin{align*} 2u_1 + u_2 & = 0 \\ 2c + u_2 & = 0 \\ u_2 & = -2c \\ \end{align*} Therefore we have determined the eigenvector \({\bf \vec{u}_2}\) \[ {\bf \vec{u}_2} =\twocol {u_1}{u_2} =\twocol {c}{-2c} =c\twocol {1}{-2} \] Since \(c\) can be any number we'll select \(c=1\) for a simple eigenvector \begin{align} \label {eq:10.4.6} {\bf \vec{u}_2}=\twocol {1}{-2} \end{align} With the eigenvalue-eigenvector pair \(r_2\) and \({\bf \vec{u}_2}\) we have the second solution to our original equation \begin{align} \label {eq:10.4.7} {\bf \vec{y}_2} = {\bf \vec{u}_2}e^{r_2 t} = \twocol {1}{-2}e^{2t} \end{align} So we have a fundamental set of solutions \[\{{\bf \vec{y}_1},{\bf \vec{y}_2}\} =\left\{ \twocol {1}{-1}e^{-t}, \twocol {1}{-2}e^{2t} \right\} \] We can finally write the general solution \begin{align*} {\bf \vec{y}} & = c_1 {\bf \vec{y}_1} + c_2 {\bf \vec{y}_2} = c_1 \twocol {1}{-1}e^{-t} + c_2 \twocol {1}{-2}e^{2t} \\ \end{align*}
In (10.4.5) and (10.4.7) the constant coefficients in the arguments of the exponential functions are the eigenvalues of the coefficient matrix in (10.4.2), and the vector coefficients of the exponential functions are associated eigenvectors. This illustrates the next theorem.
Theorem 10.4.1 Suppose the \(n\times n\) constant matrix \({\bf A}\) has \(n\) real eigenvalues \( {r} _1, {r} _2,\ldots , {r} _n\) with associated linearly independent eigenvectors \({\bf \vec{u}}_1,{\bf \vec{u}}_2,\ldots ,{\bf \vec{u}}_n\). Then the functions \[ {\bf \vec{y}}_1={\bf \vec{u}}_1e^{ {r} _1 t},\, {\bf \vec{y}}_2={\bf \vec{u}}_2e^{ {r} _2 t},\, \dots ,\, {\bf \vec{y}}_n={\bf \vec{u}}_ne^{ {r} _n t} \] form a fundamental set of solutions of \(\dfrac{d{\bf \vec{y}}}{dt}={\bf A}{\bf \vec{y}}\) and the general solution of this system is \[ {\bf \vec{y}}=c_1{\bf \vec{u}}_1e^{ {r} _1 t}+c_2{\bf \vec{u}}_2e^{ {r} _2 t} +\cdots +c_n{\bf \vec{u}}_ne^{ {r} _n t}\]
Proof Differentiating \({\bf \vec{y}}_i={\bf \vec{x}}_ie^{ {r} _it}\) and recalling that \(A{\bf \vec{x}}_i= {r} _i{\bf \vec{x}}_i\) yields \[ \dfrac{d{\bf \vec{y}}_i}{dt}= {r} _i{\bf \vec{x}}_ie^{ {r} _it}=A{\bf \vec{x}}_ie^{ {r} _it} =A{\bf \vec{y}}_i\] This shows that \({\bf \vec{y}}_i\) is a solution of \(\dfrac{d{\bf \vec{y}}}{dt}=A{\bf \vec{y}}\).
The Wronskian of \(\{{\bf \vec{y}}_1,{\bf \vec{y}}_2,\ldots ,{\bf \vec{y}}_n\}\) is \[ \left | \begin {array}{cccc} x_{11}e^{ {r} _1 t}& x_{12}e^{ {r} _2 t}&\cdots& x_{1n}e^{ {r} _n t}\\ x_{21}e^{ {r} _1 t}& x_{22}e^{ {r} _2 t}&\cdots& x_{2n}e^{ {r} _n t}\\ \vdots& \vdots& \ddots& \vdots \\ x_{n1}e^{ {r} _1 t}& x_{n2}e^{ {r} _2 t}&\cdots& x_{nn}e^{ {r} x_n t} \end {array} \right | =e^{ {r} _1 t}e^{ {r} _2 t}\cdots e^{ {r} _n t} \left | \begin {array}{cccc} x_{11}&x_{12}&\cdots&x _{1n}\cr x_{21}&x_{22}&\cdots&x _{2n}\cr \vdots& \vdots& \ddots& \vdots \cr x_{n1}&x_{n2}&\cdots&x _{nn}\cr \end {array} \right | \] Since the columns of the determinant on the right are \({\bf \vec{x}}_1\), \({\bf \vec{x}}_2\), …, \({\bf \vec{x}}_n\), which are assumed to be linearly independent, the determinant is nonzero. Therefore Theorem 10.3.3 implies that \(\{{\bf \vec{y}}_1,{\bf \vec{y}}_2,\ldots ,{\bf \vec{y}}_n\}\) is a fundamental set of solutions of \(\dfrac{d{\bf \vec{y}}}{dt}=A{\bf \vec{y}}\).

Geometric Properties of Solutions when \(n=2\)
We’ll now consider the geometric properties of solutions of a \(2\times 2\) constant coefficient system \begin {equation} \label {eq:10.4.18} \twocol {\dfrac{dy_1}{dt}}{\dfrac{dy_2}{dt}}=\left [ \begin {array}{cc} a_{11}&a_{12}\\ a_{21}&a_{22} \end {array} \right ]\twocol {y_1}{y_2} \end {equation} It is convenient to think of a “\(y_1\)-\(y_2\) plane," where a point is identified by rectangular coordinates \((y_1,y_2)\). If \({\bf \vec{y}}=\displaystyle {\twocol {y_1}{y_2}}\) is a non-constant solution of (10.4.18), then the point \((y_1(t),y_2(t))\) moves along a curve \(C\) in the \(y_1\)-\(y_2\) plane as \(t\) varies from \(-\infty \) to \(\infty \). We call \(C\) the trajectory of \(\bf y\). (We also say that \(C\) is a trajectory of the system (10.4.18).) I’s important to note that \(C\) is the trajectory of infinitely many solutions of (10.4.18), since if \(\tau \) is any real number, then \({\bf \vec{y}}(t-\tau )\) is a solution of (10.4.18) (Exercise 28 (b)), and \((y_1(t-\tau ),y_2(t-\tau ))\) also moves along \(C\) as \(t\) varies from \(-\infty \) to \(\infty \). Moreover, Exercise 28 (c) implies that distinct trajectories of (10.4.18) can’t intersect, and that two solutions \({\bf \vec{y}}_1\) and \({\bf \vec{y}}_2\) of (10.4.18) have the same trajectory if and only if \({\bf \vec{y}}_2(t)={\bf \vec{y}}_1(t-\tau )\) for some \(\tau \).
From Exercise 28 (a), a trajectory of a nontrivial solution of (10.4.18) can’t contain \((0,0)\), which we define to be the trajectory of the trivial solution \({\bf \vec{y}}\equiv 0\). More generally, if \({\bf \vec{y}}=\displaystyle {\twocol {k_1}{k_2}}\ne {\bf 0}\) is a constant solution of (10.4.18) (which could occur if zero is an eigenvalue of the matrix of (10.4.18)), we define the trajectory of \(\bf y\) to be the single point \((k_1,k_2)\).
To be specific, this is the question: What do the trajectories look like, and how are they traversed? In this section we’ll answer this question, assuming that the matrix \[ A=\left [ \begin {array}{cc} a_{11}&a_{12}\\ a_{21}&a_{22} \end {array} \right ] \] of (10.4.18) has real eigenvalues \( {r} _1\) and \( {r} _2\) with associated linearly independent eigenvectors \({\bf \vec{x}}_1\) and \({\bf \vec{x}}_2\). Then the general solution of (10.4.18) is \begin {equation} \label {eq:10.4.19} {\bf \vec{y}}= c_1{\bf \vec{x}}_1 e^{ {r} _1 t}+c_2{\bf \vec{x}}_2e^{ {r} _2 t} \end {equation} We’ll consider other situations in the next two sections.
We leave it to you (Exercise 35) to classify the trajectories of (10.4.18) if zero is an eigenvalue of \(A\). We’ll confine our attention here to the case where both eigenvalues are nonzero. In this case the simplest situation is where \( {r} _1= {r} _2\ne 0\), so (10.4.19) becomes \[ {\bf \vec{y}}=(c_1{\bf \vec{x}}_1+c_2{\bf \vec{x}}_2)e^{ {r} _1 t}\] Since \({\bf \vec{x}}_1\) and \({\bf \vec{x}}_2\) are linearly independent, an arbitrary vector \(\bf x\) can be written as \({\bf \vec{x}}=c_1{\bf \vec{x}}_1+c_2{\bf \vec{x}}_2\). Therefore the general solution of (10.4.18) can be written as \({\bf \vec{y}}={\bf \vec{x}}e^{ {r} _1 t}\) where \(\bf x\) is an arbitrary \(2\)-vector, and the trajectories of nontrivial solutions of (10.4.18) are half-lines through (but not including) the origin. The direction of motion is away from the origin if \( {r} _1>0\) (Figure 10.4.1), toward it if \( {r} _1<0\) (Figure 10.4.2). (In these and the next figures an arrow through a point indicates the direction of motion along the trajectory through the point.)
Now suppose \( {r} _2> {r} _1\), and let \(L_1\) and \(L_2\) denote lines through the origin parallel to \({\bf \vec{x}}_1\) and \({\bf \vec{x}}_2\), respectively. By a half-line of \(L_1\) (or \(L_2\)), we mean either of the rays obtained by removing the origin from \(L_1\) (or \(L_2\)).
Letting \(c_2=0\) in (10.4.19) yields \({\bf \vec{y}}=c_1{\bf \vec{x}}_1e^{ {r} _1 t}\). If \(c_1\ne 0\), the trajectory defined by this solution is a half-line of \(L_1\). The direction of motion is away from the origin if \( {r} _1>0\), toward the origin if \( {r} _1<0\). Similarly, the trajectory of \({\bf \vec{y}}=c_2{\bf \vec{x}}_2e^{ {r} _2 t}\) with \(c_2\ne 0\) is a half-line of \(L_2\).
Henceforth, we assume that \(c_1\) and \(c_2\) in (10.4.19) are both nonzero. In this case, the trajectory of (10.4.19) can’t intersect \(L_1\) or \(L_2\), since every point on these lines is on the trajectory of a solution for which either \(c_1=0\) or \(c_2=0\). (Remember: distinct trajectories can’t intersect!). Therefore the trajectory of (10.4.19) must lie entirely in one of the four open sectors bounded by \(L_1\) and \(L_2\), but do not any point on \(L_1\) or \(L_2\). Since the initial point \((y_1(0),y_2(0))\) defined by \[ {\bf \vec{y}}(0)=c_1{\bf \vec{x}}_1+c_2{\bf \vec{x}}_2 \] is on the trajectory, we can determine which sector contains the trajectory from the signs of \(c_1\) and \(c_2\), as shown in Figure 10.4.3.
The direction of \({\bf \vec{y}}(t)\) in (10.4.19) is the same as that of \begin {equation} \label {eq:10.4.20} e^{- {r} _2 t}{\bf \vec{y}}(t)= c_1{\bf \vec{x}}_1e^{-( {r} _2- {r} _1)t}+c_2{\bf \vec{x}}_2 \end {equation} and of \begin {equation} \label {eq:10.4.21} e^{- {r} _1 t}{\bf \vec{y}}(t)=c_1{\bf \vec{x}}_1+c_2{\bf \vec{x}}_2e^{( {r} _2- {r} _1)t} \end {equation} Since the right side of (10.4.20) approaches \(c_2{\bf \vec{x}}_2\) as \(t\to \infty \), the trajectory is asymptotically parallel to \(L_2\) as \(t\to \infty \). Since the right side of (10.4.21) approaches \(c_1{\bf \vec{x}}_1\) as \(t\to -\infty \), the trajectory is asymptotically parallel to \(L_1\) as \(t\to -\infty \).
The shape and direction of traversal of the trajectory of (10.4.19) depend upon whether \( {r} _1\) and \( {r} _2\) are both positive, both negative, or of opposite signs. We’ll now analyze these three cases.
Henceforth \(\|{\bf \vec{u}}\|\) denote the length of the vector \(\bf u\).
Case 1: \( {r} _2> {r} _1>0\) Figure 10.4.4 shows some typical trajectories. In this case, \(\lim _{t\to -\infty }\|{\bf \vec{y}}(t)\|=0\), so the trajectory is not only asymptotically parallel to \(L_1\) as \(t\to -\infty \), but is actually asymptotically tangent to \(L_1\) at the origin. On the other hand, \(\lim _{t\to \infty }\|{\bf \vec{y}}(t)\|=\infty \) and \[ \lim _{t\to \infty }\left \|{\bf \vec{y}}(t)-c_2{\bf \vec{x}}_2e^{ {r} _2 t}\right \|=\lim _{t\to \infty }\|c_1{\bf x_1}e^{ {r} _1t}\|=\infty \] so, although the trajectory is asymptotically parallel to \(L_2\) as \(t\to \infty \), it’s not asymptotically tangent to \(L_2\). The direction of motion along each trajectory is away from the origin.
Case 2: \(0> {r} _2> {r} _1\) Figure 10.4.5 shows some typical trajectories. In this case, \(\lim _{t\to \infty }\|{\bf \vec{y}}(t)\|=0\), so the trajectory is asymptotically tangent to \(L_2\) at the origin as \(t\to \infty \). On the other hand, \(\lim _{t\to -\infty }\|{\bf \vec{y}}(t)\|=\infty \) and \[ \lim _{t\to -\infty }\left \|{\bf \vec{y}}(t)-c_1{\bf \vec{x}}_1e^{ {r} _1 t}\right \|=\lim _{t\to -\infty }\|c_2{\bf \vec{x}}_2e^{ {r} _2t}\|=\infty \] so, although the trajectory is asymptotically parallel to \(L_1\) as \(t\to -\infty \), it’s not asymptotically tangent to it. The direction of motion along each trajectory is toward the origin.
Case 3: \( {r} _2>0> {r} _1\) Figure 10.4.6 shows some typical trajectories. In this case, \[ \lim _{t\to \infty }\|{\bf \vec{y}}(t)\|=\infty \quad \mbox{ and } \quad \lim _{t\to \infty }\left \|{\bf \vec{y}}(t)-c_2{\bf \vec{x}}_2e^{ {r} _2 t}\right \|=\lim _{t\to \infty }\|c_1{\bf \vec{x}}_1e^{ {r} _1t}\|=0\] so the trajectory is asymptotically tangent to \(L_2\) as \(t\to \infty \). The direction of motion is toward the origin on \(L_1\) and away from the origin on \(L_2\). The direction of motion along any other trajectory is away from \(L_1\), toward \(L_2\).
