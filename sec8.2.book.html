Inverse Laplace Transform
We say that \(F(s)\) is the Laplace Transform of \(f(t)\) and write \[ {\cal L} \Big\{ f(t) \Big\} = F(s) \] We will also say that \(f(t)\) is an inverse Laplace Transform of \(F(s)\) and write \[ {\cal L}^{-1} \Big[ F(s) \Big] = f(t)\] To solve differential equations with the Laplace transform, we must be able to obtain \(f(t)\) from its transform \(F(s)\). To do this, we will use the Table of Laplace Transforms that we found in Section 8.1 to find the inverse Laplace transforms that we’ll need.


 Use the Table of Laplace Transforms to find the inverse Laplace Transforms
  1. \[ {\cal L}^{-1}\left[ {1\over s^2-1}\right] \]
  2. \[ {\cal L}^{-1}\left[ {s\over s^2+9}\right]\]
Solution
  1. To find \( \displaystyle {\cal L}^{-1}\left[ {1\over s^2-1}\right] \) we seek the function \(f(t)\) such that \[ \displaystyle {\cal L} \big\{ f(t) \big\} = {1\over s^2-1}\] From the table we find the transform pair \[\displaystyle {\cal L} \big\{ \sinh \omega t \big\} = \frac{\omega}{s^2-\omega^2} \] And setting \(\omega=1\) in the transform pair we obtain our equation \[\displaystyle {\cal L} \big\{ \sinh t \big\} = \frac{1}{s^2-1} \] Therefore we have \[\displaystyle {\cal L}^{-1}\left[ {1\over s^2-1}\right] = \sinh t \]
  2. To find \( \displaystyle {\cal L}^{-1}\left[ {s\over s^2+9}\right] \) we seek the function \(f(t)\) such that \[ \displaystyle {\cal L} \big\{ f(t) \big\} = {s\over s^2+9}\] From the table we find the transform pair \[\displaystyle {\cal L} \big\{ \cos \omega t \big\} = \frac{s}{s^2+\omega^2} \] And setting \(\omega=3\) in the transform pair we obtain our equation \[\displaystyle {\cal L} \big\{ \cos 3 t \big\} = \frac{s}{s^2+9} \] Therefore we have \[\displaystyle {\cal L}^{-1}\left[ {s\over s^2+9}\right] = \cos 3t \]
Theorem 8.2.1 Linearity Property
Suppose \( F(s) \) and \( G(s) \) Laplace transforms are defined. Then the following properties are true: \[ {\cal L}^{-1} \big[ F(s) + G(s) \big] ={\cal L}^{-1} \big\{ F(s) \big\} + {\cal L}^{-1} \big\{ G(s) \big\} \] \[ {\cal L}^{-1} \big[ c \cdot F(s) \big] = c \cdot {\cal L}^{-1} \big[ f(x) \big] \] Essentially the inverse Laplace transform can break up across terms and constants can be factored out.


Use the Linearity Property and the Table of Laplace Transforms to find the inverse Laplace Transform
\[ {\cal L}^{-1}\left[ {8\over s+5}+{7\over s^2+3}\right ]\]
Solution
  1. To find \( \displaystyle {\cal L}^{-1}\left[ {8\over s+5}+{7\over s^2+3}\right ] \) we first apply the linearity \begin{align*} {\cal L}^{-1}\left[ {8\over s+5}+{7\over s^2+3}\right ] = & \, 8 \, {\cal L}^{-1}\left[ {1\over s+5}\right ] + 7 \, {\cal L}^{-1}\left[ {1\over s^2+3}\right ] \end{align*} Now we find the inverse Laplace transforms the same way we did in Example 8.2.1



  2. First to find \( \displaystyle {\cal L}^{-1}\left[ {1\over s+5}\right] \) we seek the function \(f(t)\) such that \[ \displaystyle {\cal L} \big\{ f(t) \big\} = {1\over s+5}\] From the table we find the transform pair \[\displaystyle {\cal L} \big\{ e^{at} \big\} = \frac{1}{s-a} \] And setting \(a=-5\) we obtain our exact equation \begin{align*} {\cal L} \big\{ e^{-5t} \big\} = \frac{1}{s+5} \end{align*} Thus we have \[\displaystyle {\cal L}^{-1}\left[ {1\over s+5}\right] = e^{-5t} \]



  3. Now to find \( \displaystyle {\cal L}^{-1}\left[ {1\over s^2+3}\right] \) we seek the function \(f(t)\) such that \[ \displaystyle {\cal L} \big\{ f(t) \big\} = {1\over s^2+3}\] This one is a little trickier than the previous ones. From the table we find the transform pair \[\displaystyle {\cal L} \big\{ \sin \omega \big\} = \frac{\omega}{s^2+\omega^2} \] And setting \(\omega=\sqrt{3}\) in the transform pair gets us close to our equation: \begin{align*} {\cal L} \big\{ \sin \sqrt{3} \big\} = & \frac{\sqrt{3}}{s^2+3} \end{align*} However, there is just an extra constant multiplier on the right side, so we simply divide both sides by \(\sqrt{3}\) and use the linearity to obtain our equation \begin{align*} \frac{1}{\sqrt{3}} {\cal L} \big\{ \sin \sqrt{3} \big\} = & \frac{1}{s^2+3} \Longrightarrow {\cal L} \left\{ \frac{1}{\sqrt{3}} \sin \sqrt{3} \, \right\} = \frac{1}{s^2+3} \\ \end{align*} Thus we have \[\displaystyle {\cal L}^{-1}\left[ {s\over s^2+3}\right] = \frac{1}{\sqrt{3}} \sin \sqrt{3} \]



  4. Finally substituting the inverse Laplace transforms back in we obtain our answer \begin{align*} {\cal L}^{-1}\left[ {8\over s+5}+{7\over s^2+3}\right ] = & \, 8 \, \underset{e^{-5t}}{\underbrace{{\cal L}^{-1}\left[ {1\over s+5}\right ]}} + 7 \, \underset{\frac{1}{\sqrt{3}} \sin \sqrt{3}}{\underbrace{{\cal L}^{-1}\left[ {1\over s^2+3}\right ]}} \\ = & \, \quad 8 \, e^{-5t} \quad+\quad \, \frac{7}{\sqrt{3}} \sin \sqrt{3} \end{align*}



 Use the Table of Laplace Transforms to find the inverse Laplace Transform
\[ {\cal L}^{-1}\left [{3s+8\over s^2+2s+5}\right ]\]
Solution
  1. To find \( \displaystyle {\cal L}^{-1}\left[ {3s+8\over s^2+2s+5} \right] \) we seek the function \(f(t)\) such that \[ \displaystyle {\cal L} \big\{ f(t) \big\} = {3s+8\over s^2+2s+5}\] From the table there are no transform pairs that match the equation in the format it is in. So far in Examples 8.2.1 and 8.2.2 we have been able to recognize transform pairs directly from the table. Sometimes however, such as in this case, it will be necessary to perform algebraic manipulations first. For the quadratic polynomial in the denominator \(s^2+2s+5\) we'll try to complete the square on it \begin{align*} s^2+2s+5 = & (s^2+2s+ 1) - 1+5 \\ = & (s+ 1)(s+ 1) + 4 \\ = & (s+ 1)^2 +4 \\ \end{align*} So we can rewrite our expression as \begin{align*} {3s+8\over s^2+2s+5} = {3s+8\over (s+ 1)^2 +4} \end{align*}



  2. So now to find \( \displaystyle {\cal L}^{-1}\left[ {3s+8\over (s+ 1)^2 +4} \right] \) we seek the function \(f(t)\) such that \[ \displaystyle {\cal L} \big\{ f(t) \big\} = {3s+8\over (s+ 1)^2 +4}\] While it still is not a perfect match it is certainly closer as we identify the following transform pair: \[\displaystyle {\cal L} \big\{ e^{at} \cos \omega t \big\} = \frac{s-a}{(s-a)^2+\omega^2} \] Substituting \(a=-1\) and \(\omega=2\) we obtain \[\displaystyle {\cal L} \big\{ e^{-t} \cos 2 t \big\} = \frac{s+1}{(s+1)^2+4} \] So we are getting closer but we will still need to further manipulate our expression to get it to match. Since the numerator in one of the Laplace transforms is \(s+1\) we'll go ahead and try to write our expression in terms of \(s+1\) in the numerator by splitting up the \(8\) and then factoring out the \(3\) and then splitting it into two separate terms \begin{align*} {3s+8\over (s+ 1)^2 +4} = & {(3s+3)+5\over (s+ 1)^2 +4} = {3(s+1)+5\over (s+ 1)^2 +4} \\ = & {3(s+1)\over (s+ 1)^2 +4} + {5\over (s+ 1)^2 +4} \\ \end{align*}



  3. Now we can apply the linearity to obtain \begin{align*} & \, 3 \, {\cal L}^{-1}\left[ {s+1\over (s+ 1)^2 +4} \right ] + 5 \, {\cal L}^{-1}\left[ {1\over (s+ 1)^2 +4}\right ] \end{align*} and in fact we have already found the first transform \begin{align*} {\cal L}^{-1}\left[ {s+1\over (s+ 1)^2 +4} \right ] = e^{-t} \cos 2 t \end{align*} Now what is left is the second term so seek the function \(f(t)\) such that \[ \displaystyle {\cal L} \big\{ f(t) \big\} = {1\over (s+ 1)^2 +4}\] From the table we identify the following transform pair: \[\displaystyle {\cal L} \big\{ e^{at} \sin \omega t \big\} = \frac{\omega}{(s-a)^2+\omega^2} \] Substituting \(a=-1\) and \(\omega=2\) we obtain \[\displaystyle {\cal L} \big\{ e^{-t} \sin 2 t \big\} = \frac{2}{(s+1)^2+4} \] Which is not our exact equation but pretty close as it is just a constant multiplier just as in Example 8.2.2 so we simply divide both sides by \({2}\) and use the linearity to obtain our exact equation \begin{align*} \frac{1}{2} {\cal L} \big\{ e^{-t} \sin 2 t \big\} = \frac{1}{(s+1)^2+4} \Longrightarrow {\cal L} \left\{ \, \frac{1}{2} e^{-t} \sin 2 t \right\} = \frac{1}{(s+1)^2+4} \\ \end{align*} Thus we have \begin{align*} {\cal L}^{-1}\left[ {1\over (s+ 1)^2 +4} \right ] = \frac{1}{2} e^{-t} \sin 2 t \end{align*}



  4. Finally substituting the inverse Laplace transforms back in we obtain our answer \begin{align*} {\cal L}^{-1}\left[ {3s+8\over s^2+2s+5} \right] = & \, {\cal L}^{-1}\left[ {3s + 8 \over (s+ 1)^2 +4} \right] \\ = & \, {\cal L}^{-1}\left[ {3(s+1)\over (s+ 1)^2 +4} + {5\over (s+ 1)^2 +4} \right] \\ = & \, 3 \, \underset{e^{-t} \cos 2 t}{\underbrace{ {\cal L}^{-1}\left[ {s+1\over (s+ 1)^2 +4} \right ]}} + 5 \, \underset{\frac{1}{2} e^{-t} \sin 2 t}{\underbrace{ {\cal L}^{-1}\left[ {1\over (s+ 1)^2 +4}\right ] }} \\ = & \quad \, 3 \, e^{-t} \cos 2 t \quad+\quad \, \frac{5}{2} e^{-t} \sin 2 t \\ = & \, \left( 3 \, \cos 2 t \, + \, \frac{5}{2} \sin 2 t \right) e^{-t} \\ \end{align*}
Linear Partial Fractions
Using the Laplace transform to solve differential equations often requires finding the inverse transform of a rational function \[ F(s)={P(s)\over Q(s)}\] where \(P(s)\) and \(Q(s)\) are polynomials in \(s\) with no common factors. Since it can be shown that \(\lim _{s\to \infty }F(s)=0\) if \(F(s)\) is a Laplace transform, we need only consider the case where \(\mbox {degree}(P)<\mbox {degree}(Q)\). To obtain \({\cal L}^{-1}\big[F(s)\big]\), we find the PFD (partial fraction decomposition) of \(F(s)\), obtain inverse transforms of the individual terms in the expansion from the table of Laplace transforms, and use the linearity of the inverse transform.
Recall in Example 8.2.1 we found \[ {\cal L}^{-1}\left[ {1\over s^2-1}\right] \] directly by using the table but we could have used PFD by factoring the denominator first.
Factor \(s^2-1\)
\begin{align*} \frac{1}{(s-1)(s+1)} = & \frac{A}{s-1} + \frac{B}{s+1} \end{align*}
Add \(\dfrac{A}{s-1} + \dfrac{B}{s+1}\) by finding a common denominator
\begin{align*} \frac{1}{(s-1)(s+1)} = & \frac{A(s+1) + B(s-1)}{(s-1)(s+1)} \end{align*} Now since the denominators match, so must the numerators, we we obtain the equation \begin{align*} 1 = & A(s+1) + B(s-1) \end{align*}
Simplify \(A(s+1) + B(s-1)\) and group like terms
\begin{align*} 1 = & s(A+B) + (A-B) \end{align*} So we can finally write it as a system of equations by equating the coefficients on both sides \begin{align*} \left\{ \begin{array}{rrr} A + B= 0 \\ A-B = 1 \end{array} \right. \end{align*}
Solve the system \(\left\{ \begin{array}{rrr} A + B= 0 \\ A-B = 1 \end{array} \right. \)
Adding the equations we obtain \(2A=1\) so \(A=1/2\) then substituting it back in we obtain \(1/2-B=1\) so \(B=-1/2\) so we can rewrite our expression as \begin{align*} \frac{1}{s^2-1}= \frac{1}{(s-1)(s+1)} = & \frac{1}{2}\left(\frac{1}{s-1}\right) - \frac{1}{2}\left(\frac{1}{s+1}\right) \end{align*} And now we can find the inverse Laplace transform of each term separately by applying linearity first and then combining to write in terms of the definition of hyperbolic sine. \begin{align*} {\cal L}^{-1}\left[ \frac{1}{s^2-1} \right] = & \frac{1}{2} \, \underset{e^{t}}{\underbrace{{\cal L}^{-1}\left[\frac{1}{s-1} \right]}} - \frac{1}{2} \, \underset{e^{-t}}{\underbrace{{\cal L}^{-1}\left[ \frac{1}{s+1} \right]}} \\ = & \quad \frac{1}{2} \,e^{t} \quad - \quad \frac{1}{2} \, e^{-t} \\ = & \quad \frac{1}{2} \Big( e^{t} - e^{-t} \Big) \\ = & \quad \sinh t \end{align*} We'll be using PFD extensively for the remainder of the section so time to dust off your algebra!


Find the inverse Laplace transform \begin {equation} {\cal L}^{-1}\left[ {3s+2\over s^2-3s+2} \right] \end {equation}
Solution
  1. Factor \(s^2-3s+2\)
    Factoring the denominator yields distinct linear factors \begin {equation} \label {eq:8.2.2} {3s+2\over s^2-3s+2}={3s+2\over (s-1)(s-2)} \end {equation} Using PFD we seek the coefficients \(A\) and \(B\) such that \begin {equation} \label {eq:8.2.3} {3s+2\over (s-1)(s-2)}={A\over s-1}+{B\over s-2} \end {equation}
    Add \(\dfrac{A}{s-1} + \dfrac{B}{s-2}\) by finding a common denominator
    \begin{align*} {3s+2\over (s-1)(s-2)}={A\over s-1}+{B\over s-2} = & \frac{A(s-2) + B(s-1)}{(s-1)(s-2)} \end{align*} Now since the denominators match, so must the numerators, we we obtain the equation \begin{align*} 3s+2 = & A(s-2) + B(s-1) \end{align*}
    Simplify \( A(s-2) + B(s-1) \) and group like terms
    \begin{align*} 3s+2 = & s(A+B) + (-2A-B) \end{align*} So we can finally write it as a system of equations by equating the coefficients on both sides \begin{align*} \left\{ \begin{array}{rrr} A + B= 3 \\ -2A-B = 2 \end{array} \right. \end{align*}
    Solve the system \(\left\{ \begin{array}{rrr} A + B= 3 \\ -2A-B = 2 \end{array} \right. \)
  2. Solving the system we obtain \(A=-5\) and \(B=8\) so we can rewrite our expression as \[{3s+2\over (s-1)(s-2)}=-{5\over s-1}+{8\over s-2} \] Now applying linearity, we can find the inverse Laplace transform \begin {align*} {\cal L}^{-1}\left[ {3s+2\over s^2-3s+2} \right] = & -5 \, \underset{ e^{t} }{\underbrace{ {\cal L}^{-1}\left[ {1\over s-1} \right] }} + 8 \, \underset{ e^{2t} }{\underbrace{ {\cal L}^{-1}\left[ {1\over s-2} \right] }} \\ = & \quad \quad -5 \, e^{t} \quad + \quad 8 \, e^{2t} \\ \end {align*}



  3. Since we are going to be doing PFD a lot, it would be nice to have a short cut to find the coefficients, and in fact, we have one called Heaviside's method. Start by going back to Equation (8.2.3) \begin {align*} {3s+2\over (s-1)(s-2)}={A\over s-1}+{B\over s-2} \tag{3} \end {align*} Now multiply both sides by the denominator under \(A\) to obtain \begin {align*} (s-1){3s+2\over (s-1)(s-2)} = & \, {A\over s-1} (s-1)+{B\over s-2} (s-1) \\ {3s+2\over (s-2)} = & \, A +{B\over s-2} (s-1) \\ \end {align*} Now if we evaluate both sides at \(s=1\) notice that the term with \(B\) zeros out so we obtain \(A\) \begin {align}\label {eq:8.2.4} {3 \cdot 1 + 2\over (1-2)} = & \, A +{B\over 1-2} \overset{0}{\overbrace{(1-1)}} \Longrightarrow A = -5 \end {align} Now we can do the same thing to obtain \(B\) this time multiplying both sides by the denominator under \(B\) to obtain \begin {align*} (s-2){3s+2\over (s-1)(s-2)} = & \, {A\over s-1} (s-2)+{B\over s-2} (s-2) \\ {3s+2\over (s-1)} = & \, {A\over s-1} (s-2) + B\\ \end {align*} Now if we evaluate both sides at \(s=2\) notice that the term with \(A\) zeros out so we obtain \(B\) \begin {align} \label {eq:8.2.5} {3 \cdot 2 + 2\over (2-1)} = & \, {A\over 2-1} \overset{0}{\overbrace{(2-2)}} + B \Longrightarrow B = 8 \end {align}
Theorem 8.2.2 Heaviside's method
Suppose \begin {equation} \label {eq:8.2.6} F(s)={P(s)\over (s-s_1)(s-s_2)\cdots (s-s_n)} \end {equation} where \(s_1\), \(s_2,\) …\(,\) \(s_n\) are distinct and \(P\) is a polynomial of degree less than \(n.\) Then \[ F(s)={A_1\over s-s_1}+{A_2\over s-s_2}+\cdots +{A_n\over s-s_n},\] where \(A_i\) can be computed from (8.2.6) by multiplying by the factor \(s-s_i\) and setting \(s=s_i\) elsewhere.


Apply PFD and Heaviside's method calculate the inverse Laplace transform \begin {equation} \label {eq:8.2.7} {\cal L}^{-1}\left[ {6+(s+1)(s^2-5s+11)\over s(s-1)(s-2)(s+1)} \right] \end {equation}
Solution
  1. First split up (8.2.7) using PFD \begin {equation} \label {eq:8.2.8} {6+(s+1)(s^2-5s+11)\over s(s-1)(s-2)(s+1)} ={A\over s}+{B\over s-1}+{C\over s-2}+{D\over s+1} \end {equation} Now we'll use Heaviside's method calculate the coefficients: \begin {align*} A = & \, \Bigg. {6+(s+1)(s^2-5s+11)\over (s-1)(s-2)(s+1)} \Bigg|_{s=0} \\ = & \, {6+(0+1)(0^2-5\cdot 0+11)\over (0-1)(0-2)(0+1)} \\ = & \, {6+(1)(11)\over (-1)(-2)(1)} = \, {17\over 2} \\ \end {align*}
    \begin {align*} B = & \, \Bigg. {6+(s+1)(s^2-5s+11)\over s(s-2)(s+1)} \Bigg|_{s=1} \\ = & \, {6+(1+1)(1^2- 5\cdot 1 + 11)\over 1(1-2)(1+1)} \\ = & \, {6+(2)(1- 5 + 11)\over 1(-1)(2)} = -\frac{20}{2} = -10 \end {align*}

  2. \begin {align*} C = & \, \Bigg. {6+(s+1)(s^2-5s+11)\over s(s-1)(s+1)} \Bigg|_{s=2} \\ = & \, {6+(2+1)(2^2-5\cdot 2 +11)\over 2(2-1)(2+1)} \\ = & \, {6+(3)(4-10 +11)\over 2(1)(3)} = \frac{21}{6} = \frac{7}{2} \end {align*}
    \begin {align*} D = & \, \Bigg. {6+(s+1)(s^2-5s+11)\over s(s-1)(s-2)} \Bigg|_{s=-1} \\ = & \, {6+(-1+1)\big((-2)^2-5\cdot(-1)+11\big)\over -1(-1-1)(-1-2)} \\ = & \, {6+(0)\big(4 + 5 +11\big)\over -1(-2)(-3)} = -\frac{6}{6} = -1 \end {align*}
  3. Substituting back in the coefficients \(A, B, C,\) and \(D\) into (8.2.8) we can and apply linearity to find the inverse Laplace transform \begin {align*} & {\cal L}^{-1}\left[ {6+(s+1)(s^2-5s+11)\over s(s-1)(s-2)(s+1)} \right] \\ \\ = & \frac{17}{2} \, {\cal L}^{-1} \underset{1}{\underbrace{\left[ {1\over s} \right] }} -10 \, \underset{e^{t}}{\underbrace{ {\cal L}^{-1}\left[ {1\over s-1} \right] }} + \frac{7}{2} \, \underset{e^{2t}}{\underbrace{ {\cal L}^{-1}\left[ {1\over s-2} \right] }} - \underset{e^{-t}}{\underbrace{ {\cal L}^{-1}\left[ {1\over s+1} \right] }} \\ = & \quad \frac{17}{2} \quad -\quad 10 \,e^{t} \quad+\quad \frac{7}{2} \, e^{2t} \quad-\quad e^{-t} \end {align*}



    Remark: We didn’t “multiply out” the numerator in (8.2.7) before computing the coefficients in (8.2.8), since it wouldn’t simplify the computations.



Find the inverse Laplace transform of \begin {equation} \label {eq:8.2.9} {\cal L}^{-1}\left[ {8-(s+2)(4s+10)\over (s+1)(s+2)^2} \right] \end {equation}
Solution
  1. First split up (8.2.9) using PFD \begin {equation} \label {eq:8.2.10} {8-(s+2)(4s+10)\over (s+1)(s+2)^2} ={A\over s+1}+{B\over s+2}+{C\over (s+2)^2} \end {equation} Notice that because of the repeated factor \((s+2)(s+2)\) we have to consider the fact that it could originate from separate fractions with denominators \((s+2)\) or \((s+2)^2\) thus we get possible coefficients \(B\) or \(C.\) Now that we know how to use Heaviside's method we'll try to calculate the coefficients: \begin {align*} A = & \, \Bigg. {8-(s+2)(4s+10)\over (s+2)^2} \Bigg|_{s=-1} \\ = & \, {8-(-1+2)(-4+10)\over (-1+2)^2} \\ = & \, {8-(1)(6)\over 1^2} = \, 2 \end {align*}
    Now let's see what happens when we try to apply Heaviside's method to find \(B\) (which has a repeated linear factor). Multiplying (8.2.10) by \((s+2)\) \begin {align*} (s+2){8-(s+2)(4s+10)\over (s+1)(s+2)^2} = & \, {A\over s+1}(s+2)+{B\over s+2}(s+2)+{C\over (s+2)^2}(s+2) \\ {8-(s+2)(4s+10)\over (s+1)(s+2)} = & \, {A\over s+1}(s+2)+B+{C\over (s+2)} \end {align*}
  2. While it may appear to isolate the coefficient \(B\) the next step in Heaviside's method requires evaluating the expression at \(s=-2\) but attempting to do so might zero out the \(A\) term but actually results in zeros in the denominator on the \(C\) term as well as on the left side which are undefined. \begin {align*} {8-(-2+2)(-8+10)\over (-2+1)(-2+2)} = & \, {A\over -2+1}(-2+2)+B+{C\over (-2+2)} \\ \underset{\text{undefined}}{\underbrace{ {8-(0)(2)\over (-1)(0)} }} = & \, \underset{0}{\underbrace{{A\cdot (0)\over -1}}}+B+ \underset{\text{undefined}}{\underbrace{ {C\over 0} }} \\ \end {align*} So we may not be able to find \(B\) using Heaviside's method but let's try to find \(C\) this time by multiplying (8.2.10) by \((s+2)^2\) \begin {align*} (s+2)^2{8-(s+2)(4s+10)\over (s+1)(s+2)^2} = & \, {A\over s+1}(s+2)^2+{B\over s+2}(s+2)^2+{C\over (s+2)^2}(s+2)^2 \\ {8-(s+2)(4s+10)\over (s+1)} = & \, {A\over s+1}(s+2)+B(s+2)+C \end {align*} Now that we multiplied by both factors of \((s+2)^2\) we can evaluate at \(s=-2\) \begin {align*} {8-(-2+2)(-8+10)\over (-2+1)} = & \, {A\over -2+1}(-2+2)+B(-2+2)+C \\ {8-(0)(2)\over (-1)} = & \, {A \cdot (0) \over -1}+B(0)+C \Longrightarrow C = -8 \end {align*} Using Heaviside's method we were able to find \(A=2\) and \(C=-8\) but we still need to find \(B\) so have to resort to our classic method of setting up a system of equations by equating coefficients from (8.2.10) \begin{align*} {8-(s+2)(4s+10)\over (s+1)(s+2)^2} ={A\over s+1}+{B\over s+2}+{C\over (s+2)^2} \tag{10} \end{align*}
    Add \(\displaystyle {A\over s+1}+{B\over s+2}+{C\over (s+2)^2}\) by finding a common denominator
  3. \begin{align} \label {eq:8.2.11} {8-(s+2)(4s+10)\over (s+1)(s+2)^2} ={A(s+2)^2 + B(s+1)(s+2) + C(s+1) \over (s+1)(s+2)^2} \end{align} Now the numerators must match \begin{align*} 8-(s+2)(4s+10) = A(s+2)^2 + B(s+1)(s+2) + C(s+1) \end{align*} And since we already know \(A=2\) and \(C=-8\) we have the equation \begin{align} \label {eq:8.2.12} 8-(s+2)(4s+10) = 2(s+2)^2 + B(s+1)(s+2) - 8(s+1) \end{align} Since this equation must be true for all values of \(s\) we can select an easy one, \(s=0\) to find \(B\)
    Evaluate \(\displaystyle 8-(s+2)(4s+10) = 2(s+2)^2 + B(s+1)(s+2) - 8(s+1)\) at \(s=0\)
    \begin{align*} 8-(2)(10) = 2(2)^2 + B(1)(2) - 8(1) \end{align*}
    Solve \(8-(2)(10) = 2(2)^2 + B(1)(2) - 8(1)\) for \(B\)
    Solving for \(B\) we obtain \(B=-6\) and substituting this and \(A=2\) and \(C=-8\) back in to (8.2.10) we find \begin {equation*} {8-(s+2)(4s+10)\over (s+1)(s+2)^2} ={2\over s+1}-{6\over s+2}-{8\over (s+2)^2} \end {equation*} Now we can and apply linearity to find the inverse Laplace transform \begin {align*} & {\cal L}^{-1}\left[ {8-(s+2)(4s+10)\over (s+1)(s+2)^2} \right] \\ \\ = & 2 \, {\cal L}^{-1} \underset{e^{-t}}{\underbrace{\left[ {1\over s+1} \right] }} -6 \, \underset{e^{-2t}}{\underbrace{ {\cal L}^{-1}\left[ {1\over s+2} \right] }} -8 \, \underset{te^{-2t}}{\underbrace{ {\cal L}^{-1}\left[ {1\over (s+2)^2} \right] }} \\ = & \quad 2e^{-t} \quad -\quad 6 \,e^{-2t} \quad-\quad 8te^{-2t} \end {align*}


Find the inverse Laplace transform \[ {\cal L}^{-1}\left[ {s^2-5s+7\over (s+2)^3} \right] \]
Solution
  1. First split it up using PFD with repeated factors \begin {equation*} {s^2-5s+7\over (s+2)^3} ={A\over s+2}+{B\over (s+2)^2}+{C\over (s+2)^3} \end {equation*} In Example 8.2.6 we found that Heaviside's method would not work for finding the lower powers of the repeated factor, so we can only use it to find \(C\) \begin {align*} C = & \, \Big. s^2-5s+7 \Big|_{s=-2} \\ = & \, (-2)^2-5(-2)+7 \\ = & \, 4+10+7= 21 \\ \end {align*} Now we need to find \(A\) and \(B.\) In 8.2.6 it was easier because Heaviside's method got us two of our three coefficients so we were able to find the last one with a shortcut by substituting \(s=0\) but in this case we would need to substitute 2 different values of \(s\) to get 2 more equations to solve for the other two coefficients. Feel free to do it that way or you can always go ahead and go back to our regular techniques of finding a common denominator and equating the coefficients with a system of equations to find \(A\) and \(B\)
    Add \(\displaystyle {A\over s+2}+{B\over (s+2)^2}+{C\over (s+2)^3}\) by finding a common denominator
    \begin {equation*} {s^2-5s+7\over (s+2)^3} ={A(s+2)^2+B(s+2)+C\over(s+2)^3} \end {equation*} Now equating the numerators we obtain \begin {equation*} s^2-5s+7 = A(s+2)^2+B(s+2)+C \end {equation*}
    Distribute \(\displaystyle A(s+2)^2+B(s+2)+C\) and group like terms
  2. Distributing \begin {equation*} s^2-5s+7 = s^2(A) + s(4A+B) + (4A+2B+C) \end {equation*} Equating the coefficients we obtain the system of equations \begin{align*} \left\{ \begin{array}{rrr} A&=&1 \\ 4A+B&=&-5 \\ 4A+2B+C&=&7 \end{array} \right. \end{align*}
    Solve the system \(\left\{ \begin{array}{rrr} A&=&1 \\ 4A+B&=&-5 \\ 4A+2B+C&=&7 \end{array} \right. \)
    From the system we directly have \(A=1\) and the second equation quickly yields \(B=-9\) and we don't even need to use the third equation since we already have \(C=21\) but you could always solve it to check our answer. Now substituting these back into our expression we find \begin {equation*} {s^2-5s+7\over (s+2)^3} ={1\over s+2}-{9\over (s+2)^2}+{21\over (s+2)^3} \end {equation*} Now we can and apply linearity to find the inverse Laplace transform \begin {align*} & {\cal L}^{-1}\left[ {s^2-5s+7\over (s+2)^3} \right] \\ \\ = & \, {\cal L}^{-1} \underset{e^{-2t}}{\underbrace{\left[ {1\over s+2} \right] }} -9 \, \underset{te^{-2t}}{\underbrace{ {\cal L}^{-1}\left[ {1\over (s+2)^2} \right] }} +21 \, \underset{?}{\underbrace{ {\cal L}^{-1}\left[ {1\over (s+2)^3} \right] }} \\ = & \quad e^{-2t} \quad -\quad 9 \,te^{-2t} \quad+\quad 21 \, {\cal L}^{-1}\left[ {1\over (s+2)^3} \right] \end {align*}
  3. So all we have left to find is \begin {align*} {\cal L}^{-1}\left[ {1\over (s+2)^3} \right] \end {align*} So we seek the function \(f(t)\) such that \begin {align*} {\cal L} \Big\{ f(t) \Big\} = {1\over (s+2)^3} \end {align*} From the table we find the transform pair \[\displaystyle {\cal L} \big\{ t^n e^{at} \big\} = \frac{n!}{(s-a)^{n+1}} \] And setting \(a=-2\) and \(n=2\) in the transform pair we obtain our equation \[ \displaystyle {\cal L} \left\{ t^2 e^{-2t} \right\} = \frac{2}{(s+2)^2} \] Which is perfect once we divide by two and apply linearity \[ \displaystyle {\cal L} \left\{ \frac{1}{2} t^2 e^{-2t} \right\} = \frac{1}{(s+2)^2} \] Therefore we have \[\displaystyle {\cal L}^{-1}\left[ \frac{1}{(s+2)^2} \right] = \frac{1}{2} t^2 e^{-2t} \] And now we substitute it back in to get our final answer \begin {align*} & {\cal L}^{-1}\left[ {s^2-5s+7\over (s+2)^3} \right] \\ \\ = & \, e^{-2t} - 9 \,te^{-2t} + 21 \, \underset{\frac{1}{2} t^2 e^{-2t} }{\underbrace{{\cal L}^{-1}\left[ {1\over (s+2)^3} \right]}} \\ = & \, e^{-2t} - 9 \,te^{-2t} + \frac{21}{2} t^2 e^{-2t} \\ = & \, \left(1 - 9 \,t + \frac{21}{2} t^2 \right)e^{-2t} \\ \end {align*}
Quadratic Partial Fractions
When using PFD to split up rational function with linear factors \[ F(s)={P(s)\over Q(s)}={P(s)\over (s-s_1)(s-s_2)\cdots (s-s_n)} \] We just split it up into separate terms based on each factor with possible numeric coefficients on top. However, this is insufficient when considering irreducible quadratic factors, since the numerator could be a number and a linear term in \(s\). Thus if the denominator contains irreducible quadratic factors we will update our guess to seek linear terms as well
Find the inverse Laplace transform \begin {equation} \label {eq:8.2.13} {\cal L}^{-1}\left[ {1-s(3s+5)\over s\left(s^2+2s+2\right)} \right] \end {equation}
Solution
  1. First split up (8.2.13) using PFD \begin {equation} \label {eq:8.2.14} {1-s(3s+5)\over s\left(s^2+2s+2\right)} = {A\over s} + {Bs+C\over s^2+2s+2} \end {equation} The linear term is \({A}/{s}\) just as linear terms always have been. However, things are a little different this time since \(\left(s^2+2s+2\right)\) is an irreducible quadratic factor so our guess has to account for a linear term in \(s\) as well as a number. Although Heaviside's method won't work here, we can solve using the familiar method of getting a common denominator
    Add \(\displaystyle {A\over s} + {Bs+C\over s^2+2s+2}\) by finding a common denominator
    \begin {align*} {1-s(3s+5)\over s\left(s^2+2s+2\right)} = & {A(s^2+2s+2)+s(Bs+C)\over s(s^2+2s+2)} \\ \end {align*} Then we can equate the numerators \begin {align} \label {eq:8.2.15} {1-s(3s+5)} = & {A(s^2+2s+2)+s(Bs+C)} \\ \end {align}
    Simplify \(\displaystyle {A(s^2+2s+2)+s(Bs+C)} \) and group like terms
    Simplify \(\displaystyle {1-s(3s+5)} \) \begin {align*} {1-5s-3s^2} = & s^2(A+B) + s(2A+C) + (2A) \\ \end {align*} To obtain the system of equations \begin{align*} \left\{ \begin{array}{rrr} A + B&=& -3 \\ 2A+C &=& -5 \\ 2A&=&1 \end{array} \right. \end{align*}
    Solve \(\left\{ \begin{array}{rrr} A + B&=& -3 \\ 2A+C &=& -5 \\ 2A&=&1 \end{array} \right. \)
  2. Solving the system we obtain \(A=1/2,\) \(B=-7/2,\) and \(C=-6,\) so we substitute it back in to solve \begin {align*} {\cal L}^{-1}\left[ {1-5s-3s^2\over s\left(s^2+2s+2\right)} \right] = & \, {\cal L}^{-1}\left[ {A\over s} + {Bs\over s^2+2s+2} + {C\over s^2+2s+2} \right] \\ = & \, \frac{1}{2} \, {\cal L}^{-1}\left[ {1\over s} \right] - \frac{7}{2} \, {\cal L}^{-1}\left[ {s\over s^2+2s+2} \right] - 6 \, {\cal L}^{-1}\left[ {1\over s^2+2s+2} \right] \\ \end {align*} We can find the inverse Laplace of the first one simple enough but the other one's need to be written in the correct format by completing the square in the denominator. \begin {align*} s^2+2s+2 = & (s^2+2s+1)-1+2 \\ = & (s+1)^2+1 \\ \end {align*} So we can rewrite them as \begin {align} \label {eq:8.2.16} \frac{1}{2} \, {\cal L}^{-1}\left[ {1\over s} \right] - \frac{7}{2} \, {\cal L}^{-1}\left[ {s\over (s+1)^2+1} \right] - 6 \, {\cal L}^{-1}\left[ {1\over (s+1)^2+1} \right] \\ \end {align} Now the last one is also directly on our table but the middle one needs a little work to get the numerator to be \(s+1\) we add and subtract one and simplify \begin {align*} & \, \frac{1}{2} {\cal L}^{-1}\left[ {1\over s} \right] - \frac{7}{2} {\cal L}^{-1}\left[ {(s+1)-1\over (s+1)^2+1} \right] - 6 {\cal L}^{-1}\left[ {1\over (s+1)^2+1} \right] \\ = & \, \frac{1}{2} \underset{1}{\underbrace{{\cal L}^{-1}\left[ {1\over s} \right]}} - \frac{7}{2} \underset{e^{-t}\cos t}{\underbrace{ {\cal L}^{-1}\left[ {s+1\over (s+1)^2+1} \right]}} + \underset{e^{-t}\sin t}{\underbrace{ \frac{7}{2} {\cal L}^{-1}\left[ {1\over (s+1)^2+1} \right]}} - 6 \underset{e^{-t}\sin t}{\underbrace{ {\cal L}^{-1}\left[ {1\over (s+1)^2+1} \right]}} \\ = & \, \quad \frac{1}{2} \quad - \quad \frac{7}{2} e^{-t}\cos t \quad + \quad \underbrace{ \frac{7}{2} e^{-t}\sin t \quad - \quad 6 e^{-t}\sin t} \\ = & \, \quad \frac{1}{2} \quad - \quad \frac{7}{2} e^{-t}\cos t \quad - \frac{5}{2} e^{-t}\sin t \\ = & \, \quad \frac{1}{2} - \left( \frac{7}{2} \cos t + \frac{5}{2} \sin t \right) e^{-t}\\ \end {align*}
Find the inverse Laplace transform \begin {equation} \label {eq:8.2.17} {\cal L}^{-1}\left[ {8+3s\over (s^2+1)(s^2+4)} \right] \end {equation}
Solution
  1. Noticing we have irreducible quadratic factors, we can proceed to split up (8.2.17) using PFD \begin {equation} {8+3s\over (s^2+1)(s^2+4)} = {A+Bs\over s^2+1}+{C+Ds\over s^2+4} \end {equation} However since there are no first power of \(s\) terms in the denominator, we can actually solve a simpler problem first by taking the numerator as \(1\) and treating \(s^2\) as the variable to evaluate \begin {equation*} {1\over (s^2+1)(s^2+4)} \end {equation*} Thinking of \(s^2\) as the variable, we can treat these as "linear" factors in \(s^2\) and use a linear PFD \begin {equation*} {1\over (s^2+1)(s^2+4)} = {A\over s^2+1} + {B\over s^2+4} \end {equation*} Now apply Heaviside’s method to find \(A\) and \(B\) \begin {equation*} A = \left. {1\over s^2+4} \right|_{s^2=-1} = {1\over -1+4} = {1\over 3} \end {equation*}
    \begin {equation*} B = \left. {1\over s^2+1} \right|_{s^2=-4} = {1\over -4+1} = -{1\over 3} \end {equation*}
  2. Substituting back in we obtain \begin {align*} {1\over (s^2+1)(s^2+4)} = & \, {1\over 3} \left( {1\over s^2+1} \right) - {1\over 3} \left( {1\over s^2+4} \right) \\ = & \, {1\over 3} \left( {1\over s^2+1} - {1\over s^2+4} \right) \end {align*} Now multiplying both sides by \(8+3s\) we get back to our original expression \begin {align*} {8+3s\over (s^2+1)(s^2+4)} = & \, {1\over 3} \left( {8+3s\over s^2+1} - {8+3s\over s^2+4} \right) \\ = & \, {1\over 3} \left( {8\over s^2+1} + {3s\over s^2+1} - {8\over s^2+4} - {3s\over s^2+4} \right) \\ = & \, {8\over 3} \left( {1\over s^2+1} \right) + \left( {s\over s^2+1} \right) - {8\over 3} \left( {1\over s^2+4} \right) - \left( {s\over s^2+4} \right) \\ \end {align*} And applying linearity, we can find the inverse Laplace transform \begin {align*} & \, {\cal L}^{-1}\left[ {8+3s\over (s^2+1)(s^2+4)} \right] \\ = & \, {8\over 3} \, \underset{\sin t}{\underbrace{ {\cal L}^{-1}\left[ {1\over s^2+1} \right]}} + \underset{\cos t}{\underbrace{ {\cal L}^{-1}\left[ {s\over s^2+1} \right]}} - {8\over 3} \, \underset{\sin 2t}{\underbrace{ {\cal L}^{-1}\left[ {1\over s^2+4} \right]}} - \underset{\cos 2t}{\underbrace{ {\cal L}^{-1}\left[ {s\over s^2+4}\right]}} \\ = & \, \quad {8\over 3} \,\sin t \quad+\quad\cos t \quad-\quad {8\over 3} \, \sin 2t \quad-\quad\cos 2t \end {align*}
Find the inverse Laplace transform of \begin {equation*} {\cal L}^{-1}\left[ {17 s-15\over (s^2-2s+5)(s^2+2s+10)} \right] \end {equation*}
Solution
  1. Noticing we have irreducible quadratic factors, we can proceed to split it up using PFD \begin {equation*} {17 s-15\over (s^2-2s+5)(s^2+2s+10)} = {As+B\over s^2-2s+5} + {Cs+D\over s^2+2s+10} \end {equation*}
    Add \(\displaystyle {As+B\over s^2-2s+5} + {Cs+D\over s^2+2s+10}\) by finding a common denominator
    \begin {equation*} {17 s-15\over (s^2-2s+5)(s^2+2s+10)} = {(As+B)(s^2+2s+10)+(Cs+D)(s^2-2s+5)\over (s^2-2s+5)(s^2+2s+10)} \end {equation*}
  2. Now equating the numerators we obtain \begin {equation*} 17 s-15 = (As+B)(s^2+2s+10)+(Cs+D)(s^2-2s+5) \end {equation*}
    Distribute \(\displaystyle (As+B)(s^2+2s+10)+(Cs+D)(s^2-2s+5)\) and group like terms
    \begin {align*} & \, 17 s-15 \\ = & \, (As+B)(s^2+2s+10)+(Cs+D)(s^2-2s+5) \\ = & \, As(s^2+2s+10)+B(s^2+2s+10)+Cs(s^2-2s+5)+D(s^2-2s+5) \\ = & \, A(s^3+2s^2+10s)+B(s^2+2s+10)+C(s^3-2s^2+5s)+D(s^2-2s+5) \\ = & \, s^3(A + C) + s^2 (2A + B - 2C + D) + s (10A + 2B + 5C - 2D) + (10B + 5D) \\ \end {align*} Equating the coefficients we obtain \begin{align*} \left\{ \begin{array}{rrr} A + C &=& 0 \\ 2A + B - 2C + D &=& 0 \\ 10A + 2B + 5C - 2D &=& 17 \\ 10B + 5D &=& -15 \\ \end{array} \right. \end{align*}
    Solve the system \(\left\{ \begin{array}{rrr} A + C &=& 0 \\ 2A + B - 2C + D &=& 0 \\ 10A + 2B + 5C - 2D &=& 17 \\ 10B + 5D &=& -15 \\ \end{array} \right. \)
  3. Solving the system we obtain \(A=1,\) \(B=1,\) \(C=-1,\) and \(D=-5,\) and substituting it back in we obtain \begin {equation*} {17 s-15\over (s^2-2s+5)(s^2+2s+10)} = {s+1\over s^2-2s+5} - {s+5\over s^2+2s+10} \end {equation*} And we need to write the denominators using completing the square \begin{array}{cccc} s^2-2s+5 &\quad\quad& s^2+2s+10 \\ (s^2-2s+1)-1+5 &\quad\quad& (s^2+2s+1)-1+10 \\ (s-1)^2+4 &\quad\quad& (s+1)^2+9 \\ \end{array} Rewriting we obtain \begin {equation*} {17 s-15\over (s^2-2s+5)(s^2+2s+10)} = {s+1\over (s-1)^2+4} - {s+5\over (s+1)^2+9} \end {equation*} And to get them in the form to match the table we just add and subtract numbers from the numerators so they match the shift on the bottom \begin {align*} & \, {s+1\over (s-1)^2+4} - {s+5\over (s+1)^2+9} \\ = & \, {(s-1)+1+1\over (s-1)^2+4} - {(s+1)-1+5\over (s+1)^2+9} \\ = & \, {(s-1)+2\over (s-1)^2+4} - {(s+1)+4\over (s+1)^2+9} \\ = & \, {s-1\over (s-1)^2+4} + {2\over (s-1)^2+4} - {s+1\over (s+1)^2+9} - {4\over (s+1)^2+9} \\ \end {align*}
  4. And finally we can find the inverse Laplace transforms \begin {align*} & \, {\cal L}^{-1}\left[ {17 s-15\over (s^2-2s+5)(s^2+2s+10)} \right] \\ \\ = & \, {\cal L}^{-1}\left[ {s-1\over (s-1)^2+4} \right] + 2 \, {\cal L}^{-1}\left[ {1\over (s-1)^2+4} \right] - {\cal L}^{-1}\left[ {s+1\over (s+1)^2+9} \right] - 4\, {\cal L}^{-1}\left[ {1\over (s+1)^2+9} \right] \\ = & \, \underset{e^{t}\cos 2t}{\underbrace{{\cal L}^{-1}\left[ {s-1\over (s-1)^2+4} \right]}} + \underset{e^{t}\sin 2t}{\underbrace{ {\cal L}^{-1}\left[ {2\over (s-1)^2+4} \right]}} - \underset{e^{-t}\cos 3t}{\underbrace{ {\cal L}^{-1}\left[ {s+1\over (s+1)^2+9} \right] }} - \frac{4}{3}\, \underset{e^{-t}\sin 3t}{\underbrace{ {\cal L}^{-1}\left[ {3\over (s+1)^2+9} \right] }} \\ = & \, \quad e^{t}\cos 2t \quad + \quad \, e^{t}\sin 2t \quad - \quad e^{-t}\cos 3t \quad - \quad \frac{4}{3}\, e^{-t}\sin 3t \\ = & \, \Big(\cos 2t + \sin 2t\Big) e^{t} - \Big(\cos 3t - \frac{4}{3}\, \sin 3t \Big) e^{-t} \\ \end {align*}
