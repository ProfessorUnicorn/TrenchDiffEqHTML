8.2 The Inverse Laplace Transform
Inverse Laplace Transform
In Section 8.1 we defined the Laplace transform of \(f(t)\) by \[ F(s)= {\cal L} \big\{ f(t) \big\} =\int _0^\infty \, e^{-st} \, f(t) \, dt\] We’ll also say that \(f(t)\) is an inverse Laplace Transform of \(F(s)\), and write \[ f(t)={\cal L}^{-1} \big[ F(s) \big] \] To solve differential equations with the Laplace transform, we must be able to obtain \(f(t)\) from its transform \(F(s)\). There’s a formula for doing this, but we can’t use it because it requires the theory of functions of a complex variable which is beyond the scope of this course. Instead, we will use the table of Laplace transforms (Section 8.8) to find the inverse Laplace transforms that we’ll need.


 Use the table of Laplace transforms (Section 8.8) to find the inverse Laplace Transforms
  1. \[ {\cal L}^{-1}\left[ {1\over s^2-1}\right] \]
  2. \[ {\cal L}^{-1}\left[ {s\over s^2+9}\right]\]
Solution
  1. To find \( \displaystyle {\cal L}^{-1}\left[ {1\over s^2-1}\right] \) we seek the function \(f(t)\) such that \[ \displaystyle {\cal L} \big\{ f(t) \big\} = {1\over s^2-1}\] From the table we find the transform pair \[\displaystyle {\cal L} \big\{ \sinh bt \big\} = \frac{b}{s^2-b^2} \] And setting \(b=1\) in the transform pair we obtain our exact equation \[\displaystyle {\cal L} \big\{ \sinh t \big\} = \frac{1}{s^2-1} \] Thus we have \[\displaystyle {\cal L}^{-1}\left[ {1\over s^2-1}\right] = \sinh t \]
  2. To find \( \displaystyle {\cal L}^{-1}\left[ {s\over s^2+9}\right] \) we seek the function \(f(t)\) such that \[ \displaystyle {\cal L} \big\{ f(t) \big\} = {s\over s^2+9}\] From the table we find the transform pair \[\displaystyle {\cal L} \big\{ \cos \omega t \big\} = \frac{s}{s^2+\omega^2} \] And setting \(\omega=3\) in the transform pair we obtain our exact equation \[\displaystyle {\cal L} \big\{ \cos 3 t \big\} = \frac{s}{s^2+9} \] Thus we have \[\displaystyle {\cal L}^{-1}\left[ {s\over s^2+9}\right] = \cos 3t \]
Theorem 8.2.1 Linearity Property
Suppose \( F(s) \) and \( G(s) \) Laplace transforms are defined. Then the following properties are true: \[ {\cal L}^{-1} \big[ F(s) + G(s) \big] ={\cal L}^{-1} \big\{ F(s) \big\} + {\cal L}^{-1} \big\{ G(s) \big\} \] \[ {\cal L}^{-1} \big[ c \cdot F(s) \big] = c \cdot {\cal L}^{-1} \big[ f(x) \big] \] Essentially the inverse Laplace transform can break up across terms and constants can be factored out.


 Use the table of Laplace transforms (Section 8.8) to find the inverse Laplace Transform
\[ {\cal L}^{-1}\left[ {8\over s+5}+{7\over s^2+3}\right ]\]
Solution
To find \( \displaystyle {\cal L}^{-1}\left[ {8\over s+5}+{7\over s^2+3}\right ] \) we first apply the Linearity Property \begin{align*} {\cal L}^{-1}\left[ {8\over s+5}+{7\over s^2+3}\right ] = & \, 8 \, {\cal L}^{-1}\left[ {1\over s+5}\right ] + 7 \, {\cal L}^{-1}\left[ {1\over s^2+3}\right ] \end{align*} Now we find the inverse Laplace transforms the same way we did in Example 8.2.1


First to find \( \displaystyle {\cal L}^{-1}\left[ {1\over s+5}\right] \) we seek the function \(f(t)\) such that \[ \displaystyle {\cal L} \big\{ f(t) \big\} = {1\over s+5}\] This one is a little trickier than the previous ones. From the table we find the transform pair \[\displaystyle {\cal L} \big\{ e^{at} \big\} = \frac{1}{s-a} \] And setting \(a=-5\) we obtain our exact equation \begin{align*} {\cal L} \big\{ e^{-5t} \big\} = \frac{1}{s+5} \end{align*} Thus we have \[\displaystyle {\cal L}^{-1}\left[ {1\over s+5}\right] = e^{-5t} \]


Now to find \( \displaystyle {\cal L}^{-1}\left[ {1\over s^2+3}\right] \) we seek the function \(f(t)\) such that \[ \displaystyle {\cal L} \big\{ f(t) \big\} = {1\over s^2+3}\] From the table we find the transform pair \[\displaystyle {\cal L} \big\{ \sin \omega \big\} = \frac{\omega}{s^2+\omega^2} \] And setting \(\omega=\sqrt{3}\) in the transform pair gets us close to our equation but not exact: \begin{align*} {\cal L} \big\{ \sin \sqrt{3} \big\} = & \frac{\sqrt{3}}{s^2+3} \end{align*} However, it is just a constant multiplier on the right side, so we simply divide both sides by \(\sqrt{3}\) and use the Linearity Property to obtain our exact equation \begin{align*} \frac{1}{\sqrt{3}} {\cal L} \big\{ \sin \sqrt{3} \big\} = & \frac{1}{s^2+3} \Longrightarrow {\cal L} \left\{ \frac{1}{\sqrt{3}} \sin \sqrt{3} \, \right\} = \frac{1}{s^2+3} \\ \end{align*} Thus we have \[\displaystyle {\cal L}^{-1}\left[ {s\over s^2+3}\right] = \frac{1}{\sqrt{3}} \sin \sqrt{3} \]


Finally substituting the inverse Laplace transforms back in we obtain our answer \begin{align*} {\cal L}^{-1}\left[ {8\over s+5}+{7\over s^2+3}\right ] = & \, 8 \, \underset{e^{-5t}}{\underbrace{{\cal L}^{-1}\left[ {1\over s+5}\right ]}} + 7 \, \underset{\frac{1}{\sqrt{3}} \sin \sqrt{3}}{\underbrace{{\cal L}^{-1}\left[ {1\over s^2+3}\right ]}} \\ = & \, \quad 8 \, e^{-5t} \quad+\quad \, \frac{7}{\sqrt{3}} \sin \sqrt{3} \end{align*}



 Use the table of Laplace transforms (Section 8.8) to find the inverse Laplace Transform
\[ {\cal L}^{-1}\left [{3s+8\over s^2+2s+5}\right ]\]
Solution
To find \( \displaystyle {\cal L}^{-1}\left[ {3s+8\over s^2+2s+5} \right] \) we seek the function \(f(t)\) such that \[ \displaystyle {\cal L} \big\{ f(t) \big\} = {3s+8\over s^2+2s+5}\] From the table there are no transform pairs that match the equation in the format it is in. So far in Examples 8.2.1 and 8.2.2 we have been able to recognize transform pairs directly from the table. Sometimes however, such as in this case, it will be necessary to perform algebraic manipulations first.
Since the denominator is a quadratic polynomial \(s^2+2s+5\) we'll try to complete the square on it first. \begin{align*} s^2+2s+5 = & (s^2+2s+ 1) - 1+5 \\ = & (s+ 1)(s+ 1) + 4 \\ = & (s+ 1)^2 +4 \\ \end{align*} So we can rewrite our expression as \begin{align*} {3s+8\over s^2+2s+5} = {3s+8\over (s+ 1)^2 +4} \end{align*}


So now to find \( \displaystyle {\cal L}^{-1}\left[ {3s+8\over (s+ 1)^2 +4} \right] \) we seek the function \(f(t)\) such that \[ \displaystyle {\cal L} \big\{ f(t) \big\} = {3s+8\over (s+ 1)^2 +4}\] While it still is not a perfect match it is certainly closer as we identify the following transform pair: \[\displaystyle {\cal L} \big\{ e^{at} \cos \omega t \big\} = \frac{s-a}{(s-a)^2+\omega^2} \] Substituting \(a=-1\) and \(\omega=2\) we obtain \[\displaystyle {\cal L} \big\{ e^{-t} \cos 2 t \big\} = \frac{s+1}{(s+1)^2+4} \] So we are getting closer but we will still need to further manipulate our expression to get it to match. Since the numerator in one of the Laplace transforms is \(s+1\) we'll go ahead and try to write our expression in terms of \(s+1\) in the numerator by splitting up the \(8\) and then factoring out the \(3\) and then splitting it into two separate terms \begin{align*} {3s+8\over (s+ 1)^2 +4} = & {(3s+3)+5\over (s+ 1)^2 +4} = {3(s+1)+5\over (s+ 1)^2 +4} \\ = & {3(s+1)\over (s+ 1)^2 +4} + {5\over (s+ 1)^2 +4} \\ \end{align*}


So now to find \( \displaystyle {\cal L}^{-1}\left[ {3(s+1)\over (s+ 1)^2 +4} + {5\over (s+ 1)^2 +4} \right] \) we can apply the Linearity Property to obtain \begin{align*} & \, 3 \, {\cal L}^{-1}\left[ {s+1\over (s+ 1)^2 +4} \right ] + 5 \, {\cal L}^{-1}\left[ {1\over (s+ 1)^2 +4}\right ] \end{align*} and in fact we have already found the first transform \begin{align*} {\cal L}^{-1}\left[ {s+1\over (s+ 1)^2 +4} \right ] = e^{-t} \cos 2 t \end{align*} Now what is left is the second term so seek the function \(f(t)\) such that \[ \displaystyle {\cal L} \big\{ f(t) \big\} = {1\over (s+ 1)^2 +4}\] From the table we identify the following transform pair: \[\displaystyle {\cal L} \big\{ e^{at} \sin \omega t \big\} = \frac{\omega}{(s-a)^2+\omega^2} \] Substituting \(a=-1\) and \(\omega=2\) we obtain \[\displaystyle {\cal L} \big\{ e^{-t} \sin 2 t \big\} = \frac{2}{(s+1)^2+4} \] Which is not our exact equation but pretty close as it is just a constant multiplier just as in Example 8.2.2 so we simply divide both sides by \(\sqrt{2}\) and use the Linearity Property to obtain our exact equation \begin{align*} \frac{1}{2} {\cal L} \big\{ e^{-t} \sin 2 t \big\} = \frac{1}{(s+1)^2+4} \Longrightarrow {\cal L} \left\{ \, \frac{1}{2} e^{-t} \sin 2 t \right\} = \frac{1}{(s+1)^2+4} \\ \end{align*} Thus we have \begin{align*} {\cal L}^{-1}\left[ {1\over (s+ 1)^2 +4} \right ] = \frac{1}{2} e^{-t} \sin 2 t \end{align*}


Finally substituting the inverse Laplace transforms back in we obtain our answer \begin{align*} {\cal L}^{-1}\left[ {3s+8\over s^2+2s+5} \right] = & \, {\cal L}^{-1}\left[ {3s + 8 \over (s+ 1)^2 +4} \right] \\ = & \, {\cal L}^{-1}\left[ {3(s+1)\over (s+ 1)^2 +4} + {5\over (s+ 1)^2 +4} \right] \\ = & \, 3 \, \underset{e^{-t} \cos 2 t}{\underbrace{ {\cal L}^{-1}\left[ {s+1\over (s+ 1)^2 +4} \right ]}} + 5 \, \underset{\frac{1}{2} e^{-t} \sin 2 t}{\underbrace{ {\cal L}^{-1}\left[ {1\over (s+ 1)^2 +4}\right ] }} \\ = & \quad \, 3 \, e^{-t} \cos 2 t \quad+\quad \, \frac{5}{2} e^{-t} \sin 2 t \\ = & \, \left( 3 \, \cos 2 t \, + \, \frac{5}{2} \sin 2 t \right) e^{-t} \\ \end{align*}
Rational Functions
Using the Laplace transform to solve differential equations often requires finding the inverse transform of a rational function \[ F(s)={P(s)\over Q(s)}\] where \(P\) and \(Q\) are polynomials in \(s\) with no common factors. Since it can be shown that \(\lim _{s\to \infty }F(s)=0\) if \(F\) is a Laplace transform, we need only consider the case where \(\mbox {degree}(P)<\mbox {degree}(Q)\). To obtain \({\cal L}^{-1}(F)\), we find the partial fraction expansion of \(F\), obtain inverse transforms of the individual terms in the expansion from the table of Laplace transforms, and use the linearity property of the inverse transform. The next two examples illustrate this.


Find the inverse Laplace transform of \begin {equation} \label {eq:8.2.1} F(s)={3s+2\over s^2-3s+2} \end {equation}
Solution
  1. Factoring the denominator in (8.2.1) yields \begin {equation} \label {eq:8.2.2} F(s)={3s+2\over (s-1)(s-2)} \end {equation} The form for the partial fraction expansion is \begin {equation} \label {eq:8.2.3} {3s+2\over (s-1)(s-2)}={A\over s-1}+{B\over s-2} \end {equation} Multiplying this by \((s-1)(s-2)\) yields \[ 3s+2=(s-2)A+(s-1)B\] Setting \(s=2\) yields \(B=8\) and setting \(s=1\) yields \(A=-5\). Therefore \[ F(s)=-{5\over s-1}+{8\over s-2} \] and \[ {\cal L}^{-1}(F)=-5{\cal L}^{-1}\left ({1\over s-1}\right ) +8{\cal L}^{-1}\left ({1\over s-2}\right )=-5e^t+8e^{2t}\]
  2. We don’t really have to multiply (8.2.3) by \((s-1)(s-2)\) to compute \(A\) and \(B\). We can obtain \(A\) by simply ignoring the factor \(s-1\) in the denominator of (8.2.2) and setting \(s=1\) elsewhere; thus, \begin {equation} \label {eq:8.2.4} A=\left .{3s+2\over s-2}\right |_{s=1}={3\cdot 1+2\over 1-2}=-5 \end {equation} Similarly, we can obtain \(B\) by ignoring the factor \(s-2\) in the denominator of (8.2.2) and setting \(s=2\) elsewhere; thus, \begin {equation} \label {eq:8.2.5} B=\left .{3s+2\over s-1}\right |_{s=2}={3\cdot 2+2\over 2-1}=8 \end {equation} To justify this, we observe that multiplying (8.2.3) by \(s-1\) yields \[ {3s+2\over s-2}=A+(s-1){B\over s-2}\] and setting \(s=1\) leads to (8.2.4). Similarly, multiplying (8.2.3) by \(s-2\) yields \[ {3s+2\over s-1}=(s-2){A\over s-2}+B \] and setting \(s=2\) leads to (8.2.5). (It isn’t necessary to write the last two equations. We wrote them only to justify the shortcut procedure indicated in (8.2.4) and (8.2.5).)
The shortcut employed in the second solution of Example 8.2.4 is Heaviside’s method. The next theorem states this method formally. For a proof and an extension of this theorem, see Exercise 10.
Theorem 8.2.2 Suppose \begin {equation} \label {eq:8.2.6} F(s)={P(s)\over (s-s_1)(s-s_2)\cdots (s-s_n)} \end {equation} where \(s_1\), \(s_2,\) …\(,\) \(s_n\) are distinct and \(P\) is a polynomial of degree less than \(n.\) Then \[ F(s)={A_1\over s-s_1}+{A_2\over s-s_2}+\cdots +{A_n\over s-s_n},\] where \(A_i\) can be computed from (8.2.6) by ignoring the factor \(s-s_i\) and setting \(s=s_i\) elsewhere.


Find the inverse Laplace transform of \begin {equation} \label {eq:8.2.7} F(s)={6+(s+1)(s^2-5s+11)\over s(s-1)(s-2)(s+1)} \end {equation}
Solution The partial fraction expansion of (8.2.7) is of the form \begin {equation} \label {eq:8.2.8} F(s)={A\over s}+{B\over s-1}+{C\over s-2}+{D\over s+1} \end {equation} To find \(A\), we ignore the factor \(s\) in the denominator of (8.2.7) and set \(s=0\) elsewhere. This yields \[ A={6+(1)(11)\over (-1)(-2)(1)}={17\over 2}\] Similarly, the other coefficients are given by \[ B={6+(2)(7)\over (1)(-1)(2)}=-10\] \[ C={6+3(5)\over 2(1)(3)}={7\over 2}\] and \[ D={6\over (-1)(-2)(-3)}=-1\] Therefore \[ F(s)={17\over 2}\,{1\over s}-{10\over s-1}+{7\over 2}\,{1\over s-2}-{1\over s+1} \] and
\begin {eqnarray*} {\cal L}^{-1}(F)&=& {17\over 2}{\cal L}^{-1}\left (1\over s\right )-10{\cal L}^{-1}\left (1 \over s-1\right )+{7\over 2}{\cal L}^{-1}\left (1\over s-2\right )-{\cal L}^{-1}\left (1\over s+1\right )\\ &=& {17\over 2}-10e^t+{7\over 2}e^{2t}-e^{-t} \end {eqnarray*}
Remark: We didn’t “multiply out” the numerator in (8.2.7) before computing the coefficients in (8.2.8), since it wouldn’t simplify the computations.


Find the inverse Laplace transform of \begin {equation} \label {eq:8.2.9} F(s)={8-(s+2)(4s+10)\over (s+1)(s+2)^2} \end {equation}
Solution The form for the partial fraction expansion is \begin {equation} \label {eq:8.2.10} F(s)={A\over s+1}+{B\over s+2}+{C\over (s+2)^2} \end {equation} Because of the repeated factor \((s+2)^2\) in (8.2.9), Heaviside’s method doesn’t work. Instead, we find a common denominator in (8.2.10). This yields \begin {equation} \label {eq:8.2.11} F(s)={A(s+2)^2+B(s+1)(s+2)+C(s+1)\over (s+1)(s+2)^2} \end {equation} If (8.2.9) and (8.2.11) are to be equivalent, then \begin {equation} \label {eq:8.2.12} A(s+2)^2+B(s+1)(s+2)+C(s+1)=8-(s+2)(4s+10) \end {equation} The two sides of this equation are polynomials of degree two. From a theorem of algebra, they will be equal for all \(s\) if they are equal for any three distinct values of \(s\). We may determine \(A\), \(B\) and \(C\) by choosing convenient values of \(s\).
The left side of (8.2.12) suggests that we take \(s=-2\) to obtain \(C=-8\), and \(s=-1\) to obtain \(A=2\). We can now choose any third value of \(s\) to determine \(B\). Taking \(s=0\) yields \(4A+2B+C=-12\). Since \(A=2\) and \(C=-8\) this implies that \(B=-6\). Therefore \[ F(s)={2\over s+1}-{6\over s+2}-{8\over (s+2)^2} \] and
\begin {eqnarray*} {\cal L}^{-1}(F)&=& 2{\cal L}^{-1}\left (1\over s+1\right )-6{\cal L}^{-1}\left (1\over s+2\right )-8{\cal L}^{-1}\left (1\over (s+2)^2\right )\\ &=&2e^{-t}-6e^{-2t}-8te^{-2t} \end {eqnarray*}


Find the inverse Laplace transform of \[ F(s)={s^2-5s+7\over (s+2)^3}\]
Solution The form for the partial fraction expansion is \[ F(s)={A\over s+2}+{B\over (s+2)^2}+{C\over (s+2)^3}\] The easiest way to obtain \(A\), \(B\), and \(C\) is to expand the numerator in powers of \(s+2\). This yields \[ s^2-5s+7=[(s+2)-2]^2-5[(s+2)-2]+7=(s+2)^2-9(s+2)+21\] Therefore
\begin {eqnarray*} F(s)&=&{(s+2)^2-9(s+2)+21\over (s+2)^3}\\[10pt] &=&{1\over s+2}-{9\over (s+2)^2}+{21\over (s+2)^3} \end {eqnarray*}
and
\begin {eqnarray*} {\cal L}^{-1}(F)&=& {\cal L}^{-1}\left ({1\over s+2}\right )-9{\cal L}^{-1}\left ({1\over (s+2)^2}\right )+{21\over 2}{\cal L}^{-1}\left ({2\over (s+2)^3}\right )\\[10pt]&=& e^{-2t}\left (1-9t+{21\over 2}t^2\right ) \end {eqnarray*}


Find the inverse Laplace transform of \begin {equation} \label {eq:8.2.13} F(s)={1-s(5+3s)\over s\left [(s+1)^2+1\right ]} \end {equation}
Solution One form for the partial fraction expansion of \(F\) is \begin {equation} \label {eq:8.2.14} F(s)={A\over s}+{Bs+C\over (s+1)^2+1} \end {equation} However, we see from the table of Laplace transforms that the inverse transform of the second fraction on the right of (8.2.14) will be a linear combination of the inverse transforms \[ e^{-t}\cos t\quad \mbox { and }\quad e^{-t}\sin t \] of \[ {s+1\over (s+1)^2+1}\quad \mbox { and }\quad {1\over (s+1)^2+1} \] respectively. Therefore, instead of (8.2.14) we write \begin {equation} \label {eq:8.2.15} F(s)={A\over s}+{B(s+1)+C\over (s+1)^2+1} \end {equation} Finding a common denominator yields \begin {equation} \label {eq:8.2.16} F(s)={A\left [(s+1)^2+1\right ]+B(s+1)s+Cs\over s\left [(s+1)^2+1\right ]} \end {equation} If (8.2.13) and (8.2.16) are to be equivalent, then \[ A\left [(s+1)^2+1\right ]+B(s+1)s+Cs=1-s(5+3s)\] This is true for all \(s\) if it’s true for three distinct values of \(s\). Choosing \(s=0\), \(-1\), and \(1\) yields the system \[ \begin {array}{rcr} 2A&=&1\phantom {.}\\ A-C&=&3\phantom {.}\\5A+2B+C&=&-7 \end {array} \] Solving this system yields \[ A={1\over 2},\quad B=-{7\over 2},\quad C=-{5\over 2}\] Hence, from (8.2.15), \[ F(s)={1\over 2s}-{7\over 2}\,{s+1\over (s+1)^2+1}- {5\over 2}\,{1\over (s+1)^2+1}\] Therefore
\begin {eqnarray*} {\cal L}^{-1}(F)&=& {1\over 2}{\cal L}^{-1}\left (1\over s\right )-{7\over 2}{\cal L}^{-1}\left (s+1 \over (s+1)^2+1\right )-{5\over 2} {\cal L}^{-1}\left (1\over (s+1)^2+1\right )\\ &=& {1\over 2}-{7\over 2}e^{-t}\cos t-{5\over 2}e^{-t}\sin t \end {eqnarray*}


Find the inverse Laplace transform of \begin {equation} \label {eq:8.2.17} F(s)={8+3s\over (s^2+1)(s^2+4)} \end {equation}
Solution The form for the partial fraction expansion is \[ F(s)={A+Bs\over s^2+1}+{C+Ds\over s^2+4}\] The coefficients \(A\), \(B\), \(C\) and \(D\) can be obtained by finding a common denominator and equating the resulting numerator to the numerator in (8.2.17). However, since there’s no first power of \(s\) in the denominator of (8.2.17), there’s an easier way: the expansion of \[ F_1(s)={1\over (s^2+1)(s^2+4)} \] can be obtained quickly by using Heaviside’s method to expand \[ {1\over (x+1)(x+4)}={1\over 3}\left ({1\over x+1}-{1\over x+4}\right ) \] and then setting \(x=s^2\) to obtain \[ {1\over (s^2+1)(s^2+4)}={1\over 3}\left ({1\over s^2+1}-{1\over s^2+4}\right )\] Multiplying this by \(8+3s\) yields \[ F(s)={8+3s\over (s^2+1)(s^2+4)}={1\over 3}\left ({8+3s\over s^2+1}-{8+3s\over s^2+4}\right )\] Therefore \[ {\cal L}^{-1}(F)={8\over 3}\sin t+\cos t-{8\over 3}\sin 2t-\cos 2t\]
