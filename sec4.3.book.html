4.3 Elementary Mechanics
Newton’s Second Law of Motion
In this section we consider an object with constant mass \(m\) moving along a line under a force \(F\). Let \(y=y(t)\) be the displacement of the object from a reference point on the line at time \(t\), and let \(v=v(t)\) and \(a=a(t)\) be the velocityand acceleration of the object at time \(t\). Thus, \(v=\dot{y}\) and \(a=\dot{v}=\ddot{y}\), where the dot notation represents differentiation with respect to \(t\). Newton's 2nd law of motion asserts that the force \(F\) and the acceleration \(a\) are related by the equation \begin{equation} \label{eq:4.3.1} F=ma \quad \text{or} \quad F = m\dot{v} \quad \text{or} \quad F= m\ddot{y} \end{equation}
Units
In applications there are three main sets of units in use for length, mass, force, and time: the cgs, mks, and British systems. All three use the second as the unit of time. Table 1 shows the other units. Consistent with (4.3.1), the unit of force in each system is defined to be the force required to impart an acceleration of (one unit of length)\(/s^2\) to one unit of mass.

If we assume that Earth is a perfect sphere with constant mass density, Newton’s law of gravitation asserts that the force exerted on an object by Earth’s gravitational field is proportional to the mass of the object and inversely proportional to the square of its distance from the center of Earth. \begin{equation*} F_G \propto m \quad \text{and} \quad F_G \propto \frac{1}{r^2} \end{equation*}
However, if the object remains sufficiently close to Earth’s surface, we may assume that the gravitational force is constant and equal to its value at the surface. The magnitude of this force is \(mg\), where \(g\) is called the acceleration due to gravity. This quantity has been determined experimentally. Approximate values of \(g\) are \[\begin {array}{rl} g &=980\ \mbox {cm/s}^2 \hskip 40pt \mbox {(cgs)} \\ g &=9.8\ \mbox {m/s}^2 \hskip 48pt \mbox {(mks)} \\ g &=32\ \mbox {ft/s}^2 \hskip 52pt \mbox {(British)}\end {array}\]
In general, the force \(F\) in (4.3.1) may depend upon \(t\), \(y\), and \(\dot{y}\). Since \(a=\ddot{y}\), (4.3.1) can be written in the form \begin {equation} \label {eq:4.3.2} m\ddot{y}=F(t,y,\dot{y}) \end {equation} which is a second order equation. We’ll consider this equation with restrictions on \(F\) later; however, since Chapter 2 dealt only with first order equations, we consider here only problems in which (4.3.2) can be recast as a first order equation. This is possible if \(F\) does not depend on \(y\), so (4.3.2) is of the form \[ m\ddot{y}=F(t,\dot{y}) \] Letting \(v=\dot{y}\) and \(\dot{v}=\ddot{y}\) yields a first order equation for \(v\): \begin {equation} \label {eq:4.3.3} m\dot{v}=F(t,v) \end {equation} Solving this equation yields \(v\) as a function of time \(t\). If we know \(y(t_0)\) for some time \(t_0\), we can antidifferentiate \(v\) to obtain \(y\) as a function of \(t\).
Equations of the form (4.3.3) occur in problems involving motion through a resisting medium.
Motion Through a Resisting Medium Under Constant Gravitational Force
Now we consider an object moving vertically in some medium. We assume that the only forces acting on the object are gravity and resistance from the medium. We also assume that the motion takes place close to Earth’s surface and take the upward direction to be positive, so the gravitational force can be assumed to have the constant value \(-mg\). We’ll find that, under reasonable assumptions on the resisting force, the velocity approaches a limit as \(t\to \infty \). We call this limit the terminal velocity.
An object with mass \(m\) moves under constant gravitational force through a medium that exerts a resistance with magnitude proportional to the speed of the object. Assuming that the initial velocity is \(v_0\), find the velocity of the object as a function of time \(t\), and find the terminal velocity.
  1. Set up and solve an IVP using the Newton's 2nd Law of Motion
  3. Use the result from (Part 1) to solve the original problem
Solution
  1. We assume that the only forces acting on the object are gravity and resistance from the medium, therefore the total force is given as: \begin{equation} \label{eq:4.3.4} F = -mg + F_1 \end{equation} The force due to gravity is \(-mg\) and the force due to resistance from the medium is \(F_1\). It is given that the magnitude of the force due to resistance \(\left| F_1 \right|\) is proportional to the speed of the object. Recall that the speed of an object is \(\left| v \right|\), the absolute value of its velocity \(v\). Thus where \(k\) is the positive constant of proportionality we have the equation: \begin{equation*} \left| F_1 \right| = k \left| v \right| \end{equation*} Now we will simplify the absolute value bars by considering two cases: Case (1) the object is moving downward demonstrated in Figure 4.3.1 (a), and Case (2) the object is moving upward, demonstrated in Figure 4.3.1 (b).
    Case (1) If the object is move downward, then the velocity is negative (\(v\le 0\)). Since the resisting force is opposite, the resisting force is upward, which is positive (\(F_1 \ge 0\)) so our resisting force can be represented as: \begin{align*} \left| F_1 \right| = & k \left| v \right| \\ + F_1 = & k \left( - v \right) \\ F_1 = & -k v \\ \end{align*}
    Case (2): if the object is move upward, then the velocity is positive (\(v \ge 0\)). Since the resisting force is opposite, the resisting force is downward, which is negative (\(F_1 \le 0\)) so our resisting force can be represented as: \begin{align*} \left| F_1 \right| = & k \left| v \right| \\ - F_1 = & k \left( + v \right) \\ F_1 = & -k v \\ \end{align*} In either case, our resisting force can be represented as \(F_1=-kv\) so (4.3.4) can be written as: \begin{equation} \label{eq:4.3.5} F = -mg - kv \end{equation}
    From Newton's second law of motion, \(F=ma=m\dot{v}\), substituting this into (4.3.5) yields a first order differential equation, which we can write in standard form (4.3.6) \begin{align}\label{eq:4.3.6} m\dot{v} = & -mg - kv \nonumber \\ \dot{v} = & -g - \frac{k}{m}v \nonumber \\ \dot{v} + \frac{k}{m}v = & -g \end{align} Including our initial condition \(v(0)=v_0\), we can write it as an IVP \begin{equation*} \dot{v} + \frac{k}{m}v = -g \quad v(0)=v_0 \end{equation*}
    Solve \( \dot{v} + \frac{k}{m}v = -g \) for \( \dot{v} \)
  2. Solving for \(\dot{v}\) and simplifying the right side, we obtain: \begin{align} \dot{v} = & -\frac{kv + mg}{m} \nonumber \\ \end{align} Which is separable, but requires dividing by \( kv + mg \) so we need to check for constant solutions first when \(kv + mg=0\)
    Solve \(kv + mg=0\) for \( v \)
    Solving for constant solutions, we have \( \displaystyle v = -\frac{mg}{k} \) as a constant solution.
    Verify \(\displaystyle v = -\frac{mg}{k}\) is a solution for \( \dot{v} + \frac{k}{m}v = -g \)
    Now assuming \( v \ne -\frac{mg}{k}\) we can divide by \( kv + mg \) and separate variables \begin{align} \label{eq:4.3.7} \int \frac{1}{kv + mg} dv = & -\frac{1}{m} \int dt \nonumber \\ \frac{1}{k} \int \frac{1}{ \underset{u}{\underbrace{kv + gm}} } \overset{du}{\overbrace{(kdv)}} = & -\frac{1}{m} \int dt \nonumber \\ \int \frac{1}{u} du = & -\frac{k}{m} \int dt \nonumber \\ \ln \left| u \right| = & -\frac{k}{m}t + c \nonumber \\ e^{\ln \left| kv + mg \right|} = & e^{-\frac{k}{m}t + c} \nonumber \\ \left| kv + mg \right| = & ce^{-\frac{kt}{m}} \nonumber \\ kv + mg = & ce^{-\frac{kt}{m}} \nonumber \\ kv = & -mg + ce^{-\frac{kt}{m}} \nonumber \\ v = & -\frac{mg}{k} + \frac{c}{k}e^{-\frac{kt}{m}} \nonumber \\ v = & -\frac{mg}{k} + ce^{-\frac{kt}{m}} \end{align} And it turns out that if we let \( c = 0 \) our one-parameter family also represents the constant solution \( v = -\frac{mg}{k} \)
    Verify \(\displaystyle v = -\frac{mg}{k} + ce^{-\frac{kt}{m}}\) is a solution for \( \dot{v} + \frac{k}{m}v = -g \)
    Applying our initial condition \(v(0)=v_0\) \begin{align*} v(t) = & -\frac{mg}{k} + ce^{-\frac{kt}{m}} \\ v(0) = & -\frac{mg}{k} + ce^{-\frac{k(0)}{m}} \\ v_0 = & -\frac{mg}{k} + ce^{0} \Longrightarrow c = v_0 + \frac{mg}{k} \\ \end{align*} \vskip0.5cm Substituting \(c = v_0 + \frac{mg}{k}\) back in to (4.3.7) and rearranging to factor common factors \begin{align*} v = & -\frac{mg}{k} + ce^{-\frac{kt}{m}} \\ v = & -\frac{mg}{k} + \left( v_0 + \frac{mg}{k} \right) e^{-\frac{kt}{m}} \\ v = & -\frac{mg}{k} + v_0 e^{-\frac{kt}{m}} + \frac{mg}{k}e^{-\frac{kt}{m}} \\ v = & v_0 e^{-\frac{kt}{m}} + \frac{mg}{k}e^{-\frac{kt}{m}}-\frac{mg}{k} \\ v = & v_0 e^{-\frac{kt}{m}} + \frac{mg}{k} \left(e^{-\frac{kt}{m}}-1\right) \\ \end{align*}
    Verify \(\displaystyle v = v_0 e^{-\frac{kt}{m}} + \frac{mg}{k} \left(e^{-\frac{kt}{m}}-1\right)\) is a solution for \( \dot{v} + \frac{k}{m}v = -g \)
  3. We want to find the terminal velocity, i.e. take the limit of velocity as : \begin{align*} v = & v_0 e^{-\frac{kt}{m}} + \frac{mg}{k} \left(e^{-\frac{kt}{m}}-1\right) \\ \lim_{t \to \infty} v = & \lim_{t \to \infty} \Bigg[ v_0 e^{-\frac{kt}{m}} + \frac{mg}{k} \left(e^{-\frac{kt}{m}}-1\right) \Bigg] \\ = & v_0 \lim_{t \to \infty} e^{-\frac{kt}{m}} + \frac{mg}{k} \lim_{t \to \infty}\left(e^{-\frac{kt}{m}}-1\right) \\ = & v_0 e^{-\infty} + \frac{mg}{k} \left(e^{-\infty}-1\right) \\ = & v_0 \cdot 0 + \frac{mg}{k} \left(0-1\right) \\ = & -\frac{mg}{k} \end{align*} It is interesting to notice that the terminal velocity is independent of the initial velocity \(v_0\) and the terminal velocity is equal to the constant solution!
A 960-lb object is given an initial upward velocity of 60 ft/s near the surface of Earth. The atmosphere resists the motion with a force of 3 lb for each ft/s of speed. Assuming that the only other force acting on the object is constant gravity, find its velocity \(v\) as a function of \(t\), and find its terminal velocity.
  1. Set up and solve an IVP using the Newton's 2nd Law of Motion
  3. Use the result from (Part 1) to solve the original problem
Solution
  1. We assume that the only forces acting on the object are gravity and resistance from the medium, therefore the total force is given as: \(F = -mg + F_1\), where \(-mg\) is the force due to gravity and \(F_1\) is the resisting force. The force due to gravity is the weight of the object, given as \(mg=960 \, lb\) in this case. The force due to the resistance from the medium is \(F_1\) is \(3 \, lb\) for each \(ft/s\) of velocity \(v\), so \(F_1=-3v\). Substituting in to \(F = -mg + F_1\) we have: \begin{equation*} F = -960 -3v \end{equation*} From Newton's 2nd Law of Motion, \(F=ma=m\dot{v}\), substituting this in yields a first order differential equation: \begin{align*} m\dot{v} = -960 -3v \end{align*} In Example 4.3.1 we didn't know the exact value of the mass, and we left it generically as \(m\) in the equation, but in this situation we can calculate it, since we know \(mg=960\) so \(m= {960}/{g}\) and in the British unit system \(g=32 \, {ft}/{s^2}\) so \(m= {960}/{32} = 30\) and our equation becomes: \begin{align*} 30 \dot{v} = & -960 -3v \\ \end{align*}
    Write \(30 \dot{v} = -960 -3v\) in linear standard form (2.1.1)
    Which we can rewrite in standard form: \begin{align*} \dot{v} + \frac{1}{10}v = & -32 \\ \end{align*} Including our initial condition of initial upward velocity of 60 ft/s, \(v(0)=60\), we can write our IVP: \begin{equation*} \dot{v} + \frac{1}{10}v = -32 \quad v(0)=60 \end{equation*}
  2. Our IVP is separable: \begin{align} \label{eq:4.3.8} \frac{dv}{dt} + \frac{1}{10}v = & -32 \nonumber \\ \frac{dv}{dt} = & - \frac{1}{10}v -32 \nonumber \\ -\frac{dv}{dt} = & \frac{1}{10}v + \frac{320}{10} \nonumber \\ -\frac{dv}{dt} = & \frac{v+320}{10} \nonumber \\ \frac{1}{v+320}\frac{dv}{dt} = & -\frac{1}{10} \nonumber \\ \int \left( \frac{1}{v+320}\frac{dv}{dt} \right) dt = & \int \left( -\frac{1}{10} \right) dt \nonumber \\ \int \frac{1}{ \underset{u}{\underbrace{v+320}} } \overset{du}{\overbrace{dv}} = & -\frac{1}{10} \int dt \nonumber \\ \int \frac{1}{u} du = & -\frac{1}{10} \int dt \nonumber \\ \ln \left| u \right| = & -\frac{1}{10} t + c \nonumber \\ e^{\ln \left| v+320 \right|} = & e^{-\frac{1}{10} t + c} \nonumber \\ \left| v+320 \right|= & ce^{-\frac{t}{10} } \nonumber \\ v+320 = & ce^{-\frac{t}{10} } \nonumber \\ v(t) = & -320 + ce^{-\frac{t}{10} } \end{align}
    Applying our initial condition \(v(0)=60:\) \begin{align*} v(t) = & -320 + ce^{-\frac{t}{10} } \\ v(0) = & -320 + ce^{-\frac{(0)}{10} } \\ 60 = & -320 + ce^{0} \\ c = & 60 + 320 = 380 \end{align*} Substituting \( c = 380 \) back in to (4.3.8) \begin{align*} v = & -320 + ce^{-\frac{t}{10} } \\ v = & -320 + 380e^{-\frac{t}{10} } \\ \end{align*}
  3. We want to find the terminal velocity, i.e. take the limit of velocity: \begin{align*} v = & -320 + 380e^{-\frac{t}{10} } \\ \lim_{t \to \infty} v = & \lim_{t \to \infty} \Bigg[ -320 + 380e^{-\frac{t}{10} } \Bigg] \\ = & -320 + 380 \lim_{t \to \infty} \Big[e^{-\frac{t}{10} } \Big] \\ = & -320 + 380 \Big[e^{-\infty} \Big] \\ = & -320 \, {ft}/{s} \end{align*}
A 10 kg mass is given an initial velocity \(v_0\le 0\) near Earth’s surface. The only forces acting on it are gravity and atmospheric resistance proportional to the square of the speed. Assuming that the resistance is 8 N if the speed is 2 m/s, find the velocity of the object as a function of time \(t\), and find the terminal velocity.
  1. Set up and solve an IVP using the Newton's 2nd Law of Motion
  3. Use the result from (Part 1) to solve the original problem
Solution
  1. We assume that the only forces acting on the object are gravity and resistance from the medium, therefore the total force is given as: \(F = -mg + F_1\), where \(-mg\) is the force due to gravity and \(F_1\) is the resisting force. With given \(m=10 \, kg\) and known \(g=9.8 \, {m}/{s^2}\), we can calculate as \(-mg = -98\) so we have the force due to gravity.
    The force due to the resistance from the medium is \(F_1\) and given as proportional to the square of the speed, so \(F_1=k \left|v \right|^2 = kv^2\) where \(k\) is the constant of proportionality. With the speed given as \(2 \, {m}/{s}\) with a resistance of \(8 \, N\), then we can calculate the proportionality constant \(k\): \begin{align*} F_1 = & kv^2 \\ 8 = & k(2)^2 \\ 8 = & 4k \\ k = & 2 \\ \end{align*} Thus we have the resisting force \(F_1= kv^2 = 2v^2\) so our total force can be expressed: \begin{equation} \label{eq:4.3.9} F = -98 + 2v^2 \end{equation} From Newton's second law of motion, \(F=ma=m\dot{v}\), and with mass given as \(m=10\), substituting this in yields a first order nonlinear differential equation: \begin{align*} 10\dot{v} = & -98 + 2v^2 \end{align*} Rewriting in standard form and including our initial condition of an initial velocity \(v(0)=v_0\le0\), we can write our IVP: \begin{equation} \label{eq:4.3.10} \dot{v} - \frac{1}{5}v^2 = -\frac{49}{5} \quad v(0)=v_0 \le 0 \end{equation}
    Our IVP is separable: \begin{align*} \frac{dv}{dt} - \frac{1}{5}v^2 = & -\frac{49}{5} \\ \frac{dv}{dt} = & \frac{1}{5}v^2 -\frac{49}{5} \\ \frac{dv}{dt} = & \frac{v^2 - 49}{5} \\ \end{align*} But requires dividing by the quantity \(v^2 - 49\) so we identify constant solutions first \(v=7\) and \(v=-7\), and then assuming \(v \ne 7, -7\) we can solve for \(v\): \begin{align} \label{eq:4.3.11} \frac{dv}{dt} = & \frac{v^2 - 49}{5} \nonumber \\ \frac{1}{v^2 - 49} \frac{dv}{dt} = & \frac{1}{5} \\ \int \left( \frac{1}{v^2 - 49} \frac{dv}{dt} \right) dt = &\int \left( \frac{1}{5} \right) dt \nonumber \\ \int \frac{1}{(v-7)(v+7)}dv = & \frac{1}{5} \int dt \nonumber \\ \int \frac{A}{v-7}dv + \int \frac{B}{v+7}dv = & \frac{1}{5} \int dt \nonumber \end{align} Which requires partial fraction decomposition: \begin{align*} \frac{1}{(v-7)(v+7)} = & \frac{A}{v-7}+\frac{B}{v+7} \\ = & \frac{A(v+7)}{(v-7)(v+7)}+\frac{B(v-7)}{(v+7)(v-7)} \\ = & \frac{Av+7A+By-7B}{(v-7)(v+7)} \\ = & \frac{(A+B)v+7(A-B)}{(v-7)(v+7)} \\ \end{align*} So we obtain the system \(A+B=0, 7(A-B)=1\). Solving \(A+B=0 \Longrightarrow B = -A\) and substituting it into the other equation, we get \(A-B=\frac{1}{7} \Longrightarrow A-(-A)=\frac{1}{7} \Longrightarrow 2A=\frac{1}{7}\), so we get \(A=\frac{1}{14}\) and \(B=-A=-\frac{1}{14}\).
  2. Substituting back into our integral, we can integrate: \begin{align} \label{eq:4.3.12} \frac{dv}{dt} = & \frac{v^2 - 49}{5} \nonumber \\ \vdots & \nonumber \\ \int \frac{\frac{1}{14}}{v-7}dv + \int \frac{-\frac{1}{14}}{v+7}dv = & \frac{1}{5} \int dt \nonumber \\ \frac{1}{14}\int \frac{1}{v-7}dv -\frac{1}{14} \int \frac{1}{v+7}dv = & \frac{1}{5} \int dt \\ \int \frac{1}{v-7}dv - \int \frac{1}{v+7}dv = & \frac{14}{5} \int dt \nonumber \\ \int \frac{1}{\underset{u}{\underbrace{v-7}}} \underset{du}{\underbrace{dv}} -\int \frac{1}{\underset{w}{\underbrace{v+7}}} \underset{dw}{\underbrace{dv}} = & \frac{14}{5} \int dt \nonumber \\ \int \frac{1}{u}du - \int \frac{1}{w}dw = & \frac{14}{5} \int dt \nonumber \\ \ln \left| u \right| - \ln \left| w \right| = & \frac{14}{5} t + c \nonumber \\ \ln \left| \frac{u}{w} \right| = & \frac{14}{5} t + c \nonumber \\ \ln \left| \frac{v-7}{v+7} \right| = & \frac{14}{5} t + c \nonumber \\ e^{\ln \left| \frac{v-7}{v+7} \right|} = & e^{\frac{14}{5} t + c} \nonumber \\ \left| \frac{v-7}{v+7} \right|= & ce^{\frac{14t}{5} } \nonumber \\ \frac{v-7}{v+7} = & ce^{\frac{14t}{5} } \label{eq:4.3.13} \\ v-7 = & (v+7)ce^{\frac{14t}{5} } \nonumber \\ v-7 = & vce^{\frac{14t}{5} } + 7ce^{\frac{14t}{5} } \nonumber \\ v - vce^{\frac{14t}{5} }= & 7 + 7ce^{\frac{14t}{5} } \nonumber \\ v \left(1 - ce^{\frac{14t}{5} } \right) = & 7 \left( 1 + ce^{\frac{14t}{5} } \right) \nonumber \\ % v = & 7 \left( \frac{ 1 + ce^{\frac{14t}{5} } }{ 1 - ce^{\frac{14t}{5} } } \right) \nonumber \\ v = & 7 \left( \frac{ 1 + ce^{\frac{14t}{5} } }{ 1 - ce^{\frac{14t}{5} } } \right) \left( \frac{ e^{-\frac{14t}{5} } }{e^{-\frac{14t}{5} } } \right) \nonumber \\ v = & 7 \left( \frac{ e^{-\frac{14t}{5} } + c }{ e^{-\frac{14t}{5} } - c } \right) \nonumber \\ v(t) = & -7 \left( \frac{ c + e^{-\frac{14t}{5} } }{ c - e^{-\frac{14t}{5} } } \right) \label{eq:4.3.14} \end{align}
    Applying our initial condition \(v(0)=v_0 \le 0\) \begin{align*} v(t) = & -7 \left( \frac{ c+ e^{-\frac{14t}{5} } }{ c - e^{-\frac{14t}{5} } } \right) \\ v(0) = & -7 \left( \frac{ c + e^{0} }{ c - e^{0} } \right) \\ v_0 = & -7 \left( \frac{ c + 1 }{ c - 1 } \right) \\ v_0 ( c - 1) = & -7 (c + 1) \\ v_0 c - v_0 = & -7c - 7 \\ v_0 c +7c = & v_0 - 7 \\ c (v_0 +7) = & v_0 - 7 \\ c = & \frac{v_0 - 7}{v_0 +7} \\ \end{align*} Substituting \(c = \frac{v_0 - 7}{v_0 +7}\) back in to (4.3.14) \begin{align*} v(t) = & -7 \left( \frac{ c + e^{-\frac{14t}{5} } }{ c - e^{-\frac{14t}{5} } } \right)\\ v(t) = & -7 \left( \frac{ \left(\frac{v_0 - 7}{v_0 +7}\right) + e^{-\frac{14t}{5} } }{ \left(\frac{v_0 - 7}{v_0 +7}\right) - e^{-\frac{14t}{5} } } \right) \left( \frac{v_0 + 7}{v_0 + 7} \right) \\ v(t) = & -7 \left( \frac{ \left( v_0 - 7 \right) + \left( v_0 + 7 \right)e^{-\frac{14t}{5} } }{ \left( v_0 - 7 \right) - \left( v_0 + 7 \right)e^{-\frac{14t}{5} } } \right)\\ \end{align*}
  3. We want to find the terminal velocity, i.e. take the limit of velocity: \begin{align*} v(t) = & -7 \left( \frac{ \left( v_0 - 7 \right) + \left( v_0 + 7 \right)e^{-\frac{14t}{5} } }{ \left( v_0 - 7 \right) - \left( v_0 + 7 \right)e^{-\frac{14t}{5} } } \right)\\ \lim_{t \to \infty}v(t) = & -7 \lim_{t \to \infty} \left( \frac{ \left( v_0 - 7 \right) + \left( v_0 + 7 \right)e^{-\frac{14t}{5} } }{ \left( v_0 - 7 \right) - \left( v_0 + 7 \right)e^{-\frac{14t}{5} } } \right)\\ = & -7 \left( \frac{ \left( v_0 - 7 \right) + \left( v_0 + 7 \right)\displaystyle\lim_{t \to \infty}e^{-\frac{14t}{5} } }{ \left( v_0 - 7 \right) - \left( v_0 + 7 \right)\displaystyle\lim_{t \to \infty}e^{-\frac{14t}{5} } } \right)\\ = & -7 \left( \frac{ \left( v_0 - 7 \right) + \left( v_0 + 7 \right) e^{-\infty} }{ \left( v_0 - 7 \right) - \left( v_0 + 7 \right) e^{-\infty} } \right)\\ = & -7 \frac{ \left( v_0 - 7 \right) }{ \left( v_0 - 7 \right) } \\ = & -7 \, {m}/{s} \\ \end{align*}
    More generally, it an be shown (Exercise 11) that if \(v\) is any solution of (4.3.9) such that \(v_0\le0\): $$ \lim_{t\to\infty}v(t)=-\sqrt{mg\over k} $$ (Figure 4.3.3).
A 10-kg mass is launched vertically upward from Earth’s surface with an initial velocity of \(v_0\) m/s. The only forces acting on the mass are gravity and atmospheric resistance proportional to the square of the speed. Assuming that the atmospheric resistance is 8 N if the speed is 2 m/s, find the time \(T\) required for the mass to reach maximum altitude.
  1. Set up and solve an IVP using the Newton's 2nd Law of Motion
  3. Use the result from (Part 1) to solve the original problem
Solution
  1. This is the same problem set-up as Example 4.3.3 except the object is launched vertically so that the initial condition is \( v(0)=v_0\ge0\). The force due to gravity is still \(-mg=-98\). The resisting force \(F_1\) is still proportional to the square of the speed, with the same constant of proportionality, so the magnitude of the force is the same as the previous problem \(\left| F_1 \right| = 2v^2\), however, the resisting force is always negative, so \(F_1 = -2v^2\), leading to our differential equation: \begin{equation} \label{eq:4.3.15} 10\dot{v} = -98 - 2v^2 \end{equation} Rewriting in standard form and including our initial condition of an initial velocity \(v(0)=v_0\le0\), we can write our IVP: \begin{equation*} \dot{v} + \frac{1}{5}v^2 = -\frac{49}{5} \quad v(0)=v_0 \ge 0 \end{equation*}
    Our IVP is separable: \begin{align} \frac{dv}{dt} + \frac{1}{5}v^2 = & -\frac{49}{5} \nonumber \\ \frac{dv}{dt} = & -\frac{1}{5}v^2 -\frac{49}{5} \nonumber \\ -\frac{dv}{dt} = & \frac{v^2 + 49}{5} \nonumber \\ \frac{1}{v^2 + 49} \frac{dv}{dt} = & -\frac{1}{5} \nonumber \\ \int \Big( \frac{1}{ v^2 +49} \frac{dv}{dt} \Big) dt = & \int \left( -\frac{1}{5} \right) dt \nonumber \\ \int \frac{1}{ \underset{7\tan\theta}{\underbrace{v}}^2 +49} \underset{7\sec^2\theta d\theta}{\underbrace{dv}} = & -\frac{1}{5} \int dt \nonumber \\ \int \frac{7\sec^2\theta d\theta}{ 49\tan^2\theta +49} = & -\frac{1}{5} \int dt \nonumber \\ \frac{7}{ 49} \int \frac{\sec^2\theta }{ \tan^2\theta +1} d\theta = & -\frac{1}{5} \int dt \nonumber \\ \frac{1}{ 7} \int d\theta = & -\frac{1}{5} \int dt \nonumber \\ \theta = & -\frac{7}{5}t + c \nonumber \\ \tan^{-1}\left( \frac{v(t)}{7} \right) = & -\frac{7}{5}t + c \nonumber \\ \end{align}

Applying our initial condition \(v(0)=v_0 \ge 0\) \begin{align} \tan^{-1}\left( \frac{v(t)}{7} \right) = & -\frac{7}{5}t + c \nonumber \\ \tan^{-1}\left( \frac{v(0)}{7} \right) = & -\frac{7}{5}\cdot(0) + c \nonumber \\ \tan^{-1}\left( \frac{v_0}{7} \right) = & c \\ \end{align} Substituting \(c = \tan^{-1}\left( \frac{v_0}{7} \right) \) back in and solving explicitly for \(v(t)\): \begin{align} \label{eq:4.3.17} \tan^{-1}\left( \frac{v(t)}{7} \right) = & -\frac{7}{5}t + c \nonumber \\ \tan^{-1}\left( \frac{v(t)}{7} \right) = & -\frac{7}{5}t + \tan^{-1}\left( \frac{v_0}{7} \right) \nonumber \\ \frac{v(t)}{7} = & \tan \left( -\frac{7}{5}t + \tan^{-1}\left( \frac{v_0}{7} \right) \right) \nonumber \\ v(t) = & 7 \tan \left( -\frac{7}{5}t + \tan^{-1}\left( \frac{v_0}{7} \right) \right) \end{align} Simplifying using the identity \(\displaystyle \tan(A-B)={\tan A-\tan B\over1+\tan A\tan B}\): \begin{align*} v = & 7 \tan \left( -\frac{7}{5}t + \tan^{-1}\left( \frac{v_0}{7} \right) \right) \\ = & 7 \tan \left( \underset{A}{\underbrace{\tan^{-1}\left( \frac{v_0}{7} \right)}} - \underset{B}{\underbrace{\frac{7}{5}t}} \right) \\ = & 7 \left( \frac{\tan \left( \tan^{-1}\left( \frac{v_0}{7} \right) \right) - \tan \left( \frac{7}{5}t \right)}{1 + \tan \left( \tan^{-1}\left( \frac{v_0}{7} \right)\right) \tan \left( \frac{7}{5}t \right) } \right) \\ = & 7 \left( \frac{ \frac{v_0}{7} - \tan \left( \frac{7t}{5} \right)}{1 + \left(\frac{v_0}{7}\right) \tan \left( \frac{7t}{5} \right) } \right) \left( \frac{7}{7} \right) \\ = & 7 \left( \frac{ v_0 - 7 \tan \left( \frac{7t}{5} \right)}{7 + v_0 \tan \left( \frac{7t}{5} \right) } \right) \\ \end{align*}

Escape Velocity
Suppose a space vehicle is launched vertically and its fuel is exhausted when the vehicle reaches an altitude \(h\) above Earth, where \(h\) is sufficiently large so that resistance due to Earth’s atmosphere can be neglected. Let \(t=0\) be the time when burnout occurs. Assuming that the gravitational forces of all other celestial bodies can be neglected, the motion of the vehicle for \(t > 0\) is that of an object with constant mass \(m\) under the influence of Earth’s gravitational force, which we now assume to vary inversely with the square of the distance from Earth’s center; thus, if we take the upward direction to be positive then gravitational force on the vehicle at an altitude \(y\) above Earth is \begin {equation} \label {eq:4.3.18} F=-{K\over (y+R)^2}\end {equation} where \(R\) is Earth’s radius (Figure  4.3.5 ).
Since \(F=-mg\) when \(y=0\), setting \(y=0\) in (4.3.18) yields \[ -mg=-{K\over R^2}\] therefore \(K=mgR^2\) and (4.3.18) can be written more specifically as \begin {equation} \label {eq:4.3.19} F=-{mgR^2\over (y+R)^2}\end {equation}
From Newton’s second law of motion, \[ F=m{d^2y\over dt^2}\] so (4.3.19) implies that \begin {equation} \label {eq:4.3.20} {d^2y\over dt^2}=-{gR^2\over (y+R)^2}\end {equation}
We’ll show that there’s a number \(v_e\), called the escape velocity, with these properties:
  1. If \(v_0\ge v_e\) then \(v(t)>0\) for all \(t>0\), and the vehicle continues to climb for all \(t>0\); that is, it “escapes” Earth. (Is it really so obvious that \(\lim _{t\to \infty }y(t)=\infty \) in this case? For a proof, see Exercise  20 .)
  2. If \(v_0<v_e\) then \(v(t)\) decreases to zero and becomes negative. Therefore the vehicle attains a maximum altitude \(y_m\) and falls back to Earth.
Since (4.3.20) is second order, we can’t solve it by methods discussed so far. However, we’re concerned with \(v\) rather than \(y\), and \(v\) is easier to find. Since \(v=\dot{y}\) the chain rule implies that \[ {d^2y\over dt^2}={dv\over dt}={dv\over dy}{dy\over dt}=v{dv\over dy}\] Substituting this into (4.3.20) yields the first order separable equation \begin {equation} \label {eq:4.3.21} v{dv\over dy}=-{gR^2\over (y+R)^2}\end {equation} When \(t=0\), the velocity is \(v_0\) and the altitude is \(h\). Therefore we can obtain \(v\) as a function of \(y\) by solving the initial value problem \[ v{dv\over dy}=-{gR^2\over (y+R)^2},\quad v(h)=v_0\]
\begin{align} v \frac{dv}{dy} = & - \frac{gR^2}{(y+R)^2} \nonumber \\ \int \left( v \frac{dv}{dy} \right) dy= & \int \left( - \frac{gR^2}{(y+R)^2} \right) dy \nonumber \\ \int v dy = & -gR^2 \int \frac{1}{(y+R)^2} dy \nonumber \\ \int v dy = & -gR^2 \int \frac{1}{(\underset{u}{\underbrace{y+R}})^2} \underset{du}{\underbrace{dy}} \nonumber \\ \int v dy = & -gR^2 \int \frac{1}{u^2} du \nonumber \\ \int v dy = & -gR^2 \int u^{-2} du \nonumber \\ \frac{1}{2} v^2 = & -gR^2 (-u^{-1}) + c \nonumber \\ \frac{1}{2} v^2 = & \frac{gR^2}{y+R} + c \label{eq:4.3.22} \end{align} \vskip0.5cm \noindent Applying the initial condition \(v(0)=v_0\) and \(y(0)=h\): \begin{align*} \frac{1}{2} v^2(t) = & \frac{gR^2}{y(t)+R} + c \\ \frac{1}{2} v^2(0) = & \frac{gR^2}{y(0)+R} + c \\ \frac{1}{2} v_0^2 = & \frac{gR^2}{h+R} + c \\ c = & \frac{1}{2}v_0^2 - \frac{gR^2}{h+R} \\ \end{align*}
Substituting back in: \begin{align*} \frac{1}{2} v^2 = & \frac{gR^2}{y+R} + c \nonumber \\ \frac{1}{2} v^2 = & \frac{gR^2}{y+R} + \left( \frac{1}{2}v_0^2 - \frac{gR^2}{h+R} \right) \end{align*}
Integrating (4.3.21) with respect to \(y\) yields \begin {equation} {v^2\over 2}={gR^2\over y+R}+c\end {equation} Since \(v(h)=v_0\), \[ c={v_0^2\over 2}-{gR^2\over h+R}\] so (4.3.22) becomes \begin {equation} \label {eq:4.3.23} {v^2\over 2}={gR^2\over y+R}+\left ({v_0^2\over 2}- {gR^2\over h+R}\right )\end {equation} If \[ v_0 \ge \left ({2gR^2\over h+R}\right )^{1/2}\] the parenthetical expression in (4.3.23) is nonnegative, so \(v(y)>0\) for \(y>h\). This proves that there’s an escape velocity \(v_e\). We’ll now prove that \[ v_e=\left ({2gR^2\over h+R}\right )^{1/2} \] by showing that the vehicle falls back to Earth if \begin {equation} \label {eq:4.3.24} v_0 <\left ({2gR^2\over h+R}\right )^{1/2}\end {equation} If (4.3.24) holds then the parenthetical expression in (4.3.23) is negative and the vehicle will attain a maximum altitude \(y_m>h\) that satisfies the equation \[ 0={gR^2\over y_m+R}+\left ({v_0^2\over 2}- {gR^2\over h+R}\right )\] The velocity will be zero at the maximum altitude, and the object will then fall to Earth under the influence of gravity.
