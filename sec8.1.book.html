8.1 Introduction to the Laplace Transform
Laplace Transform
To define the Laplace transform, we first recall the definition of an improper integral defined as \begin {equation} \label {eq:8.1.1} \int ^\infty _a g(t)\,dt=\lim _{x\to \infty }\int ^x_a g(t)\,dt \end {equation} We say that the improper integral converges if the limit (8.1.1) exists. Otherwise, we say that the improper integral diverges or does not exist. Now we'll define the Laplace transform of a function \(f\).
Definition 8.1.1 Let \(f(t)\) be defined for \(t\ge 0\) and let \(s\) be a real number\(.\) Then the Laplace transform of \(f(t)\) is the function \(F(s)\) defined by \begin {equation} \label {eq:8.1.2} F(s)=\int _0^\infty e^{-st} \, f(t)\,dt \end {equation} for those values of \(s\) for which the improper integral converges



It is important to keep in mind that the variable of integration in the Laplace transform (8.1.2) is \(t\), while \(s\) is a parameter independent of \(t\). We use \(t\) as the independent variable for the function \(f\) because in applications the Laplace transform is frequently applied to functions of time.
The Laplace transform can be viewed as an operator \(\cal L\) that transforms the function \(f=f(t)\) into the function \(F=F(s)\). Thus, (8.1.2) can be expressed as \[ F(s)= {\cal L} \big\{ f(t) \big\} \] The functions \(f(t)\) and \(F(s)\) form a transform pair, which we’ll sometimes denote by \[ f(t)\longleftrightarrow F(s)\] It can be shown that if \(F(s)\) is defined for \(s=s_0\) then it’s defined for all \(s>s_0\)






Find the Laplace transform of \(f(t)=1\)
Solution
From (8.1.2) with \(f(t)=1\) \begin {align} \label {eq:8.1.3} {\cal L} \big\{ 1 \big\} = & \int _0^\infty e^{-st} \cdot 1 \, dt \nonumber \\ = & \int _0^\infty e^{-st} \, dt \nonumber \\ = & -\frac{1}{s} \int _0^\infty e^{\overset{w}{\overbrace{-st}}} \, \underset{dw}{\underbrace{(-s)dt}} \nonumber \\ = & -\frac{1}{s} \lim_{x \to \infty} \Bigg. \Big[ \, e^{-st} \Big] \Bigg|_{t=0}^{t=x} \nonumber \\ = & -\frac{1}{s} \, \lim_{x \to \infty} \, \Big[ e^{-sx} - e^{0} \Big] \nonumber \\ = & \frac{1}{s} \, \lim_{x \to \infty} \, \Big[ 1 - e^{-sx} \Big] \nonumber \\ = & \frac{1}{s} \, \Big[ \, 1 - \lim_{x \to \infty}e^{-sx} \, \Big] \end {align} Now to evaluate the limit, we'll consider cases for \(s\). First if \(s < 0 \) then the limit diverges, so the Laplace transform is not defined for those values, whereas if \(s < 0 \) then the limit converges to zero, so we obtain: \begin {align} \label {eq:8.1.4} {\cal L} \big\{ 1 \big\} = & \frac{1}{s} \, \Big[ \, 1 - \lim_{x \to \infty}e^{-sx} \, \Big] \nonumber \\ = & \frac{1}{s} \, \Big[ \, 1 - 0 \, \Big] \nonumber \\ = & \frac{1}{s} \end {align} This result can be written in operator notation as \[ {\cal L} \big\{ 1 \big\} ={1\over s}\quad\quad s>0\] or as the transform pair \[ 1\longleftrightarrow {1\over s}\quad \quad s>0\]



Find the Laplace transform of \(f(t)=t\)
Solution
From (8.1.2) with \(f(t)=t\) \begin {align} \label {eq:8.1.5} {\cal L} \big\{ t \big\} = & \int _0^\infty t \, e^{-st} \, dt \\ = & \int _0^\infty t \, e^{-st} \, dt \nonumber \\ = & \frac{1}{s^2} \int _0^\infty \overset{w}{\overbrace{(-s)t}} \, e^{\overset{w}{\overbrace{-st}}} \, \underset{dw}{\underbrace{(-s)dt}} \nonumber \\ = & \frac{1}{s^2} \int _0^\infty w \, e^{w} \, dw \nonumber \\ \end {align} Then using IBP (Example 2.4.1) \begin {align} {\cal L} \big\{ t \big\} = & \frac{1}{s^2} \int _0^\infty \overset{U}{\overbrace{w}} \, \overset{dV}{\overbrace{e^{w} \, dw }} \nonumber \\ = & \frac{1}{s^2} \Bigg[ \, w e^w - \int _0^\infty e^{w} \, dw \, \Bigg] \nonumber \\ = & \frac{1}{s^2} \lim_{x \to \infty} \Bigg. \Big[ \, (w - 1) e^w \, \Big] \Bigg|_{s=0}^{s=x} \nonumber \\ = & -\frac{1}{s^2} \lim_{x \to \infty} \Bigg. \Big[ \, (st + 1) e^{-st} \, \Big] \Bigg|_{t=0}^{t=x} \nonumber \\ = & -\frac{1}{s^2} \lim_{x \to \infty} \Big[ \, (sx + 1) e^{-sx} - 1\, \Big] \nonumber \\ = & \frac{1}{s^2} \Big[ \, 1 - \lim_{x \to \infty} (sx + 1) e^{-sx} \, \Big] \nonumber \\ \end {align} Now to evaluate the limit, we'll apply L'Hopital's rule \begin {align} \lim_{x \to \infty} \, (sx + 1) e^{-sx} = & \lim_{x \to \infty} \left( \frac{sx + 1}{e^{sx}} \right) \nonumber \\ = & \lim_{x \to \infty} \left( \dfrac{\dfrac{d}{dx} \Big(sx + 1 \Big)}{\dfrac{d}{dx} \Big( e^{sx} \Big) } \right) = \lim_{x \to \infty}\left( \dfrac{s}{s \, e^{sx} } \right) \nonumber \\ = & \lim_{x \to \infty}\left( \dfrac{1}{e^{sx} } \right) = \lim_{x \to \infty} \Big( e^{-sx} \Big) = e^{-\infty} = 0 \nonumber \\ \end {align} Substituting the zero for the limit back in \begin {align} {\cal L} \big\{ t \big\} = & \frac{1}{s^2} \Big[ \, 1 - \underset{0}{\underbrace{\lim_{x \to \infty} (sx + 1) e^{-sx}}} \, \Big] = \frac{1}{s^2} \quad\quad s>0 \nonumber \\ \end {align} This result can be written in operator notation as \[ {\cal L} \big\{ t \big\} ={1\over s^2}\quad\quad s>0\] or as the transform pair \[ t \longleftrightarrow {1\over s^2}\quad \quad s>0\]



Find the Laplace transform of \(f(t)=e^{at}\), where \(a\) is a constant.
Solution
From (8.1.2) with \( f(t)=e^{at} \) \begin {align*} {\cal L} \big\{ e^{at} \big\} = & \int _0^\infty e^{at} \, e^{-st} \, dt \\ = & \int _0^\infty e^{(a-s)t} \, dt \nonumber \\ = & \frac{1}{a-s} \int _0^\infty e^{\overset{w}{\overbrace{(a-s)t}}} \, \underset{dw}{\underbrace{(a-s)dt}} \nonumber \\ = & \frac{1}{a-s} \int _0^\infty e^{w} \, dw \nonumber \\ = & \frac{1}{a-s} \lim_{x \to \infty} \Bigg. \Big[ \, e^{(a-s)t} \Big] \Bigg|_{t=0}^{t=x} \nonumber \\ = & \frac{1}{a-s} \lim_{x \to \infty} \Big[ \, e^{(a-s)x} - 1 \Big] \nonumber \\ = & \frac{1}{s-a} \Big[ \, 1 - \lim_{x \to \infty} e^{(a-s)x} \Big] \nonumber \\ \end {align*} Now to evaluate the limit, we'll consider cases for \(a-s\). First if \(a-s > 0 \) then the limit diverges, so the Laplace transform is not defined for those values, whereas if \(a-s < 0 \) then the limit converges to zero, so we obtain: \begin {align*} {\cal L} \big\{ e^{at} \big\} = & \frac{1}{s-a} \, \Big[ \, 1 - \lim_{x \to \infty}e^{(a-s)x} \, \Big] \nonumber \\ = & \frac{1}{s-a} \, \Big[ \, 1 - 0 \, \Big] \nonumber \\ = & \frac{1}{s-a} \end {align*} This result can be written in operator notation as \[ {\cal L} \big\{ e^{at} \big\} ={1\over s-a}\quad\quad s>a\] or as the transform pair \[ e^{at} \longleftrightarrow {1\over s-a}\quad \quad s>a\]


We'll now summarize the reesults from the last two examples. In Example 8.1.1 we found \[ {\cal L} \big\{ 1 \big\} = \frac{1}{s}\] And in Example 8.1.2 we found \[ {\cal L} \big\{ e^{at} \big\} = \frac{1}{s-a}\] We will be using these results later on when we discuss the First Shifting Theorem (Theorem 8.1.3)



Find the Laplace transforms of \(f(t)=\sin \omega t\) and \(g(t)=\cos \omega t\)
Solution
With \(f(t)=\sin \omega t\) and \(g(t)=\cos \omega t\) we'll define the following \[ F(s) = {\cal L} \big\{ f(t) \big\} = {\cal L} \big\{ \sin \omega t \big\} \] \[ G(s) = {\cal L} \big\{ g(t) \big\} = {\cal L} \big\{ \cos \omega t \big\} \] Then from (8.1.2) we have \begin {equation} \label {eq:8.1.6} F(s)=\int _0^\infty \, e^{-st}\sin \omega t \, dt \end {equation} \begin {equation} \label {eq:8.1.7} G(s)=\int _0^\infty \, e^{-st}\cos \omega t \, dt \end {equation} Integrating (8.1.6) requires IBP (Example 8.1.2)
\begin {align*} F(s)=\int _0^\infty \, \overset{U}{\overbrace{\sin \omega t}} \,\, \overset{dV}{\overbrace{e^{-st} \, dt }} \end {align*}
Differentiate \(U=\sin \omega t\)
Antidifferentiate \(dV= e^{-st} \, dt\)
Apply IBP with \(U=\sin \omega t\) and \(dV= e^{-st} \, dt\)
\begin {align*} F(s) =-\frac{1}{s} \lim_{x \to \infty} \Bigg. \Big[ \, e^{-st}\sin \omega t \, \Big] \Bigg|_{0}^{x} + \frac{\omega}{s} \int _0^\infty \, e^{-st}\cos \omega t \, dt \end {align*} When we studied calculus, we learned to apply IBP again and then combine the result with itself and solve for the original integral. However, in this case, after applying IBP once we actually are able to recognize the integral on the right side as (8.1.7) so we obtain the equation \begin {align*} F(s) = & \frac{1}{s} \Big[ \, \underset{0}{\underbrace{e^0 \, \sin 0}} - \underset{0}{\underbrace{ \lim_{x \to \infty} \Big( e^{-sx}\sin \omega x \Big) }} \, \Big] + \frac{\omega}{s} \, \underset{G(s)}{\underbrace{ \int _0^\infty \, e^{-st} \, \cos \omega t \, dt}} \\ \end {align*} \begin {align*} F(s) = & \frac{\omega}{s} \, G(s) \\ \end {align*} Now we'll apply IBP to (8.1.7) to obtain a second equation:
\begin {align*} G(s)=\int _0^\infty \, \overset{U}{\overbrace{\cos \omega t}} \,\, \overset{dV}{\overbrace{e^{-st} \, dt }} \end {align*}
Differentiate \(U=\cos \omega t\)
Antidifferentiate \(dV= e^{-st} \, dt\)
Apply IBP with \(U=\cos \omega t\) and \(dV= e^{-st} \, dt\)
\begin {align*} G(s) =-\frac{1}{s} \lim_{x \to \infty} \Bigg. \Big[ \, e^{-st}\cos \omega t \, \Big] \Bigg|_{0}^{x} - \frac{\omega}{s} \int _0^\infty \, e^{-st}\sin \omega t \, dt \end {align*} After applying IBP we can again recognize the integral on the right side as (8.1.6) so we obtain the equation \begin {align*} G(s) = & \frac{1}{s} \Big[ \, \underset{1}{\underbrace{e^0 \, \cos 0}} - \underset{0}{\underbrace{ \lim_{x \to \infty} \Big( e^{-sx}\cos \omega x \Big) }} \, \Big] - \frac{\omega}{s} \, \underset{G(s)}{\underbrace{ \int _0^\infty \, e^{-st} \, \cos \omega t \, dt}} \\ \end {align*} \begin {align*} G(s) = & \frac{1}{s} - \frac{\omega}{s} \, F(s) \\ \end {align*} So we have the system of equations \begin {align} \label {eq:8.1.8} \left\{ \begin{array}{rrl} F(s) &=& \dfrac{\omega}{s} \, G(s) \\ \\ G(s) &=& \dfrac{1}{s} - \dfrac{\omega}{s} \, F(s) \\ \end{array} \right. \end {align}
Solve the system (8.1.8)
\begin {align*} G(s) = & \frac{1}{s} - \frac{\omega}{s} \, F(s) \\ G(s) = & \frac{1}{s} - \frac{\omega^2}{s^2} \, G(s)\\ G(s) + \frac{\omega^2}{s^2} \, G(s) = & \frac{1}{s} \\ \left( 1 + \frac{\omega^2}{s^2} \right) \, G(s) = & \frac{1}{s} \\ \left( \frac{s^2 + \omega^2}{s^2} \right) \, G(s) = & \frac{1}{s} \\ \Longrightarrow G(s) = & \frac{s}{s^2 + \omega^2} \quad\quad\quad s>0 \\ \end {align*} Substituting back in we obtain \begin {align*} F(s) = & \dfrac{\omega}{s} \, G(s) \\ F(s) = & \dfrac{\omega}{s} \, \left( \frac{s}{s^2 + \omega^2} \right) \\ \Longrightarrow F(s) = & \, \frac{\omega}{s^2 + \omega^2} \quad\quad\quad s>0 \\ \end {align*}
Our results in operator notation \[ F(s) = {\cal L} \big\{ \sin \omega t \big\} = \frac{\omega}{s^2 + \omega^2} \quad\quad\quad s>0 \] \[ G(s) = {\cal L} \big\{ \cos \omega t \big\} = \frac{s}{s^2 + \omega^2} \quad\quad\quad s>0 \] Or written as transform pairs \[ \sin \omega t \longleftrightarrow \frac{\omega}{s^2 + \omega^2} \quad\quad\quad s>0 \] \[ \cos \omega t \longleftrightarrow \frac{s}{s^2 + \omega^2} \quad\quad\quad s>0 \]

Properties
Theorem 8.1.2 Linearity Property
Suppose \( {\cal L} \big\{ f(t) \big\} \) and \( {\cal L} \big\{ g(t) \big\} \) Laplace transforms are defined. Then the following properties are true: \[ {\cal L} \big\{ f(t) + g(t) \big\} ={\cal L} \big\{ f(t) \big\} + {\cal L} \big\{ g(t) \big\} \] \[ {\cal L} \big\{ c \cdot f(t) \big\} = c \cdot {\cal L} \big\{ f(t) \big\} \] Essentially the Laplace transform can break up across terms and constants can be factored out.
From (8.1.2) we can easily show \({\cal L} \big\{ f(x) + g(x) \big\} ={\cal L} \big\{ f(x) \big\} + {\cal L} \big\{ g(x) \big\}\) \begin {align*} {\cal L} \big\{ f(x) + g(x) \big\} = & \int _0^\infty e^{-st}\Big( f(x) + g(x) \Big) \, dt \\ = & \int _0^\infty \Big( e^{-st}f(x) + e^{-st}g(x) \Big) \, dt \\ = & \int _0^\infty e^{-st}f(x) \, dt + \int _0^\infty e^{-st}g(x) \, dt \\ = & \quad {\cal L} \big\{ f(x) \big\} \quad+\quad {\cal L} \big\{ g(x) \big\} \\ \end {align*} As well as \({\cal L} \big\{ c \cdot f(x) \big\} = c \cdot {\cal L} \big\{ f(x) \big\} \) \begin {align*} {\cal L} \big\{ c \cdot f(x) \big\} = & \, \int _0^\infty e^{-st}\Big( c \cdot f(x) \Big) \, dt \\ = & \, c \int _0^\infty e^{-st}f(x) \, dt \\ = & \, c \, \cdot \, {\cal L} \big\{ f(x) \big\} \\ \end {align*}
Find \({\cal L} \big\{ \cosh bt \big\} \) by applying the linearity property (Theorem 8.1.2) of the Laplace transform and the resulting transform pair from Example 8.1.3 \[ {\cal L} \big\{ e^{at} \big\} ={1\over s-a} \quad\quad\quad s>a\]
Solution
We'll start by rewriting the cosh function in terms of exponentials (by definition): \[ \cosh bt ={{e^{bt}+e^{-bt}\over 2} =\frac{1}{2}e^{bt} + \frac{1}{2}e^{-bt} }\] Now we apply the linearity property of the Laplace transform \begin {align} \label {eq:8.1.9} {\cal L} \big\{ \cosh bt \big\} = & \, {\cal L} \left\{ \frac{1}{2}e^{bt} + \frac{1}{2}e^{-bt} \right\} \nonumber \\ = & \, {\cal L} \left\{ \frac{1}{2}e^{bt} \right\} + {\cal L} \left\{ \frac{1}{2}e^{-bt} \right\} \nonumber \\ = & \, \frac{1}{2} {\cal L} \left\{ e^{bt} \right\} + \frac{1}{2} {\cal L} \left\{ e^{-bt} \right\} \end {align} Now we apply the given Laplace transform \( {\cal L} \big\{ e^{at} \big\} =1 / (s-a) \) to each: \[ {\cal L} \left\{ e^{bt} \right\} ={1\over s-b} \quad\quad\quad s>b \] \[ {\cal L} \left\{ e^{-bt} \right\} = {1\over s+b} \quad\quad\quad s > -b \] Now substituting them back in we can get an expression for \({\cal L} \big\{ \cosh bt \big\} \) \begin {align*} {\cal L} \big\{ \cosh bt \big\} = & \, \frac{1}{2} {\cal L} \left\{ e^{bt} \right\} + \frac{1}{2} {\cal L} \left\{ e^{-bt} \right\} \nonumber \\ = & \, \frac{1}{2} \left( \frac{1}{s-b} \right) + \frac{1}{2} \left( \frac{1}{s+b} \right) \nonumber \\ = & \, \frac{s+b}{2(s-b)(s+b)} + \frac{s-b}{2(s+b)(s-b)} \nonumber \\ = & \, \frac{2s}{2(s-b)(s+b)} \nonumber \\ = & \, \frac{s}{(s-b)(s+b)} \nonumber \\ = & \, \frac{s}{s^2-b^2} \quad\quad\quad s > \big| b \big| \nonumber \\ \end {align*}
Theorem 8.1.3 First Shifting Theorem
Recall the Laplace transform definition (8.1.2) where we define the Laplace transform of \(f(t)\) \begin {equation*} {\cal L} \big\{ f(t) \big\} = \int _0^\infty \, e^{-st} \, f(t) \, dt = F(s) \end {equation*} If we know the Laplace transform of \(f(t)=F(s)\) then we can determine the Laplace transform of \(e^{at} \, f(t)\) \begin {align} \label {eq:8.1.10} {\cal L} \big\{ e^{at} \, f(t) \big\} = & \int _0^\infty \, e^{-st} e^{at} \, f(t) \, dt \nonumber \\ = & \int _0^\infty \, e^{-(s-a)t} \, f(t) \, dt = F(s-a) \end {align} In other words if \( {\cal L} \big\{ f(t) \big\} = F(s) \) then \( {\cal L} \big\{ e^{at} \, f(t) \big\} = F(s-a) \)
Replacing \(s\) by \(s-a\) in (8.1.2) yields \begin {equation} \label {eq:8.1.11} F(s-a)=\int _0^\infty \, e^{-(s-a)t} \, f(t) \, dt \end {equation} However, (8.1.11) can be rewritten as \begin {align*} F(s-a) = & \int _0^\infty \, e^{-(s-a)t} \, f(t) \, dt \\ = & \int _0^\infty \, e^{-st} e^{at} \, f(t) \, dt \\ = & \int _0^\infty \, e^{-st} \, \Big(e^{at} \, f(t)\Big) \, dt \\ = & \, {\cal L} \big\{ e^{at} \, f(t) \big\} \end {align*} Recall we observed this pattern earlier in Example 8.1.1 and 8.1.2 but now that we have formally established it we can use this First Shifting Theorem to calculate other Laplace transforms without having to calculate the integral.
Use the First Shifting Theorem (Theorem 8.1.3) and the resulting Laplace transform pairs found in Examples 8.1.1, 8.1.2, and 8.1.4 to find the following Laplace transforms
  1. \( \quad {\cal L} \left\{ e^{at} \right\} \)
  2. \( \quad {\cal L} \left\{ t \, e^{at} \right\} \)
  3. \( \quad {\cal L} \left\{ e^{a t}\cos \omega t \right\} \)
  4. \( \quad {\cal L} \left\{ e^{a t}\sin \omega t \right\} \)
Solution
  1. In Example 8.1.1 we found \[ {\cal L} \big\{ 1 \big\} = \int _0^\infty \, 1 \cdot e^{-st} \, dt = \frac{1}{s} = F(s) \] Now applying the First Shifting Theorem (Theorem 8.1.3) we can calculate \( {\cal L} \big\{ 1 \cdot e^{at} \big\} \) \begin{align*} {\cal L} \big\{ e^{at} \big\} = & \int _0^\infty \, e^{-st} \cdot e^{at} \, dt \\ = & \int _0^\infty \, e^{-(s-a)t} \, dt = \frac{1}{s-a} = F(s-a) \end{align*}


  2. In Example 8.1.2 we found \[ {\cal L} \big\{ t \big\} = \int _0^\infty \, te^{-st} \, dt = \frac{1}{s^2} = F(s) \] Now applying the First Shifting Theorem (Theorem 8.1.3) we can calculate \( {\cal L} \big\{ t \cdot e^{at} \big\} \) \begin{align*} {\cal L} \big\{ t \cdot e^{at} \big\} = & \int _0^\infty \, t \, e^{-st} e^{at} \, dt \\ = & \int _0^\infty \, t \, e^{-(s-a)t} \, dt = \frac{1}{(s-a)^2} = F(s-a) \end{align*}


  3. In Example 8.1.4 we found \[ {\cal L} \big\{ \sin \omega t \big\} = \int _0^\infty \, e^{-st} \sin \omega t \, dt = \frac{\omega}{s^2 + \omega^2} = F(s) \] Now applying the First Shifting Theorem (Theorem 8.1.3) we can calculate \( {\cal L} \big\{ e^{at} \sin \omega t \big\} \) \begin{align*} {\cal L} \big\{ e^{at} \sin \omega t \big\} = & \int _0^\infty \, e^{-st} e^{at} \sin \omega t \, dt \\ = & \int _0^\infty \, e^{-(s-a)t} \sin \omega t \, dt = \frac{\omega}{(s-a)^2 + \omega^2} = F(s-a) \end{align*}


  4. In Example 8.1.4 we found \[ {\cal L} \big\{ \cos \omega t \big\} = \int _0^\infty \, e^{-st} \cos \omega t \, dt = \frac{s}{s^2 + \omega^2} = F(s) \] Now applying the First Shifting Theorem (Theorem 8.1.3) we can calculate \( {\cal L} \big\{ e^{at} \cos \omega t \big\} \) \begin{align*} {\cal L} \big\{ e^{at} \cos \omega t \big\} = & \int _0^\infty \, e^{-st} e^{at} \cos \omega t \, dt \\ = & \int _0^\infty \, e^{-(s-a)t} \cos \omega t \, dt = \frac{s-a}{(s-a)^2 + \omega^2} = F(s-a) \end{align*}
Discontinuities
Recall the Laplace transform definition (8.1.2) is an improper integral which is only defined when the integral converges: \begin {align*} F(s)=\int _0^\infty e^{-st} \, f(t)\,dt \end {align*} We have already found that while we can find Laplace transform of some functions, there are often restrictions on the domain to ensure the improper integral converges. For instance \[ {\cal L} \big\{ 1 \big\} ={1\over s}\quad\quad s>0\] \[ {\cal L} \big\{ t \big\} ={1\over s^2}\quad\quad s>0\] \[ {\cal L} \big\{ e^{at} \big\} ={1\over s-a}\quad\quad s>a\] \[ {\cal L} \big\{ e^{-at} \big\} ={1\over s+a}\quad\quad s>-a\] \[ {\cal L} \big\{ \cosh at \big\} = \frac{s}{s^2-a^2} \quad\quad\quad s > \big| a \big| \] \[ {\cal L} \big\{ \sin \omega t \big\} = \frac{\omega}{s^2 + \omega^2} \quad\quad\quad s>0 \] \[ {\cal L} \big\{ \cos \omega t \big\} = \frac{s}{s^2 + \omega^2} \quad\quad\quad s>0 \] So the Laplace transform exists for all of these functions but only on a restricted domain to ensure the improper integral conveges. However, while not every function has a Laplace transform. For instance, the improper integral \[ \int _0^\infty e^{-st}e^{t^2} dt=\infty \] diverges for for every real number \(s\) meaning that the function \(f(t)=e^{t^2}\) does not have a Laplace transform. Since sometimes the Laplace transform exists and sometimes it doesn't it would be helpful to establish conditions which ensure the existence of the Laplace transform of a function.
We first review some relevant definitions from calculus. Recall that a limit \[ \lim _{t\to a} f(t) \] exists if and only if the one-sided limits both exist and are equal \[ \lim _{t\to a^-} \, f(t) = \lim _{t\to a^+} \, f(t) \] Recall also that \(f(t)\) is continuous at a point \(a\) in an open interval if and only if \begin {equation} \label {eq:8.1.12} \lim _{t\to a}f(t)=f(a) \end {equation}
For simplicity, we define \[ f(t_0+)=\lim _{t\to t_0+}f(t)\quad \hbox {and }\quad f(t_0-)=\lim _{t\to t_0-}f(t)\] so (8.1.12) can be expressed as \[ f(t_0+)=f(t_0-)=f(t_0)\]
If \(\displaystyle \lim _{t\to a^+} f(t)\) and \(\displaystyle \lim _{t\to a^-} f(t) \) have finite but distinct values, we say that \(f(t)\) has a jump discontinuity at \(a\), and \[ \lim _{t\to a^+} f(t) - \lim _{t\to a^-} f(t) \] is called the jump in \(f(t)\) at \(a\) (Figure 8.1.1)
If \(\displaystyle\lim _{t\to a^+} f(t)\) and \(\displaystyle\lim _{t\to a^-}f(t)\) are finite and equal, but either \(f(t)\) isn’t defined at \(a\) or it’s defined but \[ f(a) \ne \lim _{t\to a^+} f(t) = \lim _{t\to a^-} f(t) \] we say that \(f(t)\) has a removable discontinuity at \(a\) (Figure 8.1.2). This terminology is appropriate since a function \(f(t)\) with a removable discontinuity at \(a\) can be made continuous at \(a\) by defining (or redefining) \[ f(a) = \lim _{t\to a^+} f(t) = \lim _{t\to a^-} f(t) \]
We know from calculus that a definite integral isn’t affected by changing the values of its integrand at isolated points. Therefore, redefining a function \(f(t)\) to make it continuous at removable discontinuities does not change the Laplace transform of the function \( {\cal L} \big\{ f(t) \big\} \)
Definition 8.1.4
  1. A function \(f(t)\) is said to be piecewise continuous on a finite closed interval \([0,T]\) if \(\lim _{t\to 0^+} f(t)\) and \(\lim _{t\to T^-} f(t)\) are finite and \(f(t) \) is continuous on the open interval \((0,T)\) except possibly at finitely many points, where \(f(t)\) may have jump discontinuities or removable discontinuities

  2. A function \(f(t)\) is said to be piecewise continuous on the infinite interval \([0,\infty )\) if it’s piecewise continuous on \([0,T]\) for every \(T>0\)
Figure 8.1.3 shows the graph of a typical piecewise continuous function.
It is shown in calculus that if a function is piecewise continuous on a finite closed interval then it’s integrable on that interval. And if \(f(t)\) is piecewise continuous on \([0,\infty )\), then so is \(e^{-st}f (t)\), and therefore \[ \int _0^T e^{-st}f(t)\,dt \] exists for every \(T>0\). However, piecewise continuity alone does not guarantee that the improper integral \begin {equation} \label {eq:8.1.13} \int _0^\infty e^{-st}f(t)\,dt=\lim _{T\to \infty }\int _0^T e^{-st}f(t)\, dt \end {equation} converges for \(s\) in some interval \((s_0,\infty )\). For example, we noted earlier that (8.1.13) diverges for all \(s\) if \(f(t)=e^{t^2}\). Stated informally, this occurs because \(e^{t^2}\) increases too rapidly as \(t\to \infty \). The next definition provides a constraint on the growth of a function that guarantees convergence of its Laplace transform for \(s\) in some interval \((s_0,\infty )\)
Definition 8.1.5 A function \(f(t)\) is said to be of exponential order\(s_0\) if there are constants \(M\) and \(a\) such that \begin {equation} \label {eq:8.1.14} |f(t)|\le Me^{s_0t},\quad t\ge a \end {equation} In situations where the specific value of \(s_0\) is irrelevant we say simply that \(f(t)\) is of exponential order
Theorem 8.1.6 If \(f(t)\) is piecewise continuous on \([0,\infty )\) and of exponential order \(s_0,\) then \( {\cal L} \big\{ f(t) \big\} \) is defined for \(s>s_0\)
Remark: We emphasize that the conditions of Theorem 8.1.6 are sufficient, but not necessary , for \(f\) to have a Laplace transform. For example, Exercise 14(c) shows that \(f\) may have a Laplace transform even though \(f\) isn’t of exponential order.


If \(f(t)\) is bounded on some interval \([a,\infty )\), say \[ |f(t)|\le M,\quad t\ge a\] then (8.1.14) holds with \(s_0=0\), so \(f\) is of exponential order zero. Thus, for example, \(\sin \omega t\) and \(\cos \omega t\) are of exponential order zero, and Theorem 8.1.6 implies that \( {\cal L} \big\{ \sin \omega t \big\} \) and \( {\cal L} \big\{ \cos \omega t \big\} \) exist for \(s>0\). This is consistent with the conclusion of Example 8.1.4.


It can be shown that if \(\lim _{t\to \infty }e^{-s_0t}f(t)\) exists and is finite then \(f\) is of exponential order \(s_0\) (Exercise 9). If \(\alpha \) is any real number and \(s_0>0\) then \(f(t)=t^\alpha \) is of exponential order \(s_0\), since \[ \lim _{t\to \infty }e^{-s_0t}t^\alpha =0\] by L'Hopital's rule. If \(\alpha \ge 0\), \(f\) is also continuous on \([0,\infty )\). Therefore Exercise 9 and Theorem 8.1.6 imply that \({\cal L}(t^\alpha )\) exists for \(s\ge s_0\). However, since \(s_0\) is an arbitrary positive number, this really implies that \({\cal L}(t^\alpha )\) exists for all \(s>0\). This is consistent with the results of Example 8.1.2and Exercises 6 and 8.


Show that the \(f(t)\) is piecewise continuous and then find \( {\cal L} \big\{ f(t) \big\} \) \[ f(t)=\left \{ \begin {array}{cl} 1&0\le t<1\\ -3e^{-t}&t\ge 1 \end {array}\right. \]
Solution
To show the function \(f(t)\) is piecewise continuous we will first examine each piece. The first piece is \(f(t)=1\) defined on the interval \([0,1)\) and as a constant function is continuous. The second piece \(f(t)=-3e^{-t}\) defined on the interval \([1,\infty)\) and as an exponential function is continuous. Now we have to evaluate the limit from either side of \(x=1\) \begin{align*} \lim_{t \to 1^-} \, f(t) = & \lim_{t \to 1^-} \, 1 = 1 \\ \lim_{t \to 1^+} \, f(t) = & \lim_{t \to 1^+} \, -3e^{-t} = -\frac{3}{e} \\ \end{align*} Since \(\displaystyle \lim_{t \to 1^-} f(t) \ne \displaystyle \lim_{t \to 1^+} f(t) \) and both different finite values, then \(f(t)\) has a jump discontinuity at \(x=1.\) Now to find the Laplace transform of \(f(t)\) we will split the integral based on the piece-wise definition of the function \begin{align*} {\cal L} \big\{ f(t) \big\} = & \int _0^\infty e^{-st} \, f(t)\,dt \\ = & \int _0^1 e^{-st} \, f(t)\,dt + \int _1^\infty e^{-st} \, f(t)\,dt \\ = & \int _0^1 e^{-st} \cdot 1 \,dt + \int _1^\infty e^{-st} \cdot -3e^{-t}\,dt \\ = & \int _0^1 e^{-st} \,dt -3 \int _1^\infty e^{-(s+1)t} \,dt \\ \end{align*} So now we have two integrals to evaluate. The first integrand is the same one we computed in Example 8.1.1 but this one is not an improper integral. When it was improper we considered cases and found we had to require \(s>0\) since it diverges otherwise. For this one it always converges, so we can allow \(s\) to be any value. So for cases we will consider \(s=0\) first, and the integral becomes \begin{align*} \int _0^1 e^{0} \,dt = \int _0^1 \,dt = t\Big|_0^{1}=1-0=1 \\ \end{align*} And the other case is simply \(s \ne 0\) and we already found the antiderivative in Example 8.1.1 so we will just evaluate it at these different limits of integration \begin{align*} \int _0^1 e^{-st} \,dt = -\frac{1}{s} \big[ e^{-st} \big] \Big|_{0}^{1} = -\frac{1}{s} \big[ e^{-s} - e^{0} \big] = \frac{1-e^{-s}}{s} \end{align*} Thus we have \[ \int _0^1e^{-st}\,dt= \left \{ \begin {array}{cl} \displaystyle {{1-e^{-s}\over s}}&\quad s\ne 0\\ 1&\quad s=0 \end {array} \right. \] And now we have a new improper integral to evaluate \begin{align*} -3\int _1^\infty e^{-(s+1)t} \,dt = & -3 \cdot -\frac{1}{s+1}\int _1^\infty e^{\overset{w}{\overbrace{-(s+1)t}}} \,\, \overset{dw}{\overbrace{\big(-(s+1)dt\big)}} \\ = & \frac{3}{s+1} \lim_{x \to \infty} \left[ e^{-(s+1)t} \right] \Bigg|_{t=1}^{t=x} \\ = & \frac{3}{s+1} \lim_{x \to \infty} \left[ e^{-(s+1)x} - e^{-(s+1)1} \right] \\ = & \frac{3}{s+1} \left[ e^{-\infty} - e^{-(s+1)} \right] \\ = & -\frac{3e^{-(s+1)}}{s+1} \quad\quad\quad s>-1 \end{align*} Now substituting back in, the Laplace transform \( {\cal L} \big\{ f(t) \big\} \) is the sum of these two integrals \begin{align*} F(s) = {\cal L} \big\{ f(t) \big\} = & \underset{\left \{ \begin {array}{cl} {{1-e^{-s}\over s}}&\, s\ne 0\\ 1&\, s=0 \end {array} \right. }{\underbrace{\int _0^1 e^{-st} \,dt}} -3 \underset{ \left \{ \begin {array}{cl} -\frac{3e^{-(s+1)}}{s+1}&\, s > -1, s\ne 0 \\ -\frac{3}{e}&\, s=0 \end {array} \right. }{\underbrace{\int _1^\infty e^{-(s+1)t} \,dt}} \\ \end{align*} Combing them we obtain
\[ F(s)= \left \{ \begin {array}{rl} \displaystyle {{1-e^{-s}\over s}-3{e^{-(s+1)}\over s+1}}&s>-1,\,s\ne 0\\ \displaystyle {1-{3\over e}} & s=0 \end {array} \right. \]



We stated earlier that \[ \int _0^\infty e^{-st} e^{t^2} dt=\infty \] for all \(s\), so Theorem 8.1.6 implies that \(f(t)=e^{t^2}\) is not of exponential order, since \[ \lim _{t\to \infty } {e^{t^2}\over Me^{s_0t}}=\lim _{t\to \infty } {1\over M} e^{t^2-s_0t}=\infty \] so \[ e^{t^2}>Me^{s_0t} \] for sufficiently large values of \(t\), for any choice of \(M\) and \(s_{0}\) (Exercise 3).
