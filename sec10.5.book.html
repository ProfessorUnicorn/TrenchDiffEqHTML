10.5 Constant Coefficient Homogeneous Systems II
Repeated Eigenvalues
We found in Section 10.4 that if an \(n\times n\) constant matrix \({\bf A }\) has \(n\) real eigenvalues \( {r} _1\), \( {r} _2\), …, \( {r} _n\) (which need not be distinct) with associated linearly independent eigenvectors \({\bf \vec{u}}_1\), \({\bf \vec{u}}_2\), …, \({\bf \vec{u}}_n\), then the general solution of \(\dfrac{d{\bf \vec{y}}}{dt}={\bf A } \,{\bf \vec{y}}\) is given by \[ {\bf \vec{y}}=c_1{\bf \vec{u}}_1e^{ {r} _1t}+c_2{\bf \vec{u}}_2e^{ {r} _2 t} +\cdots +c_n{\bf \vec{u}}_ne^{ {r} _n t} \] In this section we consider the case where \({\bf A }\) has \(n\) real eigenvalues, but does not have \(n\) linearly independent eigenvectors. It is shown in linear algebra that this occurs if and only if \({\bf A }\) has at least one eigenvalue of multiplicity \(r>1\) such that the associated eigenspace has dimension less than \(r\). In this case \({\bf A }\) is said to be defective. Since it’s beyond the scope of this book to give a complete analysis of systems with defective coefficient matrices, we will restrict our attention to some commonly occurring special cases.


Show that the system \begin {equation} \label {eq:10.5.1} \dfrac{d{\bf \vec{y}}}{dt}=\twobytwo {11}{-25}4{-9}{\bf \vec{y}} \end {equation} does not have a fundamental set of solutions of the form \(\{{\bf \vec{u}}_1e^{ {r} _1t},{\bf \vec{u}}_2e^{ {r} _2t}\}\), where \( {r} _1\) and \( {r} _2\) are eigenvalues of the coefficient matrix \({\bf A }\) of (10.5.1) and \({\bf \vec{u}}_1\), and \({\bf \vec{u}}_2\) are associated linearly independent eigenvectors.
Solution
  1. To solve (10.5.1) we will seek solutions of the form \[ {\bf \vec{y}} = {\bf \vec{u}} e^{rt} \] Where \({\bf \vec{u}} \) is some constant vector to be determined and \(r\) will be determined by the characteristic equation. Now differentiating \({\bf \vec{y}}\) we obtain \[ \frac{d{\bf \vec{y}}}{dt} = r{\bf \vec{u}} e^{rt} \] Substituting into (10.5.1) \begin{align*} \dfrac{d{\bf \vec{y}}}{dt} & =\twobytwo {11}{-25}4{-9} {\bf \vec{y}} \\ \\ r{\bf \vec{u}} e^{rt} & =\twobytwo {11}{-25}4{-9}{\bf \vec{u}} e^{rt} \\ \\ r{\bf \vec{u}} & =\twobytwo {11}{-25}4{-9}{\bf \vec{u}} \\ \\ {\bf \vec{0}} & =\twobytwo {11}{-25}4{-9} {\bf \vec{u}} - r{\bf \vec{u}} \\ \\ {\bf \vec{0}} & =\left[\twobytwo {11}{-25}4{-9} - r\right] {\bf \vec{u}} \\ \\ \end{align*}
  2. Rewriting \(r\) in matrix form \(r{\bf {I}}\) multiplied by the identity matrix: \begin{align} {\bf \vec{0}} & =\left[\twobytwo {11}{-25}4{-9} - r {\bf {I}} \right] {\bf \vec{u}} \nonumber \\ \nonumber \\ {\bf \vec{0}} & =\left[\twobytwo {11}{-25}4{-9} - r \twobytwo 1001 \right] {\bf \vec{u}} \nonumber \\ \nonumber \\ {\bf \vec{0}} & =\twobytwo {11-r}{-25}{4}{-9-r} {\bf \vec{u}} \\ \nonumber \\ \twocol 00 & =\twobytwo {11-r}{-25}4{-9-r}\twocol {u_1}{u_2} \nonumber\\ \nonumber\\ \end{align} The eigenvalue equation (10.5.2) shows the relationship between the eigenvalue \(r\) and associated eigenvector \({\bf \vec{u}} \) for the system of differential equations (10.5.1) and since we'll be using it to solve for the eigenvector components \(u_1\) and \(u_2\), we'll write it in augmented matrix form as the following \begin{align} \twobytwo {11-r}{-25}{4}{-9-r} {\bf \vec{u}} & = {\bf \vec{0}} \nonumber \\ \nonumber \\ \twobytwo {11-r}{-25}{4}{-9-r} \twocol{u_1}{u_2} & = \twocol{0}{0} \nonumber \\ \nonumber \\ \left(\begin{array}{cc|c} 11-r & -25 & 0 \\ 4 & -9-r & 0 \end{array}\right) \end{align}
  3. We can show that a system of equations has only the trivial solution by showing that the determinant of the coefficient matrix is nonzero. As we are only interested in nontrivial solutions we will require that the determinant of the coefficient matrix equals zero: \begin{align*} \det \left( \begin{array}{ccc} 11-r & -25 \\ 4 & -9-r \\ \end{array} \right) & =0 \\ \\ (11-r)(-9-r)+100 & =0 \\ (r^2-2r-99)+100 & =0 \\ \underset{p(r)}{\underbrace{r^2-2r+1}} & =0 \\ \end{align*} So we have our characteristic polynomial \(p(r)=r^2-2r+1\) and characteristic equation \(r^2-2r+1=0\) which we can solve by factoring \begin{align*} r^2-2r+1 & =0 \\ (r-1)(r-1) & =0 \\ (r-1)^2 & =0 \\ r-1 & =0 \\ r & =1 \\ r_1 & =1 \quad r_2 = 1 \\ \end{align*}
  4. We have the eigenvalues \(r_1= 1\) and \(r_2 = \,1\) and using what we learned in Section 10.4 we should be able to write the solutions to the system as \begin{align*} {\bf \vec{y}_1} & = {\bf \vec{u}_1} e^{r_1t}= {\bf \vec{u}_1} e^{t} \quad\text{and}\quad {\bf \vec{y}_2} = {\bf \vec{u}_2} e^{r_2t} ={\bf \vec{u}_2} e^{t} \\ \end{align*} Since we already have the eigenvalues we now need to find the associated eigenvectors \({\bf \vec{u}_1}\) and \({\bf \vec{u}_2}\) using row reduction on the augmented matrix form of the eigenvalue equation (10.5.2) \begin{align*} \twobytwo {11-r_1}{-25}{4}{-9-r_1} {\bf \vec{u}}_1 & = {\bf \vec{0}} \nonumber \\ \nonumber \\ \left(\begin{array}{cc|c} 11-1 & -25 & 0 \\ 4 & -9-1 & 0 \end{array}\right) \\ \\ \left(\begin{array}{cc|c} 10 & -25 & 0 \\ 4 & -10 & 0 \end{array}\right) \\ \\ \underset{5R_2}{\overset{2R_1}{\longrightarrow}} \left(\begin{array}{cc|c} 20 & -50 & 0 \\ 20 & -50 & 0 \end{array}\right) \\ \\ \underset{\frac{1}{10}R_2}{\overset{R_2-R_1}{\longrightarrow}} \left(\begin{array}{cc|c} 2 & -5 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & 0 \end{array}\right) \\ \\ \end{align*} The system simplifies down to the single equation \[2u_1 - 5u_2 = 0 \] Since we have only 1 equation with 2 unknowns, we have 1 free variable, so we'll let \(u_2=c\) where \(c\) is any number. Then we can solve for \(u_1\) \begin{align*} 2u_1 - 5u_2 & = 0 \\ 2u_1 - 5c & = 0 \\ 2u_1 & = 5c \\ u_1 & = \frac{5}{2}c \\ \end{align*}
  5. Therefore we have determined the eigenvector \({\bf \vec{u}_1}\) \[ {\bf \vec{u}_1} =\twocol {u_1}{u_2} =\twocol {\frac{5}{2}c}{c} =c\twocol {\frac{5}{2}}{1} \] Since \(c\) can be any number we'll select \(c=2\) for a simple eigenvector \begin{align*} {\bf \vec{u}_1}=\twocol {5}{2} \end{align*} With the eigenvalue-eigenvector pair \(r_1\) and \({\bf \vec{u}_1}\) we have one of the solutions to our original equation \begin{align*} {\bf \vec{y}_1} = {\bf \vec{u}_1}e^{r_1 t} = \twocol {5}{2}e^{t} \end{align*}
    Normally at this point we would repeat this procedure with our second eigenvalue, but in this case our second eigenvalue is the same \(r_1=1\) and \(r_2=1\) since we had a repeated root. Thus repeating this procedure would not yield any other linearly independent eigenvectors. Recall Example 10.4.3 where we previously had a repeated eigenvalue. In this case it worked out since our eigenspace was two-dimensional so it contained two linearly independent vectors due to the presence of two free variables in the system, so a repeated root previously did not cause any issue. In this case, our eigenspace is one-dimensional so all eigenvectors of \({\bf A }\) are scalar multiples of the single eigenvector \({\bf \vec{u}}_1=<5,2>\) meaning we are not able to find a second solution in this form \begin{align*} {\bf \vec{y}_2} = {\bf \vec{u}_2} e^{r_2t} ={\bf \vec{u}_2} e^{t} \\ \end{align*}


From Example 10.5.1, we know that all scalar multiples of \({\bf \vec{y}}_1=\displaystyle {\twocol 52}e^t\) are solutions of (10.5.1); however, to find the general solution we must find a second solution \({\bf \vec{y}}_2\) such that \(\{{\bf \vec{y}}_1,{\bf \vec{y}}_2\}\) is linearly independent. Based on the procedure for solving a constant coefficient scalar equation in the case where the characteristic polynomial has a repeated root (Section 5.3), we might expect to obtain a second solution of (10.5.1) by multiplying the first solution by \(t\) \[ {\bf \vec{y}}_1=\displaystyle {\twocol 52}e^t \quad\text{then}\quad {\bf \vec{y}}_2= {\bf \vec{y}}_1 t = \displaystyle {\twocol 52}te^t \] Now we'll check if \({\bf \vec{y}}_2=\displaystyle {\twocol 52}te^t\) is a solution of (10.5.1) by substituting and differentiating \begin {align*} \dfrac{d{\bf \vec{y}_2}}{dt} & \overset{?}{=}\twobytwo {11}{-25}4{-9}{\bf \vec{y}_2} \\ \twocol 52\dfrac{d}{dt}\Big(te^t\Big) & \overset{?}{=}\twobytwo {11}{-25}4{-9}\twocol 52 te^t \\ \twocol 52 \Big(te^t + e^t\Big) & \overset{?}{=}\twocol {55-50}{20-18} te^t \\ \twocol {5}{2} te^t + \twocol {5}{2} e^t & \overset{?}{=}\twocol {5}{2} te^t \\ \twocol {5}{2} e^t & \overset{}{\ne} 0 \\ \end {align*} Therefore \({\bf \vec{y}}_2=\displaystyle {\twocol 52}te^t\) is not a solution of (10.5.1) so we still do not have a fundamental set of solutions. The next theorem shows how to find a second solution in this situation.
Theorem 10.5.1 Consider the homogeneous system (10.4.1) where \({\bf A}\) is an \(n\times n\) constant matrix \begin {equation*} \dfrac{d{\bf \vec{y}}}{dt}={\bf A}{\bf \vec{y}} \end {equation*} Suppose the matrix \({\bf A }\) has a repeated eigenvalue and the associated eigenspace is one-dimensional so that all \( {r}_1\)-eigenvectors of \({\bf A }\) are scalar multiples of a single eigenvector \({\bf \vec{u}}_1\) \[ ({\bf A}-{r}_1{\bf I}){\bf \vec{u}}_1= {\bf \vec{0}} \] Then the characteristic equation only provides one solution for the system \[ {\bf \vec{y}}_1 = {\bf \vec{u}}_1e^{{r}_1 t} \] We can find a second linearly independent solution given by \begin {equation} \label {eq:10.5.3} {\bf \vec{y}}_2={\bf \vec{u}}_2e^{ {r}_1t}+{\bf \vec{u}}_1 te^{ {r}_1t} \end {equation} Where \({\bf \vec{u}}_2\) is a constant vector given by \begin {equation}\label {eq:10.5.4} ({\bf A}- {r}_1{\bf I}){\bf \vec{u}}_2={\bf \vec{u}}_1 \end {equation} Thus \(\left\{{\bf \vec{y}}_1 , {\bf \vec{y}}_2 \right\} = \left\{ {\bf \vec{u}}_1e^{{r}_1 t}, {\bf \vec{u}}_2e^{ {r}_1t}+{\bf \vec{u}}_1 te^{ {r}_1t} \right\} \) form a fundamental set for the system \(\dfrac{d{\bf \vec{y}}}{dt}={\bf A}{\bf \vec{y}}\)
It is beyond the scope of this book to prove that there’s a vector \({\bf \vec{u}}_2\) satisfying (10.5.4), and since \(\det ({\bf A}-{r}_1 {\bf I}) = 0\) it is a bit tricky to show. We’ll just assume \({\bf \vec{u}}_2\) exists and verify the rest of the theorem.
We already know that \({\bf \vec{y}}_1 = {\bf \vec{u}}_1e^{{r}_1 t}\) is a solution so we have \begin{align*} \dfrac{d{\bf \vec{y}}_1}{dt}={\bf A}{\bf \vec{y}}_1 & \Longrightarrow \dfrac{d}{dt}\Big({\bf \vec{u}}_1e^{{r}_1 t}\Big)={\bf A} {\bf \vec{u}}_1e^{{r}_1 t}\\ & \Longrightarrow {r}_1 {\bf \vec{u}}_1e^{{r}_1 t} ={\bf A} {\bf \vec{u}}_1e^{{r}_1 t} \\ & \Longrightarrow 0 ={\bf A} {\bf \vec{u}}_1e^{{r}_1 t} - {r}_1 {\bf \vec{u}}_1e^{{r}_1 t}\\ & \Longrightarrow 0 =\Big( {\bf A} - {r}_1 {\bf I} \Big){\bf \vec{u}}_1e^{{r}_1 t}\\ \end{align*}
We'll now verify that \({\bf \vec{y}}_2 = {\bf \vec{u}}_2e^{ {r}_1t}+{\bf \vec{u}}_1 te^{ {r}_1t}\) is also a solution, where \({\bf \vec{u}}_2\) is of the form given in (10.5.4). We'll start by substituting \({\bf \vec{y}}_2\) into the system \begin{align*} \dfrac{d{\bf \vec{y}}_2}{dt} & ={\bf A}{\bf \vec{y}}_2 \\ \frac{d}{dt}\Big( {\bf \vec{u}}_2e^{ {r}_1t}+{\bf \vec{u}}_1 te^{ {r}_1t} \Big) & = {\bf A} \Big( {\bf \vec{u}}_2e^{ {r}_1t}+{\bf \vec{u}}_1 te^{ {r}_1t} \Big) \\ {\bf \vec{u}}_2 \frac{d}{dt} \Big( e^{ {r}_1t} \Big) +{\bf \vec{u}}_1 \frac{d}{dt} \Big( te^{ {r}_1t} \Big) & = {\bf A} {\bf \vec{u}}_2e^{ {r}_1t} + {\bf A} {\bf \vec{u}}_1 te^{ {r}_1t} \\ {r}_1 {\bf \vec{u}}_2 e^{ {r}_1t} +{\bf \vec{u}}_1 \Big( {r}_1 te^{ {r}_1t} + e^{ {r}_1t} \Big) & = {\bf A} {\bf \vec{u}}_2e^{ {r}_1t} + {\bf A} {\bf \vec{u}}_1 te^{ {r}_1t} \\ r_1{\bf \vec{u}}_2 e^{ {r}_1t} + {r}_1 {\bf \vec{u}}_1 te^{ {r}_1t} + {\bf \vec{u}}_1 e^{ {r}_1t} & = {\bf A} {\bf \vec{u}}_2e^{ {r}_1t} + {\bf A} {\bf \vec{u}}_1 te^{ {r}_1t} \\ r_1{\bf \vec{u}}_2 e^{ {r}_1t} + {\bf \vec{u}}_1 e^{ {r}_1t} & = {\bf A} {\bf \vec{u}}_2e^{ {r}_1t} + \Big( {\bf A} {\bf \vec{u}}_1 te^{ {r}_1t} - {r}_1 {\bf \vec{u}}_1 te^{ {r}_1t} \Big) \\ r_1{\bf \vec{u}}_2 e^{ {r}_1t} + {\bf \vec{u}}_1 e^{ {r}_1t} & = {\bf A} {\bf \vec{u}}_2e^{ {r}_1t} + t \underset{0}{\underbrace{\Big( {\bf A} - {r}_1 {\bf I} \Big){\bf \vec{u}}_1e^{{r}_1 t}}} \\ r_1{\bf \vec{u}}_2 e^{ {r}_1t} + {\bf \vec{u}}_1 e^{ {r}_1t} & = {\bf A} {\bf \vec{u}}_2e^{ {r}_1t} \\ {\bf \vec{u}}_1 e^{ {r}_1t} & = {\bf A} {\bf \vec{u}}_2e^{ {r}_1t} - r_1{\bf \vec{u}}_2 e^{ {r}_1t} \\ {\bf \vec{u}}_1 e^{ {r}_1t} & = \Big( {\bf A} - {r}_1 {\bf I} \Big){\bf \vec{u}}_2e^{{r}_1 t} \\ {\bf \vec{u}}_1 & = \Big( {\bf A} - {r}_1 {\bf I} \Big){\bf \vec{u}}_2 \quad \checkmark \\ \\ \\ \end{align*}
To show that \({\bf \vec{y}}_1\) and \({\bf \vec{y}}_2\) are linearly independent, suppose \(c_1\) and \(c_2\) are constants such that \begin {equation} \label {eq:10.5.5} c_1{\bf \vec{y}}_1+c_2{\bf \vec{y}}_2=c_1{\bf \vec{u}}_1e^{ {r} _1t}+c_2({\bf \vec{u}}_2e^{ {r}_1t} +{\bf \vec{u}}_1te^{ {r} _1t})={\bf \vec{0}} \end {equation} We must show that \(c_1=c_2=0\). Multiplying (10.5.6) by \(e^{- {r} _1t}\) shows that \begin {equation*} c_1{\bf \vec{u}}_1+c_2({\bf \vec{u}}_2 +{\bf \vec{u}}_1t)={\bf \vec{0}} \end {equation*} By differentiating this with respect to \(t\), we have that \(c_2{\bf \vec{u}}_1={\bf \vec{0}}\), which implies \(c_2=0\), because \({\bf \vec{u}}_1\ne {\bf \vec{0}}\). Substituting \(c_2=0\) into (10.5.6) yields \(c_1{\bf \vec{u}}_1={\bf 0}\), which implies that \(c_1=0\), again because \({\bf \vec{u}}_1\ne {\bf \vec{0}}\)


Use Theorem 10.5.1 to find the general solution of the system considered in Example 10.5.1 \begin {equation} \label {eq:10.5.6} \dfrac{d{\bf \vec{y}}}{dt}=\twobytwo {11}{-25}4{-9}{\bf \vec{y}} \end {equation}
Solution
  1. In Example 10.5.1 we found that \( {r} _1=1\) is an eigenvalue of multiplicity \(2\) of the coefficient matrix \({\bf A }\) in (10.5.7), and that all of the eigenvectors of \({\bf A }\) are multiples of \[ {\bf \vec{u}}_1=\twocol 52\] We already found one solution of (10.5.7) using the characteristic equation in Example 10.5.1 \[ {\bf \vec{y}}_1={\bf \vec{u}}_1 e^t=\twocol 52e^t \] From Theorem 10.5.1, a second solution is given by \({\bf \vec{y}}_2={\bf \vec{u}}_2 e^t+{\bf \vec{u}}_1te^t\), where \(({\bf A}-r_1{\bf I}){\bf \vec{u}_2}={\bf \vec{u}}_1\) \begin{align*} \left[\twobytwo {11}{-25}4{-9} - r_1 \twobytwo 1001 \right] {\bf \vec{u}}_2 & = {\bf \vec{u}}_1 \\ \\ \left[\twobytwo {11}{-25}4{-9} - \twobytwo 1001 \right] {\bf \vec{u}}_2 & = {\bf \vec{u}}_1 \\ \\ \twobytwo {11-1}{-25}{4}{-9-1} {\bf \vec{u}}_2 & = \twocol 52 \\ \\ \twobytwo {10}{-25}4{-10}\twocol {u_1}{u_2} & = \twocol 52 \\ \\ \end{align*} The augmented matrix of this system is \begin{align*} \left ( \begin {array}{rr|r} 10&-25& 5\\ 4&-10& 2 \end {array} \right ) \underset{\frac{1}{2}R_2}{\overset{\frac{1}{5}R_1}{\longrightarrow}} \left ( \begin {array}{rr|r} 2&-5& 1\\ 2&-5& 1 \end {array} \right ) \end{align*} The system simplifies down to the single equation \[2u_1 - 5u_2 = 1 \]
  2. So again we have a free variable, so we'll let \(u_2=c\) where \(c\) is any number. Then we can solve for \(u_1\) in terms of \(c\) \begin{align*} 2u_1 - 5u_2 & = 1 \\ 2u_1 - 5c & = 1 \\ 2u_1 & = 1 + 5c \\ u_1 & = \frac{1}{2} + \frac{5}{2}c \\ \end{align*} Therefore we have determined the vector \({\bf \vec{u}_2}\) \[ {\bf \vec{u}_2} =\twocol {u_1}{u_2} =\twocol {\frac{1}{2} + \frac{5}{2}c}{c} =\twocol {\frac{1}{2}}{0} + \twocol {\frac{5}{2}c}{c} =\frac{1}{2} \twocol {1}{0} + c\twocol {\frac{5}{2}}{1} \] Since \(c\) can be any number we'll select \(c=0\) for a simple vector \begin{align*} {\bf \vec{u}_2}=\frac{1}{2} \twocol {1}{0} \end{align*} Therefore we have a second linearly independent solution \begin{align*} {\bf \vec{y}}_2 & ={\bf \vec{u}}_2 e^t+{\bf \vec{u}}_1te^t = \frac{1}{2} \twocol {1}{0} e^t + \twocol {5}{2} te^t \\ \end{align*} Thus we have a fundamental set of solutions of (10.5.6) \[ \left\{ {\bf \vec{y}}_1, {\bf \vec{y}}_2 \right\} = \Bigg\{ \twocol {5}{2} e^t , \frac{1}{2} \twocol {1}{0} e^t + \twocol {5}{2} te^t \Bigg\} \] Therefore the general solution is \[ {\bf \vec{y}} = c_1 {\bf \vec{y}}_1 + c_2 {\bf \vec{y}}_2 = c_1\twocol 52e^t+c_2\Bigg[\twocol 10{e^t\over 2}+\twocol 52te^t\Bigg] \]
    Note that choosing the arbitrary constant \(c_2\) to be nonzero is equivalent to adding a scalar multiple of \({\bf \vec{y}}_1\) to the second solution \({\bf \vec{y}}_2\) .


Find the general solution of \begin {equation} \label {eq:10.5.7} \dfrac{d{\bf \vec{y}}}{dt}=\threebythree 34{-10}21{-2}22{-5} {\bf \vec{y}} \end {equation}
Solution
  1. To solve (10.5.7) we will seek solutions of the form \[ {\bf \vec{y}} = {\bf \vec{u}} e^{rt} \] Where \({\bf \vec{u}} \) is some constant vector to be determined and \(r\) will be determined by the characteristic equation. Now differentiating \({\bf \vec{y}}\) we obtain \[ \frac{d{\bf \vec{y}}}{dt} = r{\bf \vec{u}} e^{rt} \] Substituting into (10.5.7) \begin{align*} \dfrac{d{\bf \vec{y}}}{dt} & = \left ( \begin {array}{rrr} 3& 4& -10 \\ 2& 1& -2\\ 2& 2& -5 \end {array} \right ) {\bf \vec{y}} \\ \\ r{\bf \vec{u}} e^{rt} & = \left ( \begin {array}{rrr} 3& 4& -10 \\ 2& 1& -2\\ 2& 2& -5 \end {array} \right ) {\bf \vec{u}} e^{rt} \\ \\ r{\bf \vec{u}} & = \left ( \begin {array}{rrr} 3& 4& -10 \\ 2& 1& -2\\ 2& 2& -5 \end {array} \right ) {\bf \vec{u}} \\ \\ {\bf \vec{0}} & = \left ( \begin {array}{rrr} 3& 4& -10 \\ 2& 1& -2\\ 2& 2& -5 \end {array} \right ) {\bf \vec{u}} - r{\bf \vec{u}} \\ \\ {\bf \vec{0}} & =\left[ \left ( \begin {array}{rrr} 3& 4& -10 \\ 2& 1& -2\\ 2& 2& -5 \end {array} \right ) - r \right] {\bf \vec{u}} \\ \\ \end{align*}
  2. Geometric Properties of Solutions when \(n=2\)
We’ll now consider the geometric properties of solutions of a \(2\times 2\) constant coefficient system \begin {equation} \label {eq:10.5.19} \twocol {\dfrac{dy_1}{dt}}{\dfrac{dy_2}{dt}}=\left [ \begin {array}{cc} a_{11}&a_{12}\\ a_{21}&a_{22} \end {array} \right ]\twocol {y_1}{y_2} \end {equation} under the assumptions of this section; that is, when the matrix \[ A=\left [ \begin {array}{cc} a_{11}&a_{12}\\ a_{21}&a_{22} \end {array} \right ] \] has a repeated eigenvalue \( {r}_1\) and the associated eigenspace is one-dimensional. In this case we know from Theorem 10.5.1 that the general solution of (10.5.19) is \begin {equation} \label {eq:10.5.20} {\bf \vec{y}}=c_1{\bf \vec{u}}e^{ {r} _1t}+c_2({\bf \vec{u}}e^{ {r} _1t}+{\bf \vec{u}}te^{ {r} _1t}) \end {equation} where \(\bf x\) is an eigenvector of \({\bf A }\) and \(\bf u\) is any one of the infinitely many solutions of \begin {equation} \label {eq:10.5.21} (A- {r} _1I){\bf \vec{u}}={\bf \vec{u}} \end {equation} We assume that \( {r} _1\ne 0\).
Let \(L\) denote the line through the origin parallel to \(\bf x\). By ahalf-lineof \(L\) we mean either of the rays obtained by removing the origin from \(L\). Eqn. (10.5.20) is a parametric equation of the half-line of \(L\) in the direction of \(\bf x\) if \(c_1>0\), or of the half-line of \(L\) in the direction of \(-{\bf \vec{u}}\) if \(c_1<0\). The origin is the trajectory of the trivial solution \({\bf \vec{y}}\equiv {\bf 0}\).
Henceforth, we assume that \(c_2\ne 0\). In this case, the trajectory of (10.5.20) can’t intersect \(L\), since every point of \(L\) is on a trajectory obtained by setting \(c_2=0\). Therefore the trajectory of (10.5.20) must lie entirely in one of the open half-planes bounded by \(L\), but does not contain any point on \(L\). Since the initial point \((y_1(0),y_2(0))\) defined by \({\bf \vec{y}}(0)=c_1{\bf \vec{u}}_1+c_2{\bf \vec{u}}\) is on the trajectory, we can determine which half-plane contains the trajectory from the sign of \(c_2\), as shown in Figure 10.5.1. For convenience we’ll call the half-plane where \(c_2>0\) thepositive half-plane. Similarly, the-half plane where \(c_2<0\) is thenegative half-plane. You should convince yourself (Exercise 35) that even though there are infinitely many vectors \(\bf u\) that satisfy (10.5.21), they all define the same positive and negative half-planes. In the figures simply regard \(\bf u\) as an arrow pointing to the positive half-plane, since wen’t attempted to give \(\bf u\) its proper length or direction in comparison with \(\bf x\). For our purposes here, only the relative orientation of \(\bf x\) and \(\bf u\) is important; that is, whether the positive half-plane is to the right of an observer facing the direction of \(\bf x\) (as in Figures 10.5.2 and 10.5.5), or to the left of the observer (as in Figures 10.5.3 and 10.5.4).
Multiplying (10.5.20) by \(e^{- {r} _1t}\) yields \[ e^{- {r} _1t}{\bf \vec{y}}(t)=c_1{\bf \vec{u}}+c_2{\bf \vec{u}}+c_2t {\bf \vec{u}}\] Since the last term on the right is dominant when \(|t|\) is large, this provides the following information on the direction of \({\bf \vec{y}}(t)\):
  1. Along trajectories in the positive half-plane (\(c_2>0\)), the direction of \({\bf \vec{y}}(t)\) approaches the direction of \(\bf x\) as \(t\to \infty \) and the direction of \(-{\bf \vec{u}}\) as \(t\to -\infty \).
  2. Along trajectories in the negative half-plane (\(c_2<0\)), the direction of \({\bf \vec{y}}(t)\) approaches the direction of \(-{\bf \vec{u}}\) as \(t\to \infty \) and the direction of \(\bf x\) as \(t\to -\infty \).
Since \[ \lim _{t\to \infty }\|{\bf \vec{y}}(t)\|=\infty \quad \mbox{ and } \quad \lim _{t\to -\infty }{\bf \vec{y}}(t)={\bf 0}\quad \mbox{ if } \quad {r} _1>0\] or \[ \lim _{t-\to \infty }\|{\bf \vec{y}}(t)\|=\infty \quad \mbox{ and } \quad \lim _{t\to \infty }{\bf \vec{y}}(t)={\bf 0}\quad \mbox{ if } \quad {r} _1<0\] there are four possible patterns for the trajectories of (10.5.19), depending upon the signs of \(c_2\) and \( {r} _1\). Figures 10.5.2-10.5.5 illustrate these patterns, and reveal the following principle:
If \( {r} _1\) and \(c_2\) have the same sign then the direction of the trajectory approaches the direction of \(-{\bf \vec{u}}\) as \(\|{\bf \vec{y}} \|\to 0\) and the direction of \(\bf x\) as \(\|{\bf \vec{y}}\|\to \infty \). If \( {r} _1\) and \(c_2\) have opposite signs then the direction of the trajectory approaches the direction of \(\bf x\) as \(\|{\bf \vec{y}} \|\to 0\) and the direction of \(-{\bf \vec{u}}\) as \(\|{\bf \vec{y}}\|\to \infty \).
