9.2 Higher Order Constant Coefficient Homogeneous Equations
Higher Order Constant Coefficient Homogeneous Equations
If \(a_0\), \(a_1\), …, \(a_n\) are constants and \(a_0\ne 0\), then \[ a_0 \frac{d^n y}{dx^n} + a_1 \frac{d^{n-1} y}{dx^{n-1}} + \cdots + a_{n-1} \frac{d y}{dx} + a_n y = F(x) \] is said to be a constant coefficient equation. In this section we consider the homogeneous constant coefficient equation \begin {equation} \label {eq:9.2.1} a_0 \frac{d^n y}{dx^n} + a_1 \frac{d^{n-1} y}{dx^{n-1}} + \cdots + a_{n-1} \frac{d y}{dx} + a_n y =0 \end {equation} Since (9.2.1) is normal on \((-\infty ,\infty )\), then the theorems in Section 9.1 all apply with \((a,b)=(-\infty ,\infty )\)
We solve homogeneous constant coefficient equations the same way we did in Section 5.2, by seeking solutions of the form \[ y=e^{rx} \] Differentiating \(y=e^{rx}\) yields \[ \frac{dy}{dx}=re^{rx} \Longrightarrow \frac{d^2y}{dx^2}=r^2e^{rx} \dots \Longrightarrow \frac{d^ny}{dx^n}=r^ne^{rx} \] Substituting in to (9.2.1) we obtain the equation \begin {align*} a_0 r^ne^{rx} + a_1 r^{n-1}e^{rx} + \cdots + a_{n-1} re^{rx} + a_n e^{rx} & = 0 \\ \end {align*} Factoring out the exponential \begin {align*} \underset{p(r)}{\underbrace{\Big( a_0 r^n + a_1 r^{n-1} + \cdots + a_{n-1} r + a_n \Big)}} e^{rx} & = 0 \\ \end {align*} we obtain the characteristic polynomial \begin {equation} \label {eq:9.2.2} p(r)=a_0r^n+a_1r^{n-1}+\cdots +a_n \end {equation} We found in Section 5.2 that when \(n=2\) the solutions of the homogeneous constant coefficient equation (9.2.1) are determined by the zeros of the characteristic polynomial. This is also true when \(n>2\), but the situation is more complicated in this case.
In the simplest case where the characteristic polynomial \(p(r)\) factors into \(n\) distinct linear factors \[ p(r)=(r-r_1)(r-r_2)\dots(r-r_n) \] Then the characteristic equation has \(n\) distinct real zeros \begin {align*} \overset{p(r)}{\overbrace{(r-r_1)(r-r_2)\dots(r-r_n)}} & = 0 \\ r=r_1 \quad r=r_2 \quad \dots \quad r=r_n & \\ \end {align*} So we obtain \(n\) distinct solutions \[ y_1=e^{r_1x} \quad y_2=e^{r_2x} \dots \quad y_n=e^{r_nx}\] It can be shown that the Wronskian of \(\{e^{r_1x},e^{r_2x},\dots ,e^{r_nx}\}\) is nonzero if \(r_1\), \(r_2\), …, \(r_n\) are distinct; hence, \(\{e^{r_1x},e^{r_2x},\dots ,e^{r_nx}\}\) is a fundamental set of solutions of in this case.
  1. Find the general solution of \begin {equation} \label {eq:9.2.3} \frac{d^3 y}{dx^3}-6\frac{d^2 y}{dx^2}+11\frac{d y}{dx}-6y=0 \end {equation}
  3. Solve the IVP \begin {align} \label {eq:9.2.4} \frac{d^3 y}{dx^3}-6\frac{d^2 y}{dx^2}+11\frac{d y}{dx}-6y &= 0 \\ y(0)=4 \quad \left. \frac{d y}{dx}\right|_0 = 5 \quad \left. \frac{d^2 y}{dx^2}\right|_0 & = 9 \nonumber \end {align}
Solution
  1. Seeking solutions of the form \[ y=e^{rx} \] Differentiating \(y=e^{rx}\) three times yields \[ \frac{dy}{dx}=re^{rx} \Longrightarrow \frac{d^2y}{dx^2}=r^2e^{rx} \Longrightarrow \frac{d^3y}{dx^3}=r^3e^{rx} \] Substituting in to (9.2.3) we obtain the equation \begin {equation*} r^3e^{rx}-6r^2e^{rx}+11re^{rx}-6e^{rx}=0 \end {equation*} Factoring out the exponential \begin {equation*} \Big( r^3-6r^2+11r-6 \Big) e^{rx}=0 \end {equation*} We now need to solve the characteristic equation of the characteristic polynomial equal to zero \begin {equation*} \underset{p(r)}{\underbrace{r^3-6r^2+11r-6}}=0 \end {equation*} In Section 5.2 we simply factored and applied the quadratic formula to find the roots. However, with third or higher order, there isn't always a simple formula that works and sometimes factoring techniques won't work either, as in this case, so we will apply the rational roots theorem from precalculus.
    The possible rational roots come from the ratio of the factors of the constant and the factors of the leading coefficient. The constant term is \(-6\) with possible factors of \(\pm \big(1,2,3,6\big)\) and the leading term is \(r^3\) with coefficient \(1\) with possible factors of \(\pm 1\) which has no impact on the ratio thus the possible rational roots to consider are \[\pm \big(1,2,3,6\big)\] Then we can use synthetic division to test roots quickly. \begin{array}{rrrrr} & {\small r^3} & {\small r^2} & {\small r} & {\small \#} \\ 1 \rfloor & 1 & -6 & 11 & -6 \\ & & 1 & -5 & 6 \\ \hline & 1 & -5 & 6 & \lfloor 0 \\ & {\small r^2} & {\small r} & {\small \#} \\ \end{array}
  2. So we have found \(r=1\) is a root so that means \((r-1)\) is a factor so the characteristic polynomial factors into \begin {align*} p(r) & = r^3-6r^2+11r-6 \\ & = (r-1)(r^2-5r+6) \end {align*} Now that we factored out one of the factors using the rational roots theorem, we can go back to our familiar factoring techniques or quadratic formula. \begin {align*} p(r) & = (r-1)\underbrace{(r^2-5r+6)} \\ & = (r-1)(r-2)(r-3) \\ \end {align*} Thus we have the characteristic equation for (9.2.3) which we can solve to find the solutions \begin {align*} (r-1)(r-2)(r-3) & = 0 \\ r=1 \quad r=2 \quad r = 3 & \\ y_1=e^{x} \quad y_2=e^{2x} \quad y_3 & =e^{3x} \\ \end {align*} So we have a set of solutions of (9.2.3) \[ \{y_1,y_2,y_3\} = \{e^x,e^{2x},e^{3x}\} \] We can verify it is a fundamental set by showing the Wronskian determinant is nonzero \begin {align*} W \big[e^x,e^{2x},e^{3x} \big](x) & =\det\left( \begin {array}{cccc} e^x & e^{2x} & e^{3x} \\ e^x & 2e^{2x} & 3e^{3x}\\ e^x & 4e^{2x} & 9e^{3x} \\ \end {array} \right) \\ & =e^x e^{2x} e^{3x}\det\left( \begin {array}{cccc} 1 & 1 & 1 \\ 1 & 2 & 3 \\ 1 & 4 & 9 \\ \end {array} \right) \\ & = e^{6x}\det\left( \begin {array}{cccc} 1 & 1 & 1 \\ 0 & 1 & 2 \\ 0 & 3 & 8 \\ \end {array} \right) \\ & = e^{6x}\det\left( \begin {array}{cccc} 1 & 2 \\ 3 & 8 \\ \end {array} \right) \\ & = e^{6x}\left( 8-6 \right) \\ & = 2e^{6x} \ne 0 \\ \end {align*}
    Therefore the general solution of (9.2.3) is \begin {equation} \label {eq:9.2.5} y=c_1e^{x}+c_2e^{2x}+c_3e^{3x} \end {equation}
  3. To solve the IVP (9.2.4) we must apply the initial conditions \[ y(0)=4 \quad \left. \frac{d y}{dx}\right|_0 = 5 \quad \left. \frac{d^2 y}{dx^2}\right|_0 = 9 \]
    Differentiate (9.2.5) twice
    Differentiating (9.2.5) twice we obtain \begin{align} \label {eq:9.2.6} \left\{ \begin{array}{rrrr} y &=& c_1e^{x} &+& c_2e^{2x} &+& c_3e^{3x} \\ \dfrac{dy}{dx} &=& c_1e^{x} &+& 2c_2e^{2x} &+& 3c_3e^{3x} \\ \dfrac{d^2y}{dx^2} &=& c_1e^{x} &+& 4c_2e^{2x} &+& 9c_3e^{3x} \\ \end{array} \right. \end{align} Evaluating (9.2.6) at the initial values we obtain a system of equations \begin{align*} & \left\{ \begin{array}{rrrr} y(0) &=& c_1e^{x} &+& c_2e^{0} &+& c_3e^{0} \\ \left.\dfrac{dy}{dx}\right|_0 &=& c_1e^{0} &+& 2c_2e^{0} &+& 3c_3e^{0} \\ \left.\dfrac{d^2y}{dx^2}\right|_0 &=& c_1e^{0} &+& 4c_2e^{0} &+& 9c_3e^{0} \\ \end{array} \right. \\ \\ & \Longrightarrow \left\{ \begin{array}{rrrr} c_1 &+& c_2 &+& c_3 &=& 4 \\ c_1 &+& 2c_2 &+& 3c_3 &=& 5 \\ c_1 &+& 4c_2 &+& 9c_3 &=& 9 \\ \end{array} \right. \\ \end{align*}
  4. To solve the system we will use row reduction with the augmented matrix \begin {align*} \left( \begin {array}{rrr|c} 1 & 1 & 1 & 4 \\ 1 & 2 & 3 & 5 \\ 1 & 4 & 9 & 9 \\ \end {array} \right) & \overset{ \small{R_2-R_1} }{\longrightarrow} \left( \begin {array}{rrr|c} 1 & 1 & 1 & 4 \\ 0 & 1 & 2 & 1 \\ 1 & 4 & 9 & 9 \\ \end {array} \right) \overset{ \small{R_3-R_1} }{\longrightarrow} \left( \begin {array}{rrr|c} 1 & 1 & 1 & 4 \\ 0 & 1 & 2 & 1 \\ 0 & 3 & 8 & 5 \\ \end {array} \right) \\ \\ & \overset{ \small{R_2-3R_3} }{\longrightarrow} \left( \begin {array}{rrr|c} 1 & 1 & 1 & 4 \\ 0 & 1 & 2 & 1 \\ 0 & 0 & 2 & 2 \\ \end {array} \right) \overset{ \small{\frac{1}{2}R_3} }{\longrightarrow} \left( \begin {array}{rrr|c} 1 & 1 & 1 & 4 \\ 0 & 1 & 2 & 1 \\ 0 & 0 & 1 & 1 \\ \end {array} \right) \\ \\ & \overset{ \small{R_2 - 2R_3} }{\longrightarrow} \left( \begin {array}{rrr|r} 1 & 1 & 1 & 4 \\ 0 & 1 & 0 & -1 \\ 0 & 0 & 1 & 1 \\ \end {array} \right) \overset{ \small{R_1-R_3} }{\longrightarrow} \left( \begin {array}{rrr|r} 1 & 1 & 0 & 3 \\ 0 & 1 & 0 & -1 \\ 0 & 0 & 1 & 1 \\ \end {array} \right) \\ \\ & \overset{ \small{R_1-R_2} }{\longrightarrow} \left( \begin {array}{rrr|r} 1 & 0 & 0 & 4 \\ 0 & 1 & 0 & -1 \\ 0 & 0 & 1 & 1 \\ \end {array} \right) \longrightarrow \left\{ \begin {array}{rrr} c_1 &=& 4 \\ c_2 &=& -1 \\ c_3 &=& 1 \\ \end {array} \right. \\ \\ \end {align*}
    Therefore the solution of (9.2.4) is \[ y=4e^x-e^{2x}+e^{3x} \]

Use the result of Example 9.2.2 to find the general solution of \begin {equation} \label {eq:9.2.10} \frac{d^3 y}{dx^3}-\frac{d^2 y}{dx^2}+\frac{d y}{dx}-y=0 \end {equation}
Solution
  1. Seeking solutions of the form \[ y=e^{rx} \] Differentiating \(y=e^{rx}\) three times yields \[ \frac{dy}{dx}=re^{rx} \Longrightarrow \frac{d^2y}{dx^2}=r^2e^{rx} \Longrightarrow \frac{d^3y}{dx^3}=r^3e^{rx} \] Substituting in to (9.2.10) we obtain the equation \begin {equation*} r^3e^{rx}-r^2e^{rx}+re^{rx}-e^{rx}=0 \end {equation*} Factoring out the exponential \begin {equation*} \Big( r^3-r^2+r-1 \Big) e^{rx}=0 \end {equation*} We now need to solve the characteristic equation of the characteristic polynomial equal to zero, which we already factored \begin {align*} \overset{p(r)}{\overbrace{r^3-r^2+r-1}} &= 0 \\ \big( r-1 \big)\big( r^2+1 \big) &= 0 \\ r-1 = 0 \quad r^2+1 &= 0 \\ r = 1 \quad r = \pm & i \\ \end {align*} Substituting \(r=1,\) \(r = i \) and \(r = -i \) into \( y = e^{rx} \) we obtain solutions of (9.2.10) \[ y_1=e^x \quad y_2 = e^{ix} \quad y_3 = e^{-ix} \] However, as discussed in Section 5.2 we'll use Euler's formula to rewrite the solutions in terms of real numbers to obtain the solution set \[ \{ e^x , \cos x , \sin x \} \]
  2. We can verify it is a fundamental set by showing the Wronskian determinant is nonzero \begin {align*} W \big[e^x,\cos x,\sin x \big](x) & =\det\left( \begin {array}{rrrr} e^x & \cos x & \sin x \\ e^x & -\sin x & \cos x \\ e^x & - \cos x & -\sin x \\ \end {array} \right) \\ & =e^x \det\left( \begin {array}{rrrrr} 1 & \cos x & \sin x \\ 1 & -\sin x & \cos x \\ 1 & - \cos x & -\sin x \\ \end {array} \right) \\ & =e^x \det\left( \begin {array}{ccccc} 1 & \cos x & \sin x \\ 0 & -\sin x-\cos x & \cos x -\sin x \\ 0 & - 2\cos x & -2\sin x \\ \end {array} \right) \\ & =2e^x \det\left( \begin {array}{ccccc} \sin x+\cos x & \sin x - \cos x \\ \cos x & \sin x \\ \end {array} \right) \\ & =2e^x \left( \sin^2 x + \cos x \sin x - \cos x \sin x + \cos^2 \right) \\ & =2e^x \left( \sin^2 x + \cos^2 \right) \\ & =2e^x \ne 0 \end {align*} Therefore \(\{e^x,\cos x,\sin x\}\) is linearly independent and the general solution of (9.2.10) is \[ y=c_1 e^x + c_2 \cos x + c_3\sin x \]


Find the general solution of \begin {equation} \label {eq:9.2.11} \frac{d^4 y}{dx^4}-16y=0 \end {equation}
Solution
  1. Seeking solutions of the form \[ y=e^{rx} \] Differentiating \(y=e^{rx}\) four times yields \begin{align*} \frac{dy}{dx}=re^{rx} \Longrightarrow \frac{d^2y}{dx^2}=r^2e^{rx} \Longrightarrow \frac{d^3y}{dx^3}=r^3e^{rx} \\ \Longrightarrow \frac{d^4y}{dx^4}=r^4e^{rx} \end{align*} Substituting in to (9.2.11) we obtain the equation \begin {equation*} r^4e^{rx}-16e^{rx}=0 \end {equation*} Factoring out the exponential \begin {equation*} \Big( r^4 - 16 \Big) e^{rx}=0 \end {equation*} We now need to solve the characteristic equation of the characteristic polynomial equal to zero, which we already factored \begin {align*} \overset{p(r)}{\overbrace{r^4 - 16}} &= 0 \\ \big( r^2-4 \big)\big( r^2+4 \big) &= 0 \\ \big( r-2 \big)\big( r+2 \big)\big( r^2+4 \big) &= 0 \\ r-2 = 0 \quad r+2 = 0 \quad r^2+4 &= 0 \\ r = 2 \quad r = -2 \quad r = \pm & 2i \\ \end {align*} Substituting \(r=2,\) \(r=-2,\) \(r = 2i \) and \(r = -2i \) into \( y = e^{rx} \) we obtain solutions of (9.2.11) \[ y_1=e^{2x} \quad y_2 = e^{-2x} \quad y_3 = e^{2ix} \quad y_4 = e^{-2ix} \] Again we'll use Euler's formula to rewrite the solutions in terms of real numbers to obtain the solution set \[ \{ e^{2x} , e^{-2x} , \cos 2x , \sin 2x \} \]
  2. We can verify it is a fundamental set by showing the Wronskian determinant is nonzero \begin {align*} & W \big[e^{2x},e^{-2x},\cos 2x,\sin 2x \big](x) \\ & =\det\left( \begin {array}{rrrr} e^{2x} & e^{-2x} & \cos 2x & \sin 2x \\ 2e^{2x} & -2e^{-2x} & -2\sin 2x & 2\cos 2x \\ 4e^{2x} & 4e^{-2x} & -4\cos 2x & -4\sin 2x \\ 8e^{2x} & -8e^{-2x} & 8\sin 2x & -8\cos 2x \\ \end {array} \right) \\ & =e^{2x}e^{-2x}\det\left( \begin {array}{rrrr} 1 & 1 & \cos 2x & \sin 2x \\ 2 & -2 & -2\sin 2x & 2\cos 2x \\ 4 & 4 & -2\cos 4x & -4\sin 2x \\ 8 & -8 & 2\sin 8x & -8\cos 2x \\ \end {array} \right) \\ & =2\cdot 4 \cdot 8 \det\left( \begin {array}{rrrr} 1 & 1 & \cos 2x & \sin 2x \\ 1 & -1 & -\sin 2x & \cos 2x \\ 1 & 1 & -\cos 2x & -\sin 2x \\ 1 & -1 & \sin 2x & -\cos 2x \\ \end {array} \right) \\ & =64 \det\left( \begin {array}{rrrr} 1 & 1 & \cos 2x & \sin 2x \\ 1 & -1 & -\sin 2x & \cos 2x \\ 0 & 0 & -2\cos 2x & -2\sin 2x \\ 0 & 0 & 2\sin 2x & -2\cos 2x \\ \end {array} \right) \\ & =64 \det\left( \begin {array}{ccccc} 1 & 1 & \cos 2x & \sin 2x \\ 0 & -2 & -\cos 2x -\sin 2x & \cos 2x - \sin 2x \\ 0 & 0 & -2\cos 2x & -2\sin 2x \\ 0 & 0 & 2\sin 2x & -2\cos 2x \\ \end {array} \right) \\ & =64 \det\left( \begin {array}{ccccc} -2 & -\cos 2x -\sin 2x & \cos 2x - \sin 2x \\ 0 & -2\cos 2x & -2\sin 2x \\ 0 & 2\sin 2x & -2\cos 2x \\ \end {array} \right) \\ & =-2 \cdot 64 \det\left( \begin {array}{ccccc} -2\cos 2x & -2\sin 2x \\ 2\sin 2x & -2\cos 2x \\ \end {array} \right) \\ & =-128\cdot -2 \cdot 2 \det\left( \begin {array}{ccccc} \cos 2x & \sin 2x \\ \sin 2x & -\cos 2x \\ \end {array} \right) \\ & =512 \det\left( \begin {array}{ccccc} \cos 2x & \sin 2x \\ \sin 2x & -\cos 2x \\ \end {array} \right) \\ & =512 \left( -\cos^2 2x - \sin^2 2x \right) \\ & =-512 \left( \cos^2 2x + \sin^2 2x \right) \\ & =-512 \ne 0 \\ \end {align*} Therefore \(\{e^{2x},e^{-2x},\cos 2x,\sin 2x\}\) is linearly independent and the general solution of (9.2.11) is \[ y=c_1e^{2x}+c_2e^{-2x}+c_3\cos 2x+c_4\sin 2x \]


It is known from algebra that every polynomial \[ p(r)=a_0r^n+a_1r^{n-1}+\cdots +a_n \] with real coefficients can be factored as \[ p(r)=a_0p_1(r)p_2(r)\cdots p_k(r)\] where no pair of the polynomials \(p_1\), \(p_2\), …, \(p_k\) has a common factor and each is either of the form \begin {equation} \label {eq:9.2.12} p_j(r)=(r-r_j)^{m_j} \end {equation} where \(r_j\) is real and \(m_j\) is a positive integer, or \begin {equation} \label {eq:9.2.13} p_j(r)=\left [(r-\lambda _j)^2+\omega _j^2\right ]^{m_j} \end {equation} where \(\lambda _j\) and \(\omega _j\) are real, \(\omega _j\ne 0\), and \(m_j\) is a positive integer. If (9.2.12) holds then \(r_j\) is a real zero of \(p\), while if (9.2.13) holds then \(\lambda +i\omega \) and \(\lambda -i\omega \) are complex conjugate zeros of \(p\). In either case, \(m_j\) is the multiplicity of the zero(s).
By arguments similar to those used in our examples, it can be shown that \begin {equation} \label {eq:9.2.14} p(D)=a_0p_1(D)p_2(D)\cdots p_k(D) \end {equation} and that the order of the factors on the right can be chosen arbitrarily. Therefore, if \(p_j(D)y=0\) for some \(j\) then \(p(D)y=0\). To see this, we simply rewrite (9.2.14) so that \(p_j(D)\) is applied first. Therefore the problem of finding solutions of \(p(D)y=0\) with \(p\) as in (9.2.14) reduces to finding solutions of each of these equations \[ p_j(D)y=0,\quad 1\le j\le k\] where \(p_j\) is a power of a first degree term or of an irreducible quadratic. To find a fundamental set of solutions \(\{y_1,y_2,\dots ,y_n\}\) of \(p(D)y=0\), we find fundamental set of solutions of each of the equations and take \(\{y_1,y_2,\dots ,y_n\}\) to be the set of all functions in these separate fundamental sets. In Exercise 40 we sketch the proof that \(\{y_1,y_2,\dots ,y_n\}\) is linearly independent, and therefore a fundamental set of solutions of \(p(D)y=0\).
To apply this procedure to general homogeneous constant coefficient equations, we must be able to find fundamental sets of solutions of equations of the form \begin{align} (r-a)^my=0 \end{align} and \[ \left [(r-\lambda )^2+\omega ^2\right ]^my=0\] where \(m\) is an arbitrary positive integer. The next two theorems show how to do this.
Theorem 9.2.1 If \(m\) is a positive integer, then \begin {equation} \label {eq:9.2.15} \{e^{ax}, xe^{ax}, x^2e^{ax}, \dots , x^{m-1}e^{ax}\} \end {equation} is a fundamental set of solutions of \begin {equation} \label {eq:9.2.16} (r-a)^my=0 \end {equation}
Proof We’ll show that if \[ f(x)=c_1+c_2x+\cdots +c_mx^{m-1} \] is an arbitrary polynomial of degree \(\le m-1\), then \(y=e^{ax}f\) is a solution of (9.2.16). First note that if \(g\) is any differentiable function then \[ (D-a)e^{ax}g=De^{ax}g-ae^{ax}g=ae^{ax}g+e^{ax}g'-ae^{ax}g\] so \begin {equation*} \label {eq:9.2.17} (D-a)e^{ax}g=e^{ax}g' \end {equation*} Therefore \[ \begin {array}{lcll} (D-a)e^{ax}f&=&e^{ax}f'&\mbox {(from \eqref {eq:9.2.17} with $g=f$)}\\ (D-a)^2e^{ax}f&=& (D-a)e^{ax}f'=e^{ax}f'' &\mbox {(from \eqref {eq:9.2.17} with $g=f'$)}\\ (D-a)^3e^{ax}f&=& (D-a)e^{ax}f''=e^{ax}f''' &\mbox {(from \eqref {eq:9.2.17} with $g=f''$)}\\ &\vdots& \\ (D-a)^me^{ax}f &=&(D-a)e^{ax}f^{(m-1)}=e^{ax}f^{(m)} &\mbox {(from \eqref {eq:9.2.17} with $g=f^{(m-1)}$)} \end {array} \] Since \(f^{(m)}=0\), the last equation implies that \(y=e^{ax}f\) is a solution of (9.2.16) if \(f\) is any polynomial of degree \(\le m-1\). In particular, each function in (9.2.15) is a solution of (9.2.16). To see that (9.2.15) is linearly independent (and therefore a fundamental set of solutions of (9.2.16)), note that if \[ c_1e^{ax}+c_2xe^{ax}+c\dots +c_{m-1}x^{m-1}e^{ax}=0 \] for all \(x\) in some interval \((a,b)\), then \[ c_1+c_2x+c\dots +c_{m-1}x^{m-1}=0 \] for all \(x\) in \((a,b)\). However, we know from algebra that if this polynomial has more than \(m-1\) zeros then \(c_1=c_2=\cdots =c_n=0\).


Find the general solution of \begin {equation} \label {eq:9.2.18} \frac{d^3 y}{dx^3}+3\frac{d^2 y}{dx^2}+3\frac{d y}{dx}+y=0 \end {equation}
Solution
  1. Seeking solutions of the form \[ y=e^{rx} \] Differentiating \(y=e^{rx}\) three times yields \begin{align*} \frac{dy}{dx}=re^{rx} \Longrightarrow \frac{d^2y}{dx^2}=r^2e^{rx} \Longrightarrow \frac{d^3y}{dx^3}=r^3e^{rx} \end{align*} Substituting in to (9.2.18) we obtain the equation \begin {equation*} r^3e^{rx}+3r^2e^{rx}+3re^{rx}+e^{rx}=0 \end {equation*} Factoring out the exponential \begin {equation*} \Big( r^3 + 3r^2 + 3r + 1 \Big) e^{rx}=0 \end {equation*} We now need to solve the characteristic equation of the characteristic polynomial equal to zero, which we can't factor so we apply the rational roots theorem. Since the constant term and leading coefficient are both \(1\) then the only possible rational roots to consider are \(\pm 1\). \begin{array}{rrrrr} & {\small r^3} & {\small r^2} & {\small r} & {\small \#} \\ 1 \rfloor & 1 & 3 & 3 & 1 \\ & & 1 & 4 & 7 \\ \hline & 1 & 4 & 7 & \lfloor 8 \\ & {\small r^2} & {\small r} & {\small \#} \\ \end{array} Since the remainder is \(8\) in this case then \(r=1\) is not a root \begin{array}{rrrrr} & {\small r^3} & {\small r^2} & {\small r} & {\small \#} \\ -1 \rfloor & 1 & 3 & 3 & 1 \\ & & -1& -2 & -1 \\ \hline & 1 & 2 & 1 & \lfloor 0 \\ & {\small r^2} & {\small r} & {\small \#} \\ \end{array}
  2. But \(r=-1\) is a root so that means \((r+1)\) is a factor so the characteristic polynomial factors into \begin {align*} p(r) & = r^3+3r^2+3r+1 \\ & = (r+1)\underbrace{(r^2+2r+1)} \\ & = (r+1)(r+1)(r+1) \\ & = (r+1)^3 \end {align*} Thus we have the characteristic equation for (9.2.18) which we can solve to find the solutions \begin {align*} (r+1)^3 & = 0 \\ r=-1 \\ y_1=e^{-x} \\ \end {align*} As discussed in Section 5.2 when we have a repeated root we can find additional solutions by multiplying times \(x\) so we have a set of solutions of (9.2.18) \[ \{y_1,y_2,y_3\} = \{e^{-x},xe^{-x},x^2e^{-x}\} \]
    Therefore the general solution of (9.2.18)is \[ y=(c_1+c_2x+c_3x^2) e^{-x} \]

Theorem 9.2.2 If \(\omega \ne 0\) and \(m\) is a positive integer, then \begin{align*} \{e^{\lambda x}\cos \omega x, xe^{\lambda x}\cos \omega x, x^2e^{\lambda x}\cos \omega x, & \dots , x^{m-1}e^{\lambda x}\cos \omega x,\\ e^{\lambda x}\sin \omega x, xe^{\lambda x}\sin \omega x, x^2 e^{\lambda x}\sin \omega x, & \dots , x^{m-1}e^{\lambda x}\sin \omega x\} \end{align*} is a fundamental set of solutions of \[ [(r-\lambda )^2+\omega ^2]^my=0\]


Find the general solution of \begin {equation} \label {eq:9.2.19} (D^2+4D+13)^3y=0 \end {equation}
Solution
The characteristic polynomial of (9.2.19) is \[ p(r)=(r^2+4r+13)^3=\left ((r+2)^2+9\right )^3\] Therefore (9.2.19) can be be written as \[ [(D+2)^2+9]^3y=0\] so Theorem 9.2.2 implies that the general solution of (9.2.19) is \[ y=(a_1+a_2x+a_3x^2)e^{-2x}\cos 3x +(b_1+b_2x+b_3x^2)e^{-2x}\sin 3x\]


Find the general solution of \begin {equation} \label {eq:9.2.20} \frac{d^4 y}{dx^4}+4\frac{d^3 y}{dx^3}+6\frac{d^2 y}{dx^2}+4\frac{d y}{dx}=0 \end {equation}
Solution
  1. Seeking solutions of the form \[ y=e^{rx} \] Differentiating \(y=e^{rx}\) four times yields \begin{align*} \frac{dy}{dx}=re^{rx} \Longrightarrow \frac{d^2y}{dx^2}=r^2e^{rx} \Longrightarrow \frac{d^3y}{dx^3}=r^3e^{rx} \Longrightarrow \frac{d^4y}{dx^4}=r^4e^{rx} \end{align*} Substituting in to (9.2.18) we obtain the equation \begin {equation*} r^4e^{rx} + 4r^3e^{rx} + 6r^2e^{rx} + 4re^{rx} =0 \end {equation*} Factoring out the exponential \begin {equation*} \Big( r^4+4r^3+6r^2+4r \Big) e^{rx}=0 \end {equation*} We now need to solve the characteristic equation of the characteristic polynomial equal to zero \begin {align*} r^4+4r^3+6r^2+4r & = 0 \\ r\Big( r^3+4r^2+6r+4 \Big) & = 0 \\ \end {align*} After factoring the common factor of \(r\) the remaining polynomial is unfactorable so we apply the rational roots theorem. Since the constant term is \(4\) and leading coefficient is \(1\) then the only possible rational roots to consider are \(\pm \big(1,2,4\big)\). \begin{array}{rrrrr} & {\small r^3} & {\small r^2} & {\small r} & {\small \#} \\ -1 \rfloor & 1 & 4 & 6 & 4 \\ & & -1& 4 & -10 \\ \hline & 1 & -4 & 10 & \lfloor -6 \\ & {\small r^2} & {\small r} & {\small \#} \\ \end{array}
  2. Since the remainder is \(-6\) in this case then \(r=-1\) is not a root \begin{array}{rrrrr} & {\small r^3} & {\small r^2} & {\small r} & {\small \#} \\ -2 \rfloor & 1 & 4 & 6 & 4 \\ & & -2& -4 & -4 \\ \hline & 1 & 2 & 2 & \lfloor 0 \\ & {\small r^2} & {\small r} & {\small \#} \\ \end{array} But \(r=-2\) is a root so that means \((r+2)\) is a factor so we can solve the characteristic equation \begin {align*} r(r+2)(r^2+2r+2) & = 0 \\ r=0 \quad r=-2 \quad r^2+2r+2 & = 0 \\ \end {align*} And we can use the quadratic formula to solve the last equation \begin {align*} r = \frac{-2\pm \sqrt{2^2-4\cdot2}}{2} =-1 \pm i \end {align*} So we have all four roots and can write four exponential solutions \begin {align*} r=0 \quad r=-2 \quad r=-1+i \quad r & =-1-i \\ y=e^{0x} \quad y=e^{-2x} \quad y=e^{(-1+i)x} \quad y & =e^{(-1-i)x} \\ y=1 \quad y=e^{-2x} \quad y=e^{-x}e^{ix} \quad y & =e^{-x}e^{ix} \\ \end {align*} Again we'll use Euler's formula to rewrite the imaginary solutions in terms of real numbers to obtain the solution set \[ \{ 1 , e^{-2x} , e^{-x}\cos x , e^{-x}\sin x \} \]
    Therefore the general solution of is \[ y=c_1 + c_2 e^{-2x} + \big( c_3 \cos x + c_4 \sin x\big) e^{-x} \]


Find a fundamental set of solutions of \begin {equation} \label {eq:9.2.21} [(D+1)^2+1]^2(D-1)^3(D+1)D^2y=0 \end {equation}
Solution
A fundamental set of solutions of (9.2.21) can be obtained by combining fundamental sets of solutions of \[ \begin {array}{c} \displaystyle \left [(D+1)^2+1\right ]^2 y=0,\quad (D-1)^3 y=0,\\ \displaystyle (D+1)y=0,\quad \mbox { and } \quad D^2y=0 \end {array} \] Fundamental sets of solutions of these equations are given by \[ \begin {array}{c} \displaystyle \{e^{-x}\cos x, xe^{-x}\cos x, e^{-x}\sin x, xe^{-x}\sin x\},\quad \{e^x, xe^x, x^2e^x\},\\ \displaystyle \{e^{-x}\},\mbox { and } \{1,x\} \end {array} \] respectively. These ten functions form a fundamental set of solutions of (9.2.21).
