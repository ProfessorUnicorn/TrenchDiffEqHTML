5.2 Constant Coefficient Homogeneous Equations
Constant Coefficient Homogeneous Equations
If \(a,b\), and \(c\) are real constants and \(a\ne 0\), then \[ a\frac{d^2y}{dx^2}+b\frac{dy}{dx}+cy=F(x) \] is said to be a constant coefficient equation. In this section we consider the homogeneous constant coefficient equation \begin {equation} \label {eq:5.2.1} a\frac{d^2y}{dx^2}+b\frac{dy}{dx}+cy=0 \end {equation} As we’ll see, all solutions of (5.2.1) are defined on \((-\infty ,\infty )\). This being the case, we’ll omit references to the interval on which solutions are defined, or on which a given set of solutions is a fundamental set, etc., since the interval will always be \((-\infty ,\infty )\).
The key to solving (5.2.1) is that if \(y=e^{rx}\) where \(r\) is a constant then the left side of (5.2.1) is a multiple of \(e^{rx}\) \[ y=e^{rx} \Longrightarrow \frac{dy}{dx}=re^{rx} \Longrightarrow \frac{d^2y}{dx^2}=r^2e^{rx} \] Substituting into (5.2.1) \begin{align} \label{eq:5.2.2} a \frac{d^2y}{dx^2} + b \frac{dy}{dx} + c y = & 0\nonumber \\ a \Big( r^2e^{rx} \Big) + b \Big( re^{rx} \Big) + c \Big( e^{rx} \Big) = & 0 \nonumber \\ ar^2e^{rx} + bre^{rx} + ce^{rx} = & 0 \nonumber \\ \big( ar^2 + br + c \big) e^{rx} = & 0 \\ ar^2 + br + c = 0 \quad \text{or} & \quad e^{rx} = 0 \nonumber \end{align} Now since the factor \( e^{rx} \) is never zero, the only way to solve (5.2.2) would be when the quadratic polynomial is zero: \[ ar^2 + br + c = 0 \]
We call this equation the characteristic equation of (5.2.1) and we call the quadratic polynomial \(p(r)=ar^2+br+c \) the characteristic polynomial. As the characteristic equation is quadratic, the roots are given by the quadratic formula \begin {equation} \label {eq:5.2.3} r={-b\pm \sqrt {b^2-4ac}\over 2a} \end {equation}
We consider three cases:
Case 1: \(b^2-4ac>0\), so the characteristic equation has two distinct real roots
Case 2: \(b^2-4ac=0\), so the characteristic equation has a repeated real root
Case 3: \(b^2-4ac<0\), so the characteristic equation has complex roots
In each case we’ll start with an example
Case 1: Distinct Real Roots
  1. Find the general solution \begin {equation} \label {eq:5.2.4} \frac{d^2y}{dx^2}+6\frac{dy}{dx}+5y=0 \end {equation}
  2. Solve the IVP \begin {equation} \label {eq:5.2.5} \frac{d^2y}{dx^2}+6\frac{dy}{dx}+5y=0 \quad y(0)=3 \quad \left. \frac{dy}{dx} \right|_{0}=-1 \end {equation}
Solution
  1. We start by seeking solutions of the form \( y=e^{rx} \) \[ y=e^{rx} \Longrightarrow \frac{dy}{dx}=re^{rx} \Longrightarrow \frac{d^2y}{dx^2}=r^2e^{rx} \] Substituting these in we obtain: \begin{align*} \frac{d^2y}{dx^2} + 6 \frac{dy}{dx} + 5 y = & 0 \\ \Big( r^2 e^{rx} \Big) + 6 \Big( r e^{rx} \Big) + 5 \Big( e^{rx} \Big) = & 0 \\ \left( r^2 + 6 r + 5 \right) e^{rx} = & 0 \\ r^2 + 6 r + 5 = & 0 \quad \text{ or } \quad e^{rx} = 0 \\ \end{align*} Where \( p(r) = r^2 + 6 r + 5 \) is the characteristic polynomial and since \( e^{rx} \ne 0 \) to solve (5.2.4) we must solve the characteristic equation which is always a quadratic equation for second order differential equations so this is always solvable using the quadratic formula (5.2.3) but this one happens to be factorable: \begin{align*} r^2 + 6 r + 5 = & 0 \\ \left( r + 1 \right) \left( r + 5 \right) = & 0 \\ r + 1= 0 \quad \text{or} \quad r + 5 = & 0 \\ r = -1 \quad \text{or} \quad r = & -5 \\ \end{align*} Substituting in \(r = -1\) and \(r = -5\) into \( y = e^{rx} \) we obtain solutions of (5.2.4) \[ y_1 = e^{-x} \quad \text{ and } \quad y_2 = e^{-5x} \]
    Now we would like to know if \( \{ y_1, y_2 \} = \{ e^{-x}, e^{-5x}\} \) is a fundamental set so we check if they are linearly independent: \begin{align*} \frac{y_2}{y_1} =& \frac{e^{-5x}}{e^{-x}} = e^{-5x+x} = e^{-4x} \\ \end{align*} Since the ratio \({y_1}/{y_2}\) is not constant, then \(y_1\) and \(y_2\) are linearly independent, so \( \{ y_1, y_2 \} = \{ e^{-x}, e^{-5x}\} \) is a fundamental set and therefore from Theorem 5.1.6 \(y=c_1y_1 + c_2y_2\) is the general solution: \begin{equation} \label{eq:5.2.6} y = c_1e^{-x} + c_2e^{-5x} \end{equation}
    Verify (5.2.6) is a general solution of (5.2.4)
  2. We already found the general solution (5.2.6) and now we apply the initial conditions to find the constants \(c_1\) and \(c_2\) to solve the IVP (5.2.5). Starting with \( y(0)=3 \) \begin{align*} y(x) = & c_1e^{-x} + c_2e^{-5x} \\ y(0) = & c_1e^{-0} + c_2e^{-5\cdot 0} \\ 3 = & c_1e^{0} + c_2e^{0} \\ 3 = & c_1 + c_2 \\ \end{align*} So we obtain the equation \( c_1 + c_2 = 3 \) and now we will need another equation in order to solve for the constants so we use the other initial condition which is at the derivative of our general solution (5.2.6)
    Differentiate (5.2.6)
    \begin{align*} y = & c_1e^{-x} + c_2e^{-5x} \nonumber \\ \frac{dy}{dx} = & \frac{d}{dx} \Big( c_1e^{-x} + c_2e^{-5x} \Big) \nonumber \\ \frac{dy}{dx} = & - c_1 e^{-x} -5 c_2 e^{-5x} \\ \end{align*} Now we can apply the initial condition \( \left. \displaystyle \frac{dy}{dx} \right|_{0}=-1 \) \begin{align} \left. \frac{dy}{dx} \right|_0 = & - c_1 e^{0} -5 c_2 e^{0} \nonumber \\ -1 = & - c_1 -5 c_2 \nonumber \\ 1 = & c_1 + 5 c_2 \nonumber \end{align}
    So we obtain the system of equations: And we obtain a system of equations which we can solve by subtracting them to find \(c_2\) then substituting that back to find \(c_1\) \begin{align*} \left\{ \begin{array}{r l l} c_1 + c_2 &=& 3 \\ c_1 + 5c_2 &=& 1 \\ \end{array} \right. \quad \longrightarrow \left\{ \begin{array}{r l r} c_1 + c_2 &=& 3 \\ 4c_2 &=& -2 \\ \end{array} \right. \quad \longrightarrow \left\{ \begin{array}{r l r} \displaystyle c_1 - \frac{1}{2} &=& 3 \\ c_2 &=& \displaystyle -\frac{1}{2} \\ \end{array} \right. \quad \longrightarrow \left\{ \begin{array}{r l l} c_2 &=& \displaystyle \frac{7}{2} \\ c_1 &=& \displaystyle -\frac{1}{2} \\ \end{array} \right. \end{align*}
    Substituting \( c_1 = -\frac{1}{2} \) and \( c_2 = \frac{7}{2} \) into (5.2.6) we obtain the particular solution of the IVP (5.2.5) \begin{equation} \label{eq:5.2.7} y = -\frac{1}{2} e^{-x} + \frac{7}{2} e^{-5x} \end{equation}
    Solve the IVP (5.2.4) \( \quad y(0)=2 \quad \left. \frac{dy}{dx} \right|_{0}=-4 \)
    Verify (5.2.7) is a solution of (5.2.5)
Case 2: A Repeated Real Root
  1. Find the general solution \begin {equation} \label {eq:5.2.8} \frac{d^2y}{dx^2}+6\frac{dy}{dx}+9y=0 \end {equation}
  3. Solve the IVP \begin {equation} \label {eq:5.2.9} \frac{d^2y}{dx^2}+6\frac{dy}{dx}+9y=0 \quad y(0)=3 \quad \left. \frac{dy}{dx} \right|_{0}=-1 \end {equation}
Solution
  1. We start by seeking solutions of the form \( y=e^{rx} \) \[ y=e^{rx} \Longrightarrow \frac{dy}{dx}=re^{rx} \Longrightarrow \frac{d^2y}{dx^2}=r^2e^{rx} \] Substituting these in we obtain: \begin{align*} \frac{d^2y}{dx^2} + 6 \frac{dy}{dx} + 9 y = & 0 \\ \Big( r^2 e^{rx} \Big) + 6 \Big( r e^{rx} \Big) + 9 \Big( e^{rx} \Big) = & 0 \\ \left( r^2 + 6 r + 9 \right) e^{rx} = & 0 \\ r^2 + 6 r + 9 = & 0 \quad \text{ or } \quad e^{rx} = 0 \\ \end{align*} Where \( p(r) = r^2 + 6 r + 9 \) is the characteristic polynomial and since \( e^{rx} \ne 0 \) to solve (5.2.8) we must solve the characteristic equation which is always a quadratic equation for second order differential equations so this is always solvable using the quadratic formula (5.2.3) but this one happens to be factorable: \begin{align*} r^2 + 6 r + 9 = & 0 \\ \left( r + 3 \right) \left( r + 3 \right) = & 0 \\ r + 3= 0 \quad \text{or} \quad r + 3 = & 0 \\ r = -3 \quad \text{or} \quad r = & -3 \\ \end{align*} Substituting in \(r = -3\) \( y = e^{rx} \) we obtain one solution of (5.2.8) \[ y_1 = e^{-3x} \] Since the other root was the same repeated root, it will not produce another linearly independent solution and as a fundamental set for a second order differential equation must have two linearly independent solutions, we will now use variation of parameters to find another solution of the form \( y = u y_1 = u \cdot e^{-3x} \)
    Differentiate \( y = u \cdot e^{-3x} \)
    Differentiating \( y = u \cdot e^{-3x} \) we obtain \[ \frac{dy}{dx} = \left( \frac{du}{dx} - 3u \right) e^{-3x} \]
    Differentiate \( \displaystyle \frac{dy}{dx} = \left( \frac{du}{dx} - 3u \right) e^{-3x} \)
    Differentiating \( \displaystyle \frac{dy}{dx} = \left( \frac{du}{dx} - 3u \right) e^{-3x}\) we obtain \[ \frac{d^2y}{dx^2} = \left( \frac{d^2u}{dx^2} - 6 \frac{du}{dx} + 9 u \right) e^{-3x} \]
  2. Now we substitute the derivatives into our original differential equation (5.2.8) to find a new simpler differential equation in \(u\) (a special case of a method called reduction of order) by substituting the new expressions for \( y ,\) \( {dy}/{dx} \) and \( {d^2y}/{dx^2} \) (in terms of \(u \)) into (5.2.8) and simplifying \begin{align} \frac{d^2y}{dx^2} + 6 \frac{dy}{dx} + 9 y = & 0 \nonumber \\ \Big( \frac{d^2y}{dx^2} \Big) + 6 \Big( \frac{dy}{dx} \Big) + 9 \Big( y \Big) = & 0 \nonumber \\ \Big( \frac{d^2u}{dx^2} - 6 \frac{du}{dx} + 9 u \Big)e^{-3x} + 6 \Big( \frac{du}{dx} - 3u \Big)e^{-3x} + 9 \Big( u \Big)e^{-3x} = & 0 \nonumber \\ \Bigg( \Big( \frac{d^2u}{dx^2} - 6 \frac{du}{dx} + 9 u \Big) + \Big( 6\frac{du}{dx} - 18u \Big) + \Big( 9u \Big) \Bigg) e^{-3x} = & 0 \nonumber \\ \frac{d^2u}{dx^2} e^{-3x} = & 0 \nonumber \\ \frac{d^2u}{dx^2} = & 0 \nonumber \\ \end{align} And we get the simple second order equation which can be solved using antidifferentiation: \begin{align} \frac{d^2u}{dx^2} = & 0 \nonumber \\ \int \Big( \frac{d^2u}{dx^2} \Big) dx = & \int \Big( 0 \Big) dx \nonumber \\ \frac{du}{dx} = & c \nonumber \\ \int \Big( \frac{du}{dx} \Big) dx = & \int \Big( c \Big) dx \nonumber \\ u =& c_1 + c_2 x \nonumber \\ \end{align} So we have found \(u\) and we multiply by \( e^{-3x}\) to get \(y\) \begin{align} y =& \left( c_1 + c_2 x \right) e^{-3x} \end{align} Now we know that any function of the form (5.2.10) is a solution of (5.2.8). Notice that substituting \(c_1=0\) and \(c_2=1\) then we get \(y=e^{-3x}\) which is the solution we already found earlier using the characteristic equation. In order to obtain a fundamental set we would like another solution of the form (5.2.10) that is linearly independent of our known solution, so let \(c_1=1\) and \(c_2=0\) and we have \(y=xe^{-3x}\) and now we just need to verify that \(e^{-3x}\) and \(xe^{-3x}\) are linearly independent \begin{align*} \frac{xe^{-3x}}{e^{-3x}} = x \end{align*} Since their ratio is non constant then they are linearly independent thus \( \{ e^{-3x} ,xe^{-3x} \}\) is a fundamental set of solutions for (5.2.8) and thus the general solution is \( y = c_1 e^{-3x} + c_2 x e^{-3x} \) which is the same as (5.2.10)
  3. We already found the general solution (5.2.10) and now we apply the initial conditions to find the constants \(c_1\) and \(c_2\) to solve the IVP (5.2.8). Starting with \( y(0)=3 \) \begin{align*} y(x) = & \left( c_1 + c_2 x \right) e^{-3x} \\ y(0) = & \left( c_1 + c_2 \cdot 0 \right) e^{-3 \cdot 0} \\ 3 = & c_1 e^{0} \Longrightarrow c_1 = 3 \end{align*} Now the other initial condition is at the derivative of our general solution (5.2.10)
    Differentiate (5.2.10)
    \begin{align} \label{eq:5.2.11} y = & \left( c_1 + c_2 x \right) e^{-3x} \nonumber \\ \frac{dy}{dx} = & \frac{d}{dx} \Big( c_1e^{-x} + c_2e^{-5x} \Big) \nonumber \\ \frac{dy}{dx} = & \Big( c_2 - 9 - 3c_2 x \Big) e^{-3x} \\ \end{align} Now we can apply the initial condition \( \left. \displaystyle \frac{dy}{dx} \right|_{0}=-1 \) \begin{align} \left. \frac{dy}{dx} \right|_{0} = & \Big( c_2 - 9 - 3c_2 \cdot 0 \Big) e^{-3 \cdot 0 } \nonumber \\ -1 = & \Big( c_2 - 9 \Big) e^{ 0 } \nonumber \\ -1 = & c_2 - 9 \Longrightarrow c_2 = 8 \nonumber \end{align}
    Substituting \( c_1 = 3 \) and \( c_2 = 8 \) into (5.2.10) we obtain the particular solution of the IVP (5.2.8) \begin{equation*} y = (3 + 8x) e^{-3x} \end{equation*}
    Solve the IVP (5.2.8) \( \quad y(0)=2 \quad \left. \frac{dy}{dx} \right|_{0}=-4 \)



If the characteristic equation of \(a\frac{d^2y}{dx^2}+b\frac{dy}{dx}+cy=0\) has an arbitrary repeated root \(r_1\), the characteristic polynomial must be \[ p(r)=a(r-r_1)^2=a(r^2-2r_1r+r_1^2)\] Therefore \[ ar^2+br+c=ar^2-(2ar_1)r+ar_1^2\] which implies that \(b=-2ar_1\) and \(c=ar_1^2\). Therefore \(a\frac{d^2y}{dx^2}+b\frac{dy}{dx}+cy=0\) can be written as \(a(\frac{d^2y}{dx^2}-2r_1\frac{dy}{dx}+r_1^2y)=0\). Since \(a\ne 0\) this equation has the same solutions as \begin {equation} \label {eq:5.2.12} \frac{d^2y}{dx^2}-2r_1\frac{dy}{dx}+r_1^2y=0 \end {equation}
Since \(p(r_1)=0\), t \(y_1=e^{r_1x}\) is a solution of \(a\frac{d^2y}{dx^2}+b\frac{dy}{dx}+cy=0\), and therefore of (5.2.12). Proceeding as in Example 5.2.2, we look for other solutions of (5.2.12) of the form \(y=ue^{r_1x}\); then \[ \frac{dy}{dx}=\frac{du}{dx}e^{r_1x}+rue^{r_1x}\quad \mbox{ and } \quad \frac{d^2y}{dx^2}=\frac{d^2u}{dx^2}e^{r_1x}+2r_1\frac{du}{dx}e^{r_1x}+r_1^2ue^{r_1x}\] so
\begin {eqnarray*} \frac{d^2y}{dx^2}-2r_1\frac{dy}{dx}+r_1^2y&=&e^{rx}\left [(\frac{d^2u}{dx^2}+2r_1\frac{du}{dx}+r_1^2u)- 2r_1(\frac{du}{dx}+r_1u)+r_1^2u\right ]\\ &=&e^{r_1x}\left [\frac{d^2u}{dx^2}+(2r_1-2r_1)\frac{du}{dx}+(r_1^2-2r_1^2+r_1^2)u\right ]=\frac{d^2u}{dx^2}e^{r_1x} \end {eqnarray*}
Therefore \(y=ue^{r_1x}\) is a solution of (5.2.12) if and only if \(\frac{d^2u}{dx^2}=0\), which is equivalent to \(u=c_1+c_2x\), where \(c_1\) and \(c_2\) are constants. Hence, any function of the form \begin {equation} \label {eq:5.2.13} y=e^{r_1x}(c_1+c_2x) \end {equation} is a solution of (5.2.12). Letting \(c_1=1\) and \(c_2=0\) here yields the solution \(y_1=e^{r_1x}\) that we already knew. Letting \(c_1=0\) and \(c_2=1\) yields the second solution \(y_2=xe^{r_1x}\). Since \(y_2/y_1=x\) is nonconstant, 5.1.6 implies that \(\{y_1,y_2\}\) is a fundamental set of solutions of (5.2.12), and (5.2.13) is the general solution.
Case 3: Complex Conjugate Roots
  1. Find the general solution of \begin {equation} \label {eq:5.2.14} \frac{d^2y}{dx^2} + 4 \frac{dy}{dx} + 13 y = 0 \end {equation}
  2. Solve the initial value problem \begin {equation} \label {eq:5.2.15} \frac{d^2y}{dx^2}+4\frac{dy}{dx}+13y=0 \quad y(0)=2 \quad \left. \frac{dy}{dx} \right|_{0}=-3 \end {equation}
Solution
  1. The characteristic polynomial of (5.2.14) is \[ p(r)=r^2+4r+13=r^2+4r+4+9=(r+2)^2+9\] The roots of the characteristic equation are \(r_1=-2+3i\) and \(r_2=-2-3i\). By analogy with Case 1, it’s reasonable to expect that \(e^{(-2+3i)x}\) and \(e^{(-2-3i)x}\) are solutions of (5.2.14). This is true (see Exercise  34); however, there are difficulties here, since you are probably not familiar with exponential functions with complex arguments, and even if you are, it’s inconvenient to work with them, since they are complex–valued. We’ll take a simpler approach, which we motivate as follows: the exponential notation suggests that \[ e^{(-2+3i)x}=e^{-2x}e^{3ix}\quad \mbox{ and } \quad e^{(-2-3i)x}=e^{-2x}e^{-3ix}\] so even though we haven’t defined \(e^{3ix}\) and \(e^{-3ix}\), it’s reasonable to expect that every linear combination of \(e^{(-2+3i)x}\) and \(e^{(-2-3i)x}\) can be written as \(y=ue^{-2x}\), where \(u\) depends upon \(x\). To determine \(u\), we note that if \(y=ue^{-2x}\) then \[ \frac{dy}{dx}=\frac{du}{dx}e^{-2x}-2ue^{-2x}\mbox {\quad and \quad } \frac{d^2y}{dx^2}=\frac{d^2u}{dx^2}e^{-2x}-4\frac{du}{dx}e^{-2x}+4ue^{-2x}\] so
    \begin {eqnarray*} \frac{d^2y}{dx^2}+4\frac{dy}{dx}+13y&=&e^{-2x}\left [(\frac{d^2u}{dx^2}-4\frac{du}{dx}+4u)+4(\frac{du}{dx}-2u)+13u\right ]\\ &=&e^{-2x}\left [\frac{d^2u}{dx^2}-(4-4)\frac{du}{dx}+(4-8+13)u\right ]=e^{-2x}(\frac{d^2u}{dx^2}+9u) \end {eqnarray*}
    Therefore \(y=ue^{-2x}\) is a solution of (5.2.14) if and only if \[ \frac{d^2u}{dx^2}+9u=0\] From Example  5.1.2, the general solution of this equation is \[ u=c_1\cos 3x +c_2\sin 3x\] Therefore any function of the form \begin {equation} \label {eq:5.2.16} y=e^{-2x}(c_1\cos 3x+c_2\sin 3x) \end {equation} is a solution of (5.2.14). Letting \(c_1=1\) and \(c_2=0\) yields the solution \(y_1=e^{-2x}\cos 3x\). Letting \(c_1=0\) and \(c_2=1\) yields the second solution \(y_2=e^{-2x}\sin 3x\). Since \(y_2/y_1=\tan 3x\) is nonconstant, 5.1.6 implies that \(\{y_1,y_2\}\) is a fundamental set of solutions of (5.2.14), and (5.2.16) is the general solution.
  2. Imposing the condition \(y(0)=2\) in (5.2.16) shows that \(c_1=2\). Differentiating (5.2.16) yields \[ \frac{dy}{dx}=-2e^{-2x}(c_1\cos 3x+c_2\sin 3x) +3e^{-2x}(-c_1\sin 3x +c_2\cos 3x)\] and imposing the initial condition \(\left. \frac{dy}{dx} \right|_{0}=-3\) here yields \(-3=-2c_1+3c_2=-4+3c_2\), so \(c_2=1/3\). Therefore the solution of (5.2.15) is \[ y=e^{-2x}(2\cos 3x+ {1\over 3}\sin 3x)\] Figure  5.2.3 is a graph of this function.



Now suppose the characteristic equation of \(a\frac{d^2y}{dx^2}+b\frac{dy}{dx}+cy=0\) has arbitrary complex roots thus \(b^2-4ac<0\) and, from (5.2.3), the roots are \[ r_1 = {-b+i\sqrt {4ac-b^2}\over 2a} \quad \quad r_2 = {-b-i\sqrt {4ac-b^2}\over 2a} \] which we rewrite as \begin {equation} \label {eq:5.2.17} r_1=\lambda +i \omega \quad \quad r_2 = \lambda - i \omega \end {equation} with \[ \lambda = -{b\over 2a} \quad \quad \omega = {\sqrt {4ac-b^2}\over 2a} \] Don’t memorize these formulas. Just remember that \(r_1\) and \(r_2\) are of the form (5.2.17), where \(\lambda \) is an arbitrary real number and \(\omega \) is positive; \(\lambda \) and \(\omega \) are the real and imaginary parts, respectively, of \(r_1\). Similarly, \(\lambda \) and \(-\omega \) are the real and imaginary parts of \(r_2\). We say that \(r_1\) and \(r_2\) are complex conjugates, which means that they have the same real part and their imaginary parts have the same absolute values, but opposite signs.
As in Example 5.2.3, it’s reasonable to to expect that the solutions of \(a\frac{d^2y}{dx^2}+b\frac{dy}{dx}+cy=0\) are linear combinations of \(e^{(\lambda +i\omega )x}\) and \(e^{(\lambda -i\omega )x}\). Again, the exponential notation suggests that \[ e^{(\lambda +i\omega )x}=e^{\lambda x}e^{i\omega x} \quad \mbox{ and } \quad e^{(\lambda -i\omega )x}=e^{\lambda x}e^{-i\omega x} \] so even though we haven’t defined \(e^{i\omega x}\) and \(e^{-i\omega x}\), it’s reasonable to expect that every linear combination of \(e^{(\lambda +i\omega )x}\) and \(e^{(\lambda -i\omega )x}\) can be written as \(y=ue^{\lambda x}\), where \(u\) depends upon \(x\). To determine \(u\) we first observe that since \(r_1=\lambda +i\omega \) and \(r_2=\lambda -i\omega \) are the roots of the characteristic equation, \(p\) must be of the form \[ \begin {array}{ccl} p(r)&=&a(r-r_1)(r-r_2)\\ &=&a(r-\lambda -i\omega )(r-\lambda +i\omega )\\ &=& a \left [(r-\lambda )^2+\omega ^2\right ]\\ &=&a(r^2-2\lambda r +\lambda ^2+\omega ^2) \end {array} \] Therefore \(a\frac{d^2y}{dx^2}+b\frac{dy}{dx}+cy=0\) can be written as \[ a\left [\frac{d^2y}{dx^2}-2\lambda \frac{dy}{dx}+(\lambda ^2+\omega ^2)y\right ]=0 \] Since \(a\ne 0\) this equation has the same solutions as \begin {equation} \label {eq:5.2.18} \frac{d^2y}{dx^2}-2\lambda \frac{dy}{dx}+(\lambda ^2+\omega ^2)y=0 \end {equation} To determine \(u\) we note that if \(y=ue^{\lambda x}\) then \[ \frac{dy}{dx} =\frac{du}{dx}e^{\lambda x}+\lambda ue^{\lambda x}\quad \mbox{ and } \quad \frac{d^2y}{dx^2} =\frac{d^2u}{dx^2}e^{\lambda x}+2\lambda \frac{du}{dx}e^{\lambda x}+\lambda ^2ue^{\lambda x} \] Substituting these expressions into (5.2.18) and dropping the common factor \(e^{\lambda x}\) yields \[ \left(\frac{d^2u}{dx^2}+2\lambda \frac{du}{dx}+\lambda ^2 u\right) -2\lambda \left(\frac{du}{dx}+\lambda u\right) +\left(\lambda ^2+\omega ^2\right)u=0 \] which simplifies to \[ \frac{d^2u}{dx^2}+\omega ^2 u=0 \] From Example  5.1.2, the general solution of this equation is \[ u=c_1\cos \omega x +c_2\sin \omega x \] Therefore any function of the form \begin {equation} \label {eq:5.2.19} y=e^{\lambda x}(c_1\cos \omega x+c_2\sin \omega x) \end {equation} is a solution of (5.2.18). Letting \(c_1=1\) and \(c_2=0\) here yields the solution \(y_1=e^{\lambda x}\cos \omega x\). Letting \(c_1=0\) and \(c_2=1\) yields a second solution \(y_2=e^{\lambda x}\sin \omega x\). Since \(y_2/y_1=\tan \omega x\) is nonconstant, so Theorem  5.1.6 implies that \(\{y_1,y_2\}\) is a fundamental set of solutions of (5.2.18), and (5.2.19) is the general solution.
Summary
The next theorem summarizes the results of this section.
Theorem 5.2.1 Let \(p(r)=ar^2+br+c\) be the characteristic polynomial of \begin {equation} \label {eq:5.2.20} a\frac{d^2y}{dx^2}+b\frac{dy}{dx}+cy=0 \end {equation} Then:
  • If \(p(r)=0\) has distinct real roots \(r_1\) and \(r_2,\) then the general solution of (5.2.20) is \[ y=c_1e^{r_1x}+c_2e^{r_2x}\]
  • If \(p(r)=0\) has a repeated root \(r_1,\) then the general solution of (5.2.20) is \[ y=e^{r_1x}(c_1+c_2x)\]
  • If \(p(r)=0\) has complex conjugate roots \(r_1=\lambda +i\omega \) and \(r_2=\lambda -i\omega \) \( (\omega >0),\) then the general solution of (5.2.20) is \[ y=e^{\lambda x}(c_1\cos \omega x+c_2\sin \omega x)\]
