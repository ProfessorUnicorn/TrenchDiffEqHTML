5.2 Constant Coefficient Homogeneous Equations
Constant Coefficient Homogeneous Equations
If \(a,b\), and \(c\) are real constants and \(a\ne 0\), then \[ a\frac{d^2y}{dx^2}+b\frac{dy}{dx}+cy=F(x) \] is said to be a constant coefficient equation. In this section we consider the homogeneous constant coefficient equation \begin {equation} \label {eq:5.2.1} a\frac{d^2y}{dx^2}+b\frac{dy}{dx}+cy=0 \end {equation} As we’ll see, all solutions of (5.2.1) are defined on \((-\infty ,\infty )\). This being the case, we’ll omit references to the interval on which solutions are defined, or on which a given set of solutions is a fundamental set, etc., since the interval will always be \((-\infty ,\infty )\).
The key to solving (5.2.1) is that if \(y=e^{rx}\) where \(r\) is a constant then the left side of (5.2.1) is a multiple of \(e^{rx}\) \[ y=e^{rx} \Longrightarrow \frac{dy}{dx}=re^{rx} \Longrightarrow \frac{d^2y}{dx^2}=r^2e^{rx} \] Substituting into (5.2.1) \begin{align} \label{eq:5.2.2} a \frac{d^2y}{dx^2} + b \frac{dy}{dx} + c y = & 0\nonumber \\ a \Big( r^2e^{rx} \Big) + b \Big( re^{rx} \Big) + c \Big( e^{rx} \Big) = & 0 \nonumber \\ ar^2e^{rx} + bre^{rx} + ce^{rx} = & 0 \nonumber \\ \big( ar^2 + br + c \big) e^{rx} = & 0 \\ ar^2 + br + c = 0 \quad \text{or} & \quad e^{rx} = 0 \nonumber \end{align} Now since the factor \( e^{rx} \) is never zero, the only way to solve (5.2.2) would be when the quadratic polynomial is zero: \[ ar^2 + br + c = 0 \]
We call this equation the characteristic equation (also auxiliary equation) of (5.2.1) and we call the quadratic polynomial \(p(r)=ar^2+br+c \) the characteristic polynomial. As the characteristic equation is quadratic, the roots are given by the quadratic formula \begin {equation} \label {eq:5.2.3} r={-b\pm \sqrt {b^2-4ac}\over 2a} \end {equation}
We consider three cases:
Case 1: \(b^2-4ac>0\), so the characteristic equation has two distinct real roots
Case 2: \(b^2-4ac=0\), so the characteristic equation has a repeated real root
Case 3: \(b^2-4ac<0\), so the characteristic equation has complex roots
Case 1: Distinct Real Roots
  1. Find the general solution \begin {equation} \label {eq:5.2.4} \frac{d^2y}{dx^2}+6\frac{dy}{dx}+5y=0 \end {equation}
  2. Solve the IVP \begin {equation} \label {eq:5.2.5} \frac{d^2y}{dx^2}+6\frac{dy}{dx}+5y=0 \quad y(0)=3 \quad \left. \frac{dy}{dx} \right|_{0}=-1 \end {equation}
Solution
  1. We start by seeking solutions of the form \( y=e^{rx} \) \[ y=e^{rx} \Longrightarrow \frac{dy}{dx}=re^{rx} \Longrightarrow \frac{d^2y}{dx^2}=r^2e^{rx} \] Substituting these in we obtain: \begin{align*} \frac{d^2y}{dx^2} + 6 \frac{dy}{dx} + 5 y = & 0 \\ \Big( r^2 e^{rx} \Big) + 6 \Big( r e^{rx} \Big) + 5 \Big( e^{rx} \Big) = & 0 \\ \left( r^2 + 6 r + 5 \right) e^{rx} = & 0 \\ r^2 + 6 r + 5 = & 0 \quad \text{ or } \quad e^{rx} = 0 \\ \end{align*} Where \( p(r) = r^2 + 6 r + 5 \) is the characteristic polynomial and since \( e^{rx} \ne 0 \) to solve (5.2.4) we must solve the characteristic equation which is always a quadratic equation for second order differential equations so this is always solvable using the quadratic formula (5.2.3) but this one also happens to be factorable: \begin{align*} r^2 + 6 r + 5 = & 0 \\ \left( r + 1 \right) \left( r + 5 \right) = & 0 \\ r + 1= 0 \quad \text{or} \quad r + 5 = & 0 \\ r = -1 \quad \text{or} \quad r = & -5 \\ \end{align*} Substituting in \(r = -1\) and \(r = -5\) into \( y = e^{rx} \) we obtain solutions of (5.2.4) \[ y_1 = e^{-x} \quad \text{ and } \quad y_2 = e^{-5x} \]
    Now we would like to know if \( \{ y_1, y_2 \} = \{ e^{-x}, e^{-5x}\} \) is a fundamental set so we check if they are linearly independent: \begin{align*} \frac{y_2}{y_1} =& \frac{e^{-5x}}{e^{-x}} = e^{-5x+x} = e^{-4x} \\ \end{align*} Since the ratio \({y_1}/{y_2}\) is not constant, then \(y_1\) and \(y_2\) are linearly independent, so \( \{ y_1, y_2 \} = \{ e^{-x}, e^{-5x}\} \) is a fundamental set and therefore from Theorem 5.1.6 \(y=c_1y_1 + c_2y_2\) is the general solution: \begin{equation} \label{eq:5.2.6} y = c_1e^{-x} + c_2e^{-5x} \end{equation}
    Verify (5.2.6) is a general solution of (5.2.4)
  2. We already found the general solution (5.2.6) and now we apply the initial conditions to find the constants \(c_1\) and \(c_2\) to solve the IVP (5.2.5). Starting with \( y(0)=3 \) \begin{align*} y(x) = & c_1e^{-x} + c_2e^{-5x} \\ y(0) = & c_1e^{-0} + c_2e^{-5\cdot 0} \\ 3 = & c_1e^{0} + c_2e^{0} \\ 3 = & c_1 + c_2 \\ \end{align*} So we obtain the equation \( c_1 + c_2 = 3 \) and now we will need another equation in order to solve for the constants so we use the other initial condition which is at the derivative of our general solution (5.2.6)
    Differentiate (5.2.6)
    \begin{align*} y = & c_1e^{-x} + c_2e^{-5x} \nonumber \\ \frac{dy}{dx} = & \frac{d}{dx} \Big( c_1e^{-x} + c_2e^{-5x} \Big) \nonumber \\ \frac{dy}{dx} = & - c_1 e^{-x} -5 c_2 e^{-5x} \\ \end{align*} Now we can apply the initial condition \( \left. \displaystyle \frac{dy}{dx} \right|_{0}=-1 \) \begin{align} \left. \frac{dy}{dx} \right|_0 = & - c_1 e^{0} -5 c_2 e^{0} \nonumber \\ -1 = & - c_1 -5 c_2 \nonumber \\ 1 = & c_1 + 5 c_2 \nonumber \end{align}
    Solve the system \(\left\{ \begin{array}{r l l} c_1 + c_2 &=& 3 \\ c_1 + 5c_2 &=& 1 \\ \end{array} \right.\)
    So we obtain the system of equations: And we obtain a system of equations which we can solve by subtracting them to find \(c_2\) then substituting that back to find \(c_1\) \begin{align*} \left\{ \begin{array}{r l l} c_1 + c_2 &=& 3 \\ c_1 + 5c_2 &=& 1 \\ \end{array} \right. \quad \longrightarrow \left\{ \begin{array}{r l r} c_1 + c_2 &=& 3 \\ 4c_2 &=& -2 \\ \end{array} \right. \quad \longrightarrow \left\{ \begin{array}{r l r} \displaystyle c_1 - \frac{1}{2} &=& 3 \\ c_2 &=& \displaystyle -\frac{1}{2} \\ \end{array} \right. \quad \longrightarrow \left\{ \begin{array}{r l l} c_2 &=& \displaystyle \frac{7}{2} \\ c_1 &=& \displaystyle -\frac{1}{2} \\ \end{array} \right. \end{align*}
    Substituting \( c_1 = -\frac{1}{2} \) and \( c_2 = \frac{7}{2} \) into (5.2.6) we obtain the particular solution of the IVP (5.2.5) \begin{equation} \label{eq:5.2.7} y = -\frac{1}{2} e^{-x} + \frac{7}{2} e^{-5x} \end{equation}
    Verify (5.2.7) is a of (5.2.5)
    Solve the IVP with equation (5.2.4) and initial conditions \( \quad y(0)=2 \quad \left. \frac{dy}{dx} \right|_{0}=-4 \)
Case 2: A Repeated Real Root
  1. Find the general solution \begin {equation} \label {eq:5.2.8} \frac{d^2y}{dx^2}+6\frac{dy}{dx}+9y=0 \end {equation}
  3. Solve the IVP \begin {equation} \label {eq:5.2.9} \frac{d^2y}{dx^2}+6\frac{dy}{dx}+9y=0 \quad y(0)=3 \quad \left. \frac{dy}{dx} \right|_{0}=-1 \end {equation}
Solution
  1. We start by seeking solutions of the form \( y=e^{rx} \) \[ y=e^{rx} \Longrightarrow \frac{dy}{dx}=re^{rx} \Longrightarrow \frac{d^2y}{dx^2}=r^2e^{rx} \] Substituting these in we obtain: \begin{align*} \frac{d^2y}{dx^2} + 6 \frac{dy}{dx} + 9 y = & 0 \\ \Big( r^2 e^{rx} \Big) + 6 \Big( r e^{rx} \Big) + 9 \Big( e^{rx} \Big) = & 0 \\ \left( r^2 + 6 r + 9 \right) e^{rx} = & 0 \\ r^2 + 6 r + 9 = & 0 \quad \text{ or } \quad e^{rx} = 0 \\ \end{align*} Where \( p(r) = r^2 + 6 r + 9 \) is the characteristic polynomial and since \( e^{rx} \ne 0 \) to solve (5.2.8) we must solve the characteristic equation which is always a quadratic equation for second order differential equations so this is always solvable using the quadratic formula (5.2.3) but again this one happens to be factorable: \begin{align*} r^2 + 6 r + 9 = & 0 \\ \left( r + 3 \right) \left( r + 3 \right) = & 0 \\ r + 3= 0 \quad \text{or} \quad r + 3 = & 0 \\ r = -3 \quad \text{or} \quad r = & -3 \\ \end{align*} Substituting \(r = -3\) into \( y = e^{rx} \) we obtain one solution of (5.2.8) \[ y_1 = e^{-3x} \] Since the other root was the same repeated root, it will not produce another linearly independent solution. For instance, substituting in \(r = -3\) again we obtain \[ y_2 = e^{-3x} \] But \(y_1\) and \(y_2\) are not linearly independent since their ratio is constant, and in fact, equal to one: \begin{align*} \frac{y_2}{y_1} =& \frac{e^{-3x}}{e^{-3x}} = 1 \\ \end{align*} Therefore we only have one solution from our fundamental set but since a second order differential equation must have two linearly independent solutions to form a fundamental set, we will now use variation of parameters to find another solution of the form \( y = u y_1 = u \cdot e^{-3x} \)
    Differentiate \( y = u \cdot e^{-3x} \)
    Differentiating \( y = u \cdot e^{-3x} \) we obtain \[ \frac{dy}{dx} = \left( \frac{du}{dx} - 3u \right) e^{-3x} \]
    Differentiate \( \displaystyle \frac{dy}{dx} = \left( \frac{du}{dx} - 3u \right) e^{-3x} \)
    Differentiating \( \displaystyle \frac{dy}{dx} = \left( \frac{du}{dx} - 3u \right) e^{-3x}\) we obtain \[ \frac{d^2y}{dx^2} = \left( \frac{d^2u}{dx^2} - 6 \frac{du}{dx} + 9 u \right) e^{-3x} \]
  2. Now we substitute the derivatives into our original differential equation (5.2.8) to find a new simpler differential equation in \(u\) (a special case of a method called reduction of order) by substituting the new expressions for \( y ,\) \( {dy}/{dx} \) and \( {d^2y}/{dx^2} \) (in terms of \(u \)) into (5.2.8) and simplifying \begin{align} \frac{d^2y}{dx^2} + 6 \frac{dy}{dx} + 9 y = & 0 \nonumber \\ \Big( \frac{d^2y}{dx^2} \Big) + 6 \Big( \frac{dy}{dx} \Big) + 9 \Big( y \Big) = & 0 \nonumber \\ \Big( \frac{d^2u}{dx^2} - 6 \frac{du}{dx} + 9 u \Big)e^{-3x} + 6 \Big( \frac{du}{dx} - 3u \Big)e^{-3x} + 9 \Big( u \Big)e^{-3x} = & 0 \nonumber \\ \Bigg( \Big( \frac{d^2u}{dx^2} - 6 \frac{du}{dx} + 9 u \Big) + \Big( 6\frac{du}{dx} - 18u \Big) + \Big( 9u \Big) \Bigg) e^{-3x} = & 0 \nonumber \\ \frac{d^2u}{dx^2} e^{-3x} = & 0 \nonumber \\ \frac{d^2u}{dx^2} = & 0 \nonumber \\ \end{align} And we get the simple second order equation which can be solved using antidifferentiation: \begin{align} \frac{d^2u}{dx^2} = & 0 \nonumber \\ \int \Big( \frac{d^2u}{dx^2} \Big) dx = & \int \Big( 0 \Big) dx \nonumber \\ \frac{du}{dx} = & c \nonumber \\ \int \Big( \frac{du}{dx} \Big) dx = & \int \Big( c \Big) dx \nonumber \\ u =& c_1 + c_2 x \nonumber \\ \end{align} So we have found \(u\) and we multiply by \( e^{-3x}\) to get \(y\) \begin{align} y =& \left( c_1 + c_2 x \right) e^{-3x} \end{align} Now we know that any function of the form (5.2.10) is a solution of (5.2.8). Notice that substituting \(c_1=0\) and \(c_2=1\) then we get \(y=e^{-3x}\) which is the solution we already found earlier using the characteristic equation. In order to obtain a fundamental set we would like another solution of the form (5.2.10) that is linearly independent of our known solution, so we let \(c_1=1\) and \(c_2=0\) to obtain a the solution \(y_2=xe^{-3x}\) and now we just need to verify that \(e^{-3x}\) and \(xe^{-3x}\) are linearly independent \begin{align*} \frac{y_2}{y_1} = \frac{xe^{-3x}}{e^{-3x}} = x \end{align*} Since their ratio is non constant then they are linearly independent thus \( \{ e^{-3x} ,xe^{-3x} \}\) is a fundamental set of solutions for (5.2.8) and thus the general solution is \( y = c_1 e^{-3x} + c_2 x e^{-3x} \) which is the same solution we got earlier in equation (5.2.10)
  3. We already found the general solution (5.2.10) and now we apply the initial conditions to find the constants \(c_1\) and \(c_2\) to solve the IVP (5.2.9). Starting with \( y(0)=3 \) \begin{align*} y(x) = & \left( c_1 + c_2 x \right) e^{-3x} \\ y(0) = & \left( c_1 + c_2 \cdot 0 \right) e^{-3 \cdot 0} \\ 3 = & c_1 e^{0} \Longrightarrow c_1 = 3 \end{align*} Now the other initial condition is at the derivative of our general solution (5.2.10)
    Differentiate (5.2.10)
    \begin{align} \label{eq:5.2.11} y = & \left( c_1 + c_2 x \right) e^{-3x} \nonumber \\ \frac{dy}{dx} = & \frac{d}{dx} \Big( c_1e^{-x} + c_2e^{-5x} \Big) \nonumber \\ \frac{dy}{dx} = & \Big( c_2 - 9 - 3c_2 x \Big) e^{-3x} \\ \end{align} Now we can apply the initial condition \( \left. \displaystyle \frac{dy}{dx} \right|_{0}=-1 \) \begin{align} \left. \frac{dy}{dx} \right|_{0} = & \Big( c_2 - 9 - 3c_2 \cdot 0 \Big) e^{-3 \cdot 0 } \nonumber \\ -1 = & \Big( c_2 - 9 \Big) e^{ 0 } \nonumber \\ -1 = & c_2 - 9 \Longrightarrow c_2 = 8 \nonumber \end{align}
    Substituting \( c_1 = 3 \) and \( c_2 = 8 \) into (5.2.10) we obtain the particular solution of the IVP (5.2.9) \begin{equation*} y = (3 + 8x) e^{-3x} \end{equation*}
    Verify \(y = (3 + 8x) e^{-3x}\) is a solution of (5.2.8)
    Solve the IVP (5.2.8) with initial conditions \( \quad y(0)=2 \quad \left. \frac{dy}{dx} \right|_{0}=-4 \)






If the characteristic equation of \(a\frac{d^2y}{dx^2}+b\frac{dy}{dx}+cy=0\) has an arbitrary repeated root \(r_1\), the characteristic polynomial must be \[ p(r)=a(r-r_1)^2=a(r^2-2r_1r+r_1^2)\] Therefore \[ ar^2+br+c=ar^2-(2ar_1)r+ar_1^2\] which implies that \(b=-2ar_1\) and \(c=ar_1^2\). Therefore \(a\frac{d^2y}{dx^2}+b\frac{dy}{dx}+cy=0\) can be written as \(a(\frac{d^2y}{dx^2}-2r_1\frac{dy}{dx}+r_1^2y)=0\). Since \(a\ne 0\) this equation has the same solutions as \begin {equation} \label {eq:5.2.12} \frac{d^2y}{dx^2}-2r_1\frac{dy}{dx}+r_1^2y=0 \end {equation}
Since \(p(r_1)=0\), t \(y_1=e^{r_1x}\) is a solution of \(a\frac{d^2y}{dx^2}+b\frac{dy}{dx}+cy=0\), and therefore of (5.2.12). Proceeding as in Example 5.2.2, we look for other solutions of (5.2.12) of the form \(y=ue^{r_1x}\); then \[ \frac{dy}{dx}=\frac{du}{dx}e^{r_1x}+rue^{r_1x}\quad \mbox{ and } \quad \frac{d^2y}{dx^2}=\frac{d^2u}{dx^2}e^{r_1x}+2r_1\frac{du}{dx}e^{r_1x}+r_1^2ue^{r_1x}\] so
\begin {eqnarray*} \frac{d^2y}{dx^2}-2r_1\frac{dy}{dx}+r_1^2y&=&e^{rx}\left [(\frac{d^2u}{dx^2}+2r_1\frac{du}{dx}+r_1^2u)- 2r_1(\frac{du}{dx}+r_1u)+r_1^2u\right ]\\ &=&e^{r_1x}\left [\frac{d^2u}{dx^2}+(2r_1-2r_1)\frac{du}{dx}+(r_1^2-2r_1^2+r_1^2)u\right ]=\frac{d^2u}{dx^2}e^{r_1x} \end {eqnarray*}
Therefore \(y=ue^{r_1x}\) is a solution of (5.2.12) if and only if \(\frac{d^2u}{dx^2}=0\), which is equivalent to \(u=c_1+c_2x\), where \(c_1\) and \(c_2\) are constants. Hence, any function of the form \begin {equation} \label {eq:5.2.13} y=e^{r_1x}(c_1+c_2x) \end {equation} is a solution of (5.2.12). Letting \(c_1=1\) and \(c_2=0\) here yields the solution \(y_1=e^{r_1x}\) that we already knew. Letting \(c_1=0\) and \(c_2=1\) yields the second solution \(y_2=xe^{r_1x}\). Since \(y_2/y_1=x\) is nonconstant, 5.1.6 implies that \(\{y_1,y_2\}\) is a fundamental set of solutions of (5.2.12), and (5.2.13) is the general solution. Although this is true in general but we will still always use variation of parameters to show the work to find the second solution in the case of repeated roots.
Case 3: Complex Conjugate Roots
  1. Find the general solution of \begin {equation} \label {eq:5.2.14} \frac{d^2y}{dx^2} + 4 \frac{dy}{dx} + 13 y = 0 \end {equation}
  2. Solve the initial value problem \begin {equation} \label {eq:5.2.15} \frac{d^2y}{dx^2}+4\frac{dy}{dx}+13y=0 \quad y(0)=2 \quad \left. \frac{dy}{dx} \right|_{0}=-3 \end {equation}
Solution
  1. We start by seeking solutions of the form \( y=e^{rx} \) \[ y=e^{rx} \Longrightarrow \frac{dy}{dx}=re^{rx} \Longrightarrow \frac{d^2y}{dx^2}=r^2e^{rx} \] Substituting these in we obtain: \begin{align*} \frac{d^2y}{dx^2} + 4 \frac{dy}{dx} + 13 y = & 0 \\ \Big( r^2 e^{rx} \Big) + 4 \Big( r e^{rx} \Big) + 13 \Big( e^{rx} \Big) = & 0 \\ \left( r^2 + 4 r + 13 \right) e^{rx} = & 0 \\ r^2 + 4 r + 13 = & 0 \quad \text{ or } \quad e^{rx} = 0 \\ \end{align*} Where \( p(r) = r^2 + 4 r + 13 \) is the characteristic polynomial and since \( e^{rx} \ne 0 \) to solve (5.2.8) we must solve the characteristic equation which is always a quadratic equation for second order differential equations so this is always solvable using the quadratic formula (5.2.3): \begin{align*} r= & \frac{-(4)\pm\sqrt{(4)^2-4\cdot(1)\cdot(13)}}{2\cdot(1)} \\ r= & \frac{-4\pm\sqrt{4\cdot(4-13)}}{2} = \frac{-4\pm\sqrt{4\cdot(-9)}}{2} \\ r= & \frac{-4\pm\sqrt{-36}}{2} = \frac{-4\pm6i}{2} = -2 \pm 3i\\ r= & -2 + 3i \quad \text{or} \quad r= -2 - 3i\\ \end{align*} Substituting \(r = -2 + 3i \) and \(r = -2 - 3i \) into \( y = e^{rx} \) we obtain solutions of (5.2.14) \[ y_1 = e^{( -2 + 3i)x} \quad \text{or} \quad y_2 = e^{( -2 - 3i)x} \]
    So we have two distinct solutions, just as in Example 5.2.1 and we can check them exactly the same way, by differentiating and substituting
    Differentiate \( y_1 = e^{( -2 + 3i)x} \) twice
    Differentiating \( y_1 = e^{( -2 + 3i)x} \) we obtain \begin{align*} \frac{dy_1}{dx} = & ( -2 + 3i)e^{( -2 + 3i)x} \\ \frac{d^2y_1}{dx^2} = & ( -2 + 3i)^2 e^{( -2 + 3i)x} \\ \end{align*}
    Simplify \(( -2 + 3i)^2\) by distributing and using \(i^2=-1\)
    Substituting \(y_1,\) \(dy_1/dx,\) and \(d^2y_1/dx^2\) back into (5.2.14) \begin{align*} \begin{array}{rrrrr} \displaystyle\frac{d^2y_1}{dx^2} &=& &\Big(& -&5& - &12i& &\Big)& e^{( -2 + 3i)x} \\ 4\displaystyle\frac{dy_1}{dx} &=& &\Big(& -&8& + &12i& &\Big)& e^{( -2 + 3i)x} \\ + \quad\quad\quad 13y_1 &=& &\Big(& -&13& & & &\Big)& e^{( -2 + 3i)x} \\ \hline {\small \displaystyle\frac{d^2y_1}{dx^2} + 4\frac{dy_1}{dx} + 13y_1} &=& &\Big(& &0& + &0i& &\Big)& e^{( -2 + 3i)x} \\ \end{array} \end{align*} So we find that \(y_1 = e^{( -2 + 3i)x} \) does satisfy equation (5.2.14) \begin{align*} \frac{d^2y_1}{dx^2} + 4\frac{dy_1}{dx} + 13y_1 = 0 \end{align*}
    Differentiate \( y_2 = e^{( -2 - 3i)x} \) twice
    Simplify \(( -2 - 3i)^2\) by distributing and using \(i^2=-1\)
    Substitute \( y_2 = e^{( -2 - 3i)x} \) and its two derivatives back into (5.2.14) to verify
    Going through the same steps with \( y_2 = e^{( -2 - 3i)x} \) yields that both are solutions to (5.2.14) so we might just be tempted to say we found our fundamental set of solutions and have the general solution: \[ y = c_1 e^{( -2 + 3i)x} + c_2 e^{( -2 - 3i)x} \] However, while the answer may seem simple enough on first inspection, further reflection reminds us that this function is much different from all others we have encountered so far in that it is a function of complex variables, something we really haven't ever studied at all up to this point in school. You probably remember studying complex numebers themselves and the basic arithematic operations on them, which we used for verifying that \( y_1 = e^{( -2 + 3i)x} \) and \( y_2 = e^{( -2 - 3i)x} \) were even solutions of our equation, but you probably have never worked with functions of complex variables as this topic is typically reserved for a more advanced class. In general we prefer to work with real-valued functions (the only kind we have ever worked with) so we will instead do a little bit of work to translate this complex solution into real values. To start we'll split up the exponential into the real and imaginary parts, starting with \( y_1 = e^{( -2 + 3i)x} \): \begin{align*} y_1 =& e^{( -2 + 3i)x} = e^{-2x}e^{3ix}\\ \end{align*}
    The first factor \(e^{-2x}\) is familiar enough as it is a typical solution we have encountered the the previous cases. However, the second factor \(e^{3ix}\) is not clearly defined to us since we have not really studied complex-valued functions before. Although \(e^{3ix}\) is unfamiliar, we will use a familiar technique of Taylor series expansions from Calculus to grasp it. Recall the Maclaurin series of a function \(f(x)\) is defined by \begin{align*} \sum _{n=0}^\infty {f^{(n)}(0)\over n!}x^n = {f(0)} + {f^{'}(0)} \, x + {f^{''}(0)\over 2!} \, x^2 + \cdots \end{align*} Now let's expand \(f(x) = e^{3ix}\) using this method. To start we will need several derivatives, so let's calculate the first seven derivatives
    Differentiate \(f(x) = e^{3ix}\) seven times and simplify using \(i^2=-1\)
    Evaluate \(f(x) = e^{3ix}\) and its seven derivatives at \(x=0\)
    \begin{array}{ccrccr} f(x) &=& e^{3ix} \quad & \quad & \quad f(0) &=& 1 \\ f^{'}(x) &=& 3ie^{3ix} \quad & \quad & \quad f^{'}(0) &=& 3i \\ f^{''}(x) &=& -9e^{3ix} \quad & \quad & \quad f^{''}(0) &=& -9 \\ f^{'''}(x) &=& -27i e^{3ix} \quad & \quad & \quad f^{'''}(0) &=& -27i \\ f^{''''}(x) &=& 81 e^{3ix} \quad & \quad & \quad f^{''''}(0) &=& 81 \\ f^{^V}(x) &=& 243i e^{3ix} \quad & \quad & \quad f^{^V}(0) &=& 243i \\ f^{^{VI}}(x) &=& -729 e^{3ix} \quad & \quad & \quad f^{^{VI}}(0) &=& -729 \\ f^{^{VII}}(x) &=& -2187i e^{3ix} \quad & \quad & \quad f^{^{VII}}(0) &=& -2187i \\ \end{array}
    Expand the Maclaurin series formula \(\displaystyle \sum _{n=0}^\infty {f^{(n)}(0)\over n!}x^n\) to eight terms
    Expanding the Maclaurin series to eight terms we obtain \begin{align*} \sum _{n=0}^\infty {f^{(n)}(0)\over n!}x^n = & {f(0)} + {f^{'}(0)} \, x + {f^{''}(0)\over 2!} \, x^2 + {f^{'''}(0)\over 3!} \, x^3 + {f^{''''}(0)\over 4!} \, x^4 \\ & + {f^{^{V}}(0)\over 5!} \, x^5 + {f^{^{VI}}(0)\over 6!} \, x^6 + {f^{^{VII}}(0)\over 7!} \, x^7 + \cdots \end{align*}
    Substitute in the coefficients into the Maclaurin series expansion
    Substituting in the coefficients we calculated from differentiating \(f(x) = e^{3ix}\) and evaluating them at \(x=0\) we obtain the series expansion for \(e^{3ix}\) and grouping the real terms together and imaginary terms together we are able to rewrite our function in terms of sine and cosine \begin{align*} e^{3ix} = & 1 + 3i\, x - {9\over 2!} \, x^2 - {27i\over 3!} \, x^3 + {81\over 4!} \, x^4 + {243i\over 5!} \, x^5 - {729\over 6!} \, x^6 - {2187i\over 7!} \, x^7 + \cdots \\ = & \underset{\cos(3x)}{\underbrace{\left( 1 - {9\over 2!} \, x^2 + {81\over 4!} \, x^4 - {729\over 6!} \, x^6 + \cdots \right)}} + i \underset{\sin(3x)}{\underbrace{\left( 3\, x - {27\over 3!} \, x^3 + {243\over 5!} \, x^5 - {2187\over 7!} \, x^7 + \cdots \right)}} \\ \end{align*}
    Differentiate \(\sin 3x\) and \(\cos 3x\) six times
    Write out seven terms of the Maclaurin series expansion of \(\sin 3x\) and \(\cos 3x\)
    Interestingly we have stumbled upon a way to convert exponentials with imaginary arguments into to sines and cosines with real arguments with the identity: \[ e^{3ix} = \cos 3x + i \sin 3x \] And, in fact, this is a famous identity, known as Euler's formula: \[ e^{ix} = \cos x + i \sin x \]
    Substituting \(e^{3ix} = \cos 3x + i \sin 3x\) back in to our solutions so we have \begin{align*} y_1 =& e^{( -2 + 3i)x} = e^{-2x}e^{3ix} = \big( \cos 3x + i \sin 3x \big) e^{-2x} \\ y_2 =& e^{( -2 - 3i)x} = e^{-2x}e^{-3ix} = \big( \cos 3x - i \sin 3x \big) e^{-2x} \\ \end{align*}
    And again we can check them exactly the same way, by differentiating and substituting
    Differentiate \( y_1 = \big( \cos 3x + i \sin 3x \big) e^{-2x} \) twice

    Differentiate \( y_2 = \big( \cos 3x - i \sin 3x \big) e^{-2x} \) twice

    Going through the same steps with \( y_2 = \big( \cos 3x - i \sin 3x \big) e^{-2x} \) yields that both are solutions to (5.2.14) so we might just be tempted to say we found our fundamental set of solutions \[ y_1 = \big( \cos 3x + i \sin 3x \big) e^{-2x} \quad y_2 = \big( \cos 3x - i \sin 3x \big) e^{-2x} \] However we still have a couple of simplification issues we need to resolve since to start with, we still have an imaginary number \(i\) present in the solutions, although at least it is just as a constant multiplier and not as an argument on the exponential nor on the trig functions. Additionally, both solutions \(y_1\) and \(y_2\) have \(\cos 3x\) and \(\sin 3x\) terms, which implies we might be able to simplify them, and in fact, we can.
    Now from Theorem 5.1.2, we know that since \(y_1\) and \(y_2\) are solutions of the homogeneous equation (5.2.14) then any linear combination \(y=c_1y_1+c_2y_2 \) is also a solution. Thus adding \(y_1 + y_2\) and dividing by \(2\) we obtain one solution \[ \frac{y_1 + y_2}{2} = e^{-2x} \cos 3x \]
    Verify \(y = e^{-2x} \cos 3x\) is a solution to (5.2.14)

Now suppose the characteristic equation of \(a\frac{d^2y}{dx^2}+b\frac{dy}{dx}+cy=0\) has arbitrary complex roots thus \(b^2-4ac<0\) and, from (5.2.3), the roots are \[ r_1 = {-b+i\sqrt {4ac-b^2}\over 2a} \quad \quad r_2 = {-b-i\sqrt {4ac-b^2}\over 2a} \] which we rewrite as \begin {equation} \label {eq:5.2.17} r_1=\lambda +i \omega \quad \quad r_2 = \lambda - i \omega \end {equation} with \[ \lambda = -{b\over 2a} \quad \quad \omega = {\sqrt {4ac-b^2}\over 2a} \] Don’t memorize these formulas. Just remember that \(r_1\) and \(r_2\) are of the form (5.2.17), where \(\lambda \) is an arbitrary real number and \(\omega \) is positive; \(\lambda \) and \(\omega \) are the real and imaginary parts, respectively, of \(r_1\). Similarly, \(\lambda \) and \(-\omega \) are the real and imaginary parts of \(r_2\). We say that \(r_1\) and \(r_2\) are complex conjugates, which means that they have the same real part and their imaginary parts have the same absolute values, but opposite signs.
As in Example 5.2.3, it’s reasonable to to expect that the solutions of \(a\frac{d^2y}{dx^2}+b\frac{dy}{dx}+cy=0\) are linear combinations of \(e^{(\lambda +i\omega )x}\) and \(e^{(\lambda -i\omega )x}\). Again, the exponential notation suggests that \[ e^{(\lambda +i\omega )x}=e^{\lambda x}e^{i\omega x} \quad \mbox{ and } \quad e^{(\lambda -i\omega )x}=e^{\lambda x}e^{-i\omega x} \] Then we will use Euler's formula to rewrite the exponential function of imaginary arguments as a trig functions with real arguments \begin{align} \label {eq:5.2.18} e^{i \omega x} = \cos \omega x + i \sin \omega x \end{align} Then finally we will use Theorem 5.1.2 to rewrite the solutions as linear combinations thus simplifying to a fundamental set \[ \big\{ y_1, y_2 \big\} = \Big\{ e^{\lambda x} \cos \omega x, e^{\lambda x} \sin \omega x \Big\} \] From Example  5.1.2, the general solution of this equation is \begin {equation} \label {eq:5.2.19} y= \big( c_1\cos \omega x+c_2\sin \omega x \big) e^{\lambda x} \end {equation} Letting \(c_1=1\) and \(c_2=0\) here yields the solution \(y_1=e^{\lambda x}\cos \omega x\). Letting \(c_1=0\) and \(c_2=1\) yields a second solution \(y_2=e^{\lambda x}\sin \omega x\). Since \(y_2/y_1=\tan \omega x\) is nonconstant, so Theorem  5.1.6 implies that \(\{y_1,y_2\}\) is a fundamental set of solutions and (5.2.19) is the general solution.



Theorem 5.2.1 Let \(p(r)=ar^2+br+c\) be the characteristic polynomial of \begin {equation} \label {eq:5.2.20} a\frac{d^2y}{dx^2}+b\frac{dy}{dx}+cy=0 \end {equation} Then:
  • If \(p(r)=0\) has distinct real roots \(r_1\) and \(r_2,\) then the general solution of (5.2.20) is \[ y=c_1e^{r_1x}+c_2e^{r_2x}\]
  • If \(p(r)=0\) has a repeated root \(r_1,\) then the general solution of (5.2.20) is \[ y=(c_1+c_2x) e^{r_1x} \]
  • If \(p(r)=0\) has complex conjugate roots \(r_1=\lambda +i\omega \) and \(r_2=\lambda -i\omega \) \( (\omega >0),\) then the general solution of (5.2.20) is \[ y= (c_1\cos \omega x+c_2\sin \omega x) e^{\lambda x} \]
