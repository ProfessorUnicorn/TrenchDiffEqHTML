5.3 Nonhomogeneous Linear Equations
Linear Nonhomogeneous Equations
We’ll now consider the nonhomogeneous linear second order equation \begin {equation} \label {eq:5.3.1} \frac{d^2y}{dx^2}+p(x) \frac{dy}{dx}+q(x)y=f(x) \end {equation} where the forcing function \(f\) isn’t identically zero. The next theorem, an extension of Theorem 5.1.1, gives sufficient conditions for existence and uniqueness of solutions of IVPs for (5.3.1). We omit the proof, which is beyond the scope of this book.
Theorem 5.3.1 Suppose \(p,\) \(,q\) and \(f\) are continuous on an open interval \((a,b),\) let \(x_0\) be any point in \((a,b),\) and let \(k_0\) and \(k_1\) be arbitrary real numbers. Then the IVP \[ \frac{d^2y}{dx^2}+p(x) \frac{dy}{dx}+q(x)y=f(x), \quad y(x_0)=k_0,\quad \left. \frac{dy}{dx} \right| _{x_0}=k_1 \] has a unique solution on \((a,b)\).
To find the general solution of (5.3.1) on an interval \((a,b)\) where \(p\), \(q\), and \(f\) are continuous, it’s necessary to find the general solution of the associated homogeneous equation \begin {equation} \label {eq:5.3.2} \frac{d^2y}{dx^2}+p(x) \frac{dy}{dx}+q(x)y=0 \end {equation} on \((a,b)\). We call (5.3.2) the complementary equation for (5.3.1).
The next theorem shows how to find the general solution of (5.3.1) if we know one solution \(y_p\) of (5.3.1) and a fundamental set of solutions of (5.3.2). We call \(y_p\) a particular solution of (5.3.1); it can be any solution that we can find, one way or another.
Theorem 5.3.2 Suppose \(p\), \(q\), and \(f\) are continuous on \((a,b)\). Let \(y_p\) be a particular solution of \begin {equation} \label {eq:5.3.3} \frac{d^2y}{dx^2}+p(x) \frac{dy}{dx}+q(x)y=f(x) \end {equation} on \((a,b)\), and let \(\{y_1,y_2\}\) be a fundamental set of solutions of the complementary equation \begin {equation} \label {eq:5.3.4} \frac{d^2y}{dx^2}+p(x) \frac{dy}{dx}+q(x)y=0 \end {equation} on \((a,b)\). Then \(y\) is a solution of (5.3.3) on \((a,b)\) if and only if \begin {equation} \label {eq:5.3.5} y=y_p+c_1y_1+c_2y_2 \end {equation} where \(c_1\) and \(c_2\) are constants.
Proof We first show that \(y\) in (5.3.5) is a solution of (5.3.3) for any choice of the constants \(c_1\) and \(c_2\). Differentiating (5.3.5) twice yields \[ \frac{dy}{dx}= \frac{dy_p}{dx}+c_1 \frac{dy_1}{dx}+c_2 \frac{dy_2}{dx}\quad \mbox{ and } \quad \frac{d^2y}{dx^2}= \frac{d^2y_p}{dx^2}+ c_1 \frac{d^2y_1}{dx^2}+c_2 \frac{d^2y_2}{dx^2}\] so
\begin {eqnarray*} \frac{d^2y}{dx^2}+p(x) \frac{dy}{dx}+q(x)y&=&( \frac{d^2y_p}{dx^2}+c_1 \frac{d^2y_1}{dx^2}+c_2 \frac{d^2y_2}{dx^2}) +p(x)( \frac{dy_p}{dx}+c_1 \frac{dy_1}{dx}+c_2 \frac{dy_2}{dx})\\&&\; +q(x)(y_p+c_1y_1+c_2y_2)\\ &=&( \frac{d^2y_p}{dx^2}+p(x) \frac{dy_p}{dx}+q(x)y_p)+c_1( \frac{d^2y_1}{dx^2}+p(x) \frac{dy_1}{dx}+q(x)y_1)\\ &&\; +c_2( \frac{d^2y_2}{dx^2}+p(x) \frac{dy_2}{dx}+q(x)y_2)\\ &=& f+c_1\cdot 0+c_2\cdot 0=f \end {eqnarray*}
since \(y_p\) satisfies (5.3.3) and \(y_1\) and \(y_2\) satisfy (5.3.4).
Now we’ll show that every solution of (5.3.3) has the form (5.3.5) for some choice of the constants \(c_1\) and \(c_2\). Suppose \(y\) is a solution of (5.3.3). We’ll show that \(y-y_p\) is a solution of (5.3.4), and therefore of the form \(y-y_p=c_1y_1+c_2y_2\), which implies (5.3.5). To see this, we compute
\begin {eqnarray*} \frac{d^2}{dx^2} (y-y_p) + p(x) \frac{d}{dx}(y-y_p)+q(x)(y-y_p)&=&( \frac{d^2y}{dx^2}- \frac{d^2y_p}{dx^2})+p(x)( \frac{dy}{dx}- \frac{dy_p}{dx})\\ && +q(x)(y-y_p)\\ &=&( \frac{d^2y}{dx^2}+p(x) \frac{dy}{dx}+q(x)y)\\ && -( \frac{d^2y_p}{dx^2}+p(x) \frac{dy_p}{dx}+q(x)y_p)\\ &=&f(x)-f(x)=0 \end {eqnarray*}
since \(y\) and \(y_p\) both satisfy (5.3.3).
We say that (5.3.5) is the general solution of (5.3.3) on \((a,b)\).
If \(P_0\), \(P_1\), and \(F\) are continuous and \(P_0\) has no zeros on \((a,b)\), then Theorem 5.3.2 implies that the general solution of \begin {equation} \label {eq:5.3.6} P_0(x) \frac{d^2y}{dx^2}+P_1(x) \frac{dy}{dx}+P_2(x)y=F(x) \end {equation} on \((a,b)\) is \(y=y_p+c_1y_1+c_2y_2\), where \(y_p\) is a particular solution of (5.3.6) on \((a,b)\) and \(\{y_1,y_2\}\) is a fundamental set of solutions of \[ P_0(x) \frac{d^2y}{dx^2}+P_1(x) \frac{dy}{dx}+P_2(x)y=0 \] on \((a,b)\). To see this, we rewrite (5.3.6) as \[ \frac{d^2y}{dx^2}+{P_1(x)\over P_0(x)} \frac{dy}{dx}+{P_2(x)\over P_0(x)}y={F(x)\over P_0(x)} \] and apply Theorem 5.3.2 with \(p=P_1/P_0\), \(q=P_2/P_0\), and \(f=F/P_0\).
To avoid awkward wording in examples and exercises, we won’t specify the interval \((a,b)\) when we ask for the general solution of a specific linear second order equation, or for a fundamental set of solutions of a homogeneous linear second order equation. Let’s agree that this always means that we want the general solution (or a fundamental set of solutions, as the case may be) on every open interval on which \(p\), \(q\), and \(f\) are continuous if the equation is of the form (5.3.3), or on which \(P_0\), \(P_1\), \(P_2\), and \(F\) are continuous and \(P_0\) has no zeros, if the equation is of the form (5.3.6). We leave it to you to identify these intervals in specific examples and exercises.
For completeness, we point out that if \(P_0\), \(P_1\), \(P_2\), and \(F\) are all continuous on an open interval \((a,b)\), but \(P_0\) does have a zero in \((a,b)\), then (5.3.6) may fail to have a general solution on \((a,b)\) in the sense just defined. Exercises 42–44 illustrate this point for a homogeneous equation.
In this section we to limit ourselves to applications of Theorem 5.3.2 where we can guess at the form of the particular solution.
Polynomials
Given the nonhomogeneous equation \begin {equation} \label {eq:5.3.7} \frac{d^2y}{dx^2}+y=1 \end {equation}
  1. Solve the complementary equation
  2. Find a particular solution
  3. Find the general solution
  4. Solve the IVP \begin {equation} \label {eq:5.3.8} \frac{d^2y}{dx^2}+y=1 \quad y(0)=2 \quad \left. \frac{dy}{dx} \right|_{0}=7 \end {equation}
Solution
  1. Solve the complementary equation \[ \frac{d^2y}{dx^2}+y=0 \]
    Differentiate and substitute \( y = e^{rx} \) to find and solve the characteristic equation
    We obtain the characteristic equation \[ r^2 +1 = 0 \]
    Use the method in Example 5.2.3 to find a fundamental set of solutions
    Solving the characteristic equation obtain the solutions \( y = e^{ix} \) and \( y = e^{-ix} \) which leads to the fundamental set of solutions \[ y_1 = \cos x \quad y_2 = \sin x \]
  2. Now we will try to find a particular solution for (5.3.7) by guessing a solution based on the forcing function \[ \frac{d^2y}{dx^2}+y= \underset{f(x)}{\underbrace{1}} \]
    Since the forcing function \( f(x) = 1 \) is just a constant we will guess the constant solution \( y=C\) then differentiating we obtain \[ \frac{dy}{dx} = 0 \quad \frac{d^2y}{dx^2} = 0 \]
    Substituting into (5.3.7) \begin{align*} \underset{0}{\underbrace{\frac{d^2y}{dx^2}}} + \underset{C}{\underbrace{y}} = & 1 \\ 0 + C = & 1 \Longrightarrow C = 1\\ \end{align*}
    Thus we obtain the particular solution \[ y = 1 \]
  3. Now that we have a fundamental set of solutions to the complementary equation \( \{ y_1, y_2\} = \{ \cos x, \sin x \}\) and a particular solution to the nonhomogeneous equation \( y_p = 1 \), then we can use Theorem 5.3.2 to find the general solution \( y = y_p + c_1 y_1 + c_2 y_2 \) \[ y = 1 + c_1 \cos x + c_2 \sin x \]
    Verify \( y = 1 + c_1 \cos x + c_2 \sin x \) is a solution of (5.3.7)
  4. Solve the IVP (5.3.8)
    We have the general solution (5.3.7) and now we apply the initial conditions to find the constants.
    Applying \( y(0) = 2 \) we obtain \begin{align*} y(x) = & 1 + c_1 \cos x + c_2 \sin x \\ y(0) = & c_1 \cos 0 + c_2 \sin 0 \\ 2 = & c_1 \\ \end{align*} Substituting it in our solution becomes \begin{align*} y = & 1 + 2 \cos x + c_2 \sin x \\ \end{align*}
    Differentiate \( y = 1 + 2 \cos x + c_2 \sin x \)
    Differentiating we obtain \begin{align*} \frac{dy}{dx} = & 2 \sin x + c_2 \cos x \end{align*}
    Apply the initial condition \( \left. \frac{dy}{dx} \right| _{0}=7 \) to \( \frac{dy}{dx} = 2 \sin x + c_2 \cos x \) to find \( c_2\)
    So we obtain the particular solution of our IVP (5.3.8) \begin{equation} y = 1 + 2 \cos x + 7 \sin x \end{equation}
Given the nonhomogeneous equation \begin {equation} \label {eq:5.3.10} \frac{d^2y}{dx^2}-2 \frac{dy}{dx}+y=-3-x+x^2 \end {equation}
  1. Solve the complementary equation
  2. Find a particular solution
  3. Find the general solution
  4. Solve the IVP \begin {equation} \label {eq:5.3.11} \frac{d^2y}{dx^2}-2 \frac{dy}{dx}+y=x^2-x-3 \quad y(0)=-2 \quad \left. \frac{dy}{dx} \right| _{0}=1 \end {equation}
Solution
  1. Solve the complementary equation \[ \frac{d^2y}{dx^2} - 2 \frac{dy}{dx} + y = 0 \]
    Differentiate and substitute \( y = e^{rx} \) to find and solve the characteristic equation
    We obtain the characteristic equation \[ r^2 -2r + 1 = 0 \]
    Use the method in Example 5.2.2 to find a fundamental set of solutions
    Solving the characteristic equation obtain the solution \( y = e^{x}\) and we must use variation of parameters to find a second solution of the form \( y = ue^{x}\)
    Differentiate \( y = ue^{x}\) and substitute into the complementary equation and solve
    After obtaining \(u=c_1x+c_2\) and we choose a simple \(u=x\) with \(c_1=1\) and \(c_2=0\) and thus we have a fundamental set of solutions \[ y_1 = e^x \quad y_2 = x e^x \]
  2. Now we will try to find a particular solution for (5.3.10) by guessing a solution based on the forcing function \[ \frac{d^2y}{dx^2} - 2 \frac{dy}{dx}+y = \underset{f(x)}{\underbrace{x^2-x-3}} \]
    Since the forcing function \( f(x) = x^2-x-3 \) is a quadratic polynomial, we will guess an arbitrary quadratic polynomial \[ y = Ax^2 + Bx + C \]
    Differentiate \( y = Ax^2 + Bx + C\) twice
    After differentiating we obtain \( dy/dx = 2Ax + B\) and \(d^2y/dx^2 = 2A \) which we substitute into the nonhomogeneous equation (5.3.10) \begin{align*} \frac{d^2y}{dx^2} - 2 \frac{dy}{dx}+y = & x^2-x-3 \\ \overset{\frac{d^2y}{dx^2} }{\overbrace{\Big( 2A \Big)}} - 2 \overset{\frac{dy}{dx} }{\overbrace{\Big( 2Ax + B \Big)}} + \overset{y }{\overbrace{\Big( Ax^2 + Bx + C \Big)}} = & x^2-x-3 \\ \end{align*}
    Distribute the \( - 2\Big( 2Ax + B \Big) \) and collect all the like terms
    Expanding out all the terms, and rearranging them we obtain: \begin{align*} \big( A \big)x^2 + \big( -4A + B \big)x + \big( 2A - 2 B + C \big) = & x^2 - x - 3 \\ \end{align*}
    Just like when using partial fraction decomposition (PFD), we equate the coefficients on both sides of the equation to obtain a system of equations: \begin{align*} \left\{ \begin{array}{r r r} 2A - 2 B + C &=& -3 \\ -4A + B &=& -1 \\ A &=& 1 \end{array} \right. \end{align*}
    Solve the system for \(A,B,\) and \(C\)
    Substituting \(A=1\) into the first two equations we obtain
    \begin{align*} \left\{ \begin{array}{r l} - 2 B + C =&-5 \\ B =&3 \\ \end{array} \right. \end{align*}
    And finally substituting \(B=3\) we obtain \(C=1.\) Now we can substitute the values we obtained for \(A,B,\) and \(C\) in the arbitrary quadratic polynomial \( y = Ax^2 + Bx + C\) to obtain the particular solution \[ y = x^2 + 3x + 1 \]
    Verify \( y = x^2 + 3x + 1 \) is a solution of (5.3.10)
  3. Now that we have a fundamental set of solutions to the complementary equation \( \{ y_1, y_2\} = \{ e^x, xe^x \}\) and a particular solution to the nonhomogeneous equation \( y_p = x^2 + 3x + 1 \), then we can use Theorem 5.3.2 to find the general solution \( y = y_p + c_1 y_1 + c_2 y_2 \) \[ y = x^2 + 3x + 1 + c_1 e^x + c_2 xe^x \]
    Verify \( y = x^2 + 3x + 1 + c_1 e^x + c_2 xe^x \) is a solution of (5.3.10)
  4. Solve the IVP (5.3.11)
    We have the general solution (5.3.10) and now we apply the initial conditions to find the constants.
    Applying \( y(0) = -2 \) we obtain \begin{align*} y(x) = & x^2 + 3x + 1 + c_1 e^x + c_2 xe^x \\ y(0) = & 0 + 0 + 1 + c_1 e^0 + c_2 \cdot 0 \\ -2 = & 1 + c_1 \Longrightarrow c_1 = -3 \\ \end{align*} Substituting it in our solution becomes \begin{align*} y = & x^2 + 3x + 1 - 3 e^x + c_2 xe^x \\ \end{align*}
    Differentiate \( y = x^2 + 3x + 1 -3 e^x + c_2 xe^x \)
    Differentiating we obtain \begin{align*} \frac{dy}{dx} = & 2x + 3 - 3 e^x + c_2 (x + 1)e^x \end{align*}
    Apply the initial condition \( \left. \frac{dy}{dx} \right| _{0}=1 \) to \( \frac{dy}{dx} = 2x + 3 - 2 e^x + c_2 (x + 1)e^x \) to find \( c_2\)
    So we obtain the particular solution of our IVP (5.3.11) \begin{equation} y = x^2 + 3x + 1 +(x-3)e^x \end{equation}
    Verify \( y = x^2 + 3x + 1 +(x-3)e^x \) is a solution of (5.3.10)
Power Functions
Given the nonhomogeneous equation \begin {equation} \label {eq:5.3.13} x^2 \frac{d^2y}{dx^2}+x \frac{dy}{dx}-4y=2x^4 \end {equation}
  1. Solve the complementary equation
  2. Find a particular solution
  3. Find the general solution
Solution
  1. Solve the complementary equation \[ x^2 \frac{d^2y}{dx^2}+x \frac{dy}{dx}-4y = 0 \]
    Although we don't have methods to solve this equation yet since we have only solved constant coefficient equations so far, we did already verify the solutions to this equation in Example 5.1.3 so we already have a fundamental set of solutions: \[ \{y_1,y_2\} = \left\{x^2, \frac{1}{x^2} \right\} \]
  2. Now we will try to find a particular solution for (5.3.13) by guessing a solution based on the forcing function \[ x^2 \frac{d^2y}{dx^2}+x \frac{dy}{dx}-4y = \underset{f(x)}{\underbrace{2x^4}} \]
    Since the forcing function \( f(x) = 2x^4 \) is a power function, we will guess an arbitrary power function of the same power \[ y = Ax^4 \]
    Differentiate \( y = Ax^4 \) twice
    After differentiating we obtain \( dy/dx = 4Ax^3 \) and \(d^2y/dx^2 = 12Ax^2 \) which we substitute into the nonhomogeneous equation (5.3.10) \begin{align*} x^2 \frac{d^2y}{dx^2} + x \frac{dy}{dx}-4y = & 2x^4 \\ x^2 \overset{\frac{d^2y}{dx^2} }{\overbrace{\Big( 12Ax^2 \Big)}} + x \overset{\frac{dy}{dx} }{\overbrace{\Big( 4Ax^3 \Big)}} -4 \overset{y }{\overbrace{\Big( Ax^4 \Big)}} = & 2x^4 \\ \big( 12A \big) x^4 + \underbrace{\big(4A \big) x^4 - \big(4A \big)x^4} = & 2x^4 \\ \big(12A\big)x^4 = & 2x^4 \\ A \, x^4 = & \frac{1}{6}x^4 \\ \end{align*}
    Again equating the coefficients we obtain \(A=1/6\) and substituting the value in the arbitrary power function \( y = Ax^4 \) to obtain the particular solution \[ y = \frac{1}{6} x^4 \]
    Verify \( y = \frac{1}{6} x^4 \) is a solution of (5.3.13)
  3. Now that we have a fundamental set of solutions to the complementary equation \( \{ y_1, y_2\} = \left\{x^2, \frac{1}{x^2} \right\} \) and a particular solution to the nonhomogeneous equation \( y_p = \frac{1}{6} x^4 \), then we can use Theorem 5.3.2 to find the general solution \( y = y_p + c_1 y_1 + c_2 y_2 \) \[ y = \frac{1}{6} x^4 + c_1 x^2 + \frac{c_2}{x^2} \]
    Find the domain of \(y = \frac{1}{6} x^4 + c_1 x^2 + \frac{c_2}{x^2}\)
The Principle of Superposition
The next theorem enables us to break a nonhomogeneous equation into simpler parts, find a particular solution for each part, and then combine their solutions to obtain a particular solution of the original problem.
Theorem 5.3.3 The Principle of Superposition
A particular solution for a differential equation \[ \frac{d^2y}{dx^2}+p(x) \frac{dy}{dx}+q(x)y=f_1(x)+f_2(x) \] May be found by breaking it up into separate differential equations: \[ \frac{d^2y}{dx^2}+p(x) \frac{dy}{dx}+q(x)y=f_1(x) \quad \text{and}\quad \frac{d^2y}{dx^2}+p(x) \frac{dy}{dx}+q(x)y=f_2(x) \]


Therefore if \(y_{p_1}\) is a particular solution of \[ \frac{d^2y}{dx^2}+p(x) \frac{dy}{dx}+q(x)y=f_1(x) \] and \(y_{p_2}\) is a particular solution of \[ \frac{d^2y}{dx^2}+p(x) \frac{dy}{dx}+q(x)y=f_2(x) \]
Then \[ y_p=y_{p_1}+y_{p_2} \] is a particular solution of \[ \frac{d^2y}{dx^2}+p(x) \frac{dy}{dx}+q(x)y=f_1(x)+f_2(x) \]
Proof Substituting \(y_p=y_{p_1}+y_{p_2}\) into \( \frac{d^2y_p}{dx^2}+p(x) \frac{dy_p}{dx} + q(x)y_p \) we obtain \begin{align*} \frac{d^2y_p}{dx^2} + & p(x) \frac{dy_p}{dx} + q(x) y_p \\ \frac{d^2}{dx^2} \Big( y_p \Big) + p(x) & \frac{d}{dx} \Big( y_p \Big) + q(x) \Big( y_p \Big) \\ \frac{d^2}{dx^2} \overset{y_p}{\overbrace{\Big( y_{p_1}+y_{p_2} \Big)}} + p(x) \frac{d}{dx} & \overset{y_p}{\overbrace{\Big( y_{p_1}+y_{p_2} \Big)}} + q(x) \overset{y_p}{\overbrace{\Big( y_{p_1}+y_{p_2} \Big)}} \\ \Bigg[ \frac{d^2}{dx^2} \Big( y_{p_1} \Big) + p(x) \frac{d}{dx} \Big( y_{p_1} \Big) + q(x) \Big( y_{p_1} \Big) \Bigg] +& \Bigg[ \frac{d^2}{dx^2} \Big( y_{p_2} \Big) + p(x) \frac{d}{dx} \Big( y_{p_2} \Big) + q(x) \Big( y_{p_2} \Big) \Bigg] \\ \underset{f_1(x)}{\underbrace{ \Bigg[ \frac{d^2y_{p_1}}{dx^2} + p(x) \frac{dy_{p_1}}{dx} + q(x) y_{p_1} \Bigg]}} +& \underset{f_2(x)}{\underbrace{ \Bigg[ \frac{d^2y_{p_2}}{dx^2} + p(x) \frac{dy_{p_2}}{dx} + q(x) y_{p_2} \Bigg]}} = f_1(x) + f_2(x) \end{align*}

Theorem 5.3.3 generalizes to the equation \begin {equation} \label {eq:5.3.14} \frac{d^2y}{dx^2}+p(x) \frac{dy}{dx}+q(x)y=f_1(x)+f_2(x)+\cdots +f_k(x) \end {equation} If \(y_{p_1}\) is a particular solution of \[ \frac{d^2y}{dx^2}+p(x) \frac{dy}{dx}+q(x)y=f_1(x) \] and \(y_{p_2}\) is a particular solution of \[ \frac{d^2y}{dx^2}+p(x) \frac{dy}{dx}+q(x)y=f_2(x) \] and so on so that finally \(y_{p_k}\) is a particular solution of \[ \frac{d^2y}{dx^2}+p(x) \frac{dy}{dx}+q(x)y=f_k(x) \] Then \[y_{p_1}+y_{p_2}+\cdots +y_{p_k}\] is a particular solution of (5.3.14)



Moreover, by a proof similar to the proof of Theorem 5.3.3 we can formulate the principle of superposition in terms of a linear equation written in the form \[ P_0(x) \frac{d^2y}{dx^2}+P_1(x) \frac{dy}{dx}+P_2(x)y=F(x) \] If \(y_{p_1}\) is a particular solution of \[ P_0(x) \frac{d^2y}{dx^2}+P_1(x) \frac{dy}{dx}+P_2(x)y=F_1(x) \] and \(y_{p_2}\) is a particular solution of \[ P_0(x) \frac{d^2y}{dx^2}+P_1(x) \frac{dy}{dx}+P_2(x)y=F_2(x) \] Then \(y_{p_1}+y_{p_2}\) is a solution of \[ P_0(x) \frac{d^2y}{dx^2}+P_1(x) \frac{dy}{dx}+P_2(x)y=F_1(x)+F_2(x) \]
Given the differential equation \begin {equation} \label {eq:5.3.17} x^2 \frac{d^2y}{dx^2}+4x \frac{dy}{dx}+2y=2x^4+4x^2 \end {equation}
  1. Split the differential equation into two separate differential equations using the principle of superposition
  2. Find the particular solution for the first of the two separate differential equations
  3. Find the particular solution for the second of the two separate differential equations
  4. Use the principle of superposition to find a particular solution for (5.3.15)
Solution
  1. Notice the forcing function on (5.3.15) can be split into two: \begin{align*} x^2 \frac{d^2y}{dx^2}+4x \frac{dy}{dx}+2y= \overset{f(x)}{ \overbrace{ \underset{f_1(x)}{\underbrace{2x^4}}+\underset{f_2(x)}{\underbrace{4x^2}} }} \end{align*} Therefore we can split the differential equation into two equations as (5.3.16) and (5.3.17): \begin{align} \label {eq:5.3.15} x^2 \frac{d^2y}{dx^2}+4x \frac{dy}{dx}+2y = & \overset{f_1(x)}{\overbrace{2x^4}}\\ \label {eq:5.3.16} x^2 \frac{d^2y}{dx^2}+4x \frac{dy}{dx}+2y = & \overset{f_2(x)}{\overbrace{4x^2}} \end{align}
  2. To find a particular solution of (5.3.16) notice their forcing function is a power functions so we guess an arbitrary power function of the same power: \[ y=Ax^4 \]
    Differentiating twice we obtain \begin{align*} \frac{dy}{dx}=4Ax^3 \quad \text{and}& \quad \frac{d^2y}{dx^2}=12Ax^2 \end{align*}
    Substituting into (5.3.16) \begin{align*} x^2 \frac{d^2y}{dx^2}+4x \frac{dy}{dx}+2y = & 2x^4 \\ x^2 \overset{\frac{d^2y}{dx^2} }{\overbrace{\Big( 12Ax^2 \Big)}} + 4x \overset{\frac{dy}{dx} }{\overbrace{\Big( 4Ax^3 \Big)}}+ 2 \overset{y }{\overbrace{\Big( Ax^4 \Big)}} = & 2x^4 \\ \big( 12 A \big) x^4 + \big( 16A \big) x^4 + \big( 2A \big) x^4 = & 2x^4 \\ \big( 30A \big) x^4 = & 2x^4 \\ A \, x^4 = & \frac{1}{15}x^4 \\ \end{align*}
    Equating the coefficients we obtain \(A=1/15\) and substituting the value in the arbitrary power function \( y = Ax^4 \) to obtain the particular solution \[ y = \frac{1}{15} x^4 \]
    Verify \( y = \frac{1}{15} x^4 \) is a solution of (5.3.16)
  3. To find a particular solution of (5.3.17), notice the forcing function is also a power function so we guess an arbitrary power function of the same power: \[ y=Bx^2 \]
    Differentiating twice we obtain \begin{align*} \frac{dy}{dx}=2Bx \quad \text{and}& \quad \frac{d^2y}{dx^2}=2B \end{align*}
    Substituting into (5.3.17) \begin{align*} x^2 \frac{d^2y}{dx^2}+4x \frac{dy}{dx}+2y = & 4x^2 \\ x^2 \overset{\frac{d^2y}{dx^2} }{\overbrace{\Big( 2B \Big)}} + 4x \overset{\frac{dy}{dx} }{\overbrace{\Big( 2Bx \Big)}}+ 2 \overset{y }{\overbrace{\Big( Bx^2 \Big)}} = & 4x^2 \\ \big( 2 B \big) x^2 + \big( 8B \big) x^2 + \big( 2B \big) x^2 = & 4x^2 \\ \big( 12B \big) x^2 = & 4x^2 \\ B \, x^2 = & \frac{1}{3}x^2 \\ \end{align*}
    Equating the coefficients we obtain \(B=1/3\) and substituting the value in the arbitrary power function \( y = Bx^2 \) to obtain the particular solution \[ y = \frac{1}{3} x^2 \]
    Verify \( y = \frac{1}{3} x^2 \) is a solution of (5.3.17)
  4. Since \( y = \frac{1}{15} x^4 \) is a particular solution of (5.3.16) \( y = \frac{1}{3} x^2 \) is a particular solution of (5.3.17) then from the principle of superposition (Theorem 5.3.3) the particular solution of (5.3.15) is \[ y = \frac{1}{15} x^4 + \frac{1}{3} x^2 \]
    Verify \( y = \frac{1}{15} x^4 + \frac{1}{3} x^2 \) is a solution of (5.3.15)
Sines and Cosines
  1. Find a particular solution \begin {equation*} \frac{d^2y}{dx^2} + \frac{dy}{dx} = -8 \cos 2x + 6 \sin 2x \end {equation*}
  2. Find a particular solution \begin {equation*} \frac{d^2y}{dx^2} + \frac{dy}{dx} = 4x^3 \end {equation*}
  3. Use the principle of superposition to find a particular solution for \begin {equation*} \frac{d^2y}{dx^2} + \frac{dy}{dx} = 4x^3 -8 \cos 2x + 6 \sin 2x \end {equation*}
Solution
  1. Solve the complementary equation \[ x^2 \frac{d^2y}{dx^2}+x \frac{dy}{dx}-4y = 0 \]
    Although we don't have methods to solve this equation yet since we have only solved constant coefficient equations so far, we did already verify the solutions to this equation in Example 5.1.3 so we already have a fundamental set of solutions: \[ \{y_1,y_2\} = \left\{x^2, \frac{1}{x^2} \right\} \]
  2. Now we will try to find a particular solution for (5.3.13) by guessing a solution based on the forcing function \[ x^2 \frac{d^2y}{dx^2}+x \frac{dy}{dx}-4y = \underset{f(x)}{\underbrace{2x^4}} \]
    Since the forcing function \( f(x) = 2x^4 \) is a power function, we will guess an arbitrary power function of the same power \[ y = Ax^4 \]
    Differentiate \( y = Ax^4 \) twice
    After differentiating we obtain \( dy/dx = 4Ax^3 \) and \(d^2y/dx^2 = 12Ax^2 \) which we substitute into the nonhomogeneous equation (5.3.10) \begin{align*} x^2 \frac{d^2y}{dx^2} + x \frac{dy}{dx}-4y = & 2x^4 \\ x^2 \overset{\frac{d^2y}{dx^2} }{\overbrace{\Big( 12Ax^2 \Big)}} + x \overset{\frac{dy}{dx} }{\overbrace{\Big( 4Ax^3 \Big)}} -4 \overset{y }{\overbrace{\Big( Ax^4 \Big)}} = & 2x^4 \\ \big( 12A \big) x^4 + \underbrace{\big(4A \big) x^4 - \big(4A \big)x^4} = & 2x^4 \\ \big(12A\big)x^4 = & 2x^4 \\ A \, x^4 = & \frac{1}{6}x^4 \\ \end{align*}
    Again equating the coefficients we obtain \(A=1/6\) and substituting the value in the arbitrary power function \( y = Ax^4 \) to obtain the particular solution \[ y = \frac{1}{6} x^4 \]
    Verify \( y = \frac{1}{6} x^4 \) is a solution of (5.3.13)
  3. Now that we have a fundamental set of solutions to the complementary equation \( \{ y_1, y_2\} = \left\{x^2, \frac{1}{x^2} \right\} \) and a particular solution to the nonhomogeneous equation \( y_p = \frac{1}{6} x^4 \), then we can use Theorem 5.3.2 to find the general solution \( y = y_p + c_1 y_1 + c_2 y_2 \) \[ y = \frac{1}{6} x^4 + c_1 x^2 + \frac{c_2}{x^2} \]
    Find the domain of \(y = \frac{1}{6} x^4 + c_1 x^2 + \frac{c_2}{x^2}\)
