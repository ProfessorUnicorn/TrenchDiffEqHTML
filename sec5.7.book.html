5.7 Variation of Parameters
Variation of Parameters
In this section we give a method called variation of parameters for finding a particular solution of \begin {equation} \label {eq:5.7.1} P_0(x) \frac{d^2y}{dx^2} +P_1(x) \frac{dy}{dx} +P_2(x)y=F(x) \end {equation} If we know a fundamental set \(\{y_1,y_2\}\) of solutions of the complementary equation \begin {equation} \label {eq:5.7.2} P_0(x) \frac{d^2y}{dx^2} +P_1(x) \frac{dy}{dx} +P_2(x)y=0 \end {equation} Having found a particular solution \(y_p\) by this method, we can write the general solution of (5.7.1) as \[ y=y_p+c_1y_1+c_2y_2\]
Since we need only one nontrivial solution of the complementary equation (5.7.2) to find the general solution of (5.7.1) by reduction of order, it’s natural to ask why we’re interested in variation of parameters, which requires two linearly independent solutions of (5.7.2) to achieve the same goal. Here’s the answer:
  • If we already know two linearly independent solutions of (5.7.2) then variation of parameters will probably be simpler than reduction of order.
  • Variation of parameters generalizes naturally to a method for finding particular solutions of higher order linear equations (Section 9.4) and linear systems of equations (Section 10.7), while reduction of order doesn’t.
  • Variation of parameters is a powerful theoretical tool used by researchers in differential equations. Although a detailed discussion of this is beyond the scope of this book, you can get an idea of what it means from Exercises 37–39
We’ll now derive the method. As usual, we consider solutions of (5.7.1) and (5.7.2) on an interval \((a,b)\) where \(P_0\), \(P_1\), \(P_2\), and \(F\) are continuous and \(P_0\) has no zeros. Suppose that \(\{y_1,y_2\}\) is a fundamental set of solutions of the complementary equation (5.7.2). We look for a particular solution of (5.7.1) in the form \begin {equation} \label {eq:5.7.3} y_p=u_1y_1+u_2y_2 \end {equation} where \(u_1\) and \(u_2\) are functions to be determined so that \(y_p\) satisfies (5.7.1). You may not think this is a good idea, since there are now two unknown functions to be determined, rather than one. However, since \(u_1\) and \(u_2\) have to satisfy only one condition, that \(y_p\) is a solution of (5.7.1), we will impose a second condition that produces a convenient simplification, as follows.
Differentiating (5.7.3) yields \begin {equation} \label {eq:5.7.4} \frac{dy_p}{dx} = u_1 \frac{dy_1}{dx} + u_2 \frac{dy_2}{dx} + \underset{\text{set}=0}{\underbrace{\frac{du_1}{dx} y_1 + \frac{du_2}{dx} y_2}} \end {equation} As our second condition on \(u_1\) and \(u_2\) we require that \begin {equation} \label {eq:5.7.5} \frac{du_1}{dx} y_1+ \frac{du_2}{dx} y_2=0 \end {equation} Then (5.7.4) becomes \begin {equation} \label {eq:5.7.6} \frac{dy_p}{dx} =u_1 \frac{dy_1}{dx} +u_2 \frac{dy_2}{dx} \end {equation} that is, (5.7.5) permits us to differentiate \(y_p\) (once!) as if \(u_1\) and \(u_2\) are constants. Differentiating (5.7.4) yields \begin {equation} \label {eq:5.7.7} \frac{d^2y_p}{dx^2} = u_1 \frac{d^2y}{dx^2} _1 + u_2 \frac{d^2y}{dx^2} _2 + \frac{du_1}{dx} \frac{dy_1}{dx} + \frac{du_2}{dx} \frac{dy_2}{dx} \end {equation} Note that there are no terms involving \( \frac{d^2u_1}{dx^2} \) and \( \frac{d^2u_2}{dx^2} \) here, as there would be if we hadn’t required (5.7.5). Substituting (5.7.3), (5.7.6), and (5.7.7) into (5.7.1) and collecting the coefficients of \(u_1\) and \(u_2\) yields \[ u_1 \underset{0}{\underbrace{\Big(P_0 \frac{d^2y}{dx^2} _1+P_1 \frac{dy_1}{dx} +P_2y_1\Big)}} + u_2 \underset{0}{\underbrace{\Big(P_0 \frac{d^2y}{dx^2} _2+P_1 \frac{dy_2}{dx} +P_2y_2\Big)}} + P_0\Big( \frac{du_1}{dx} \frac{dy_1}{dx} + \frac{du_2}{dx} \frac{dy_2}{dx} \Big) = F \] As in the derivation of the method of reduction of order, the coefficients of \(u_1\) and \(u_2\) here are both zero because \(y_1\) and \(y_2\) satisfy the complementary equation. Hence, we can rewrite the last equation as \begin {equation} \label {eq:5.7.8} P_0 \Big( \frac{du_1}{dx} \frac{dy_1}{dx} + \frac{du_2}{dx} \frac{dy_2}{dx} \Big) = F \end {equation} Therefore \(y_p\) in (5.7.3) satisfies (5.7.1) if \begin{align*} \left\{ \begin{array}{rrl} \label {eq:5.7.9} \displaystyle\frac{du_1}{dx} y_1+ \frac{du_2}{dx} y_2 &=& 0 \nonumber \\ \nonumber \\ \displaystyle\frac{du_1}{dx} \frac{dy_1}{dx} + \frac{du_2}{dx} \frac{dy_2}{dx} &=& \displaystyle\frac{F}{P_0} \end{array} \right. \end{align*} where the first equation is the same as (5.7.5) and the second is from (5.7.8)
We’ll now show that you can always solve (5.7.9) for \( \displaystyle \frac{du_1}{dx} \) and \( \displaystyle \frac{du_2}{dx} \).
The method that we use here will always work, but simpler methods usually work when you’re dealing with specific equations. To obtain \( \displaystyle \frac{du_1}{dx} \), multiply the first equation in (5.7.9) by \( \displaystyle \frac{dy_2}{dx} \) and the second equation by \(y_2\). This yields \begin{array}{rrrll} \displaystyle\frac{du_1}{dx} \frac{dy_2}{dx} y_1 &+& \displaystyle\frac{du_2}{dx} \frac{dy_2}{dx} y_2 = & 0 \nonumber \\ \nonumber \\ - \quad\quad \Big(\,\, \displaystyle\frac{du_1}{dx} \frac{dy_1}{dx} y_2 &+& \displaystyle\frac{du_2}{dx} \frac{dy_2}{dx} y_2 = & \displaystyle\frac{Fy_2}{P_0} \,\, \Big) \\ \hline \frac{du_1}{dx} \left( \frac{dy_2}{dx} y_1 - \frac{dy_1}{dx} y_2 \right) &+& \Big( \, 0 \, \Big) \, y_2 = & -\frac{Fy_2}{P_0} \nonumber \\ \nonumber \\ \end{array}
Subtracting the second equation from the first yields \begin {align} \label {eq:5.7.10} \frac{du_1}{dx} \, \underbrace{\Big(y_1 \frac{dy_2}{dx} - \frac{dy_1}{dx} y_2 \Big)} = & -{Fy_2\over P_0} \\ \frac{du_1}{dx} \underbrace{\left| \begin{array}{cc} y_1 & y_2 \nonumber \\ \displaystyle\frac{dy_1}{dx} & \displaystyle\frac{dy_2}{dx} \\ \end{array} \right|} = & -{Fy_2\over P_0} \\ \frac{du_1}{dx} \, W[y_1,y_2] = & -{Fy_2\over P_0} \\ \end {align} Since \(\{y_1,y_2\}\) is a fundamental set of solutions of (5.7.2) on \((a,b)\), Theorem 5.1.6 implies that the Wronskian \(W[y_1,y_2]\) has no zeros on \((a,b)\). Therefore we can solve (5.7.10) for \( \displaystyle\frac{du_1}{dx} \), to obtain \begin {align} \label {eq:5.7.11} \frac{du_1}{dx} = & -\frac{Fy_2}{P_0 \, W[y_1,y_2]} \\ \end {align} We leave it to you to start from (5.7.9) and show by a similar argument that \begin {equation} \label {eq:5.7.12} \frac{du_2}{dx} ={Fy_1\over P_0 \, \, W[y_1,y_2]} \end {equation}
We can now obtain \(u_1\) and \(u_2\) by integrating \( \displaystyle\frac{du_1}{dx} \) and \( \displaystyle\frac{du_2}{dx} \). The constants of integration can be taken to be zero, since any choice of \(u_1\) and \(u_2\) in (5.7.3) will suffice.
We will not memorize (5.7.11) and (5.7.12) to apply as formulas. As we learn the method we will start by deriving the two equations ourselves:
Variation of Parameters
  1. Establish a fundamental set of solutions to the complementary equation \(\{y_1,y_2\}\) and the write \(y_p\) as \begin {equation} \label {eq:5.7.13} y_p=u_1y_1+u_2y_2 \end {equation}
  2. Differentiate (5.7.13) using the product rule and set the \(u\) derivatives to zero to obtain \begin {equation*} \frac{dy_p}{dx}=u_1\frac{dy_1}{dx}+u_2\frac{dy_2}{dx}+ \underset{\frac{du_1}{dx} y_1+ \frac{du_2}{dx} y_2 = 0}{\underbrace{\displaystyle\frac{du_1}{dx}y_1+\frac{du_2}{dx}y_2}} \end {equation*}
  3. Differentiate \(\frac{dy_p}{dx}\) using the product rule to obtain \begin {equation*} \frac{d^2y_p}{dx^2} =\frac{du_1}{dx}\frac{dy_1}{dx}+\frac{du_2}{dx}\frac{dy_2}{dx} + u_1\frac{d^2y_1}{dx^2}+u_2\frac{d^2y_2}{dx^2} \end {equation*}
  4. Substitute \(y_p ,\) \({dy_p}/{dx},\) and \({d^2y_p}/{dx^2} ,\) into the differential equation and simplify to obtain the system of two equations and two unknowns (the \(u\) derivatives \begin{align}\label {eq:5.7.14} \left\{ \begin{array}{rrl} \displaystyle\frac{du_1}{dx} y_1+ \frac{du_2}{dx} y_2 &=& 0 \\ \\ \displaystyle\frac{du_1}{dx} \frac{dy_1}{dx} + \frac{du_2}{dx} \frac{dy_2}{dx} &=& \displaystyle\frac{F}{P_0} \end{array} \right. \end{align}
  5. Solve the system (5.7.14) for \( \displaystyle\frac{du_1}{dx} \) and \( \displaystyle\frac{du_2}{dx} \) using substitution and elimination
  6. Obtain \(u_1\) and \(u_2\) by finding the antiderivatives \( \displaystyle\frac{du_1}{dx} \) and \( \displaystyle \frac{du_2}{dx} \) (taking the constants of integration to be zero)
  7. Substitute \(u_1\) and \(u_2\) into (5.7.13) to obtain \(y_p\)
  8. Check if any terms of \(y_p\) are linearly dependent on \(y_1\) or \(y_2\) and if so, remove from \(y_p\)
Variation of Parameters Examples
Consider the differential equation \begin {equation} \label {eq:5.7.15} x^2 \frac{d^2y}{dx^2} -2x \frac{dy}{dx} +2y=x^{9/2} \end {equation}
  1. Verify that \(y_1=x\) and \(y_2=x^2\) are solutions of the complementary equation
  2. Find the first equation in the system by differentiating \(y_p\) and setting the \(u\) derivative terms to zero
  3. Find the second equation in the system by substituting \(y_p,\) \(dy_p/dx,\) and \(d^2y_p/dx^2\) into (5.7.15)
  4. Write the system of equations as a system for \(du_1/dx\) and \(du_2/dx\)
  5. Find the antiderivatives to find \(u_1\) and \(u_2\)
  6. Find the particular solution \(y_p\) and the general solution
Solution
  1. We wish to verify that \(y_1=x\) is a solution of the complementary equation: \begin {equation*} \frac{d^2y}{dx^2} -2x \frac{dy}{dx} +2y=0 \end {equation*}
    Differentiate \(y_1=x\) twice
    Differentiating \(y_1=x\) we obtain \[ \frac{d y_1}{dx} = 1 \quad \text{and} \quad \frac{d^2 y_1}{dx^2} = 0 \]
    Substituting \(y_1=x\) and its derivatives in to the complementary equation \begin{align*} x^2\frac{d^2y_1}{dx^2} -2x \frac{dy_1}{dx} +2y_1 \overset{?}{=} & 0 \\ x^2\Big( 0 \Big) -2x \Big( 1 \Big) +2 \Big( x \Big) \overset{?}{=} & 0 \\ \underset{0}{\underbrace{-2x + 2x}} = 0 \quad \checkmark \end{align*} Therefore \(y_1=x\) is a solution to the complementary equation

    Now we wish to verify that \(y_2=x^2\) is a solution of the complementary equation: \begin {equation*} \frac{d^2y}{dx^2} -2x \frac{dy}{dx} +2y=0 \end {equation*}
    Differentiate \(y_2=x^2\) twice
    Differentiating \(y_2=x^2\) we obtain \[ \frac{d y_2}{dx} = 2x \quad \text{and} \quad \frac{d^2 y_2}{dx^2} = 2 \]
    Substituting \(y_2=x^2\) and its derivatives in to the complementary equation \begin{align*} x^2\frac{d^2y_2}{dx^2} -2x \frac{dy_2}{dx} +2y_2 \overset{?}{=} & 0 \\ x^2\Big( 2 \Big) -2x \Big( 2x \Big) +2 \Big( x^2 \Big) \overset{?}{=} & 0 \\ \underset{0}{\underbrace{2x^2 - 4x^2 + 2x^2}} = 0 \quad \checkmark \end{align*} Therefore \(y_2=x^2\) is a solution to the complementary equation


  2. We will find the first equation in the system using variation of parameters by differentiating \(y_p=u_1y_1 + u_2y_2\) and setting the \(u\) derivative terms to zero. We start with the particular solution \[ y_p=u_1 x + u_2 x^2 \]
    Differentiate \(y_p=u_1 x + u_2 x^2\) and set the \(u\) derivatives equal to zero
    Differentiating \(y_p\) we obtain \[ \frac{dy_p}{dx}=u_1 + 2u_2 x + \underset{0}{\underbrace{x \frac{du_1}{dx} + x^2 \frac{du_2}{dx} }} \] And setting the \(u\) derivatives equal to zero we obtain the first equation in the system of equations \[ x \frac{du_1}{dx} + x^2 \frac{du_2}{dx} = 0 \]
  3. Now we will find the second equation in the system by substituting \(y_p,\) \(dy_p/dx,\) and \(d^2y_p/dx^2\) into (5.7.15)
    We have \(y_p\) and \(dy_p/dx\) \begin{align*} y_p = & u_1 x + u_2 x^2 \\ \frac{dy_p}{dx} = & u_1 + 2u_2 x \\ \end{align*}
    Differentiate \(\displaystyle\frac{dy_p}{dx}=u_1 + 2u_2 x\)
    Differentiating \(dy_p/dx\) we obtain \[ \frac{d^2y_p}{dx^2}= \frac{du_1}{dx} + 2x\frac{du_2}{dx} + 2u_2 \] And now we substitute \(y_p,\) \(dy_p/dx,\) and \(d^2y_p/dx^2\) into (5.7.15)
    \begin{array}{rrrrrrrr} x^2 \displaystyle\frac{d^2y_p}{dx^2} &=& x^2 \displaystyle\frac{du_1}{dx} &+& 2x^3 \displaystyle\frac{du_2}{dx} & & & & &+& 2\,u_2x^2 \\ -2 \displaystyle\frac{dy_p}{dx} &=& && && &-& 2\,u_1 x &-& 4\,u_2x^2 \\ +\quad\quad\quad 2y_p &=& && && &+& 2\,u_1 x &+& 2\,u_2x^2 \\ \hline \underbrace{{\small x^2 \displaystyle\frac{d^2y_p}{dx^2} -2x\frac{dy_p}{dx} 2y}} &=& x^2 \displaystyle\frac{du_1}{dx} &+& 2x^3 \displaystyle\frac{du_2}{dx} & & &+& 0\,u_1x &+& 0\,u_2x^2 \\ x^{9/2} \quad\quad &=& x^2 \displaystyle\frac{du_1}{dx} &+& 2x^3 \displaystyle\frac{du_2}{dx} & & & & & & \\ \end{array}
    Divide \( x^{9/2} = x^2 \displaystyle\frac{du_1}{dx} +2x^3\frac{du_2}{dx}\) by \(x^2\)
    Dividing by \(x^2\) to simplify we obtain the second equation in our system of equations: \[ \frac{du_1}{dx} + 2x\frac{du_2}{dx} = x^{5/2} \]
  4. Write the system of equations as a system and solve using substitution and elimination \begin{align*} \left\{ \begin{array}{rrr} x \displaystyle\frac{du_1}{dx} &+& x^2 \displaystyle\frac{du_2}{dx} &=& 0 \quad\quad\quad\quad (1) \\ \displaystyle\frac{du_1}{dx} &+& 2x \displaystyle\frac{du_2}{dx} &=& x^{5/2} \quad\quad\quad\quad (2) \end{array} \right. \end{align*}
    Solve the system for \(\displaystyle\frac{du_1}{dx}\) and \(\displaystyle\frac{du_2}{dx}\)
    Solving the first equation (1) for \(\displaystyle\frac{du_1}{dx}\) \[ \frac{du_1}{dx} = -x \frac{du_2}{dx} \quad\quad\quad\quad (3) \] Substituting in to the second equation (2) \begin{align*} \underbrace{\frac{du_1}{dx}} + 2x \frac{du_2}{dx} =& x^{5/2} \\ -x \frac{du_2}{dx} + 2x \frac{du_2}{dx} =& x^{5/2} \\ x \frac{du_2}{dx} =& x^{5/2} \Longrightarrow \frac{du_2}{dx} = x^{3/2} \end{align*}
    Now substituting this into the third equation (3) \begin{align*} \frac{du_1}{dx} = & -x \underbrace{\frac{du_2}{dx}} \\ \frac{du_1}{dx} = & -x \cdot x^{3/2} \Longrightarrow \frac{du_1}{dx} = -x^{5/2} \end{align*}
    So we have found \(du_1/dx\) and \(du_2/dx\) \[ \frac{du_1}{dx} = -x^{5/2} \quad \quad \frac{du_2}{dx} = x^{3/2} \]
  5. Having found \(du_1/dx\) and \(du_2/dx\) we now simplify antidifferentiate to find \(u_1\) and \(u_2\)
    Antidifferentiate \(-x^{5/2} \)
    Antidifferentiate \( x^{3/2} \)
    Finding the antiderivatives and taking the constants of integration to be zero we obtain \(u_1\) and \(u_2\) \begin{array}{rccrcl} \displaystyle\frac{du_1}{dx} &=& -x^{5/2} &\quad& \displaystyle\frac{du_2}{dx} &=& x^{3/2} \\ \int \displaystyle\frac{du_1}{dx} dx &=& -\int x^{5/2}dx &\quad& \int \displaystyle\frac{du_2}{dx} dx &=& \int x^{3/2}dx \\ u_1 &=& -\displaystyle\frac{2}{7} x^{7/2} &\quad& u_2 &=& \displaystyle\frac{2}{5} x^{5/2} \\ \end{array}
  6. Substituting back \(y_p = u_1 x + u_2 x^2 \) we obtain the particular solution: \begin{align*} y_p = & -\frac{2}{7} x^{7/2} \cdot x + \frac{2}{5} x^{5/2} \cdot x^2 \\ = & -\frac{2}{7} x^{9/2} + \frac{2}{5} x^{9/2} \\ = & \frac{4}{35} x^{9/2} \\ \end{align*}
    Verify \(y_p=\frac{4}{35} x^{9/2}\) is a solution of (5.7.15)
    Finally we have the general solution \(y = y_p + c_1 y_1 + c_2 y_2 \) \[ y = \frac{4}{35} x^{9/2} + c_1 x + c_2 x^2 \]
    Verify \(y=\frac{4}{35} x^{9/2} + c_1 x + c_2 x^2\) is a solution of (5.7.15)
Consider the differential equation \begin {equation} \label {eq:5.7.16} (x-1) \frac{d^2y}{dx^2} -x \frac{dy}{dx} +y=(x-1)^2 \end {equation}
  1. Verify that \(y_1=x\) and \(y_2=e^x\) are solutions of the complementary equation
  2. Find the first equation in the system by differentiating \(y_p\) and setting the \(u\) derivative terms to zero
  3. Find the second equation in the system by substituting \(y_p,\) \(dy_p/dx,\) and \(d^2y_p/dx^2\) into (5.7.16)
  4. Write the system of equations as a system for \(du_1/dx\) and \(du_2/dx\)
  5. Find the antiderivatives to find \(u_1\) and \(u_2\)
  6. Find the particular solution \(y_p\) and the general solution
Solution
  1. We wish to verify that \(y_1=x\) and \(y_2=e^x\) is a solution of the complementary equation: \begin {equation*} (x-1) \frac{d^2y}{dx^2} -x \frac{dy}{dx} +y = 0 \end {equation*}
    Differentiate \(y_1=x\) twice and substitute into the complementary equation
    Differentiate \(y_2=e^x\) twice and substitute into the complementary equation
    Substituting \(y_1=x\) and \(y_2=e^x\) into the complementary equation you can verify that they are solutions
  2. We will find the first equation in the system using variation of parameters by differentiating \(y_p=u_1y_1 + u_2y_2\) and setting the \(u\) derivative terms to zero. We start with the particular solution \[ y_p=u_1 x + u_2 e^x \]
    Differentiate \(y_p=u_1 x + u_2 e^x\) and set the \(u\) derivatives equal to zero
    Differentiating \(y_p\) we obtain \[ \frac{dy_p}{dx}= u_1 + u_2 e^x + \underset{0}{\underbrace{x \frac{du_1}{dx} + e^x \frac{du_2}{dx} }} \] And setting the \(u\) derivatives equal to zero we obtain the first equation in the system of equations \[ x \frac{du_1}{dx} + e^x \frac{du_2}{dx} = 0 \]
  3. Now we will find the second equation in the system by substituting \(y_p,\) \(dy_p/dx,\) and \(d^2y_p/dx^2\) into (5.7.16)
    We have \(y_p\) and \(dy_p/dx\) \begin{align*} y_p = & u_1 x + u_2 e^x \\ \frac{dy_p}{dx} = & u_1 + u_2 e^x \\ \end{align*}
    Differentiate \(\displaystyle\frac{dy_p}{dx}=u_1 + u_2 e^x\)
    Differentiating \(dy_p/dx\) we obtain \[ \frac{d^2y_p}{dx^2}= \frac{du_1}{dx} + e^x \frac{du_2}{dx} + u_2 e^x \] And now we substitute \(y_p,\) \(dy_p/dx,\) and \(d^2y_p/dx^2\) into (5.7.16)
    \begin{array}{rrrrrrrr} x \displaystyle\frac{d^2y_p}{dx^2} &=& x \displaystyle\frac{du_1}{dx} &+& x e^x \displaystyle\frac{du_2}{dx} & & & & & & &+& u_2 x e^x \\ -1 \displaystyle\frac{d^2y_p}{dx^2} &=& - \displaystyle\frac{du_1}{dx} &-& e^x \displaystyle\frac{du_2}{dx} & & & & &-& u_2 e^x \\ -x \displaystyle\frac{dy_p}{dx} &=& && && &-& u_1 x & & &-& u_2 x e^x \\ +\quad\quad\quad y_p &=& && && &+& u_1 x &+& u_2 e^x \\ \hline \underbrace{{\small (x-1) \displaystyle\frac{d^2y_p}{dx^2} -x \displaystyle\frac{dy_p}{dx} +y_p }} &=& {\small (x-1)} \displaystyle\frac{du_1}{dx} &+& {\small (x-1) e^x } \displaystyle\frac{du_2}{dx} & & &+& 0\,u_1x &+& 0\,u_2 e^x &+& 0 \, u_2 x e^x \\ (x-1)^2 \quad\quad &=& {\small (x-1)} \displaystyle\frac{du_1}{dx} &+& {\small (x-1) e^x } \displaystyle\frac{du_2}{dx} & & & & & & \\ \end{array}
    Divide \( (x-1)^2 = (x-1) \displaystyle\frac{du_1}{dx} + (x-1) e^x \displaystyle\frac{du_2}{dx} \) by \((x-1)\)
    Dividing by \((x-1)\) to simplify we obtain the second equation in our system of equations: \[ \frac{du_1}{dx} + e^x \frac{du_2}{dx} = x-1 \]
  4. Write the system of equations as a system and solve using substitution and elimination \begin{align*} \left\{ \begin{array}{rrr} x \displaystyle\frac{du_1}{dx} &+& e^x \displaystyle\frac{du_2}{dx} &=& 0 \quad\quad\quad\quad (1) \\ \displaystyle\frac{du_1}{dx} &+& e^x \displaystyle\frac{du_2}{dx} &=& x-1 \quad\quad\quad\quad (2) \\ \end{array} \right. \end{align*}
    Solve the system for \(\displaystyle\frac{du_1}{dx}\) and \(\displaystyle\frac{du_2}{dx}\)
    Subtracting the first equation (1) from the second (2) \begin{array}{rrrrl} \displaystyle\frac{du_1}{dx} &+& e^x \displaystyle\frac{du_2}{dx} &=& x-1 \\ - \quad\quad \Big( \quad\quad\quad x \displaystyle\frac{du_1}{dx} &+& e^x \displaystyle\frac{du_2}{dx} &=& 0 \quad\quad\quad \Big) \\ \hline (1-x)\displaystyle\frac{du_1}{dx} & & &=& x-1 \\ \end{array}
    Solve \((1-x)\displaystyle\frac{du_1}{dx} = x-1 \) for \( \displaystyle\frac{du_1}{dx} \)
    Dividing by \((1-x)\) and simplifying we obtain \[ \frac{du_1}{dx} = -1 \]
    Substituting that back in to the first equation (1) \begin{align*} x \underbrace{\frac{du_1}{dx}} + e^x \frac{du_2}{dx} = & 0 \\ x \cdot -1 + e^x \frac{du_2}{dx} = & 0 \\ -x + e^x \frac{du_2}{dx} = & 0 \Longrightarrow \frac{du_2}{dx} = xe^{-x} \\ \end{align*}
    So we have found \(du_1/dx\) and \(du_2/dx\) \[ \frac{du_1}{dx} = -1 \quad \quad \frac{du_2}{dx} = xe^{-x} \]
  5. Having found \(du_1/dx\) and \(du_2/dx\) we now simplify antidifferentiate to find \(u_1\) and \(u_2\)

Comparison of Methods
We’ve now considered three methods for solving nonhomogeneous linear equations: undetermined coefficients, reduction of order, and variation of parameters. It’s natural to ask which method is best for a given problem.
The method of undetermined coefficients could be used for constant coefficient equations with forcing functions that are linear combinations of polynomials multiplied by functions of the form of a polynomial \(P(x) = a_0 + a_1 x + a_2 x^2 + \cdots +a_n x^n\), an exponential \(e^{r x}\), a sine or cosine \(\sin \omega x\) or \(\cos \omega x\), or any of those multiplied together \(P(x)e^{r x}\), \(e^{r x}\cos \omega x\) or \(e^{r x}\sin \omega x\), P(x)\(e^{r x}\cos \omega x\) or P(x)\(e^{r x}\sin \omega x\). Although the other two methods can be used to solve such problems, they will be more difficult except in the most trivial cases, because of the integrations involved.
If the equation isn’t a constant coefficient equation or the forcing function isn’t of the form specified, the method of undetermined coefficients does not apply and the choice between reduction of order and variation of parameters
The case could be made that reduction of order is better because it requires only one solution of the complementary equation while variation of parameters requires two. However, variation of parameters will probably be easier if you already know a fundamental set of solutions of the complementary equation.
